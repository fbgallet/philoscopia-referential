# Antigone

*Character from « Antigone » (Sophocles, 441 BCE)*

Daughter of Oedipus, Antigone stands at the heart of Sophocles' tragedy: Creon, the new ruler of Thebes, has forbidden, on pain of death, the burial of her brother Polynices, fallen while assaulting the city. She stands in that bare conflict between a young woman's conscience and the decree of power, between what the city commands and what she holds owed to the gods and to her own. Reworked by Anouilh in 1944, she became the very figure of solitary refusal, the one who says no in the name of a demand higher than the state.

## Core positions

1. [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md) : **The gods' unwritten laws are not of yesterday or today, and no mortal's decree can override them.**
2. [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md) : **I did it, I deny nothing: no edict of the city binds me where it commands against the gods and against my own.**
3. [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md) : **I was born to share in love, not in hate: it is to my brother, who cannot be replaced, that I owe what I do.**
4. [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md) : **The letter of a human decree weighs nothing against what justice owes the dead, and on that due I will not compromise.**

## Positions

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

**Declared** : Transcendence 90% · Immanence 10%

**Practiced** : Transcendence 85% · Immanence 15%

Summoned by Creon to account for herself, she pleads neither mistake nor ignorance: she sets against his decree an order that overruns it. It was not the gods, she answers, who forbade her act, and no mortal could have the strength to override heaven's unwritten and unshakable laws, which are not of yesterday or today. Her deed, performing the rites in defiance of the edict, carries this conviction into action before she puts it into words: human order is legitimate only so far as it does not collide with what precedes and overhangs it.

*Sources : Second episode, confronting Creon (ll. 450-457), the unwritten laws speech · First episode, the guard's report: the rites performed despite the ban*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

**Practiced** : Anarchism 80% · Authority 20%

Brought before the king, she neither evades nor denies: "I did it, I deny nothing." This head-on avowal is the core of her defiance: she does not disobey in secret but contests Creon's very right to command this. What is at stake is not anarchy: against power she sets another authority, that of the gods and of the due owed the dead, older than the city. Where the edict claims to bind conscience, she holds that no decree grounds an obedience that would violate this higher order.

*Sources : Prologue, dialogue with Ismene: she announces she will defy the edict · Second episode: "I did it, I deny nothing"*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

**Declared** : Equity-phronesis 80% · Legalism 20%

**Practiced** : Equity-phronesis 80% · Legalism 20%

Facing Creon, she does not debate the decree's terms: she denies it from the outset any force against what justice owes the dead, the rule a mortal wrote weighing nothing against a due older than itself. Yet her stance is no search for supple equity: where Ismene pleads measure and submission to the stronger, Antigone cuts through without accommodation, as if the spirit of divine law admitted no shading. To the letter of power she opposes, not a compromise, but another letter, an intransigent one.

*Sources : Second episode (ll. 450-470): a mortal's edict cannot override the laws of the gods · Prologue: refusal to heed the ban despite Ismene's warnings*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

**Declared** : Partialism 85% · Impartialism 15%

**Practiced** : Partialism 85% · Impartialism 15%

What sets her in motion is not an abstract duty toward all the dead, but a face: Polynices, her brother. From the prologue she demands to act for him, and declares she was born to share in love, not in hate. She goes further, in a famous and disputed line of reasoning: a husband or a child could be replaced, but a brother, her parents being dead, will never return, which makes this bond of blood irreplaceable and grounds the priority she grants it. However universal in form the divine law she invokes may be, she exercises it wholly for her own.

*Sources : Prologue, dialogue with Ismene: the duty owed to Polynices · Second episode (l. 523): "I was born to join in love, not in hate" · Fourth episode (ll. 904-915, contested passage): a brother is irreplaceable*

Antigone refuses all concealment: she orders Ismene to proclaim her deed to the whole city rather than keep it silent, performs the second rite in broad daylight, and confesses without evasion before Creon. The prudent lie, even a protective one, is hateful to her.
