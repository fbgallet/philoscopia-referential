# Callicles

*Character from « Gorgias » (Plato, 380 BCE)*

An ambitious young Athenian of the deme of Acharnae, host to the sophist [Gorgias](../philosophers/gorgias.md) and about to enter political life, Callicles is the last and most formidable of the three interlocutors of [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s *[Gorgias](../works/gorgias-plato.md)*. Where Gorgias and then Polus retreat, overcome by shame, he takes up the argument without disguise and pushes to its limit the thesis the others dared not own: received morality is only a trap laid for the strong by the multitude of the weak. A character perhaps drawn from a real man but known to us through Plato alone, he embodies one of the most formidable adversaries Socrates ever had to face, close to the [Thrasymachus](../philosophers/thrasymachus.md) of the Republic and a figure whom readers of [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) have often likened to his morality of masters.

## Core positions

1. [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md) : **What is called justice is only the [convention](../glossary/physis-nomos.md) by which the weak bridle the strong; by nature, the better should rule the inferior and possess more.**
2. [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : **To live well is not to prune one's desires but to let them grow as large as they can, then to have the strength to fill them: license is virtue itself.**
3. [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md) : **To content oneself with an equal share when one is worth more is a slave's soul: the superior man pursues his own advantage and takes what his strength affords him.**
4. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **The pleasant and the good are one: the sovereign good is to feel as much [pleasure](../glossary/hedonism.md) as possible, and nothing else.**
5. [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md) : **Philosophy is a child's game: past youth it makes a man ridiculous and defenceless; life is in action, not in self-examination.**

## Positions

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Relativism 60% · Universalism 20% · Constructivism 20%

Callicles unmasks received justice as a contrivance: the weak, being the majority, framed the laws and the words "just" and "unjust" to bridle the strong and secure equality for themselves. That justice is mere [convention](../glossary/physis-nomos.md) (nomos), variable and self-serving; the only real norm is that of nature (physis), where the better rules and holds more. His position thus blends a corrosive relativism toward established morality with the assertion of a natural right of the stronger that he takes to be a fact.

> “Nature herself shows that it is just for the better to have more than the worse, and the more capable than the less.”, Gorgias, 483d

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 482e-484c (the nature/law distinction) · Plato, Gorgias, 483b-d (the weak make the laws)*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Affirmation 90% · Reasoned selection 10%

Against temperance, he holds that living well means letting one's desires grow as large as possible instead of pruning them, then having the courage to fill them; [license](../glossary/akolasia.md) unleashed without restraint is virtue itself. Socrates counters with the leaky jar, the insatiable soul no inflow can fill; Callicles embraces it, preferring the life that ceaselessly streams and refills to that of the stone, motionless and sated.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 491e-492c (the praise of intemperance) · Plato, Gorgias, 493a-494c (the leaky jar)*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Ethical egoism 95% · Altruism 5%

His principle is that each should pursue their own advantage and that the superior man is right to have more ([pleonexia](../glossary/pleonexia.md)): to content oneself with an equal share, when one is worth more, is the mark of a slave. Self-sacrifice and moderation are the values by which the weak tame the strong.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 483c-d · Plato, Gorgias, 490a-491d*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Hedonism 90% · Eudaimonism 10%

Pressed to say what the good is, he flatly identifies the pleasant and the good: to live well is to feel as much [pleasure](../glossary/hedonism.md) as possible. This is not Epicurus's measured [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md) but a hedonism of intensity. Cornered by Socrates, who makes him grant that even base pleasures count, he gives ground, but the opening thesis remains his own avowal.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 494e-495a (the pleasant and the good are one) · Plato, Gorgias, 495a-499b (the Socratic refutation)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Primacy of action 95% · The examined life 5%

To Socratic self-examination he opposes that philosophy is charming in a young man but makes the grown man who lingers over it ridiculous and helpless, ignorant of the laws and of affairs, exposed to being slapped with no recourse. Life lies in action, reputation and power; he presses Socrates to give up such trifles for "the music of affairs".

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 484c-486d (the praise then the critique of philosophy)*

### [The point of philosophy](../axes/metaphilosophy.md)

*rejects the alternative · major*

Callicles picks no function for philosophy: his position is that it has none. Fit at most as a formative game for the young, it is a worthless pastime for the grown man, whom he urges to abandon it for action and power.

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Celebration 95% · Asceticism 5%

Extending his praise of desire, he takes bodily pleasures to be the very stuff of happiness: the strong man has the right to procure them amply; asceticism is only a morality of impotence dressed up as wisdom.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 491e-492c*

### [Political liberty](../axes/political-liberty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Negative liberty 85% · Republican non-domination 15%

Liberty, for him, is the absence of all restraint; he expressly rejects liberty as self-government, judging it servitude: how could one be happy who is a slave to anything, even to one's own temperance? The only freedom is to do as one pleases, appetites unfurled.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 491e-492c*

### [Who should decide](../axes/who-decides.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Epistocracy 80% · Radical democracy 20%

To rule and to hold more belongs by right to "the better and more intelligent", not to the crowd that chance assembles; his criterion of the "better" is not the Socratic knowledge of the good but the capacity to will and to obtain. It is an aristocracy of nature, at the opposite pole from Athenian democratic equality.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 483c-d; 490a*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Naturalism 90% · Culturalism 10%

His whole case erects [nature](../glossary/physis-nomos.md) into a norm: what beasts do among themselves, cities among themselves, the strong over the weak, that is justice "according to nature", which human convention only masks. He cites the conquests, the expedition of Xerxes, and Pindar's line making law the "king" that justifies the violence of the stronger.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 483d-484b (nature, animals, cities, Pindar)*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological pessimism 80% · Anthropological optimism 20%

Everyone, if they dared, would pursue their own advantage and want to possess more than others: displayed moderation is only an admission of weakness or a ruse. This bedrock of egoism and rivalry, which law contains without abolishing, places him on the side of an anthropological pessimism.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 483b-c; 491e-492a*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Libertarianism 78% · Egalitarianism 22%

He contests equal shares as an extortion by the weak: just distribution, according to nature, gives more to the superior, not an identical portion to all. His rejection of egalitarianism rests not on self-ownership or the desert of labor but on natural superiority; it is by this refusal of equal sharing that he meets, from afar, the anti-redistributive side.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 483c; 490a-491a*

### [Violence](../axes/violence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Force as an instrument of politics 70% · Just war 15% · Emancipatory violence 15%

Since nature gives the strong the right to take, coercion and conquest are not an evil to be contained but the normal expression of order: Pindar's line and the example of Xerxes marching on Greece serve him to legitimate force as the ground of right. The violence of the powerful need not justify itself before a morality: it is justice itself.

*Sources : Plato, Gorgias, 483d-484b*
