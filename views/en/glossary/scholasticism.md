# Scholasticism

An approach to teaching and thought in the medieval schools and universities, from the eleventh to the fifteenth century, whose name comes from the Latin scholasticus, 'of the school': it works by commentary on authorities and by the disputed question, weighing objections and replies to reconcile reason with revealed faith. It is not, strictly speaking, a current with shared theses, but a style of intellectual work: its masters disagree on almost everything, from the quarrel of universals, between [realism](../glossary/realism-of-universals.md) and [nominalism](../glossary/nominalism.md), to the freedom of the will. In the strict sense scholasticism is that of the Latin Christian schools, but the same undertaking among Jewish and Muslim thinkers, from [Averroes](../philosophers/averroes.md) and [Maimonides](../philosophers/maimonides.md) to [Thomas Aquinas](../philosophers/aquinas.md), goes by the same name: a disparate prolongation of [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md), whose logic and metaphysics are used to think through revelation.
