# Aristotle

*384 BCE – 322 BCE · Greek · [Aristotelianism](../movements/aristotelianism.md)*

Born at Stagira in Macedon, Aristotle joined [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s Academy at seventeen, studying there for some twenty years before becoming tutor to the young Alexander and then founding his own school at Athens, the Lyceum. Antiquity's encyclopedist, he wrote on logic, physics, biology, metaphysics, ethics, politics and poetics, founding or reorganizing most of these disciplines. His method starts from observed phenomena and received opinions (the **endoxa**), surveys the difficulties, then seeks causes and principles: a patient mode of inquiry, often turned against his master's theses. Transmitted by the Greek, then Arabic and Latin commentators, his thought became in the Middle Ages "the Philosopher" without qualification and the framework of scholasticism, before being challenged by modern science and reread ceaselessly down to our own day.

## Core positions

1. [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : **Nature does nothing in vain: every being tends toward an end inscribed in its form, and it is the final cause, not mechanism alone, that explains what comes to be.**
2. [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : **Virtue is not knowledge but a disposition of character acquired by habit: one becomes just by doing just acts, aiming in each situation at the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md).**
3. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **Happiness is neither pleasure nor honour but [eudaimonia](../glossary/eudaimonism.md): the activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, carried out over a whole life and crowned by contemplation.**
4. [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md) : **The essence of things is not a separate [Form](../glossary/theory-of-forms.md) but their [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md) form: every sensible substance is an inseparable compound of matter and form.**
5. [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md) : **The human is by nature a political animal: the city was not born of a pact but of a natural necessity, and whoever can live outside it is a beast or a god.**

## Positions

### [Being and becoming](../axes/being-becoming.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Permanence of being 50% · Becoming and flux 50%

Against Parmenides, who made change unthinkable (being cannot come from non-being), Aristotle distinguishes **potentiality** and **actuality**: becoming is not an emergence out of nothing but the actualization of what was potential, within a persisting substrate. Substance endures through the change of its accidents, form realizes itself in matter: being and becoming cease to be rivals, becoming is the way beings accomplish their form.

> “Nature is a principle of motion and change.”, Physics, III.1 (200b)

*Sources : Physics, I.8 · Metaphysics, IX (Theta)*

### [The point of philosophy](../axes/metaphilosophy.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Understanding the world 75% · Transforming oneself 25%

First science is born of wonder and is pursued for its own sake, not for its usefulness: like the free man, it is its own end. And the **theoretical life**, the activity of what is most divine in us, is the highest happiness. Yet Aristotle keeps a place for the practical aim: we do not study ethics to know what virtue is, but to become good.

> “This alone of all the sciences is free, for it alone exists for its own sake.”, Metaphysics, I.2 (982b)

*Sources : Metaphysics, I.1-2 · Nicomachean Ethics, X.7-8 · Nicomachean Ethics, II.2 (1103b)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 65% · Fecundity and redemption 35%

Against Stoic indifference, Aristotle holds pain and misfortune to be real evils: great misfortunes, like Priam's, can spoil a happy life, for eudaimonia also requires external goods. Suffering therefore has no value in itself and should be avoided; but courage consists in enduring it nobly when the good demands it, without however seeking it out.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, I, 10-11 · Nicomachean Ethics, III, 6-9*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Freeing ourselves from the fear 45% · Refusing death 25% · Preparing for the passage 20% · Living in view of one's death 10%

Aristotle holds death to be “the most fearful of things”, for it is the end and deprives of all goods: it is a real evil, not a nothing, before which courage finds its meaning in facing it without flight. The soul, the form of the body, perishes with it for the most part, only the intellect (nous) being able to count as eternal, but impersonally. Hence a finitude assumed, tinged with the gravity of a loss.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, III, 6 and 9 · On the Soul, III, 5*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Eudaimonism 80% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 15% · Hedonism 5%

If every action aims at an end, and ends link up as means, then, on pain of infinite regress, there must be a final end willed for its own sake and never for the sake of anything else: this self-sufficient good is [eudaimonia](../glossary/eudaimonism.md). What it consists in is settled by the **function argument** (ergon): as the flute-player has his excellence, the human has his own, the activity of the soul's rational part carried out in accordance with virtue. Happiness is therefore neither pleasure, nor honour, nor wealth, but an excellent activity unfolded over a whole life, whose highest form is contemplation.

> “The good of man is an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are several virtues, in accordance with the best and most complete.”, Nicomachean Ethics, I.7 (1098a)

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, I.7 (1097b) · Nicomachean Ethics, X.7*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 90% · Consequentialism 5% · Deontologism 5%

Against Socratic intellectualism, for which no one does evil willingly and virtue reduces to knowledge, Aristotle observes that knowing the good does not suffice to do it: virtue is not a science but a disposition (hexis) of character, acquired by the repetition of just acts as one becomes a lyre-player by playing the lyre. The right action thus cannot be deduced from any general rule: it consists in aiming, in each situation, at the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md) relative to us between excess and defect, determined by the [practical wisdom](../glossary/phronesis.md) of the discerning person. Hence a morality governed neither by consequences nor by a law, but by the kind of person one becomes.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, II.1 and II.6*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 85% · Constructed meaning 15%

From the general finalism follows a consequence for human existence: what holds for the eye or the plant holds for the human, who likewise has a **proper function** (ergon) inscribed in its nature. The meaning of life is accordingly neither received from elsewhere nor freely invented: it is to be actualized from a nature already oriented toward its end, rational activity. Hence the leaning toward a given meaning, while leaving each individual the task of bringing into act what is at first only in potency.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, I.7 (the function argument) · Physics, II.8*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Finalism 80% · Mechanism 15% · Vitalism 5%

Why does a thing come to be? Aristotle finds incomplete any answer that invokes only matter or the collision of bodies, as the Presocratic physicists did: four [causes](../glossary/four-causes.md) are needed (material, formal, efficient, final), and it is the final cause that orders them. His decisive argument comes from living things, where observation reveals organs made for a function: the acorn becomes an oak, the eye serves for seeing. He extends this oriented regularity to all of nature, which does nothing in vain: the end is no conscious plan but the immanent term toward which a process tends by its very form.

> “Nature does nothing in vain.”, Politics, I.2 (1253a)

*Sources : Physics, II.3 and II.8 · Metaphysics, I.3*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Essentialism 90% · Existentialism 10%

Aristotle turns [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s own demand for essence against him: if the essence of a thing is what makes it what it is, lodging it in a separate world of [Forms](../glossary/theory-of-forms.md) makes it unable to explain the sensible thing, from which it is then cut off. The form must therefore be [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md) in the individual, as the soul is in the body: every sensible substance is a compound of matter and form, [hylomorphism](../glossary/hylomorphism.md). Essence is no longer a celestial model but the **what-it-was-to-be**, what the thing is in itself, grasped in the definition, without thereby denying that form is the most real and most knowable thing in it.

*Sources : Metaphysics, VII (Zeta).4 and 17*

### [Wholes and parts](../axes/reduction-emergence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Emergentism 85% · Reductionism 15%

The soul is defined as the **form** of an organized body, its first actuality, neither a separable substance nor a mere aggregate of matter. Living beings and their functions thus do not reduce to their components: an emergentist reading of [hylomorphism](../glossary/hylomorphism.md) is warranted.

*Sources : On the Soul (De Anima), II.1 (412a-b)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 70% · Rationalism 30%

Against Platonic [innatism](../glossary/innate-ideas.md), knowledge starts from perception, from which induction draws out universal principles, a legacy the Scholastic tradition would later sum up as "nothing is in the intellect that was not first in the senses". The empiricism remains moderate, since the active intellect then grasps essences, hence a non-extreme position.

*Sources : Posterior Analytics, II.19 · On the Soul, III.4-8*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Correspondence 85% · Coherence 10% · Pragmatism 5%

Aristotle gives the classic formulation of **correspondence**: the truth of a statement lies in its agreement with what is the case.

> “To say of what is that it is not, or of what is not that it is, is false; while to say of what is that it is, and of what is not that it is not, is true.”, Metaphysics, IV (Gamma).7 (1011b)

*Sources : Metaphysics, IV (Gamma).7 (1011b)*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Equity-phronesis 90% · Legalism 10%

**Equity** (epieikeia) corrects the law where its generality fails in the singular case: it is a rectification of law. It is [practical wisdom](../glossary/phronesis.md) (phronesis), knowledge of the particular, that judges in situation rather than mechanically applying a rule.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, V.10 (equity) · Nicomachean Ethics, VI.5 (practical wisdom)*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Organic community 93% · Contractualism 7%

Against the sophists who held the city to be a mere convention (nomos) opposed to nature, Aristotle derives it from a natural genesis: from the couple comes the household, from households the village, from villages the city, the term that at last achieves self-sufficiency and living well. The proof lies in the [logos](../glossary/logos.md): only the human has the speech that distinguishes the just from the unjust, and such a power would be pointless were the human not made for shared life. The city is therefore not posterior to a pact of already-constituted individuals; it is by nature prior, and whoever can do without it is “either a beast or a god”.

> “Man is by nature a political animal.”, Politics, I.2 (1253a)

*Sources : Politics, I.2 (1253a)*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Holism 90% · Individualism 10%

For Aristotle the criterion of priority is not chronological but functional: prior is that without which the rest cannot fulfil its function. Now a hand severed from the body is a hand only in name, since it can no longer grasp; likewise the individual outside the city cannot fulfil its nature and remains an unfinished human. The city is therefore by nature prior to the individual as the whole to the part, an avowed holism that does not, for all that, erase the city's end, the living-well of its citizens.

> “The city is by nature prior to the individual, for the whole is necessarily prior to the part.”, Politics, I.2 (1253a)

*Sources : Politics, I.2 (1253a)*

### [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Necessity, leisure as the end 80% · Fulfillment 10% · Alienation 10%

Manual and wage labour is **banausic**: necessary to life but degrading for the soul, it occupies the slave and the artisan so as to free the citizen for leisure (scholè), politics and contemplation. Work belongs to necessity, not to fulfilment.

*Sources : Politics, I.5-7; VIII.2*

### [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Anthropocentrism 90% · Pathocentrism 10%

The anthropocentrism is explicit: plants exist for the sake of animals, and animals for the sake of humans. A hierarchy of souls (vegetative, sensitive, rational) places the human at the summit, other living beings being ordered to its use.

*Sources : Politics, I.8 (1256b)*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Science (epistēmē) is demonstrative knowledge: a knowledge of the necessary, derived by syllogism from true, immediate **first principles** better known than the conclusion. This confidence in a certain, grounded knowledge places Aristotle on the side of [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md), tempered by his admission that the practical domain admits only an approximate exactness.

*Sources : Posterior Analytics, I.2 · Nicomachean Ethics, I.3 (1094b)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Theism 75% · Atheism 25%

Aristotle demonstrates the existence of a [first unmoved mover](../glossary/unmoved-mover.md), pure actuality and final cause that moves the world as the beloved moves the lover, without being moved. This is a form of theism, but of an impersonal god that does not create, exercises no providence and does not know particulars, hence a value held well back from the extreme.

> “Thought thinks itself in grasping the intelligible, and its thinking is a thinking of thinking.”, Metaphysics, XII (Lambda).9 (1074b)

*Sources : Metaphysics, XII (Lambda).7 and 9 · Physics, VIII.6*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Reasoned selection 80% · Affirmation 10% · Extinction and detachment 10%

Aristotle does not repress desire but orders it: appetite (orexis) must obey practical reason as a child lives according to its father's command. The virtue of **temperance** aims at the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md), desiring what one ought, when and as one ought, neither asceticism nor unbridled affirmation but a reasoned selection of desires.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, III.11-12; I.13 (1102b)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of feeling 70% · The primacy of reason 30%

Against Socratic intellectualism, Aristotle grants the passions a properly ethical role: trained to the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md), fear, anger or pity felt when one ought and toward whom one ought belong to virtue, and character is formed by habituation as much as by reason. The leaning toward sentimentalism stays moderate, reason keeping the lead.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, II.6 (1106b); II.3*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Partialism 85% · Impartialism 15%

Friendship (philia) is a good indispensable to the good life, and it is ordered: one owes more to one's own, parents and benefactors, than to strangers, and civic **philia** holds the city together. The legitimate priority given to those close to us places Aristotle on the side of partialism.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, VIII-IX; IX.2*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Celebration 55% · Asceticism 45%

Pleasure is neither the good nor an evil: it completes the activity as the bloom of youth crowns the young. The virtue of temperance is the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md) between intemperance and insensibility, the latter defect being itself a vice, hence a position just past the balance point, on the side of a measured celebration.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, X.4 (1174b); III.11*

### [Ideal and reality](../axes/political-ideal.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Political realism 85% · Utopianism 15%

Aristotle rejects [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s ideal city and starts from actual constitutions: the legislator must aim not at the absolutely best regime but at the best possible for given circumstances. Hence his praise of the **mixed** regime (politeia) resting on a broad middle class, a realist and empirical stance.

*Sources : Politics, IV.1 and 11*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Libertarianism 65% · Egalitarianism 35%

Distributive justice follows a **proportional** (geometric) equality: goods and honours are apportioned according to each person's merit, not in equal shares. Refusing arithmetical equality in favour of proportion to merit inclines moderately toward the meritocratic pole, without absolutizing property.

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, V.3 (1131a); Politics, III.9*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 65% · Hard determinism 35%

The analysis of **voluntary** action and of [deliberate choice](../glossary/prohairesis.md) (prohairesis) presupposes that the origin of action is in us: we are responsible for our acts, of which we are the origin, which grounds praise, blame and punishment. This defence of self-mastery leans toward [free will](../glossary/free-will.md), without thematizing modern [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md).

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, III.1-5*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mastery 80% · Stewardship 15% · Member among others 5%

Nature does nothing in vain and has arranged everything for an end: plants for animals, animals for humans. This hierarchical teleology orders living things to human use, hence a clear orientation toward an anthropocentric mastery of nature.

*Sources : Politics, I.8 (1256b); Physics, II.8*

### [Nature of art](../axes/art-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mimesis 90% · Creation 10%

For Aristotle, the arts are modes of **imitation** (mimēsis): tragedy, epic and painting represent human beings in action. Imitation is natural to humankind and a source of pleasure and learning, which firmly anchors the conception of art as mimesis.

*Sources : Poetics, 1-4 (1447a-1448b)*

### [Purpose of art](../axes/art-purpose.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Truth 45% · Pleasure 30% · Transformation 25%

Poetry yields pleasure, but it aims above all at truth: more philosophical than history, it speaks the universal and the possible rather than the particular. The catharsis of the passions, a purgation of fear and pity through tragedy, adds a transformative function, hence a shared weight tilted toward truth.

> “Poetry is more philosophical and weightier than history: it speaks of the universal, history of the particular.”, Poetics, 9 (1451b)

*Sources : Poetics, 6 (1449b) and 9 (1451b)*

### [Universals](../axes/universals.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Realism about universals 80% · Nominalism 20%

Against Plato, Aristotle rejects separate Forms: the universal is real, but it exists only within individuals, as their form. The form of the horse does not exist apart from singular horses, yet it is genuinely real in them. This is an immanent realism.

*Sources : Metaphysics, Z; Categories, 5*

### [Nature of love](../axes/love-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Philia (friendship) 90% · Agape as gift 10%

Against Platonic [erōs](../glossary/eros.md), which makes love the desire for a beauty one does not possess, Aristotle sets at the summit not lack but the bond: [philia](../glossary/philia.md). In books VIII and IX of the *[Nicomachean Ethics](../works/nicomachean-ethics-aristotle.md)*, he distinguishes three friendships by their spring, the useful, the pleasant and the good; the first two are fragile, for they love the other for what he provides. Perfect friendship, that of good people who wish each other well for the other's own sake, is stable, rare and reciprocal, to the point that the friend counts as an extension of oneself; to will their good for their sake alone, expecting nothing in return, keeps a share of gift at the very heart of the reciprocity. Far from being a supplement to the good life, it is a constitutive part of it: no one, however well supplied with all other goods, would choose to live without friends.

> “The friend is another self.”, Nicomachean Ethics, IX, 4

*Sources : Nicomachean Ethics, VIII-IX (the three friendships; the friend as another self) · Nicomachean Ethics, IX, 9 (happiness and the need for friends)*

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The primacy of truth 85% · Vital illusions 15%

The desire to know is not an instrument of happiness: it is constitutive of our nature, and contemplation (theōria), the most self-sufficient and continuous of activities, realizes what is highest in the human being; an accomplished life culminates in knowing, not in usefulness.

> “All men by nature desire to know.”, Metaphysics, I, 1, 980a

*Sources : Metaphysics, I, 1 · Nicomachean Ethics, X*

## Influenced by

- **[Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)** (reacts against) : Trained for twenty years at the Academy, [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) keeps from [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) the demand for science and definition, but rejects the separation of the Forms: form exists only in individual substances, not in a world apart.

## Influence on

- **[Thomas Aquinas](../philosophers/aquinas.md)** (synthesizes) : [Aquinas](../philosophers/aquinas.md) integrates the physics, psychology and metaphysics of [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md), "the Philosopher", into a vast synthesis with Christian doctrine, making Aristotelian reason the instrument of theology.
- **[Averroes](../philosophers/averroes.md)** (reinterprets) : [Averroes](../philosophers/averroes.md), "the Commentator", makes [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) the axis of his philosophy and writes great commentaries aimed at recovering the authentic doctrine, against Neoplatonising readings.
- **[Avicenna](../philosophers/avicenna.md)** (reinterprets) : [Avicenna](../philosophers/avicenna.md) recasts [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s metaphysics by Neoplatonising it: the distinction of essence and existence, the necessary being, the hierarchy of intellects.
- **[Boethius](../philosophers/boethius.md)** (transmits) : [Boethius](../philosophers/boethius.md) set himself the task of translating and commenting on [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s Organon: he thereby became the only logical Aristotle known to the early Latin Middle Ages.
- **[Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md)** (continues) : [Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md) holds [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) to be one of the most speculative of thinkers: he takes from him actuality (energeia), immanent end, and the universal that exists only in its individuals, against the abstract universal, and closes his Encyclopedia with a citation from the Metaphysics, thought thinking itself.
- **[Maimonides](../philosophers/maimonides.md)** (reinterprets) : [Maimonides](../philosophers/maimonides.md)'s Guide for the Perplexed sets out to reconcile [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s physics and metaphysics with the Torah, even at the cost of limiting his reach on the creation of the world.
- **[Martha Nussbaum](../philosophers/nussbaum.md)** (continues) : [Nussbaum](../philosophers/nussbaum.md) renews the ethics of [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md): the human good is the flourishing of capacities proper to our nature. From it she draws the capabilities approach, a list of the real freedoms a just society must guarantee to each person.
- **[Plotinus](../philosophers/plotinus.md)** (reinterprets) : [Plotinus](../philosophers/plotinus.md) absorbs major pieces of [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) into his Neoplatonism, foremost the Intellect as thought thinking itself, while rejecting the categories as applied to the intelligible.
