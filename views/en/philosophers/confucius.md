# Kong Qiu (Kongzi, "Master Kong")

*551 BCE – 479 BCE · Chinese · [Confucianism](../movements/confucianism.md)*

An itinerant master of Spring-and-Autumn China (sixth to fifth century BCE), born in the small state of Lu in an age of warfare and of the unravelling of the Zhou feudal order. A magistrate for a time, then a wandering counsellor vainly seeking a ruler to apply his views, he formed disciples above all: his sayings, collected after his death in the Analects (Lunyu), are the primary source of his thought and ground [Confucianism](../movements/confucianism.md). To the disorder of his age his answer is neither religious nor legal but ethical: to reform the person and the relationships so as to regenerate society, through the study of the Ancients and self-cultivation. Regarded as China's first educator and later raised into the tutelary figure of the imperial state, which made his texts the basis of the mandarin examinations, he remains a major reference of East Asian cultures.

## Core positions

1. [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md) : **One becomes humane only by first loving one's own: xiao (filial piety) is the root of ren 仁 (humaneness), and affection must stay graded, not spread equally over all.**
2. [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : **What must be formed is not a rule but a person: the measure of the good is the junzi 君子 (person of noble character), whose virtue of ren 仁 (humaneness) is won through self-cultivation.**
3. [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md) : **The [Way](../glossary/dao.md) was already realized by the Ancients: I transmit and invent nothing, for restoring their heritage is worth more than inventing a new morality.**
4. [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md) : **Social order hangs on the exactness of words: if names are not rectified, the duties bound to roles unravel, and everything begins with zhengming (the rectification of names).**
5. [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md) : **One governs by virtue and example, not by fear: punishments make a people that evades without shame, while the ruler's example gives them a sense of honour.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 75% · Abolitionism 25%

Adversity is not for Confucius a pure misfortune: it is in ordeal that the gentleman is revealed and tempered, staying upright where lesser men give way. Mencius systematizes this fruitfulness: when Heaven is about to confer a great mission on someone, it first “exercises his heart with suffering and his sinews with toil”, so as to strengthen what he lacked.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), XV.2 · Mencius, VI B, 15*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 60% · Constructed meaning 40%

Meaning, for Confucius, is rooted in the Mandate of Heaven (Tian ming): a normative order, a Way that surpasses the individual and to which one must attune one's conduct. But this order is fulfilled only through human effort, self-cultivation, the rites and the justice of relationships: life takes on meaning by realizing, here below, the share that falls to us in the harmony willed by Heaven.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), II.4 and XVI.8*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Death is nothing 50% · Immortalism 40% · Being-toward-death 10%

Asked about death and the spirits, Confucius redirects attention to life: “you do not yet know how to serve the living, how could you know how to serve the dead?”. He sets aside speculation about the hereafter without denying it, and shifts care onto present conduct. But the cult of ancestors maintains a continuity: the dead remain present through memory and the rites, a form of survival in the lineage.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), XI.12*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Reasoned selection 70% · Affirmation 15% · Extinction and detachment 15%

Confucianism does not condemn desire but civilizes it through the rites and the sense of propriety: one satisfies one's inclinations in the right forms and to the right measure. The ideal is the perfect accord of desire and norm, which Confucius says he reached only at the end of a long self-cultivation.

> “At seventy, I could follow my heart's desire without overstepping the line.” (Analects, II.4)

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), II.4 and XII.1*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 55% · Existentialism 45%

Confucius says little of human nature, save that “by nature men are close, by practice they grow apart”. The common ground is thin, and it is self-cultivation, study and the rites that make the gentleman: what is essential plays out in formation more than in a given essence, hence a position near the center, barely inclined toward the given.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), XVII.2 and V.13*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 60% · Hard determinism 40%

Confucius divides one's lot between what belongs to Heaven and what depends on us: the span of life, wealth and honors hang on the decree (ming) that the sage welcomes without surrendering to it; but to become fully humane (ren) depends on oneself alone. Outer fortune is thus largely determined, while moral perfection remains the ever-possible work of the will.

> “Is humaneness far off? No sooner do I desire it than it is here.” (Analects, VII.29)

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), XII.5 and VII.29 · Analects, XX.3*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 80% · Deontologism 10% · Ethics of care 10%

Because morality can neither be codified into rules nor reduced to a reckoning of outcomes, what must be formed is a person, their character and their situated discernment. Hence the shift of appraisal from the act to the agent: the measure of the good is the junzi 君子 (person of noble character) animated by ren 仁 (humaneness), and this virtue cannot be decreed, it is won through long self-cultivation and the imitation of exemplars. Virtue is not obedience to a law but an inner disposition made stable by habit, which anchors the foundation on the side of virtue ethics. A deontological share remains in fidelity to the li 禮 (rites), and a share of [care](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) in the primacy of familial affection.

> “To master oneself and return to the rites: that is humaneness (ren).” (Analects, XII, 1)

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), XII, 1-2 (ren and self-mastery through the rites) · Analects, IV, 5 and VI, 30 (the junzi never departs from humaneness)*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Partialism 90% · Impartialism 10%

Humaneness does not descend from an abstract principle: it grows out of the affection lived in the family and then radiates in concentric circles. Xiao (filial piety) is accordingly presented as the very root of ren 仁 (humaneness), and this is what licenses a graded favour rather than equal regard for all. The case of the son who screens his father's fault, where the master sides with family solidarity against denunciation, is the deliberate test of the thesis: to sever love from its partial, affective source would dry up morality itself, against any impartialism that claims to love strangers and kin alike.

> “Uprightness lies in this: the father screens his son, and the son screens his father.” (Analects, XIII, 18)

*Sources : Analects, I, 2 (filial piety, the root of humaneness) · Analects, XIII, 18 (the father screens the son, the son screens the father)*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Conservatism 95% · Progressivism 5%

The premise is that the [Way (dao)](../glossary/dao.md) was already fully realized by the sage-kings and the li 禮 (rites) of the Zhou dynasty, and that the disorder of the age stems from their decay, not from a want of novelty. If the good has an accomplished precedent, the rational task is to restore and transmit it, not to invent: to call oneself a transmitter and not a maker is therefore not modesty but a thesis, the refusal to ground the norm in present invention. The normative reference is the exemplary past, which makes him a [conservative](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md) in the strong sense, against any faith in progress.

> “I transmit and do not create; I love the Ancients and trust them.” (Analects, VII, 1)

*Sources : Analects, VII, 1 (I transmit and do not create, I love and trust the Ancients) · Analects, III, 14 (Confucius follows the Zhou)*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Memory and transmission 85% · Presentism 10% · Project 5%

Confucian time is oriented toward the past as a model: “reviving the old to understand the new” is the master's way. Studying the Ancients, the rituals and the odes transmits a wisdom to embody; the future counts less as a project to invent than as a continuity to preserve. This is an orientation of [memory and transmission](../axes/time-orientation.md).

> “He who revives the old to understand the new is fit to be a teacher.” (Analects, II, 11)

*Sources : Analects, II, 11 (reviving the old to know the new) · Analects, VII, 19 (a man fond of antiquity, untiring in his study)*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Communitarianism and patriotism 90% · Cosmopolitanism 10%

The individual exists only as caught in a fabric of particular relationships: family, lineage, community, state, ordered by the li 禮 (rites). Xiao (filial piety) and loyalty to one's own take priority over any abstract belonging to humanity, anchoring Confucius on the side of [communitarianism](../axes/belonging.md).

*Sources : Analects, I, 2 (the familial order, foundation of the social order) · Analects, XII, 11 (let the father be a father, the son a son)*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Organic community 95% · Contractualism 5%

Society is not a pact among free and equal individuals, but an organic order modelled on the family: the state continues the natural relations of kinship and authority. Zhengming (the rectification of names) assigns each his place and duty, the ruler to be a ruler and the subject a subject, as the father must be a father. This is an [organic community](../axes/social-contract.md).

*Sources : Analects, XII, 11 (let the ruler be a ruler, the subject a subject) · Analects, II, 21 (filial piety extended is already governing)*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Holism 85% · Individualism 15%

A person is intelligible only through their roles and relations: one is a son, a brother, a subject, a friend, and it is by fulfilling these relations according to the li 禮 (rites) that one becomes fully human. Identity is relational, not monadic: the leaning is markedly holist, even if self-cultivation keeps an irreducibly personal demand.

*Sources : Analects, XII, 11 (each defined by his relation) · Analects, II, 5-8 (the relational duties of filial piety)*

### [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Truthful rigorism 85% · Benevolent lying permitted 15%

The argument starts from a function of language: a name ("father", "ruler", "friend") does not merely label, it prescribes a duty bound to the role. A word misused, or belied by deeds, therefore unsettles the very order it was meant to uphold, and zhengming (the rectification of names) becomes the condition of a working society. Truthfulness ceases to be a private matter of conscience: trustworthiness of speech (xin) and sincerity (cheng) are demanded because lying and the gap between word and deed dissolve the social fabric. Hence a rigorism of truthfulness, in which the junzi 君子 (person of noble character) is ashamed when his words outrun his deeds.

> “If names are not correct, language is not in accord with the truth of things; then affairs cannot succeed.” (Analects, XIII, 3)

*Sources : Analects, XIII, 3 (the rectification of names) · Analects, XIV, 27 (the junzi is ashamed when his words exceed his deeds)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The examined life 65% · Primacy of action 35%

The Confucian way is a demanding self-cultivation: the disciple Zengzi “examines himself daily on three points”, and the inner perfecting of ren 仁 (humaneness) conditions all right action. Yet this interiority is not contemplative: it is tested and completed in conduct, service and the li 禮 (rites), hence a moderate position on the examined-life side, balanced by the primacy of action.

> “Each day I examine myself on three points: have I been faithful in acting for others, sincere with my friends, and have I practised what was taught to me?” (Analects, I, 4)

*Sources : Analects, I, 4 (Zengzi examines himself daily on three points) · Analects, IV, 17 (seeing a worthy man, think of equalling him; seeing an unworthy one, examine yourself)*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Equity-phronesis 70% · Legalism 30%

The li 禮 (rites) are an indispensable form, but worthless without the spirit that animates them: “a man without humaneness, what has he to do with the rites?”. Ritual must be performed with the right feeling, and the master tailors his teaching to each disciple: the letter without the heart is empty. The leaning is thus toward [equity and discernment](../axes/rule-spirit.md), without abolishing the ritual form.

> “A man without humaneness, what has he to do with the rites? A man without humaneness, what has he to do with music?” (Analects, III, 3)

*Sources : Analects, III, 3 (without humaneness, what use are the rites?) · Analects, XI, 22 (the master answers each disciple differently)*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Authority 85% · Anarchism 15%

The argument rests on a psychology of obedience: coercion by laws and punishments wins only a shameless avoidance, an outward compliance that dissolves the moment surveillance lifts, whereas the ruler's virtuous example awakens in the people a sense of honour and an internalized assent. Better, then, an authority that radiates, “like the pole star” around which the other stars arrange themselves, than one that represses. This legitimation of hierarchy by the good of the governed stands squarely against rule by force and penalty alone, and authority here remains subordinate to the virtue of the one who wields it.

> “Lead the people by virtue, keep them in order by the rites, and they will have a sense of shame and come to you.” (Analects, II, 3)

*Sources : Analects, II, 1 (governing by virtue, like the pole star) · Analects, II, 3 (governing by virtue and the rites rather than by punishments)*

### [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Maximalism 90% · Minimalism 10%

The moral demand is maximalist: to become a junzi 君子 (person of noble character) is a lifelong, never-finished task, in which one must “master oneself” relentlessly. The disciple Zengzi speaks of a heavy burden and a long road, for he carries ren 仁 (humaneness) until death. Morality does not rest content with a minimum of non-harm: it calls for a continuous perfecting of the person.

*Sources : Analects, VIII, 7 (the burden is heavy and the road long, for he carries humaneness until death) · Analects, XII, 1 (to master oneself and return to the rites)*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 70% · Eudaimonism 25% · Hedonism 5%

The sovereign good is the moral excellence of the accomplished person, the junzi 君子 (person of noble character) who has attained full humaneness: a [moral perfectionism](../axes/sovereign-good.md). A share of flourishing remains, for the practice of virtue yields a serene joy, yet sensory pleasure is never the criterion.

*Sources : Analects, VII, 15 (joy amid a frugal life, unjust wealth as a passing cloud) · Analects, IV, 5 (the junzi never abandons humaneness, not even for the space of a meal)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Sentimentalism 65% · Intellectualism 35%

Confucian morality does not repress feelings but cultivates and orders them: sincere mourning matters more than strict observance, and it is lived affection, love of one's parents, that grounds xiao (filial piety). The li 禮 (rites) give emotions a fitting form rather than erasing them, hence a moderate sentimentalist leaning, tempered by the demand for discernment.

*Sources : Analects, III, 4 (in mourning, real grief is better than strict observance) · Analects, XVII, 21 (the three-year mourning and the affection owed to parents)*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Altruism 70% · Ethical egoism 30%

The junzi 君子 (person of noble character) minds righteousness (yi) where the petty man minds gain: humaneness means helping others to stand and not inflicting on them what one would not wish for oneself (the golden rule in negative form, shu, reciprocity). This is an altruistic orientation, without self-renunciation: one raises oneself by raising others.

> “What you do not wish for yourself, do not do to others.” (Analects, XV, 24)

*Sources : Analects, IV, 16 (the junzi understands righteousness, the petty man profit) · Analects, XV, 24 and VI, 30 (not inflicting on others what you would not wish; helping others to stand)*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Universalism 70% · Relativism 15% · Constructivism 15%

The [Way (dao)](../glossary/dao.md) and ren 仁 (humaneness) hold as objective norms, anchored in the order of Heaven (tian) and the wisdom of the Ancients: the good is not a matter of arbitrary convention. Yet this objectivity is embodied in historically situated li 禮 (rites), inherited from a tradition, which leaves some room for the instituted character of the norm.

*Sources : Analects, IV, 8 (hearing the Way in the morning, one may die content at evening) · Analects, VII, 23 (Heaven has begotten the virtue that is in me)*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Culturalism 80% · Naturalism 20%

“By nature men are close to one another; by practice they grow apart”: shared nature is not enough, it is study, habit and the li 禮 (rites) that make the moral difference between men. Confucius stakes everything on formation and culture (wen) more than on an innate endowment, hence a culturalist leaning.

> “By nature men are close to one another; through practice they grow far apart.” (Analects, XVII, 2)

*Sources : Analects, XVII, 2 (by nature close, by practice far apart) · Analects, XVI, 9 (the grades of knowing: innate, gained by study, gained through difficulty)*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological optimism 70% · Anthropological pessimism 30%

Confucius holds man to be perfectible: any human being can, through study and effort, rise toward ren 仁 (humaneness), and he teaches “without distinction of class”. This optimism stays measured: the wise are rare, most betray the Way, and the master laments never having met one who loves virtue as much as pleasures. Yet trust in educability prevails.

*Sources : Analects, XV, 38 (in teaching there are no distinctions of class) · Analects, IX, 17 (I have not seen one who loves virtue as he loves pleasure)*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Egalitarianism 70% · Libertarianism 30%

Confucius worries less about poverty than about inequality and disorder: “what troubles the ruler is not scarcity of people but unequal distribution, not poverty but instability”. The sovereign must provide for the people's welfare before instructing them, which betrays a concern for the equitable sharing of resources, within a hierarchical order.

*Sources : Analects, XVI, 1 (what is to be feared is not poverty but inequality) · Analects, XIII, 9 (enrich the people, then instruct them)*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Transcendence 60% · Immanence 40%

Confucius acknowledges Heaven (tian) as an instance ordering the course of things and grounding his mission, and he respects the cult of ancestors and spirits, to be honoured “while keeping one's distance”. Yet his aim stays firmly turned to this world: “not yet able to serve men, how could one serve the spirits?”. Transcendence is affirmed, but subordinated to the ethics of the human world.

*Sources : Analects, XI, 11 (not yet able to serve men, how serve the spirits?) · Analects, VI, 20 (honour the spirits but keep them at a distance)*

### [Who should decide](../axes/who-decides.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Epistocracy 85% · Radical democracy 15%

Power should fall to the wisest and most virtuous, not to the many: one promotes the upright, sets aside the crooked, and “the people may be made to follow a path, not made to understand it”. This trust in the rule of the best, formed by study and virtue, is markedly epistocratic.

*Sources : Analects, II, 19 (promote the upright over the crooked) · Analects, VIII, 9 (the people may be made to follow, not to understand)*

### [The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Rehabilitation 40% · Restorative justice 40% · Retributivism 10% · Deterrence 10%

Confucius prefers to prevent wrongdoing by forming the moral sense rather than punishing it: governing by punishments yields a people that evades them without shame, governing by virtue and the li 禮 (rites) gives them honour and reforms them from within. The aim is moral amendment, not retribution or mere deterrence: “why kill in order to govern?”. Punishment, when it must come, should restore harmony and reintegrate the offender into the community rather than cast them out.

*Sources : Analects, II, 3 (punishments yield a shameless people; virtue reforms them) · Analects, XII, 19 (what use is killing in order to govern?)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 65% · Rationalism 35%

Confucian knowledge is won by assiduous study of the Ancients, observation and practical experience, joined to reflection: “learning without thought is vain, thought without learning is perilous”. Knowledge is not pure speculation but wisdom to be applied, anchored in tradition and the scrutiny of the real, hence an empiricist leaning tempered by the demand for reflection.

> “Learning without thought is labour lost; thought without learning is perilous.” (Analects, II, 15)

*Sources : Analects, II, 15 (learning without thought, thought without learning) · Analects, VII, 8 and II, 17 (active study; knowing what one knows and does not know)*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Skepticism 60% · Dogmatism 40%

Confucius teaches a measured epistemic honesty: “to hold as known what you know, and as unknown what you do not, that is true knowledge”. He refrains from pronouncing on the beyond or the spirits and admits he was not born knowing. Without lapsing into scepticism, he makes room for doubt and avowed ignorance, hence a slight tilt away from dogmatism.

*Sources : Analects, II, 17 (to hold as known what you know, as unknown what you do not) · Analects, VII, 19 (I was not born knowing; I love antiquity and study it diligently)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*contextualist*

Confucius acknowledges Heaven (tian) and honours the spirits, but his thought is neither personal theism nor atheism: Heaven is an impersonal moral order rather than a creator god, and the master deliberately sets aside questions about the beyond to attend to human affairs. The axis, cut for the Western tradition, does not adequately capture this position.
