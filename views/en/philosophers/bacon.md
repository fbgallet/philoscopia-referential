# Francis Bacon

*1561 – 1626 · English · [Empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md)*

An English Renaissance statesman and philosopher, Lord Chancellor under James I, Francis Bacon is the great herald of modern science. Against the [scholastic logic](../movements/aristotelianism.md) inherited from Aristotle, judged barren, he calls in the *[Novum Organum](../works/novum-organum-bacon.md)* for a "great instauration" of learning: an experimental [induction](../glossary/induction.md) that starts from facts to uncover the laws of nature, and a science turned at last toward utility and the "relief of man's estate." A forerunner and tutelary figure of [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md), he inspired the **Royal Society** his *[New Atlantis](../works/new-atlantis-bacon.md)* foreshadows, and [Diderot](../philosophers/diderot.md) and the Enlightenment encyclopedists hailed him as their master.

## Core positions

1. [Scientific method](../axes/scientific-method.md) : **The syllogism discovers nothing: only an experimental, eliminative [induction](../glossary/induction.md), rising step by step from facts to laws, can interpret nature.**
2. [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md) : **Knowledge is power: a truth is worth only the works it makes possible, and science exists for the relief of man's estate.**
3. [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md) : **Nature, to be commanded, must be obeyed: **mastery** is not violence but the knowledge of her laws turned into power.**
4. [Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md) : **Before knowing, one must unlearn: four [idols](../glossary/idols-of-the-mind.md), tribe, cave, marketplace, theatre, distort every mind in advance, and method begins by purging them.**
5. [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md) : **The Ancients were the youth of the world: we are the true elders, and knowledge is no inheritance to revere but a building site to reopen.**

## Positions

### [Scientific method](../axes/scientific-method.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Proof by experience 95% · Refutation 5%

The syllogism, Bacon objects, only links notions already received: it discovers nothing, it merely dresses up prejudice. To this logic of **anticipations** of nature he opposes a logic of **interpretation**: not to fly like the bee from experience alone, nor to spin like the spider from reason alone, but to climb step by step from facts to laws. His [induction](../glossary/induction.md) is therefore not the bare enumeration that generalizes from a few agreeing cases; it is an **eliminative** induction, drawing up tables of presence, absence, and degrees so as to rule out false causes methodically and isolate the form of the phenomenon. Science sets out from no preconceived system: it is built from the bottom up, by exclusion.

> “The only hope lies in a true induction.”, Novum Organum, I, §14

*Sources : Novum Organum, I (aphorisms 19, 95, 105) · Novum Organum, II (tables and exclusion)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 90% · Rationalism 10%

All knowledge worthy of the name begins in the senses and observation: this is the empiricist foundation that [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) and [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) will make a school of. Yet Bacon does not trust the senses left to themselves, which he holds to be fallible and deceptive; he supplements them with provoked experience, the experimentum, and the methodical apparatus that corrects them. Experience is primary only on condition of being disciplined: neither pure rationalism nor naive empiricism, but reason in the service of regulated observation.

*Sources : Novum Organum, I, §50 and §95 · The Advancement of Learning, II*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Scientific realism 85% · Constructivism 15%

If method is worth reforming, it is because there are, beneath the phenomena, real forms and laws to be discovered, not invented. The whole Baconian reform presupposes an ordered nature, independent of our mind, whose right knowledge fits its structure; knowledge is moreover measured by its operative power over things, the warrant that it has grasped the real and not a fiction of the mind.

*Sources : Novum Organum, II, §1-4 (the doctrine of forms)*

### [Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Reliabilism 80% · Foundationalism 15% · Coherentism 5%

Before building, Bacon means to purge the mind of what distorts it: these are the four [idols](../glossary/idols-of-the-mind.md) ([idola](../glossary/idols-of-the-mind.md)), the systematic biases of knowledge. Idols of the **tribe**, inherent in the species, which lends nature more order than it has; idols of the **cave**, proper to each individual and his history; idols of the **marketplace**, slipped into thought by language and ill-made words; idols of the **theatre**, the received philosophical systems taken as so many fables played out on a stage. A belief is therefore justified not by tracing it back to a first indubitable intuition, but by the reliability of the process that produces it, once the mind is scoured of its idols and submitted to the experimental rule. The warrant lies on the side of method, not of an inner foundation.

> “Four kinds of idols beset the human mind.”, Novum Organum, I, §39

*Sources : Novum Organum, I, §38-68 (the idols)*

### [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Techno-optimism 95% · Techno-criticism 5%

Knowledge is worth not for contemplation but for its fruits: to know causes is to be able to produce effects, and the measure of a truth is the work it makes possible. Bacon judges this by the mechanical arts, which have grown without cease while the philosophy of the schools stood still: printing, gunpowder, and the compass have changed the world more than all the systems. Technology is thus no suspect by-product of knowledge but its legitimate end and the lever of human progress, against the resignation that holds the present condition to be beyond improvement.

> “Human knowledge and human power meet in one, for where the cause is not known the effect cannot be produced.”, Novum Organum, I, §3

*Sources : Novum Organum, I, §3, §81 and §129*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Mastery 90% · Stewardship 10%

The Fall, for Bacon, cost humanity its dominion over creation, and science has the task of restoring it: to command nature for the “relief of man's estate”. But this mastery rests on a paradox that sets it apart from any naive domination: one commands nature only by **obeying** her, by bending to her laws in order to bend her. Know first, then constrain, and only along the grain of her own causes. Nature remains a resource to be transformed in the service of humanity, but a resource that obeys only those who have first understood her.

> “Nature, to be commanded, must be obeyed.”, Novum Organum, Book I, aphorism 3

*Sources : Novum Organum, I, §3 and §129*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Scientism 80% · Epistemic pluralism 20%

Long before positivism, Bacon raises the methodical knowledge of nature into the royal road of human learning, alone capable of growing and bearing fruit, where the [philosophy of the schools](../movements/aristotelianism.md) turns in circles. He does not go so far as to dismiss every other truth, for he reserves a domain of its own to religion, but he strips the metaphysics of the Ancients of its authority and makes experimental inquiry the arbiter of what we may legitimately hold to be known.

*Sources : The Advancement of Learning, I-II · Novum Organum, I, §79-92*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 60% · Skepticism 40%

Bacon rejects at once the [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) that despairs of knowledge and the dogmatic assurance of systems, which "decide" too quickly. His way is gradual ascent: rising from facts to axioms by sure degrees, without leaping to premature generalities. Certainty is not a starting point but the term of a patient, correctable march, in which one posits nothing one has not tested. It is less a [fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md) than a certainty slowly won, by the exclusion of errors.

*Sources : Novum Organum, I, §19-20 and §104*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Progressivism 88% · Conservatism 13%

Against the prejudice that reveres Antiquity as the age of wisdom, Bacon reverses the argument: the Ancients were the youth of the world, and it is we, coming after so many accumulated centuries, who are the true elders. The authority of the received masters has therefore no value in itself; knowledge is not an inheritance to preserve but an open building site, indefinitely perfectible. This trust in the reforming power of reason holds first for the sciences; in politics, the great servant of the Crown remained cautious, which holds the score back from the extreme.

*Sources : Novum Organum, I, §84 · The Advancement of Learning, I*

### [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Directional meaning 80% · Contingency 20%

Knowledge, for Bacon, is cumulative: each generation inherits the gains of the last and can surpass them, so that the history of the sciences traces a march toward the better, not a cycle nor a decline from a golden age. His "great instauration" assumes that humanity can progress and that the future, better equipped, will outdo the past. This direction remains above all that of knowledge and its fruits, not a general law of all history.

*Sources : Novum Organum, I, §84 and §129*

### [Future generations](../axes/future-generations.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Responsibility principle 70% · Priority to the present 30%

Bacon conceives his enterprise as a bequest: he sows for harvests he will not see, founds a knowledge whose benefits will accrue to ages to come, and works for posterity more than for his own time. Yet this orientation toward those who will come is not the preservation principle of a [Jonas](../philosophers/jonas.md), who seeks to shield future generations from our power: it is the reverse, the hope of handing down that increased power to them as a benefit.

*Sources : Novum Organum, preface to the Great Instauration*

### [Ideal and reality](../axes/political-ideal.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Utopianism 65% · Political realism 35%

*[New Atlantis](../works/new-atlantis-bacon.md)* sketches an ideal city, Bensalem, organized around "Salomon's House": Bacon uses the utopian genre to picture what a society governed by science would be. But it is a utopia of a singular kind, which does not project a perfect social order to be imposed on people: it imagines above all the institution of collective knowledge, while the seasoned statesman of the *[Essays](../works/essays-bacon.md)* remains a shrewd judge of human beings as they are, hence a position only moderate.

*Sources : New Atlantis*

### [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Fulfillment 65% · Necessity, leisure as the end 35%

Salomon's House makes the inquiry into nature a collective work, parceled into coordinated tasks, and sets it at the summit of human dignity: to transform the world through methodical labor honors and fulfills the human being, far from being a mere chore. Still, Bacon prizes this work for its fruits and the utility it yields, which keeps an instrumental share in it.

*Sources : New Atlantis (Salomon's House)*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Separate domains 80% · Harmony 15% · Fideism 5%

Bacon sharply separates the two books in which God may be read, that of Scripture and that of nature: science must keep to second causes and borrow nothing from theology, which for its part falls to faith and revelation, not to proof. To confuse the two spoils both, as the scholastics did. He thus keeps for religion its own domain, intact but distinct, which places him on the side of separate domains rather than Thomist harmony.

*Sources : The Advancement of Learning, II · Novum Organum, I, §65 and §89*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Theism 85% · Atheism 15%

Bacon professes a sincere Christian faith and holds God's existence to be assured: it is, he argues, shallow philosophy that inclines to atheism, while a deeper knowledge of nature brings the mind back to religion, for the order of the world points to its author. Theism is never put in question, even though Bacon keeps it outside the field of scientific inquiry.

> “A little philosophy inclineth man's mind to atheism, but depth in philosophy bringeth men's minds about to religion.”, Essays, "Of Atheism"

*Sources : Essays, "Of Atheism"*

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The primacy of truth 75% · Vital illusions 25%

Knowledge begins with a purge: the idols, of the tribe, the cave, the marketplace, the theatre, are the natural and acquired illusions that distort the understanding, and the method's first task is to dispel them. The truth so won is not prized for itself alone: it proves itself by its works, in the service of the human condition.

*Sources : Novum Organum, I (1620)*

## Influenced by

- **[Aristotelianism](../movements/aristotelianism.md)** (reacts against) : [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md) builds his reform of method against scholastic [Aristotelianism](../movements/aristotelianism.md), judged barren: to syllogistic logic he opposes a “Novum Organum”, induction grounded in experience and observation.

## Influence on

- **[The Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md)** (continues) : [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md) bequeaths to the Enlightenment the programme of a knowledge founded on experience and turned toward utility, « knowledge is power ». [Diderot](../philosophers/diderot.md) and [d'Alembert](../philosophers/d-alembert.md) salute him as the inspiration of the Encyclopédie, whose Preliminary Discourse traces his genealogy of the sciences.
- **[Transhumanism](../movements/transhumanism.md)** (continues) : Transhumanism extends [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md)'s program: turning knowledge into effective power over nature, through experimental science, to relieve the human condition. It radicalizes that promise by stretching this command of nature to the human body and mind themselves.
