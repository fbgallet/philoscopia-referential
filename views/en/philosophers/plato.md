# Plato

*428 BCE – 348 BCE · Greek · [Platonism](../movements/platonism.md)*

An Athenian born into an aristocratic family, a disciple of Socrates whose execution turned him away from a political career, Plato (c. 428-348 BCE) founded the Academy around 387, an institution that would last nearly nine centuries. His entire work is written in dialogues, with Socrates' voice leading the inquiry: this literary choice makes dialectic, the contradictory examination of opinions, the very method of philosophy. In them he confronts the challenge of the sophists and the relativism of his day, the question "how should one live and govern justly?" inherited from Socrates' trial, and the problem of stable knowledge in a changing world. From the *[Republic](../works/republic-plato.md)* to the *[Timaeus](../works/timaeus-plato.md)* and the *[Laws](../works/laws-plato.md)*, his influence is such that the whole Western tradition has been read, in Whitehead's phrase, as "a series of footnotes to Plato".

## Core positions

1. [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md) : **The sensible world is not what is truly real: behind it exist intelligible [Forms](../glossary/theory-of-forms.md), eternal and unchanging, of which things are only imperfect copies.**
2. [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : **To learn is to recollect: through [anamnesis](../glossary/anamnesis.md), the soul recovers the Forms it contemplated before birth, not by the senses but by reason.**
3. [Who should decide](../axes/who-decides.md) : **Governing is a knowledge, not a matter of opinion: until philosophers become kings, cities will not cease from their ills.**
4. [Death](../axes/death.md) : **The soul is immortal: death is no annihilation but its separation from the body, and to philosophize rightly is to practise dying.**
5. [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : **The true self is not the body but the soul, a simple, immaterial substance for which the body is only a provisional instrument.**

## Positions

### [Being and becoming](../axes/being-becoming.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Permanence of being 90% · Becoming and flux 10%

The sensible, in perpetual becoming, cannot be an object of knowledge: nothing stable is to be found in it to know. What really is are the **intelligible Forms**, immutable and eternal, of which changing things are only participations. Yet Plato grants becoming its own consistency: the sensible world is not nothing, it is a moving image of eternity.

> “What is that which always is and has no becoming, and what is that which is always becoming and never is?”, Timaeus, 27d

*Sources : Timaeus, 27d-28a · Republic, VI-VII · Cratylus, 439c-440*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 70% · Abolitionism 30%

Plato does not hold suffering to be a mere evil to flee: just punishment is a remedy that heals the soul of injustice, and it is better to undergo it than to escape it. More broadly, access to the true demands a painful tearing away from the sensible, the ascent out of the cave at first dazzling the one set free: ordeal has a purifying and forming virtue.

*Sources : Gorgias, 476-479 · Republic, VII (allegory of the cave)*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Essentialism 100%

Plato is one of the major sources of essentialism: each sensible thing is what it is only by participating in an intelligible, eternal and immutable **Form** that constitutes its true essence. The [theory of Forms](../glossary/theory-of-forms.md), especially in the *[Phaedo](../works/phaedo-plato.md)* and the *[Republic](../works/republic-plato.md)*, makes essence a separate being, prior and superior to individual existence.

*Sources : Phaedo, 74a-75d · Republic, V, 476a-480a*

### [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Idealism 70% · Dualism 30%

The argument starts from a premise inherited from Heraclitus: the sensible is in perpetual flux, and of what never ceases to become there can be no science. Yet since we do know the equal, the just, the circle, there must exist stable, intelligible objects of which sensible things are only imperfect reflections: this is the higher degree of reality. The allegory of the cave, where prisoners take shadows for the real, dramatizes this [reversal](../glossary/transcendence.md). A dualism Plato never dissolves remains, two distinct orders held side by side, whose relation of "participation" stays problematic, as he himself concedes in the Parmenides.

> “To them, would not the truth be literally nothing but the shadows of the artificial objects?”, Republic, VII, 515c

*Sources : Republic, VII, 514a-518b (allegory of the cave) · Timaeus, 27d-29d*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Substantialism 100%

If the soul knows the eternal and unchanging intelligible, it is because it is akin to it: what grasps the simple must itself be simple. From this kinship Plato concludes that the true self is not the bodily composite but the soul, an immaterial substance that preexists birth and survives death. The Alcibiades sharpens the divide: a person is neither the body nor the compound of both, but "is" the soul, the body being merely an instrument it uses. This identification of the person with a separable [substance](../axes/self-nature.md), against any self bound up with embodiment, grounds the whole of Platonic psychology and eschatology.

*Sources : Phaedo, 78b-80b · Alcibiades, 129e-130c*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Transcendence 95% · Immanence 5%

The analogy of the sun carries the argument: just as the sun makes things visible and makes them grow, the Good gives the Forms their being known and their being at all. As the cause of both intelligibility and existence, the Good cannot itself be one essence among others: it surpasses them, set "beyond essence". Plato thus pushes [transcendence](../glossary/transcendence.md) to its extreme, against any [immanence](../glossary/immanence.md) that would lodge the principle within the world, and makes the whole of dialectic an ascent, out of the cave, toward this separate principle that the soul beholds only at the price of a conversion of its gaze.

> “The Good is not essence, but still transcends essence in dignity and power.”, Republic, VI, 509b

*Sources : Republic, VI, 508e-509b · Symposium, 210a-212a*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Rationalism 95% · Empiricism 5%

The doctrine answers the paradox of the Meno: one can seek neither what one already knows nor what one wholly ignores, having no way to recognize it. Plato concludes that all learning must be a recollection, [anamnesis](../glossary/anamnesis.md), of Forms the soul contemplated before birth. The proof is experimental, not asserted: questioned by the method of questions alone, the slave boy of the *[Meno](../works/meno-plato.md)*, who never learned geometry, recovers a theorem on his own, which rules out his having received it through the senses. Knowledge therefore comes from within, from reason turned toward the intelligible, against all empiricism.

> “Seeking and learning are in fact nothing but recollection.”, Meno, 81d

*Sources : Meno, 81a-86c · Phaedo, 72e-77a*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dogmatism 90% · Skepticism 10%

Against sophistic relativism, Plato holds, especially in the central dialogues, that stable knowledge, **epistēmē**, is possible: it bears on the Forms and is rigorously distinct from mere opinion, **doxa**, shifting and groundless. This confidence in a science of being brings him close to [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md), even if the interrogative form of the dialogues tempers any systematisation.

*Sources : Republic, V, 476d-480a · Theaetetus, 187a-201c*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 75% · Eudaimonism 25%

The sovereign good is likeness to the divine through contemplation of the Good and the justice of the soul, a moral perfectionism in which a life ordered by virtue is worth choosing for itself. A [eudaimonist](../glossary/eudaimonism.md) dimension remains, since this life of the just soul is presented as the happiest, with pleasure kept subordinate and deceptive.

*Sources : Republic, IX, 580a-588a · Theaetetus, 176a-b*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 85% · Atheism 15%

The *[Timaeus](../works/timaeus-plato.md)* presents a **Demiurge**, a good craftsman-god who orders the world by looking to the Forms as models; the *[Laws](../works/laws-plato.md)* assert the existence of gods and providence against atheism. This rational, impersonal theism, with no creation ex nihilo and no single God in the monotheist sense, holds the score back from the extreme.

*Sources : Timaeus, 28a-30c · Laws, X, 885b-907d*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Preparing for the passage 100%

The *[Phaedo](../works/phaedo-plato.md)* does not posit survival, it argues for it, through four linked proofs: the generation of opposites, [recollection](../glossary/anamnesis.md) which presupposes knowledge prior to birth, the soul's affinity with the simple and indestructible intelligible, and finally the essence argument, the soul that carries life within it being unable to admit its contrary. The reasoning rests on the [dualism](../axes/self-nature.md) of soul and body: what is simple does not decompose, hence does not perish. Death then ceases to be annihilation and becomes the soul's passage toward the intelligible, and philosophy itself, as detachment from the body, is its anticipated rehearsal.

> “Those who practise philosophy in the right way are in training for dying.”, Phaedo, 67e

*Sources : Phaedo, 64a, 102a-107b · Republic, X, 614a-621d (myth of Er)*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Asceticism 85% · Celebration 15%

The soul-body dualism calls for detachment from bodily pleasures, which "rivet" the soul to the sensible and hinder knowledge: the philosopher reduces them as far as possible. The stance stays measured, for the *[Philebus](../works/philebus-plato.md)* grants a place to pure, true pleasures within a well-ordered mixed life.

*Sources : Phaedo, 64c-67b, 82e-83d · Philebus, 63e-64a*

### [Nature of love](../axes/love-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Eros as lack 85% · Agape as gift 15%

In the *[Symposium](../works/symposium-plato.md)*, Diotima defines erōs as desire born of lack: child of Penia (Poverty) and Poros (Resource), love desires what it does not have and strives to possess it. This upward drive rises from beautiful bodies to Beauty itself, which lends a residual weight to gift.

*Sources : Symposium, 199c-204c · Phaedrus, 244a-257b*

### [Beauty](../axes/beauty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Objective beauty 100%

Beauty is neither a matter of taste nor an attribute of things: there exists a **Form of the Beautiful**, eternal and absolute, which neither comes to be nor perishes, and in which beautiful things participate. This is aesthetic objectivism at its purest, the terminus of the erotic ascent of the *[Symposium](../works/symposium-plato.md)*.

*Sources : Symposium, 210e-211b · Greater Hippias, 287c-289d*

### [Ideal and reality](../axes/political-ideal.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Utopianism 95% · Political realism 5%

The *[Republic](../works/republic-plato.md)* builds the just city in thought, an ideal model ruled by the Form of the Good, with its three classes, community of property and of women among the guardians, and education as the art of turning the soul. This is an avowed normative utopianism: the actual city must conform to the paradigm, not the reverse.

*Sources : Republic, II-V, 369a-473e · Republic, IX, 592a-b*

### [Who should decide](../axes/who-decides.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Epistocracy 95% · Radical democracy 5%

The argument proceeds by analogy with the **technai**, the crafts: one entrusts the helm not to the many but to the pilot who knows, and a ship where the crew elected its captain by acclamation would be deemed mad. Governing is likewise a knowledge, that of the Good and of justice, acquired only through a long education. From this Plato infers, against the Athenian [democracy](../axes/who-decides.md) that condemned Socrates, that authority must fall to competence and not to the opinion of the greater number. The thesis cuts all the deeper for striking at his own city's ruling regime.

*Sources : Republic, V, 473c-e · Republic, VI, 488a-489d (the ship metaphor)*

### [Nature of art](../axes/art-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mimesis 95% · Creation 5%

Art is **mimēsis**, imitation: painter and poet copy sensible things, which are themselves copies of the Forms, thus producing an image at the third remove, far from the truth. This definition of art as reproduction, not creation, grounds the critique of the poets and their banishment from the city.

*Sources : Republic, X, 595a-602c · Sophist, 235d-236c*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 100%

Platonic ethics is an ethics of virtue: a person's good lies in the state of the soul, and **justice** is defined as the harmony of its three parts, each performing its own function under the rule of reason. The good life is not a matter of rules or of calculating consequences but of an ordered, virtuous character.

*Sources : Republic, IV, 441c-444e · Gorgias, 506c-508c*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Universalism 100%

Against the relativism of Protagoras, for whom "man is the measure of all things", Plato grounds values in objective Forms, the **Just** itself and the **Good** itself, which depend on neither convention nor opinion. Good and justice are universal and real, knowable by reason.

*Sources : Theaetetus, 152a, 170a-172b · Republic, VI, 504e-509b*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The primacy of reason 90% · The primacy of feeling 10%

The [soul is tripartite](../glossary/tripartite-soul.md) and reason must govern it: the image of the **winged chariot** in the *[Phaedrus](../works/phaedrus-plato.md)* shows the charioteer (intellect) mastering the obedient horse (spirit) and the unruly horse (appetite). The passions are not guides but forces to be disciplined and ordered under the command of logos.

*Sources : Phaedrus, 246a-254e · Republic, IV, 439d-441c*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 100%

The whole order of things is directed toward the **Good**, the principle and end of all reality: in the *[Timaeus](../works/timaeus-plato.md)* the Demiurge fashions the world as beautiful and good as possible by looking to the eternal model. Human life finds its meaning in this cosmic teleology, by becoming as far as possible like the divine and the order of the whole.

*Sources : Timaeus, 29d-30c, 47a-c · Republic, VI, 505a-b*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Holism 90% · Individualism 10%

The *[Republic](../works/republic-plato.md)* examines justice first in the city, "writ large", in order then to read it in the soul: the political whole is prior and illuminates the individual part. The city is an organism in which each class is a member, and the common good takes precedence over private interest, to the point of abolishing property and family among the guardians.

*Sources : Republic, II, 368c-369a · Republic, V, 462a-466d*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 60% · Reasoned selection 40%

The appetites form the lowest part of the soul and must be **mastered** and curtailed under reason's guidance, for a soul given over to its desires becomes a "leaky jar" that nothing can fill. Plato preaches not the extinction of desire but its redirection: sublimated erōs becomes the drive that carries the soul toward the Beautiful and the Good.

*Sources : Gorgias, 493a-494a · Republic, IX, 571a-580a*

### [Beauty in life](../axes/aesthetic-life.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Primacy of the ethical 80% · Aestheticism 20%

Beauty plays a unique anagogic role: it is the only Form that shines forth in the sensible and, through erōs, sets the soul moving toward the intelligible. Yet it remains subordinate to the Good and to justice: seductive poetry is banished from the city if it corrupts the soul, and the beautiful is valuable only insofar as it leads to the true and the good.

*Sources : Phaedrus, 250d-251b · Republic, III, 401b-403c*

### [Purpose of art](../axes/art-purpose.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Truth 50% · Transformation 50%

Art is never judged by the pleasure it gives but by its truth and its moral effect: mimetic poetry is condemned for flattering the soul's lower part and straying from the truth. Conversely, the *[Republic](../works/republic-plato.md)* admits a regulated music and stories that shape character and tune the soul to harmony, making art an instrument of formation.

*Sources : Republic, X, 605a-608b · Republic, III, 398c-401d*

### [Hierarchy of tastes](../axes/taste-hierarchy.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Elitism of taste 90% · Egalitarianism of taste 10%

There is an objective hierarchy of pleasures and of the soul's objects: in the *[Republic](../works/republic-plato.md)*, only the philosopher, who has tasted all three kinds of pleasure, can judge which are truest, and his verdict ranks the pleasures of knowledge highest. Pleasures are not of equal worth, and competence to rank them belongs only to the well-ordered soul.

*Sources : Republic, IX, 580d-583a · Philebus, 51a-53c*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Correspondence 80% · Coherence 20%

Truth is the soul's correspondence with what really is, the Forms: true discourse "says of things that they are as they are", whereas the false says them otherwise than they are. This conception, close to correspondence with intelligible being, carries a coherentist component, true knowledge forming a dialectical system bound to the principle of the Good.

*Sources : Sophist, 262e-263d · Cratylus, 385b-c*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Harmony 100%

The divine is not the object of a faith opposed to reason but is grasped by intellect: the Good and the Forms serve as the divine principle, and the *[Laws](../works/laws-plato.md)* set out to prove the existence of gods rationally. The myths (the cave, Er, the chariot) do not contradict the logos but extend it where demonstration stops, in a continuity of the religious and the rational.

*Sources : Laws, X, 893b-899d · Republic, VII, 517b-c*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Egalitarianism 65% · Libertarianism 35%

The justice of the city rests on a distribution according to each person's function and natural aptitude: to each the task for which the soul is suited, and the community of goods among the guardians abolishes private wealth as a source of discord. This principle of "to each according to nature and function", foreign to market desert, leans toward a distribution fixed by the whole rather than by free exchange.

*Sources : Republic, IV, 433a-434c · Republic, III, 416d-417b*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological pessimism 70% · Anthropological optimism 30%

The ordinary person is a prisoner in the cave, chained to shadow and opinion, and most souls are ruled by the appetites: the ascent toward the true is rare, hard and reserved for a few. Without being a dark pessimism, this view stresses the soul's spontaneous blindness, which only a long education can set right.

*Sources : Republic, VII, 514a-517a · Phaedrus, 248a-249d*

### [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Necessity, leisure as the end 80% · Fulfillment 20%

The city arises because no one is self-sufficient: the division of labour answers the necessity of providing for needs, each producing best what nature fits him for. Manual work counts as a function in the service of the whole rather than as self-fulfilment, the highest flourishing being reserved for the philosopher's contemplative life.

*Sources : Republic, II, 369b-371e · Republic, IV, 421e-423d*

### [Universals](../axes/universals.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Realism about universals 98% · Nominalism 3%

The theory of Forms is realism about universals par excellence: the beautiful, the just, the circle exist in themselves, beyond the sensible, and particular things are only imperfect reflections of them. The universal is more real than the individual.

*Sources : Republic, VI-VII; Phaedo*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The body as instrument 95% · The lived body 5%

In the Phaedo, the body is the tomb of the soul: it hinders it, deceives it through the senses and turns it away from the contemplation of truth. The philosopher practises dying, that is, separating soul from body. The true self is the soul.

*Sources : Phaedo, 64a-67b*

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The primacy of truth 90% · Vital illusions 10%

Leaving the cave is painful, blinding, and yet the only liberation: to stay among the shadows is to take reflections for reality and live in chains without knowing it; knowing is not an ornament of happiness but the conversion of the whole soul toward what is. Yet the philosopher-king administers a noble lie to the city: the primacy of truth holds first for the soul being educated, and Plato accepts that the common order may itself rest on a myth.

*Sources : Republic, VII · Republic, III*

## Influenced by

- **[Parmenides](../philosophers/parmenides.md)** (reinterprets) : [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) inherits from [Parmenides](../philosophers/parmenides.md) the demand for a stable, intelligible being, the bedrock of his theory of Forms; but to account for error and change he commits, in the Sophist, a "parricide", admitting against the master a measure of non-being.
- **[Socrates](../philosophers/socrates.md)** (continues) : [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md), a pupil of [Socrates](../philosophers/socrates.md), makes him the central figure of his dialogues and carries on his questioning of definition and virtue, which he then unfolds into a metaphysics of the Forms that Socrates never stated.

## Influence on

- **[Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md)** (reinterprets) : The influence runs not through [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s own theses but through his character [Callicles](../characters/callicles.md): in the *[Gorgias](../works/gorgias-plato.md)*, Plato puts into his mouth the right of the stronger and the denunciation of the morality of the weak, precisely in order to refute them. [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) reactivates this immoralist position, which scholars liken to his morality of masters, and turns it against the morality of Socratic and Platonic inspiration.
- **[Plotinus](../philosophers/plotinus.md)** (reinterprets) : [Plotinus](../philosophers/plotinus.md) rereads [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) through his texts, six centuries later, and draws from them [Neoplatonism](../movements/platonism.md): a hierarchical metaphysics of the One, the Intellect and the Soul that goes beyond the letter of the dialogues.
- **[Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md)** (reinterprets) : [Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md) names [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) and [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) his two masters. He takes up the Platonic Ideas but shifts them: no longer separate intelligible realities, they become the eternal grades of the [will](../glossary/vouloir-vivre.md)'s objectification, which only [genius](../glossary/genie.md) contemplates in aesthetic experience.
- **[Simone Weil](../philosophers/simone-weil.md)** (reinterprets) : [Weil](../philosophers/simone-weil.md) reads [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) as a mystic: the Good beyond being, the conversion of the gaze out of the cave become in her the waiting for God and attention, a Christianized Platonism in which the true is received rather than conquered.
- **[Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md)** (continues) : [Stoic](../movements/stoicism.md) physics draws from [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s Timaeus the idea of a living cosmos, ordered and governed by a rational providence, which it recasts into a materialism of breath and immanent logos.
- **[Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)** (reacts against) : Trained for twenty years at the Academy, [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) keeps from [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) the demand for science and definition, but rejects the separation of the Forms: form exists only in individual substances, not in a world apart.
- **[Diogenes of Sinope](../philosophers/diogenes.md)** (reacts against) : [Diogenes](../philosophers/diogenes.md) defines his Cynicism against [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md): he mocks the [theory of Forms](../glossary/theory-of-forms.md) ("I see the cup, but not cupness") and "Plato's man", which he ridicules with a plucked chicken, opposing to the idealist's abstractions a nominalist, bodily naturalism.
