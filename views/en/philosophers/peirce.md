# Charles Sanders Peirce

*1839 – 1914 · American · [Pragmatism](../movements/pragmatism.md)*

American logician, scientist and philosopher, founder of [pragmatism](../movements/pragmatism.md) and of modern [semiotics](../glossary/semiotics.md). Trained in the exact sciences, a geodesist in state service for thirty years, in 1877-1878 he published in the Popular Science Monthly the series Illustrations of the Logic of Science, of which *[The Fixation of Belief](../works/the-fixation-of-belief-peirce.md)* and *[How to Make Our Ideas Clear](../works/how-to-make-our-ideas-clear-peirce.md)* lay down the [pragmatic maxim](../glossary/pragmatic-maxim.md). It was [William James](../philosophers/william-james.md) who, taking it up, popularised the word « pragmatism »; judging James's use too subjective, Peirce renamed his own doctrine « pragmaticism », a word « ugly enough to be safe from kidnappers ». Kept out of academia, he died in poverty, leaving an immense, scattered body of work, rediscovered in the twentieth century, that feeds [Dewey](../philosophers/dewey.md), logic and the philosophy of language.

## Core positions

1. [The meaning of words](../axes/meaning.md) : **To clarify an idea, ask what [conceivable practical effects](../glossary/pragmatic-maxim.md) its object would have: its whole meaning lies there, and nowhere else.**
2. [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md) : **The true is the opinion the [community of inquirers](../glossary/community-of-inquiry.md) is fated to come to agree upon in the end, and its object is the real, not what works for me.**
3. [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : **We never reach absolute certainty, and so much the better: the strength of science is to be an inquiry that knows itself [fallible](../glossary/fallibilism.md) and corrects itself.**
4. [Scientific method](../axes/scientific-method.md) : **Every discovery begins with an [abduction](../glossary/abduction.md), the disciplined wager of a hypothesis, which deduction unfolds and [induction](../glossary/induction.md) puts to the test.**
5. [Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md) : **One cannot doubt at will nor start from scratch: there is no first intuition, all thought is inference from other thoughts.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of words](../axes/meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Use theory 85% · Referentialism 15%

The whole of pragmatism is born of a rule about meaning: to make a conception clear, ask what [conceivable practical effects](../glossary/pragmatic-maxim.md) its object would have, for meaning resides nowhere but in those effects. To say a diamond is hard is to say it would resist being scratched; an idea from which no conceivable difference of experience could be drawn simply has no meaning. The move breaks with the referentialism that lodges meaning in the named object: Peirce places it in regulated conduct, the habit of action the idea engages, which brings him close to a theory of meaning as use, while keeping an experimental anchorage that sets him apart from a Wittgenstein. It is a weapon against verbal metaphysics, those disputes where one believes oneself to be thinking though no practical difference is at stake.

> “Consider what effects, that might conceivably have practical bearings, we conceive the object of our conception to have. Then, our conception of these effects is the whole of our conception of the object.”, How to Make Our Ideas Clear (1878)

*Sources : How to Make Our Ideas Clear (1878)*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Pragmatism 80% · Correspondence 20%

Peirce defines the true by the ideal limit of inquiry, not by its immediate success: the true opinion is the one all who inquire are fated to come to agree upon in the end, and the object of that final opinion is the real. Truth is thus neither a frozen copy of things nor the mere coherence of a system, but the point of convergence toward which a [community of inquirers](../glossary/community-of-inquiry.md) tends in the long run, provided it carries the inquiry far enough. This definition, which makes him a figure of the pragmatist pole, nonetheless keeps a realist core that sets him apart from [James](../philosophers/william-james.md): the true is not « what works for me » nor what it is useful for me to believe, but what inquiry, constrained by a real independent of our desires, is fatally led to acknowledge. The individual's utility has no part in it.

> “The opinion which is fated to be ultimately agreed to by all who investigate, is what we mean by the truth, and the object represented in this opinion is the real.”, How to Make Our Ideas Clear (1878)

*Sources : How to Make Our Ideas Clear (1878) · The Fixation of Belief (1877)*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Skepticism 55% · Dogmatism 45%

Against the Cartesian craving for indubitable foundations, Peirce erects fallibility into a principle: we can never reach absolute certainty nor perfect exactitude about anything. Yet far from sliding into [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md), he draws from it the strength of science, which advances only by knowing itself correctable: a belief is worth holding not because it is safe from doubt, but because it belongs to a process able to detect and repair its own errors. [Fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md) is not a concession wrung from the skeptic, it is the very engine of inquiry, which would be missing in anyone who believed himself already arrived. Hence an assumed middle position, leaning slightly toward the skeptical avowal of uncertainty, yet without renouncing knowledge.

*Sources : Collected Papers, I, 8-14 (Fallibilism) · The Fixation of Belief (1877)*

### [Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Coherentism 60% · Reliabilism 40%

Peirce attacks head-on [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s [hyperbolic doubt](../glossary/hyperbolic-doubt.md): one cannot doubt at will nor feign a doubt one does not have, real doubt is born of a surprise, a jolt of experience, never of a decision. He likewise rejects intuition, that supposed first cognition deriving from no other: all thought is inference from prior thoughts, sign referring to other signs. As a result, justification rests on no indubitable basic belief, but on the holding of the whole: knowledge is a cable whose strength comes from the multitude of intertwined fibres, not a chain as weak as its frailest link. This coherentist bent is doubled by a reliabilist strand, since what validates a belief is also the tested reliability of the method that produced it.

> “Reasoning should not form a chain which is no stronger than its weakest link, but a cable whose fibers may be ever so slender, provided they are sufficiently numerous and intimately connected.”, Some Consequences of Four Incapacities (1868)

*Sources : Questions Concerning Certain Faculties Claimed for Man (1868) · Some Consequences of Four Incapacities (1868)*

### [Scientific method](../axes/scientific-method.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Proof by experience 70% · The calculus of probabilities 30%

None of the three poles captures Peirce exactly, for he added precisely the missing piece: [abduction](../glossary/abduction.md), the inference that invents the hypothesis, upstream of the deduction that draws out its consequences and the [induction](../glossary/induction.md) that tests them. Science for him is this complete cycle, not one of its moments. He is nonetheless ranged on the inductivist side, since he remains an experimenter who grounds everything on the test of facts and makes induction the decisive trial; but he claims to defuse the flaw [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) denounced by holding that repeated induction corrects itself in the long run. His training as a statistician also gives him a probabilist strand, attentive to frequencies and to error, that foreshadows Bayesianism without reducing to it. What makes science is not a single rule of inference but the long-run self-correction of a [community](../glossary/community-of-inquiry.md) accountable to the real.

*Sources : The Fixation of Belief (1877) · Collected Papers, V, 180-212 (on abduction)*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Scientific realism 85% · Constructivism 15%

The real, for Peirce, is that whose characters are independent of what you or I may think of it: my fancy changes nothing in it, it forces itself upon inquiry. This scientific realism is the counterpart of his definition of truth, for the final opinion toward which inquiry converges is binding only because an external real compels it. He thus parts ways with any constructivism that would make the true a matter of paradigm or arbitrary consensus: the consensus of inquirers is not the cause of truth but its symptom, the effect of a reality that compels every sufficiently patient mind to yield. Peirce even calls himself a realist in the medieval sense, holding laws and generalities to be real, not individuals alone.

*Sources : How to Make Our Ideas Clear (1878) · The Fixation of Belief (1877)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 65% · Rationalism 35%

Peirce is a scientist for whom all knowledge is tested against experience, and doubt itself arises only from a sensible jolt, never from a decision of the mind: the bent is clearly empiricist. But he refuses the atomist empiricism of pure sensation and restores to inference, logic and real generalities a constitutive role, [abduction](../glossary/abduction.md) introducing ideas no observation contains. Neither blank slate nor innatism: an empiricism tempered by logic, nearer to criticism than to [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md).

*Sources : Some Consequences of Four Incapacities (1868)*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Scientism 60% · Epistemic pluralism 40%

*[The Fixation of Belief](../works/the-fixation-of-belief-peirce.md)* sets four methods against one another, tenacity, authority, the a priori taste, science, and keeps only the last: only the scientific method submits our opinions to something external capable of contradicting, and so of correcting, us. This trust in method brings Peirce close to a certain scientism. But his fallibilism and his refusal of all closure hold him at a distance from a Comte: science has no ready answer to everything, it is the indefinite march of a community that knows itself correctable, nearer to a critical rationalism than to a sovereign knowledge.

*Sources : The Fixation of Belief (1877)*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Bundle theory 60% · Psychological continuity 20% · Narrativism 20%

From his rejection of intuition Peirce draws a radical consequence about the self: there is no immediate self-consciousness, the self is a late inference, which the child constructs to account for its errors and the ignorance others reveal to it. Above all, since all thought is sign, man himself is a sign: what I am unfolds in the semiotic fabric of my thoughts referring to other thoughts, with no substantial core beneath the flux. We are far from [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s thinking thing; the position leans toward the bundle, but a bundle reinterpreted in terms of signs rather than pure perceptions.

> “The word or sign which man uses is the man himself.”, Some Consequences of Four Incapacities (1868)

*Sources : Some Consequences of Four Incapacities (1868)*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Finalism 50% · Vitalism 40% · Mechanism 10%

Against the strict mechanism that sees in nature only rigid, necessary laws, Peirce upholds [tychism](../glossary/tychism.md): chance is an objective reality, and laws are not given in advance but are habits that have formed and grow firmer in the course of an evolution. The cosmos has a history, it grows, and this growth is oriented by a tendency, evolutionary love or **agapasm**, which tips Peirce toward a finalist kind of explanation, while keeping the creative spontaneity that recalls vitalism. Far from the Cartesian machine, his nature is an organism in the making.

*Sources : The Doctrine of Necessity Examined (1892) · Evolutionary Love (1893)*

### [Wholes and parts](../axes/reduction-emergence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Emergentism 85% · Reductionism 15%

[Synechism](../glossary/synechism.md), the principle of continuity, commands in Peirce a refusal of sharp breaks and holds the continuous to be primary: between matter and mind, between the levels of the real, there are transitions, not jumps. Nature evolves by forming habits that bring forth new orders, irreducible to the mechanics of their elements. Against atomist reductionism, this is a metaphysics of emergence and growth, where the whole is more than the sum of parts that are, besides, never truly separate.

*Sources : The Law of Mind (1892)*

### [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · extrapolated · minor*

Idealism 85% · Materialism 15%

In his late, speculative metaphysics Peirce calls himself an [objective idealist](../glossary/objective-idealism.md): the one intelligible theory of the universe makes matter an effete mind, habits grown so inveterate as to become physical laws. This is neither the materialism that reduces mind to matter, nor [Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md)'s subjective idealism for which the world exists only as perceived: mind here is primary and objective, and continuity ([synechism](../glossary/synechism.md)) forbids separating the two into substances. The position stays fragile, stated in programmatic, unfinished texts, hence an extrapolated status.

*Sources : The Architecture of Theories (1891)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Theism 70% · Atheism 30%

In A Neglected Argument for the Reality of God, Peirce holds that the mind's free reverie, **musement**, leads naturally to the hypothesis of God as to the finest of [abductions](../glossary/abduction.md), a fruitful hypothesis rather than a demonstrated dogma. Peirce's God is less the personal God of the religions than an ordering reality correlated with his growing cosmos, hence a theism real but restrained, with no extreme.

*Sources : A Neglected Argument for the Reality of God (1908)*

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The primacy of truth 75% · Vital illusions 25%

His first rule of reason: do not block the way of inquiry. The methods that fix belief by tenacity or authority procure a real but fragile quiet; only inquiry, which accepts being disappointed by the real, durably stabilizes belief, by settling it on what does not depend on us.

*Sources : The Fixation of Belief (1877)*

## Influenced by

- **[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)** (reinterprets) : [Peirce](../philosophers/peirce.md) reads [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) closely and works through him at length: he recasts the table of categories and the architectonic of the first *[Critique](../works/critique-of-pure-reason-kant.md)* into his own triad (Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness) and his realism about universals. Kant remains the philosopher from whom he thinks, even where he corrects him.

## Influence on

- **[John Dewey](../philosophers/dewey.md)** (continues) : [Dewey](../philosophers/dewey.md) takes up [Peirce](../philosophers/peirce.md)'s logic of inquiry and his [community of inquiry](../glossary/community-of-inquiry.md), extending pragmatism toward instrumentalism and democratic inquiry: the fixation of belief becomes a social, experimental process oriented to action.
- **[William James](../philosophers/william-james.md)** (reinterprets) : [James](../philosophers/william-james.md) popularises and loosens the pragmatist maxim of [Peirce](../philosophers/peirce.md), which tied the meaning of an idea to its conceivable effects, steering it toward a psychological, pluralist theory of truth and belief.
