# John Stuart Mill

*1806 – 1873 · English · [Utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md), [Liberalism](../movements/liberalism.md)*

A major figure of British liberalism and heir to [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md)'s [utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md), philosopher, economist, logician and reformer, he was subjected from childhood by his father James Mill to an intensive education meant to make him the spokesman of philosophical radicalism. The depressive crisis he undergoes at twenty leads him to broaden that inheritance, making room within it for the cultivation of the feelings, for individual liberty and for a more demanding conception of happiness. A rigorous empiricist in the System of Logic, he sets out his ethics in Utilitarianism, his political philosophy in On Liberty and his feminism in The Subjection of Women, while serving in Parliament. A pivotal nineteenth-century thinker, he remains a central reference in debates on liberty, democracy and the foundation of morality.

## Core positions

1. [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md) : **Over what concerns himself alone, the individual is sovereign: society may coerce him only to prevent harm to others, never for his own good.**
2. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **Pleasures differ in quality and not in quantity alone: **better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied**.**
3. [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md) : **The free development of individuality is no private luxury but the very condition of progress, the source from which society as a whole draws.**
4. [Hierarchy of tastes](../axes/taste-hierarchy.md) : **The only judge of the worth of pleasures is the one acquainted with both: the verdict of the competent judges sets the pleasures of the mind at the top.**
5. [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : **To silence an opinion is to claim infallibility: even a truth, if never contested, decays into a dead dogma.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Abolitionism 90% · Fecundity and redemption 10%

Faithful to utilitarianism, Mill makes the reduction of suffering, together with the increase of happiness, the very end of morality: no pain is good in itself. He acknowledges that effort and ordeal can develop character, but never relinquishes the goal of a world where avoidable suffering would be abolished through moral and social progress.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, II · On Liberty*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 85% · Teleology and providence 10% · Absurdism 5%

Mill makes meaning depend on no cosmic purpose, which he holds undecidable: he locates it in the development of individuality and in devotion to ends beyond one's own interest, up to the service of humanity. A life has worth through the faculties it unfolds and the good it does, an immanent, cultivated meaning, not received from on high.

*Sources : On Liberty, III · Utilitarianism, II-III*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Death is nothing 70% · Immortalism 20% · Revolt / refusal 10%

Mill judges the immortality of the soul neither provable nor refutable by reason: experience rather pleads for the end of consciousness with the body, but the question stays open. He grants that the hope of a future life, though without secure foundation, may have value for conduct, without one's being able to rely on it as on knowledge.

*Sources : Three Essays on Religion (Theism)*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Reasoned selection 60% · Affirmation 35% · Extinction and detachment 5%

Mill corrects Bentham's hedonism with a qualitative hierarchy: not all desires and pleasures are equal, those that engage the higher faculties are worthier than those of the body. The sorting no longer bears on quantity alone but on the quality of pleasures, against a background that affirms satisfaction as the end of life.

> “It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied.” (Utilitarianism, ch. II)

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. II*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Existentialism 55% · Essentialism 45%

Against the idea of a fixed nature to be poured into the molds of custom, Mill holds that “human nature is not a machine, but a tree” that must grow and unfold from within. Each has to choose his own plan of life: individuality and self-development prevail over any preset model, even if an organic ground remains to be cultivated.

*Sources : On Liberty, ch. III*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Consequentialism 100%

Mill seeks to ground morality on a fact rather than an undemonstrable intuition: the only proof that a thing is desirable, he argues, is that people actually desire it, and what everyone ultimately desires is happiness. Thence the [greatest happiness principle](../glossary/greatest-happiness-principle.md) as the sole criterion of the right, and the consequentialism he opposes to moral intuitionism and to [Kantian duty](../glossary/categorical-imperative.md) alike: he charges Kant with being unable, in practice, to rule out a universalisable maxim except by invoking the desirability of its consequences, thus smuggling back the very utility he claimed to dismiss.

> “Actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness.” (Utilitarianism, ch. 2)

*Sources : Utilitarianism (1863), ch. 2 (the greatest happiness doctrine) · Utilitarianism, ch. 1 (critique of the Kantian a priori)*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Hedonism 70% · Eudaimonism 30%

Against the old objection that brands [hedonism](../glossary/hedonism.md) a doctrine fit for swine, Mill replies that it errs not about pleasure but about man: our higher faculties demand a happiness of another kind. Hence his break with [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md)'s purely quantitative calculus: pleasures differ in quality as well, and those of the intellect, the feelings and the imagination intrinsically prevail, because whoever knows both would not consent for anything to sink to the lower. At the heart of the argument lies a sense of dignity that an existence confined to bodily satisfactions would wound, which tilts Mill's hedonism toward a flourishing close to [eudaimonism](../glossary/eudaimonism.md).

> “It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied.” (Utilitarianism, ch. 2)

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (quality versus quantity of pleasures) · Utilitarianism, ch. 4 (happiness, the only thing desirable as an end)*

### [Hierarchy of tastes](../axes/taste-hierarchy.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Elitism of taste 85% · Egalitarianism of taste 15%

Distinguishing higher pleasures raises a difficulty: by what measure does one rank a quality that, by definition, does not reduce to quantity? Mill's original answer is an empirical criterion: the only judge is comparative experience, the settled preference of those who have known both orders of pleasure alike, the competent judges. Their verdict leans toward the pleasures of the mind, even at the cost of a lesser contentment. The resulting [taste elitism](../axes/taste-hierarchy.md) is therefore no matter of birth or class but of cultivation and experience, in principle open to all.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (the verdict of the competent judges)*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Anarchism 85% · Authority 15%

All conduct divides into what concerns the agent alone and what affects others: from this distinction Mill draws that coercion is never legitimate in the first sphere, where no one is a better judge of his own good than the person concerned. The harm principle follows: preventing harm to others is the only warrant for social power, never a person's supposed good. Mill thereby targets state paternalism and the tyranny of the majority alike, that diffuse despotism of opinion which, in a democracy, threatens the individual more surely than the tyrant of old.

> “The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilised community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others.” (On Liberty, ch. 1)

*Sources : On Liberty (1859), ch. 1 (statement of the harm principle) · On Liberty, ch. 1 (the tyranny of the majority)*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Individualism 88% · Holism 13%

Because no form of life holds a priori for everyone and no one knows in advance what suits him, Mill makes liberty the condition of those experiments of living through which each tests and shapes his own character. Individuality is therefore no mere private amenity but the mainspring of all progress: it is from original minds that the new truths and practices the whole of society inherits arise. Hence his target, the despotism of custom and the conformism that level characters and dry up that source, a threat all the more pressing as the power of mass opinion grows.

> “Human nature is not a machine to be built after a model, but a tree, which requires to grow and develop itself on all sides.” (On Liberty, ch. 3)

*Sources : On Liberty, ch. 3 (of individuality as one of the elements of well-being)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 95% · Rationalism 5%

All knowledge derives from experience: Mill denies any [innate](../glossary/innate-ideas.md) or a priori truths, even in mathematics, which he holds to be very well-confirmed inductive generalisations. The System of Logic makes induction the fundamental method of every science: a radical [empiricism](../axes/knowledge-source.md), in the line of [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) and [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md).

*Sources : A System of Logic (1843), book II (the inductive character of mathematical truths) · A System of Logic, book III (the methods of induction)*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Progressivism 93% · Conservatism 7%

A committed reformer, Mill sees in the progress of humanity the horizon of morals and politics. He campaigns for the extension of the suffrage, popular education, social reform and, in The Subjection of Women, for the emancipation of women, which he also carries into Parliament. Freedom of thought matters precisely because it is the engine of human improvement.

*Sources : The Subjection of Women (1869), ch. 1 (sexual equality as a demand of progress) · On Liberty, ch. 2 (freedom of opinion as a condition of progress)*

### [Who should decide](../axes/who-decides.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Epistocracy 73% · Radical democracy 28%

Mill judges a regime on two counts: what it does for public affairs and what it does to the citizens. Representative democracy wins because it best develops those who take part in it, yet that very participation exposes it to incompetence and the tyranny of the majority. His answer seeks to reconcile the two demands: a plural voting scheme that excludes no one from the franchise but weights votes by education, so that number does not silence competence, which inclines him toward [epistocracy](../axes/who-decides.md).

*Sources : Considerations on Representative Government (1861), ch. 8 (plural voting) · Considerations on Representative Government, ch. 3 (the superiority of representative government)*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Egalitarianism 73% · Libertarianism 28%

Concerned with the condition of workers, Mill criticises the distribution of wealth under laissez-faire and, in his later years, leans toward forms of cooperative socialism and limits on inheritance. The principle of utility, which counts each person's happiness equally, and the diminishing marginal utility of income tilt his distributive justice toward [egalitarianism](../axes/distributive-justice.md), without abolishing the market.

*Sources : Principles of Political Economy (1848), book II (on distribution, socialism and inheritance) · Utilitarianism, ch. 5 (each to count for one, all happiness counted equally)*

### [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Pathocentrism 75% · Anthropocentrism 25%

Since the moral criterion is pleasure and pain, the circle of moral concern extends to all sentient beings capable of suffering, not to humans alone: a pathocentrism. Mill explicitly counts the happiness of animals in the utilitarian calculus and supports early legislation against cruelty toward them.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (the happiness of all sentient creatures) · Whewell on Moral Philosophy (1852), on the moral standing of animals*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Impartialism 90% · Partialism 10%

In the calculus of happiness, “everybody to count for one, nobody for more than one”: utilitarianism demands a strict [impartiality](../axes/partiality.md), a strictly disinterested and benevolent spectator. The agent's own happiness counts no more than a stranger's, which Mill likens to the golden rule of Jesus of Nazareth.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (the disinterested and benevolent spectator) · Utilitarianism, ch. 5 (each to count for one, the formula ascribed to Bentham)*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Altruism 85% · Ethical egoism 15%

The moral end is not the agent's happiness but the general happiness: the utilitarian virtue is [altruism](../axes/self-interest.md), devotion to the good of all. Mill accordingly admires the spirit of sacrifice when it serves another's happiness, while denying any value to a sacrifice that increases no one's sum of happiness.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (sacrifice has value only if it increases happiness)*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Cosmopolitanism 80% · Communitarianism and patriotism 20%

The principle of utility knows no borders: the happiness of all humanity is its measure, giving Mill's morality a [cosmopolitan](../axes/belonging.md) reach. He condemns slavery wherever it occurs and judges progress by the improvement of the lot of all mankind, beyond national belonging.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (the happiness of all concerned) · The Contest in America (1862), against slavery*

### [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Maximalism 70% · Minimalism 30%

Maximising the general happiness exposes one to a potentially very heavy moral demand, since any action can be weighed against the greatest good. Yet Mill tempers this maximalism: everyday conduct is governed by tried secondary rules, and he does not expect from everyone the constant motive of the universal good, only that one do no harm and contribute on occasion.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (the secondary rules and the motive of actions)*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Legalism 60% · Equity-phronesis 40%

Mill denies that utility must be recomputed at each act: humanity has accumulated, through experience, secondary rules (do not lie, do not kill) that condense the lessons of what serves happiness. These rules guide ordinary conduct, yet remain subordinate to the principle of utility and may yield in cases of conflict: a tempered legalism, open to [equity](../axes/rule-spirit.md).

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (the corollaries of the principle and conflicts of rules)*

### [The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Deterrence 65% · Rehabilitation 35%

Justice itself reduces, for Mill, to a demand of utility: punishment is justified not by the retribution of a past wrong but by its effects, the deterrence and protection of society, and secondarily the reform of the offender. He rejects the notion of a desert of punishment independent of its consequences, without excluding rehabilitation.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 5 (justice and punishment reduced to utility)*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Free will 60% · Hard determinism 40%

Mill holds our volitions to be caused like every phenomenon, in keeping with the principle of universal causation; but he refuses to read this as fate: character itself can be reshaped by our desires and efforts. This doctrine, which he distinguishes from fatalism, is a [compatibilism](../glossary/compatibilism.md) reconciling causal [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md) with an effective moral [freedom](../glossary/free-will.md).

*Sources : A System of Logic, book VI, ch. 2 (of liberty and necessity)*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Scientism 80% · Epistemic pluralism 20%

The System of Logic aims to extend the inductive method of the natural sciences to moral and social facts, grounding a science of man (the moral sciences). This confidence in the unity of scientific method and its extension to the social marks a clear [scientistic](../axes/science-place.md) leaning, without thereby denying the specificity of human phenomena.

*Sources : A System of Logic, book VI (on the logic of the moral sciences)*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Universalism 85% · Constructivism 10% · Relativism 5%

The principle of utility serves as a universal criterion of the good, the same for all men and all ages: Mill does not relativise morality to cultures. The good is an objective fact, happiness, measurable by its effects, which ranks him on the side of a naturalistic [universalism](../axes/moral-scope.md) rather than relativism.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 and 4 (the greatest happiness principle as a universal criterion)*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Culturalism 80% · Naturalism 20%

For the empiricist Mill, the mind is largely shaped by experience, education and the association of ideas rather than by a fixed nature. In The Subjection of Women he argues that the alleged feminine nature is in fact a product of custom and education: a [culturalism](../axes/human-nature.md) that makes character a modifiable social construction.

*Sources : The Subjection of Women, ch. 1 (women's character as a product of upbringing) · A System of Logic, book VI, ch. 4-5 (ethology, the science of the formation of character)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Atheism 65% · Theism 35%

Raised without religion, Mill examines the question of God in terms of empirical proof: the argument from design makes at most probable a creator of limited power and goodness, never an omnipotent God compatible with the world's evil. This agnosticism, which allows only a faint hope of an afterlife, places him slightly on the side of unbelief.

*Sources : Three Essays on Religion (posthumous, 1874), Theism*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Skepticism 78% · Dogmatism 22%

To silence an opinion presupposes that one knows it false, hence that one believes oneself infallible: yet no one is, and history is full of truths first persecuted. From this fallibility Mill draws the demand for debate: suppressing an opinion may rob us of a truth, and even a false one sharpens, by contradiction, the received truth, which without it decays into a dead dogma professed without grasp of its meaning. Doubt for him is thus no paralysing scepticism but the condition of living knowledge, which ranks him, without surrendering truth, on the side of [fallibilism](../axes/certainty.md).

> “To silence the expression of an opinion is to rob the human race; those who dissent from it still more than those who hold it.” (On Liberty, ch. 2)

*Sources : On Liberty, ch. 2 (the argument from fallibility and the dead dogma)*

### [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Truthful rigorism 65% · Benevolent lying permitted 35%

Truthfulness ranks among the rules of highest utility, for mutual trust is the foundation of social well-being, and to weaken it harms all. Mill thus holds lying to be gravely harmful; but, utility remaining the ultimate criterion, he admits rare recognised exceptions, which keeps him clearly, without absolutism, on the side of truthful rigorism.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. 2 (veracity, a rule of transcendent utility, and its exceptions)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The examined life 65% · Primacy of action 35%

The depressive crisis Mill undergoes at twenty reveals to him the insufficiency of a purely analytic life: reading Wordsworth's poetry restores his sense of the value of inner culture and the cultivation of the feelings. Without renouncing reforming action, he henceforth grants a real place to the [examined life](../axes/interiority.md) and self-formation.

*Sources : Autobiography (posthumous, 1873), ch. 5 (a crisis in my mental history)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Sentimentalism 57% · Intellectualism 43%

Trained by his father in the cult of analysis and reasoning, Mill realises after his crisis that intellect alone withers: one must also cultivate the feelings and sensibility, which poetry and art nourish. Without denying reason's primacy in knowledge, he gives the emotions their due as an irreducible part of a good life, hence a near-central position.

*Sources : Autobiography, ch. 5 (the cultivation of the feelings and the debt to Wordsworth)*

### [Can a machine think](../axes/machine-mind.md)

The question of machine minds (artificial intelligence, functionalism) postdates Mill and does not arise in his work.

### [Scientific method](../axes/scientific-method.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Inductivism 85% · Falsificationism 10% · Bayesianism 5%

The System of Logic codifies induction: it is the experimental methods (agreement, difference) that, from the observation of regularities, establish the laws of nature. Scientific knowledge is built from the particular toward the general.

*Sources : A System of Logic, Book III*

### [Nature of rights](../axes/rights-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Rights from utility 90% · Legal positivism 10%

Mill rejects natural rights prior to any society and grounds rights in utility: to have a right is to have something society ought to secure for us because doing so serves the general well-being, utility in the largest sense, grounded in the permanent interests of humanity.

*Sources : Utilitarianism, ch. V; On Liberty*

### [Political liberty](../axes/political-liberty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Negative liberty 80% · Positive liberty 10% · Capability freedom 10%

The principle of On Liberty is negative: the only legitimate ground for constraining an individual is to prevent harm to others. Over himself, over his own body and mind, each is sovereign. Liberty is measured by the space shielded from interference.

*Sources : On Liberty, ch. I*

## Influenced by

- **[Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md)** (reinterprets) : Raised in [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md)'s [utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md), [Mill](../philosophers/mill.md) keeps its principle but reforms it: he introduces a qualitative hierarchy of pleasures and makes room for individual liberty against the mere calculus of quantities.
- **[Comte](../philosophers/comte.md)** (continues) : [Mill](../philosophers/mill.md) corresponds with [Comte](../philosophers/comte.md) and adopts the positive method, explanation by laws rather than by ultimate causes, while rejecting his religion of Humanity and his contempt for individual liberty.

## Influence on

- **[Russell](../philosophers/russell.md)** (continues) : [Mill](../philosophers/mill.md)'s secular godson, [Russell](../philosophers/russell.md) inherits his empiricism and liberalism: knowledge grounded in experience and the defence of liberties run through all his public work.
- **[Sidgwick](../philosophers/sidgwick.md)** (continues) : [Sidgwick](../philosophers/sidgwick.md) systematises [utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md) after [Mill](../philosophers/mill.md), making it more rigorous and more demanding in point of impartiality, seeking to reconcile it with the intuitions of common sense.
