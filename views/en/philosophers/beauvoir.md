# Simone de Beauvoir

*1908 – 1986 · French · [Existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md), [Phenomenology](../movements/phenomenology.md)*

A French philosopher of the twentieth century, a figure of [existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md) alongside [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md), trained in the [phenomenology](../movements/phenomenology.md) of Husserl and [Merleau-Ponty](../philosophers/merleau-ponty.md). A novelist, memoirist and theorist, she is above all the author of The Second Sex (1949), an inquiry that weaves together biology, history, psychoanalysis and Marxism to understand the condition imposed on women, and of The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), where she attempts the ethics existentialism had promised without writing it. Long received as Sartre's disciple, she is now read as a thinker in her own right, the founder of contemporary philosophical feminism and a major source of gender theory.

## Core positions

1. [Social identity](../axes/social-identity.md) : **One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman, for **femininity** is not a nature but a situation constructed by a whole civilization.**
2. [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : **Freedom is always situated: oppression does not leave the oppressed inwardly free, it concretely mutilates their power to project themselves.**
3. [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : **In a world without God, freedom is the only foundation of morality: to act well is to will one's freedom by willing that of others, with no rule given in advance.**
4. [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md) : **My freedom is fulfilled not against the freedom of others but through it: to oppress anyone is to impoverish the very meaning of my own project.**
5. [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md) : **Woman has been made man's Other: a subject reduced to the inessential by culture, with nothing in her biology destining her to that rank.**

## Positions

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Revolt / refusal 70% · Being-toward-death 20% · Death is nothing 10%

For Beauvoir, death is never natural: it is a violence, an unjustifiable scandal that shatters projects and outrages life, however long and consented to. Rather than greet it with serenity, she prefers revolt and indignation, refusing to dress up as meaningful what is only a defeat. The death of loved ones, witnessed up close, appears to her as an unacceptable rupture, not a fulfillment.

> “There is no natural death.” (A Very Easy Death)

*Sources : A Very Easy Death · The Coming of Age*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eudaimonism 55% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 30% · Hedonism 15%

The good, for Beauvoir, is neither pleasure nor a nature to be fulfilled, but the authentic existence that assumes its freedom and realizes itself in open projects. This self-realization is inseparable from a moral demand: to will one's own freedom is also to will that of others. Self-realization and the value of freedom are knotted together, against the abdication of seriousness.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Affirmation 60% · Reasoned selection 30% · Extinction and detachment 10%

Beauvoir refuses to mutilate desire in the name of a higher order: to take up one's existence is also to take up one's passions and desires in a free project, against the abdication of the spirit of seriousness that subjects them to fixed values. Desire is neither to be extinguished nor merely sorted, but to be lucidly assumed as an impulse toward the world and toward others.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 70% · Rationalism 30%

A phenomenologist, Beauvoir holds knowledge to be rooted in lived, embodied experience: the body is not one object among others but the situated standpoint from which the world takes on meaning. Her method, in The Second Sex, starts from concrete data, biology, history, testimony, rather than from a rational principle laid down a priori. This sets her on the side of experience, without making her a classical empiricist, since experience is always that of a freedom in situation.

*Sources : The Second Sex, Introduction · The Ethics of Ambiguity*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Skepticism 70% · Dogmatism 30%

The **ambiguity** of the human condition is to be taken up, not fled: this is exactly what the spirit of seriousness refuses, reassuring itself by treating values as fixed truths inscribed in things. Yet nothing is given in advance, neither meaning nor the good: they take shape in the free project, always revisable and without guarantee. Hence a principled wariness toward absolute certainties, which mask an abdication, without thereby lapsing into [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md), since freedom grounds values that are real though not founded in any nature.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Existentialism 100%

Beauvoir takes up the existentialist thesis that, for the human being, existence precedes essence: no nature defines in advance what one must be. Her formula “one is not born, but rather becomes, a woman” is its most famous application: the **feminine** is not a given essence but a becoming shaped by culture, which each woman must nonetheless take up in a free project.

> “One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman. No biological, psychic, or economic destiny defines the figure that the human female assumes within society.” (The Second Sex, vol. II)

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), vol. II, opening ("one is not born a woman") · The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947)*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Culturalism 95% · Naturalism 5%

Every consciousness, by a movement no sex grounds, sets up before itself an “Other” it devalues in order to assert itself as the essential one: racism, antisemitism and the oppression of women share this structure. What is peculiar to the feminine condition, and the riddle Beauvoir means to solve, is that women have consented to this role of absolute Other without turning it back, lacking a shared past, solidarity and common labour. The so-called feminine traits are therefore not a **nature** but the product of this instituted situation, hence a [culturalism](../axes/human-nature.md) that grants the bodily given without ever letting it settle one's destiny.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), Introduction (woman as "the Other") · The Second Sex, vol. II, "Formative Years"*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Free will 85% · Hard determinism 15%

A freedom is never pure spontaneity: it is always exercised from a body, a history and a social place that open or close the range of its possibles. From this situated character Beauvoir derives her break with the [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md) of Being and Nothingness, for whom even the slave remains ontologically free: to say so is to confuse freedom with its bare possibility, and to hide that oppression concretely mutilates freedom by sealing off the future and the means to project oneself. Freedom thus remains the primary fact, yet it can be injured from without, which makes oppression intelligible as a wrong and not merely a misfortune.

> “There is freedom only as engaged, willing itself by willing the disclosure of being through a world.” (The Ethics of Ambiguity)

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), on situated freedom and oppression · The Second Sex (1949), Introduction (the situation of woman)*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 50% · Ethics of care 30% · Consequentialism 10% · Deontologism 10%

If God is dead and no value falls from any heaven, the foundation of morality can only be freedom itself, the one thing that posits itself as unconditionally desirable in choosing itself. But a freedom cannot coherently will itself without also willing the other freedoms that give a world and a meaning to its project: this is Beauvoir's reply to the charge of nihilism levelled at existentialism. She nonetheless refuses any universal rule fixed in advance, for the good is decided each time within the thickness of a singular situation, hence an ethics of engagement and responsibility toward others, with no recipe and no alibi.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), "Freedom and Liberation"*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Altruism 85% · Ethical egoism 15%

My project takes shape only by disclosing a world, and that world has relief only when peopled with other freedoms that take it up and extend it: this is why “to will man free is to will that there be being”. The tension between self and other, which tradition resolves by sacrificing one to the other, thus dissolves at the root: my freedom is fulfilled not despite the freedom of others but through it, so that to oppress anyone is to impoverish the very meaning of what I aim at. Hence an altruism that is neither a duty imposed from without nor a renunciation, but the internal requirement of a freedom lucid about its own conditions.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), "Ambiguity and Freedom"*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Atheism 100%

Beauvoir is an atheist: raised in fervent Catholicism, she broke with faith in adolescence and never returned to it. Her existentialism starts from a world without God in which the human being, having no value guaranteed from above, must himself bring forth meaning and ground his morality on his freedom alone.

*Sources : Memoirs of a Dutiful Daughter (1958), the loss of faith · The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), a morality without God*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Constructed meaning 85% · Absurdism 10% · Teleology and providence 5%

In a world without given values or transcendent purpose, existence has no meaning other than the one freedom invents for it through its projects: meaning is constructed, never received. Beauvoir acknowledges the gratuitousness and ambiguity of the human condition, without turning it into a sterile absurd: this void is precisely what makes the creation of meaning both possible and required.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), on the meaning and gratuitousness of existence*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Constructivism 75% · Universalism 15% · Relativism 10%

Values are neither inscribed in an intelligible heaven nor purely relative: they are instituted by freedoms that recognize one another and commit themselves. Beauvoir always starts from singular, historical situations, yet the freedom she sets up as a requirement holds for all: a situated constructivism, universalizing without relativism or moral realism.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), the grounding of values in freedom*

### [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Truthful rigorism 90% · Benevolent lying permitted 10%

Beauvoir takes up the existentialist critique of [bad faith](../glossary/bad-faith.md): lying to oneself to flee one's freedom and responsibility. [Authenticity](../glossary/authenticity.md) consists in lucidly assuming the ambiguity of the human condition, without taking refuge in a fixed role, in the seriousness of ready-made values, or in the image one presents to others. Truthfulness toward oneself is a strong moral requirement.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), figures of evasion (the serious man, the sub-man)*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Holism 60% · Individualism 40%

The free individual remains the source of meaning and values, yet does not exist outside a web of relations: he is constituted in the gaze of others, in institutions and historical conditions that situate him. Beauvoir holds together the irreducibility of singular freedom and a concrete dependence on the collective, hence a position only slightly inclined toward the relational pole.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), freedom and the common world · The Second Sex (1949), woman situated within social relations*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Partialism 65% · Impartialism 35%

The ethics of ambiguity always starts from the concrete situation and from singular bonds, not from an overarching, impartial standpoint: one commits from where one is, alongside those to whom one is tied. Yet this partial anchoring opens onto the universal requirement of willing the freedom of all, which tempers the partialist leaning.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), engagement from within the situation*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Narrativism 60% · Psychological continuity 20% · Bundle theory 20%

The self is neither a substance nor a fixed essence: it is what it makes of itself through its choices and its history. In The Second Sex as in her Memoirs, the subject appears as a becoming constituted by a life-narrative, a series of projects taken up and reinterpreted, with no core given once and for all, hence a clear narrativist dominance.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), becoming-woman · Memoirs of a Dutiful Daughter (1958)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 80% · Fecundity and redemption 20%

The suffering of oppression has no redemptive fruitfulness: it is an evil to be fought, and any morality that would justify it in the name of a radiant future is a mystification Beauvoir denounces. Without denying the share of finitude and failure inherent in any existence, she leans toward the concrete abolition of avoidable suffering rather than toward its sacralization.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), critique of sacrificing the present to the future · The Second Sex (1949), suffering as a product of oppression*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Progressivism 95% · Conservatism 5%

Beauvoir is resolutely emancipatory: The Second Sex is a call to transform the structures that make woman the Other, and her engagement (feminism, anticolonialism, support for liberation struggles) aims at abolishing inherited oppressions. The traditions that fix roles are, for her, alienation, not an order to be conserved.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), perspective of liberation · Force of Circumstance (1963), political engagements*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Immanence 90% · Transcendence 10%

With no God or backworld, meaning and values play out entirely in this world, in the **immanence** of human projects and history. Beauvoir nonetheless gives the word **transcendence** a particular existential sense: not a beyond, but the surpassing by which freedom thrusts toward the future, oppression consisting in confining the other to the immanence of a fixed condition.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), Introduction (transcendence and immanence)*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Tragic thought 75% · Metaphysical optimism 25%

The human condition is fundamentally ambiguous: we are at once freedom and facticity, subject and thing, consciousness and a body doomed to death, with no reconciliation possible in a guaranteed harmony. Beauvoir assumes this tension as a tragic given of existence, to be lived lucidly rather than fled, yet without lapsing into despair.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), the ambiguity of the human condition*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · extrapolated · minor*

Dualism 50% · Materialism 40% · Idealism 10%

Beauvoir elaborates no systematic metaphysics, but her existentialist-inflected phenomenology presupposes a primary material and bodily reality, grasped through the consciousness that gives it meaning. The body, neither pure thing nor pure idea, is lived as “situation”, which forbids ranging her squarely under materialism or idealism.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), "The Data of Biology" and the body as situation*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Primacy of action 70% · The examined life 30%

Lucid self-examination matters, but freedom proves and realizes itself only in **action** and engagement: one becomes what one is only by acting in the world, not in inner contemplation alone. The leaning goes toward the primacy of action, without abandoning the demand for lucidity.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), freedom realized in action*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Celebration 75% · Asceticism 25%

Far from any asceticism, Beauvoir reclaims the body and sexuality as full dimensions of existence, to be lived freely and not under the weight of shame or of taboos imposed on women. Liberation includes the reappropriation of desire and pleasure as an assumed part of embodied freedom.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), vol. I, on sexual initiation and female sexuality*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Project 80% · Presentism 10% · Memory and transmission 10%

Existence is structured by the project: freedom is defined by the future it aims at and toward which it surpasses itself. Beauvoir conceives oppression precisely as the deprivation of a future, confinement in a repetitive present with no opening onto freely posited ends, which confirms the primacy of the temporality of the project.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), freedom as a project toward the future*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · extrapolated · minor*

Egalitarianism 80% · Libertarianism 20%

Close to Marxism and committed on the left, Beauvoir sees material inequalities as a concrete obstacle to the freedom of the oppressed: liberation requires transforming economic and social conditions, not merely mentalities. This egalitarian leaning is, however, inferred from her engagements more than from any systematic theory of distributive justice.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), the material conditions of emancipation · Force of Circumstance (1963), her commitments on the left*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological optimism 50% · Anthropological pessimism 50%

For Beauvoir, the human being is neither good nor bad by nature, since it has no nature: it is what it makes of itself, capable of the noblest engagement as of the basest complicity with oppression. Moral worth depends on the use one makes of one's freedom, which forbids any blanket verdict, optimistic or pessimistic, on human nature.

*Sources : The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), freedom between engagement and evasion*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anarchism 75% · Authority 25%

Beauvoir distrusts authorities that fix individuals in imposed roles (patriarchy, traditions, dogmas) and treats unquestioned obedience as a form of bad faith. Without advocating the abolition of all institutions, she makes a power's legitimacy depend on whether it widens each person's concrete freedom, hence a marked wariness toward authority.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), critique of patriarchal authority · The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947), the "serious man" and submission to established values*

### [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Fulfillment 60% · Alienation 30% · Necessity, leisure as the end 10%

Beauvoir sees work and productive activity as a decisive lever of emancipation: gaining access to work means, for woman, winning economic independence and transcendence through the project, against confinement in domestic immanence. Alienated labour exists (and then belongs to oppression), but freely assumed work is first of all a path of fulfilment.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), vol. II, "Toward Liberation" (work and independence)*

### [Social identity](../axes/social-identity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Social construction 90% · Social realism 10%

If no biological, psychic or economic destiny settles in advance what a woman is, then **femininity** cannot be an essence: it is the outcome of an elaboration civilization imposes on the human female. Beauvoir here pries apart sex, a bodily given, and the becoming-woman shaped by upbringing, law and the gaze of others, grounding the distinction the theories of gender would take up. The consequence is political: what was built in history can be undone there, against any naturalism that would justify the hierarchy of the sexes by nature.

> “One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman.” (The Second Sex, vol. II)

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), vol. II, opening*

### [Nature of love](../axes/love-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Philia (friendship) 70% · Agape as gift 30%

Beauvoir criticizes the love in which woman alienates herself and makes herself the other's object: the woman in love who loses herself in the beloved, for want of a project of her own, is a figure of bad faith. To this love-as-lack that seeks to complete itself in another, she opposes authentic love, the reciprocal bond of two freedoms that recognize and choose one another without either dissolving into the other. It is an existential [philia](../glossary/philia.md), made of mutual recognition, in which the share of gift lies in each willing the other's freedom as their own.

*Sources : The Second Sex (1949), vol. II, "The Woman in Love"*
