# Epicurus of Samos

*341 BCE – 270 BCE · Greek · [Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md)*

A Greek philosopher of the Hellenistic age, Epicurus founded the school of the Garden in Athens around 306 BCE, a community open to women and slaves where one lived withdrawn from the city. In a world unsettled by the fall of the free cities, he conceived philosophy as a medicine of the soul: “empty is the word of the philosopher that heals no human suffering”. Taking up the atomism of Democritus, he drew from it a physics meant to free men from the fear of the gods and of death, and grounded his whole ethics on the analysis of [pleasure](../glossary/hedonism.md). His work, almost entirely lost, reaches us through three letters and the *[Principal Doctrines](../works/principal-doctrines-epicurus.md)* preserved by Diogenes Laertius, and above all through the great poem of Lucretius. Long caricatured as a sensualist and charged with impiety, Epicureanism was one of antiquity's main currents, rival to [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md), and nourished modern materialist thought.

## Core positions

1. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **The sovereign good is [pleasure](../glossary/hedonism.md), but true pleasure is not enjoyment: it is [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md), the stable state in which, once all pain is removed, nothing more is lacking.**
2. [Death](../axes/death.md) : **[Death](../axes/death.md) is nothing to us: where it is, we no longer are, and no one can suffer a state in which he does not exist.**
3. [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : **Not all desires are equal: only those that are natural and necessary, easily met, matter, and it is by sorting them that happiness is made attainable for all.**
4. [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : **An atom can swerve from its fall without cause, the [clinamen](../glossary/clinamen.md): this grain of indeterminacy in matter saves [free will](../glossary/free-will.md) from the physicists' fate.**
5. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **The gods exist but take no part in human affairs: perfectly happy, they know neither anger nor [providence](../glossary/providence.md), and so are not to be feared.**

## Positions

### [The point of philosophy](../axes/metaphilosophy.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Transforming oneself 100%

Philosophy is medicine for the soul: its word is empty if it heals no suffering. Young or old, it is never too early nor too late to philosophize, that is, to work at the health of the soul; even the study of nature has no other end than to dispel the fears, of the gods and of death, that trouble our life.

> “Empty is the word of the philosopher that heals no human suffering.”, fr. 221 Usener (Porphyry, Letter to Marcella, 31)

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 122 · Principal Doctrines, XI*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 85% · Absurdism 10% · Teleology and providence 5%

Epicurus' gods, blessed and remote, neither govern nor destine anything: no providence assigns life an end. Meaning lies wholly on the human side, in the good life within our reach, ataraxia, friendship and simple, well-measured pleasures. Freed from the fear of the gods and of death, existence finds its sufficiency in itself.

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus · Principal Doctrines*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 75% · Existentialism 25%

Epicurus reads the human end in nature itself: from the cradle, before any reasoning, the animal seeks pleasure and shuns pain. This given natural constitution defines the sovereign good and the successful life, an essentialism of natural ends set within a materialist frame.

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus · Cicero, On Ends, I*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Hedonism 90% · Eudaimonism 10%

The criterion is drawn from the very nature of the living being: from birth, every creature pursues [pleasure](../glossary/hedonism.md) and shuns pain without any reasoning persuading it, which proves pleasure to be the first, connatural good. But Epicurus redefines what this pleasure is worth: its summit is not enjoyment in motion, which merely fills a lack, but the stable state in which, once the lack is filled, nothing remains to be added, the [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md) of the soul and the aponia of the body. Pleasure therefore has a limit, the absence of all pain, which makes it fully attainable and turns back the objection that pleasure is insatiable. This is his break both with vulgar pleasure and with the intellectualist eudaimonism of [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) and [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md).

> “Pleasure is the beginning and the end of the blessed life.”, Letter to Menoeceus, 128

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 128-129 · Principal Doctrines, III and XVIII (the limits of pleasure)*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Death is nothing 100%

The argument rests on two premises drawn from physics: all good and evil reside in sensation, and death, the dissolution of the soul's atoms, is precisely the total privation of sensation. It follows that death can be an evil for no one, since the subject who would suffer it never coexists with it: “while we are, it is not present; when it is present, we are no more”. The fear of dying therefore targets a nothingness, and to dread a state in which one will not be is as empty as grieving over the time before one's birth. This reasoning, the second plank of the [tetrapharmakos](../glossary/tetrapharmakos.md), removes the object of the anxiety that, on Epicurus' view, poisons the whole of life.

> “Death is nothing to us; for that which is dissolved is without sensation, and that which lacks sensation is nothing to us.”, Principal Doctrines, II

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 124-125 · Principal Doctrines, II*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Reasoned selection 85% · Extinction and detachment 10% · Affirmation 5%

Unhappiness comes from desires one cannot fulfil, or whose satisfaction soothes nothing: the therapy is therefore to sort desires by whether they are grounded in nature and necessary to happiness. The natural and necessary desires, few in number, are easily met; the empty desires, born of a limitless opinion such as glory or wealth, are insatiable and yield only disturbance. Wisdom thus neither suppresses desire wholesale, against Cynic asceticism, nor frees it from all bounds: it schools it, confining what one should want to what nature demands and makes easy. This is a [reasoned selection](../axes/desire.md) that brings happiness within everyone's reach.

> “Of the desires, some are natural and necessary, some natural but not necessary, and some neither natural nor necessary but arising from empty opinion.”, Principal Doctrines, XXIX

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 127-130 · Principal Doctrines, XXIX and XXX (the classification of desires)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Abolitionism 93% · Fecundity and redemption 7%

Suffering has no redemptive value: it is the very evil that the whole of Epicurean therapy seeks to abolish. The [tetrapharmakos](../glossary/tetrapharmakos.md) reassures: intense pain is brief, lasting pain is mild, and the sage can stay happy “even on the rack” through the memory of past pleasures. Far from sanctifying the evil to be endured, Epicurus teaches how to neutralize it, hence a clear abolitionist leaning.

*Sources : Principal Doctrines, IV (pain does not last continuously in the flesh) · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, X, 118 (the sage happy even amid torments)*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Materialism 100%

All that exists reduces to atoms and void: nothing comes from non-being, nothing dissolves into it, the whole is body and space. The soul itself is made of very fine atoms and dissolves at death. This is a thoroughgoing materialism, inherited from Democritus and developed by Lucretius, with no room for any immaterial reality.

> “The totality of things is made of bodies and void.”, Letter to Herodotus, 39

*Sources : Letter to Herodotus, 39-44 (atoms and void, nothing comes from nothing) · Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, I-II*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Free will 85% · Hard determinism 15%

Democritus' [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md), in which atoms fall in parallel lines by an unbroken necessity, makes responsibility impossible and reduces man to a puppet: if all is settled in advance, to blame or to deliberate loses its sense. Epicurus answers with the [clinamen](../glossary/clinamen.md), the slight, uncaused and unpredictable swerve by which an atom deviates from its fall at an undetermined moment: he thereby introduces a grain of [indeterminacy](../glossary/indeterminism.md) into matter itself, at the root of compounds, from which an action can emerge that is not wholly the effect of antecedents. The aim is less metaphysical than practical: to save [free will](../glossary/free-will.md), for enslavement to the physicists' “fate” would be worse even than the fear of the gods.

*Sources : Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, II, 251-293 (the swerve) · Letter to Menoeceus, 133-134 (better the myths than slavery to fate)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 65% · Atheism 35%

Epicurus starts from the common preconception of the gods as blessed and incorruptible beings, and takes it seriously to the end: a perfectly happy being knows neither anger nor favour, for the care and toil of governing a world would betray a need, hence an imperfection. It follows that the gods, set apart in the intermundia, exercise no [providence](../glossary/providence.md) and dispense neither reward nor punishment: popular piety, which pictures them jealous and vengeful, is the real impiety. This theology is therefore not a covert atheism, of which he was accused, but a therapeutic strategy, the first remedy of the [tetrapharmakos](../glossary/tetrapharmakos.md), which removes the ground of the fear of the gods.

> “The blessed and incorruptible being neither has trouble itself nor causes trouble to another; hence it is moved neither by anger nor by favour.”, Principal Doctrines, I

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 123-124 (the blessed gods, unconcerned with men) · Principal Doctrines, I (the blessed and incorruptible being knows neither trouble nor anger)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 95% · Rationalism 5%

If sensation is our only contact with the real, it must be the measure of all truth: a perception can be challenged only in the name of another, never in the name of reason, which has nothing else to judge by. Hence the Canonic, which sets sensation up as the first criterion and derives every concept from the repetition of perceptions, sedimented into preconceptions (prolepses) that then serve to recognise and name. Reason only elaborates these data; there are no [innate ideas](../glossary/innate-ideas.md), and the mind is born void of all content. This is a radical [empiricism](../axes/knowledge-source.md), set against Platonic innatism.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, X, 31-33 (the Canonic: sensations, preconceptions, feelings) · Letter to Herodotus, 37-38 (holding to sensations and preconceptions)*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Against [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md), Epicurus holds that “all sensations are true”: sensation, infallible because irrational and memoryless, never errs; error arises only from the judgment that opinion adds. This confidence in a secure criterion of truth is a [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md), even if grounded on the senses rather than reason.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, X, 31-32 (all sensations are true) · Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, IV, 469-521 (defence of the truth of the senses)*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Immanence 95% · Transcendence 5%

There is no transcendent order, no world of Ideas nor providence overhanging nature: everything, including the soul and the gods, belongs to the single universe of atoms and void. Meaning and happiness are found here below, in sensation and [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md), with no recourse to a beyond. This is a thoroughgoing [immanence](../axes/transcendence.md).

*Sources : Letter to Herodotus, 63-67 (the corporeal soul, dissolved at death) · Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, III (the mortality of the soul)*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Asceticism 60% · Celebration 40%

Although pleasure is the good, Epicurus advocates a measured sobriety: the highest pleasure is not the abundance of enjoyments but the absence of pain, which a little bread and water suffices to procure. “When we say that pleasure is the end, we do not mean the pleasures of the dissolute” but the absence of pain in the body and of trouble in the soul. The value leans slightly toward asceticism, out of frugality rather than contempt for the body.

> “Bread and water confer the highest pleasure when one brings them to needy lips.”, Vatican Sayings, 59 (cf. Letter to Menoeceus)

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 130-132 (the praise of frugality) · Principal Doctrines, XV (the wealth required by nature is easily obtained)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Intellectualism 70% · Sentimentalism 30%

The troubles of the soul, above all the fears of the gods and of death, arise from false opinions about nature: it is therefore exact knowledge of physics that cures anxiety. “Empty is the word of the philosopher that heals no human suffering”: the therapy of the passions proceeds through reasoning and an understanding of the limits of desire, which marks an intellectualist leaning.

*Sources : Letter to Herodotus, 81-82 (physics frees us from fears) · Vatican Sayings, 54 (philosophy as medicine of the soul)*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Metaphysical optimism 80% · Tragic thought 20%

The universe, infinite in atoms and worlds, is neither hostile nor tragic: once the fears of the gods and of death are dispelled, nature offers all that is needed for happiness, the good being easy to attain. This therapeutic optimism, where happiness is “within everyone's reach”, sets aside the tragic vision without lapsing into a benevolent providence.

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 122 and 133 (accessible happiness, the sage and fate) · Principal Doctrines, XXVII-XXVIII (friendship and confidence)*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Communitarianism and patriotism 60% · Cosmopolitanism 40%

The motto “live unnoticed” (lathe biosas) turns the sage away from public life: the city, its honours and ambitions are sources of disturbance, and true security lies in withdrawal and friendship, not in civic engagement. This preference for a small elective community, the Garden, over an active cosmopolitanism inclines slightly toward communitarianism.

> “Live unnoticed.”, Fragment 551 Usener (lathe biosas)

*Sources : Vatican Sayings, 58 (freeing oneself from the prison of public affairs) · Principal Doctrines, XIV (security through withdrawal from the crowd)*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Consequentialism 70% · Virtue ethics 30%

The criterion of action is its contribution to pleasure understood as freedom from disturbance: one must weigh by reckoning (the “calculus of pleasures and pains”) the good and ill each choice brings. This consequentialist, hedonist structure nonetheless grants a place to prudence ([phronesis](../glossary/phronesis.md)) as the cardinal virtue, hence a share of virtue ethics: “one cannot live pleasantly without living prudently, honourably and justly”.

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 129-132 (the calculus of pleasures; prudence, the source of the virtues) · Principal Doctrines, V (living pleasantly, prudently, honourably and justly)*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Ethical egoism 75% · Altruism 25%

The starting point is each person's pursuit of his own happiness, which seems egoistic; but friendship, born of utility, becomes for Epicurus the greatest of the goods that wisdom procures and a thing sought for its own sake. “Of all that wisdom provides for the blessedness of the whole life, much the greatest is friendship”: self-interest rightly understood thus blossoms into a disinterested bond.

*Sources : Principal Doctrines, XXVII (friendship, the greatest of goods) · Vatican Sayings, 23 and 39 (friendship sought for its own sake)*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Contractualism 90% · Organic community 10%

Justice is nothing absolute or natural in itself: it is a “compact of utility” made so as not to harm one another, varying with place and circumstance. This conventionalist conception, where the just reduces to a contract grounded in common advantage, is an early form of contractualism.

> “Natural justice is a compact of advantage, made to the end that men may neither harm one another nor be harmed.”, Principal Doctrines, XXXI

*Sources : Principal Doctrines, XXXI-XXXVIII (justice as a compact of utility)*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Lucidity without hope 70% · The principle of hope 30%

Epicurean happiness is won in the present: one must know how to enjoy the present good and beware of hanging everything on the expectation of an uncertain future, for “the future is neither wholly ours nor wholly not ours”. This distrust of hope, which diverts us from present pleasure, leans toward lucidity, without tipping into despair.

*Sources : Vatican Sayings, 14 and 47 (enjoying the present, not living in expectation) · Letter to Menoeceus, 127 (the future is neither wholly ours nor wholly not ours)*

### [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Amor fati 80% · Revolt 20%

The memory of past pleasures is a resource for happiness: the sage summons it to stay serene amid present pain, and gratitude for what has been tempers regret. This way of welcoming the past as a good to be savoured, rather than contesting it, leans toward a serene acceptance on the amor fati side.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, X, 22 (Epicurus' farewell letter, joy of recollection amid pains) · Cicero, On Moral Ends, I (the memory of past goods)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The examined life 75% · Primacy of action 25%

Wisdom demands a lucid examination of oneself and of one's desires: one must “meditate day and night” on the precepts that lead to [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md), distinguish within oneself empty desires from natural ones, and keep watch over one's opinions. This reflective practice, turned more toward inner quietude than toward action in the world, belongs to the examined life.

*Sources : Letter to Menoeceus, 122 and 135 (meditate on these precepts day and night)*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mechanism 100%

The world results from the merely mechanical encounter of atoms in the void, without any final cause or design: nature was not made for men, and it is an error to seek a purpose in organs or stars. Lucretius explicitly mocks the finalist illusion. This is a thoroughgoing [mechanism](../axes/causality.md), tempered only by the chance of the [clinamen](../glossary/clinamen.md).

*Sources : Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, IV, 823-857 (against the purposiveness of organs) · Letter to Herodotus, 76-77 (celestial phenomena without divine intervention)*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Bundle theory 100%

The soul is not a simple, immortal substance but an aggregate of very fine atoms distributed through the body: it forms with the body and scatters at death, as atomism requires. The self thus reduces to a transient material composition, which corresponds to a bundle theory rather than to a substantial subject.

*Sources : Letter to Herodotus, 63-68 (the corporeal nature of the soul) · Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, III, 417-829 (the material, mortal soul)*

### [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Argument from evil (atheism) 95% · Revolt / anti-theodicy 5%

The so-called Epicurean dilemma, transmitted by Lactantius, concludes that a God at once good and all-powerful is incompatible with the existence of evil: either he wishes to prevent it and cannot, or he can and will not. For Epicurus, the blessed gods do not meddle with the world.

*Sources : Lactantius, On the Anger of God, 13 (attributed dilemma); Letter to Menoeceus*

### [Nature of love](../axes/love-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Philia (friendship) 90% · Eros as lack 10%

The Epicurean sage seeks neither passion nor the ecstasy of union, but [friendship](../glossary/philia.md): of all the things wisdom provides for the happiness of a whole life, he makes it the greatest (*[Principal Doctrines](../works/principal-doctrines-epicurus.md)*, *[Vatican Sayings](../works/vatican-sayings-epicurus.md)*). Born at first from the need for security, it frees us from the fear of others and sustains [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md); yet Epicurus moves from calculation to bond, for the sage comes to cherish the friend for his own sake and would, if need be, risk his life for him. This is a friendship with neither heaven nor the instinct of the species, reciprocal and cultivated in the Garden; only the trace of its utilitarian origin leaves it a slight share of lack being filled.

> “Of all the things that wisdom provides for the happiness of a whole life, by far the greatest is the possession of friendship.”, Principal Doctrines, XXVII

*Sources : Principal Doctrines, XXVII · Vatican Sayings, 23, 34 and 52*

## Influence on

- **[Lucretius](../philosophers/lucretius.md)** (transmits) : Three centuries after [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md), [Lucretius](../philosophers/lucretius.md) transmits to the Roman world, in a great poem, his atomist physics and his ethics of release from the fear of the gods and of death.
- **[Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md)** (reinterprets) : Across the rivalry of the schools, [Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md) quotes and appropriates [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) as a moral authority in his Letters, on death, friendship and the right measure of desire.
