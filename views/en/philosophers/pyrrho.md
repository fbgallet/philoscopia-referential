# Pyrrho of Elis

*360 BCE – 270 BCE · Greek · [Skepticism](../movements/skepticism.md)*

A Greek philosopher of Elis, in the Peloponnese, held to be the father of ancient [skepticism](../movements/skepticism.md). He wrote nothing: his doctrine is known to us only through his disciple Timon of Phlius, and then through Aenesidemus, Cicero, Diogenes Laertius and [Sextus Empiricus](../philosophers/sextus-empiricus.md), who claims him as his inspiration. Tradition reports that he followed Alexander's expedition as far as India, where he frequented the naked sages (the "gymnosophists"), an experience often linked to his ideal of indifference and imperturbability. His skepticism is not in the first place a theory of knowledge but a way of life: an existential withdrawal before things judged indeterminate, in which the [suspension of judgment](../glossary/epoche.md) opens onto tranquillity ([ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md)). Where [Sextus](../philosophers/sextus-empiricus.md) would later turn this into an argued method, Pyrrho stays with the figure of the sage whom nothing moves.

## Core positions

1. [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : **Things are no more this than that: indeterminate and indiscriminable, they deserve neither our senses nor our opinions, so [hold no opinion](../glossary/epoche.md).**
2. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **Do not seek peace: to one who stops judging things, [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md) follows of itself, as a shadow follows the body.**
3. [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : **Since nothing is good or bad by nature, nothing is worth pursuing: be [indifferent](../glossary/adiaphora.md) to all that passes, and nothing will trouble you any longer.**
4. [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : **Disturbance comes from the opinion that a thing is an evil: withdraw the judgment, and the passion that depended on it dies away.**
5. [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md) : **Nothing is just or unjust by nature: faced with the diversity of customs, [suspend your judgment](../glossary/epoche.md) and follow the usage of your country without believing it true.**

## Positions

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Skepticism 100%

The testimony of Aristocles, gathered from Timon, reduces all wisdom to three questions: how are things by nature, what attitude should we take toward them, and what follows from this for us. The answer to the first is what makes Pyrrho original: things are indeterminate, unstable and indiscriminable, so that neither our sensations nor our opinions tell the true or the false. From this verdict about the object, and not from an examination of our faculties, he concludes that we should place no trust in them and remain [without opinion](../glossary/epoche.md). More radical than fallibilism, which revises its beliefs, he refuses even to assert that nothing is knowable, and is content to say that things are "no more this than that".

> “Things are no more this than that, nor both at once, nor neither.”, Aristocles, cited by Eusebius, Preparation for the Gospel, XIV, 18

*Sources : Aristocles (after Timon), cited by Eusebius, Preparation for the Gospel, XIV, 18 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 61-108*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Eudaimonism 75% · Hedonism 25%

The testimony's third question gives the end aimed at: to one who holds things indeterminate and gives up pronouncing on them, there comes first [aphasia](../glossary/aphasia.md) (saying nothing), then [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md), the absence of disturbance. The highest good is thus neither pleasure nor virtue, but a peace reached by subtraction: it is by ceasing to believe things good or bad by nature that one ceases to pursue or flee them, and the disturbance falls away. Timon is said to have likened this imperturbability to the calm of a sage whom nothing reaches. The eudaimonist strand remains, since tranquillity is indeed the flourishing sought, but without the proper function or the excellence of the Aristotelian successful life.

*Sources : Aristocles (after Timon), cited by Eusebius, Preparation for the Gospel, XIV, 18 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 107-108*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Extinction and detachment 75% · Reasoned selection 25%

If nothing is good or bad by nature, then no object deserves to be pursued or feared: Pyrrho draws from this an [indifference](../glossary/adiaphora.md) (adiaphoria) toward things, the lived spring of his detachment. Where [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) sorts desires into the necessary and the empty, Pyrrho does not sort: it is the value judgment itself that he withdraws, which defuses desire at its root rather than ordering it. Tradition credits the sage with such evenness of soul that nothing moves him, yet without advocating an ascetic extinction: he lives by what appears, hunger, thirst, customs, without attaching to them as goods.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 61-66 · Cicero, On Moral Ends, II, 35 and IV, 43*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

The primacy of reason 80% · The primacy of feeling 20%

Disturbance, for Pyrrho, arises from the opinion that a thing is good or bad: remove the judgment, and the emotion that depended on it falls away. This is an intellectualism of the passions, shared with the Stoics, but he pushes it further than they do, since he withdraws not only false judgments but the very act of judging things. One nuance holds him back from the extreme: tradition grants that the sage still suffers in the body, from cold or pain, affections no suspension abolishes. Diogenes Laertius indeed reports that Pyrrho, caught one day fleeing a dog, admitted it is hard to strip the human being away entirely.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 66-68*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Relativism 80% · Constructivism 20%

The verdict of indeterminacy applies to values as well: nothing is just or unjust, beautiful or ugly "by nature", but only by convention and custom. Timon reports that for Pyrrho "nothing is beautiful or ugly, just or unjust" in itself. Lacking a criterion to decide, the sage [suspends judgment](../glossary/epoche.md) on the existence of universal norms and regulates himself, by default, on received usage, without holding it true. Hence a null universalism and a thin constructivist strand, since following custom is not grounding it: it is conforming to it in the absence of any established moral truth.

*Sources : Aristocles (after Timon), cited by Eusebius, Preparation for the Gospel, XIV, 18 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 61 and 101*

### [Ethics of belief](../axes/belief-ethics.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Evidentialism 85% · Non-evidentialism 15%

Pyrrho's first move is to withdraw his assent from every opinion: while things remain indeterminate, to grant belief to one rather than another would be to rush. This restraint meets the **evidentialist** spirit, to believe only in proportion to what shows itself, but in an extreme form, since nothing shows itself enough to deserve belief. The Pyrrhonian nuance is that he makes of this restraint no moral duty: it is the lived effect of the indeterminacy of things, the impossibility of assenting where nothing tips the scale, rather than a rule one would impose on oneself.

*Sources : Aristocles (after Timon), cited by Eusebius, Preparation for the Gospel, XIV, 18 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 61-62*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Theism 50% · Atheism 50%

The indeterminacy of all things extends to the divine: since neither the existence nor the non-existence of the gods can be established, the sage does not decide. This suspension is more radical than **agnosticism**, which asserts undecidability as a thesis, since Pyrrho does not even posit that the divine is unknowable. In practice, following the custom of his city, he honors the gods like everyone else, without attaching dogmatic belief to it. It is this lived suspension, and not a position of balance between faith and atheism, that the median value marks here.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 64 and 108 · Sextus Empiricus, Outlines of Pyrrhonism, III, 2 (on the gods)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 55% · Fecundity and redemption 45%

Pyrrho grants suffering neither a fruitfulness that would forge the soul, nor the status of an absolute evil to be abolished: either would be to judge it by nature twice over. The greater part of the torment, he holds, comes from the added opinion that what happens to us is an evil; to withdraw that judgment lightens it accordingly, and only the forced pain of the body remains. The position is thus close to the median indifference, a suffering defused by the suspension of the value judgment rather than magnified or fought on principle.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 66-68 · Cicero, Tusculan Disputations, V (on the imperturbability of the sage)*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Lucidity without hope 75% · The principle of hope 25%

To hope, like to fear, is to judge that a non-evident future will be good or bad: yet it is precisely this judgment on the indeterminate that Pyrrho suspends. With no opinion about what tomorrow must bring, he does not hang his peace on an expectation, and welcomes what presents itself. The tone is one of lucid tranquillity rather than a principle of hope, yet without the tragic despair foreign to one who has given up judging the future.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 107-108*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The examined life 65% · Primacy of action 35%

Pyrrho's skepticism is first an attitude turned toward the testing of appearances rather than the world-transforming deed, which leans it toward the examined life. But unlike the Socratic examination of the self, it carries no doctrine of the soul to be deepened: no inner self to decipher, only the discipline of a gaze that frees itself from opinions. Hence an interiority that is real but thin, and held back from the extreme.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives of the Philosophers, IX, 61-70*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*not applicable*

Holding things to be indeterminate, Pyrrho can adopt no theory of the true, whether by correspondence, coherence or otherwise, without contradicting his own suspension. He holds to what appears and takes no stand on what would be true "by nature".

### [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*not applicable*

Materialism, idealism and dualism are theses on the real "by nature", precisely what Pyrrho declares indeterminate and indiscriminable. To decide would be to adopt the dogmatism his skepticism frees itself from.

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*not applicable*

Every theory of the self, substance, continuity or bundle, is a thesis on what the soul would be by nature; yet Pyrrho leaves that nature indeterminate. He speaks of the human being and its affections at the level of appearance, with no doctrine of personal identity.

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*not applicable*

To decide between mechanism, finalism and vitalism presupposes knowing the hidden causes behind appearances, which Pyrrhonian indeterminacy forbids. Pyrrho grants only the phenomenon, never an explanation of the ground of things.
