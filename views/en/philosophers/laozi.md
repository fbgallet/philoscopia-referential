# Laozi 老子 ("the Old Master")

*571 BCE – 471 BCE · Chinese · [Taoism](../movements/taoism.md)*

A semi-legendary Chinese sage, traditionally placed in the sixth century BCE and presented by tradition as an archivist at the Zhou court and an elder of [Confucius](../philosophers/confucius.md). Historical scholarship now regards the figure as uncertain and dates the *[Dao De Jing](../works/dao-de-jing-laozi.md)* attributed to him rather to the fourth-third centuries BCE. This brief collection of aphorisms, now one of the most translated texts in the world, is the foundation of [Taoism](../movements/taoism.md): it unfolds the [Dao](../glossary/dao.md), the ineffable way and source of all things, [wu wei](../glossary/wu-wei.md) (non-action) and [ziran](../glossary/ziran.md) (natural spontaneity). Reacting against Confucian ritualism and the Legalist schools, his thought shaped Chinese culture, Chan (Zen) Buddhism and, in the West, a whole ecological and anti-productivist current.

## Core positions

1. [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md) : **[Act without forcing](../glossary/wu-wei.md): espouse the spontaneous course of things as water flows around the obstacle, and nothing is left undone.**
2. [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md) : **The human models himself on the earth, the earth on heaven, heaven on the [Dao](../glossary/dao.md): his place is to follow nature, not to master it.**
3. [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md) : **Multiplying clever tools breeds artifice and desire: true wealth lies in [simplicity](../glossary/ziran.md), not in the refinement of means.**
4. [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : **When the great [Dao](../glossary/dao.md) is lost, benevolence and righteousness arise: a proclaimed virtue is already a fallen one.**
5. [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : **No disaster is worse than not knowing what is enough: lessen your desires and you recover the [uncarved block](../glossary/pu.md) before all craving.**

## Positions

### [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

What is up to us 93% · Prometheanism 8%

Against the voluntarism that would bend the course of things to a design, Laozi observes that forced intervention turns against itself: the more one grasps, the more one loses; the more laws are made, the more thieves arise. [Wu wei](../glossary/wu-wei.md) is not passivity but the art of espousing the [spontaneous course](../glossary/ziran.md) of things, like water that flows around the obstacle without fighting it: act without forcing, and everything is accomplished. It is the exact reverse of the Prometheanism that measures the human by their grip on the world.

> “The Dao does nothing, and yet nothing is left undone.” (Dao De Jing, ch. 37)

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 37 and 48 · Dao De Jing, ch. 57 and 64*

### [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Techno-optimism 90% · Techno-criticism 10%

The refinement of means is for Laozi not progress but a source of agitation and cunning that estranges us from the [Dao](../glossary/dao.md). His ideal community owns machines and weapons but does not use them: multiplying clever tools breeds artifice and desire, whereas [simplicity](../glossary/ziran.md) brings one back to essentials. This distrust of instrumental ingenuity, voiced long before [Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md) and Ellul, makes him a distant ancestor of techno-criticism.

> “The more clever tools there are, the more the state sinks into disorder.” (Dao De Jing, ch. 57)

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 80 · Dao De Jing, ch. 57*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 75% · Reasoned selection 25%

It is not needs that trouble us but the desires manufactured by craving: "the five colors blind the eye." Wisdom lies in diminishing desires and being content, for "to know contentment is true wealth" and "no disaster is worse than not knowing what is enough." The detachment aims less at a [Buddhist](../movements/buddhism.md) extinction of desire than at a return to the [natural](../glossary/ziran.md): to fill the belly, not the craving eye.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 12 and 19 · Dao De Jing, ch. 33, 44 and 46*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Immanence 85% · Transcendence 15%

The [Dao](../glossary/dao.md) is not a separate god ordering the world from above: it is the immanent matrix from which all things arise and to which all return, which "gives birth to the ten thousand beings" without commanding them, nourishing them without possessing them. The sacred, here, has no world-behind: it coincides with the very course of nature. Yet this immanence keeps a residue of the inaccessible, since the Dao remains obscure and prior to all distinction, which holds the score back from the extreme.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 25 and 51 · Dao De Jing, ch. 4 and 42*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Member among others 75% · Stewardship 25%

"The human takes the earth as model, the earth the heavens, the heavens the Dao, and the Dao that which is so of itself": far from dominating nature, the human is set within a nesting in which he ranks after it. He is neither master nor steward charged with a mission, but one being among others called to follow the [spontaneous course](../glossary/ziran.md) of the living. This humble inscription within the natural order brings him close to the deep ecology of Næss.

> “The human models himself on the earth, the earth on heaven, heaven on the Dao, and the Dao on itself.” (Dao De Jing, ch. 25)

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 25 · Dao De Jing, ch. 51 and 64*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Anarchism 78% · Authority 22%

The good prince governs by [non-interference](../glossary/wu-wei.md): the best ruler is the one whose people barely know he exists, and when the work is done, each says "it happened of itself." The less the state legislates, taxes and agitates, the better the people fare. This is not quite anarchism, since a sage-king remains and an order subsists, but a minimal government that effaces itself, the opposite of the contemporary Legalists.

> “Governing a great state is like frying a small fish.” (Dao De Jing, ch. 60)

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 17 and 60 · Dao De Jing, ch. 57 and 75*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Virtue ethics 100%

Laozi distrusts proclaimed codes and duties: "when the great Dao is lost, benevolence and righteousness arise," a sign of a virtue already fallen into artifice. Right action is neither obedience to a rule nor the calculation of an outcome, but the expression of an inner state attuned to the Dao, the [virtue](../glossary/de-virtue.md) (de) that acts without parading itself as virtuous. It is, for want of any alternative, a virtue ethics, but a spontaneous virtue that denies itself as a code the moment it is named.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 18, 19 and 38*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Equity-phronesis 85% · Legalism 15%

The proliferation of rules is for Laozi a symptom of decadence, not a remedy. Rightness cannot be codified; it comes from a supple, living accord with the situation, which places Laozi on the side of the spirit against the letter, out of distrust of every letter.

> “The more laws and edicts there are, the more thieves and robbers multiply.” (Dao De Jing, ch. 57)

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 57 and 38*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological optimism 80% · Anthropological pessimism 20%

Evil does not come from a corrupt human nature but from what corrupts it: affected knowledge, craving, institutions that pit a person against their [naturalness](../glossary/ziran.md). "Abolish wisdom, discard knowledge, and the people will benefit a hundredfold": it suffices to cut away artifice for an original goodness to reappear, like uncarved wood before it is sculpted. This trust in the natural, close to [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md), sets him apart from pessimism.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 19 and 28 · Dao De Jing, ch. 3 and 65*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 85% · Existentialism 15%

Each being has its own nature, its [way of being so of itself](../glossary/ziran.md), which wisdom consists in letting unfold rather than thwarting. To live well is not to invent oneself freely but to recover the [uncarved block](../glossary/pu.md) (pu), the unhewn simplicity one had before culture sculpted it. The ideal is not self-creation but a return to a given nature.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 28 and 19 · Dao De Jing, ch. 32 and 37*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Skepticism 70% · Dogmatism 30%

"The Dao that can be named is not the constant Dao": ultimate reality eludes language and concepts, which carve up and fix what is fluid. Laozi mocks busy knowledge and exalts a "knowing not-knowing," but this is not the [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) of suspended judgment: it is a higher, intuitive and silent knowledge, attuned to the Dao. The doubt bears on discursive knowledge, not on access to truth, hence a median position leaning toward distrust.

> “The Dao that can be named is not the constant Dao.” (Dao De Jing, ch. 1)

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 1 and 56 · Dao De Jing, ch. 48 and 71*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The examined life 78% · Primacy of action 22%

"Without going out the door, one knows the world; without looking through the window, one sees the way of heaven": wisdom is not won in outward agitation but in a turn inward, through emptiness and stillness. This cultivation of recollection and inner quietude takes precedence over active engagement in affairs, without however disowning a right action, the kind that does not force.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 47 and 16*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Asceticism 70% · Celebration 30%

"The five flavors dull the palate, racing and hunting madden the heart": sensory excess wears and misleads, and the sage prefers sobriety, "the belly rather than the eye." This is not an asceticism of mortification but a frugality attuned to the [natural](../glossary/ziran.md), which trims excess without condemning simple pleasure.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 12 · Dao De Jing, ch. 9 and 67*

### [Ideal and reality](../axes/political-ideal.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Utopianism 75% · Political realism 25%

Chapter 80 sketches a dreamed community: a small, sparsely peopled country that forgoes machines and travel, returns to knotted cords for counting, where neighboring villages hear each other's singing yet never visit. This nostalgia for a pre-civilizational simplicity is a regulative ideal turned toward the past, more utopian than realist, even though it presents itself as a return rather than a construction.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 80*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Conservatism 70% · Progressivism 30%

Laozi does not believe history climbs toward the better: the proper movement of the Dao is return, and civilization has made the human fall from his original simplicity. But his aim is not the conservation of institutions, rites and hierarchies, which he criticizes just as much: it is a primitivism that would go back behind tradition itself, which places him on the side of refusing progress without making him a conservator of established forms.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 18, 19 and 38 · Dao De Jing, ch. 16 and 25*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Intellectualism 65% · Sentimentalism 35%

The sage cultivates calm and an empty heart, and one finds "heaven and earth are not benevolent," an image of serenity without sentimentality. Emotional disturbance arises from attachment and desire, that is, from a wrong relation to the course of things, and dissolves in quietude. The leaning is toward mastery of the affects, but without the Stoic intellectualism of a judging reason: rather an appeasement through return to the natural.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 5 and 16*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Presentism 65% · Memory and transmission 35%

The Taoist sage dwells in the present moment and the flowing course, without projecting himself into designs for the future, which belong precisely to the forced action he rejects. Yet there remains an orientation toward an original state to be recovered, the Dao before distinctions, which lends his thought a strand of memory-return. The future as project is all but absent.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 16 and 64*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Altruism 70% · Ethical egoism 30%

The sage "hoards nothing: the more he gives to others, the more he has." Self-effacement, holding back and not pushing oneself forward, is paradoxically what raises and preserves one: it is by struggling for nothing that one becomes indispensable. Altruism here is less a duty than an effect of non-action, which dissolves calculating egoism without turning it into heroic sacrifice.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 81 · Dao De Jing, ch. 7 and 66*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Metaphysical optimism 75% · Tragic thought 25%

The world is not ruled by a benevolent providence: "heaven and earth are not benevolent, they treat the ten thousand beings as straw dogs," with the indifference of an impersonal process. Yet this impersonal course is not tragic: whoever accords with it finds peace, and the alternation of opposites ensures no extreme lasts. It is a serene, unillusioned tonality rather than a metaphysical optimism.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 5 · Dao De Jing, ch. 2 and 58*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Universalism 60% · Relativism 40%

The distinctions of beautiful and ugly, good and bad, are for Laozi correlative carvings the mind projects: "all know the beautiful as beautiful, and there is the ugly." One might read a relativism here. But these oppositions are relative to a ground that is not, the Dao and the course of nature, which supplies a norm: to follow the natural. The norm is thus real and universal, even as it dissolves particular human codes.

*Sources : Dao De Jing, ch. 2 · Dao De Jing, ch. 25 and 51*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*rejects the alternative*

The opposition between free will and determinism, forged by Greek and then Christian metaphysics of the will, has no equivalent in the Dao De Jing: Laozi does not think a willing-subject asking whether it is free, but invites one to dissolve forced willing into accord with the course of things. To place him on this axis would betray his point.
