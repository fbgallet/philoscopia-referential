# Friedrich Nietzsche

*1844 – 1900 · German*

A German philosopher and philologist, a professor at Basel at twenty-four before illness forced him to withdraw. First marked by Schopenhauer and the composer Wagner, from whom he broke away, he waged a radical critique of metaphysics, morality and Christianity, in a style of aphorisms and genealogy. Having sunk into madness in 1889 and long disfigured by posthumous misappropriations, he stands as one of the major sources of twentieth-century thought, from psychoanalysis to existentialism.

## Core positions

1. [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md) : **There is no universal morality: every morality is the creation of a type of life, which a genealogy traces back to its origins.**
2. [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : **All that lives wills not to preserve itself but to grow: the [will to power](../glossary/will-to-power.md) is to be affirmed, not extinguished.**
3. [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md) : **Greatness is [amor fati](../glossary/amor-fati.md): to will that everything recur eternally, to desire nothing other than what is.**
4. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **God is dead, and we have killed him: this event undermines all our bearings and demands that we create new values.**
5. [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : **All knowledge is a perspective: there is no absolute standpoint, only interpretations in the service of life.**

## Positions

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Being-toward-death 60% · Death is nothing 35% · Revolt / refusal 5%

Nietzsche dismisses the Christian beyond as a slander on life, and likewise refuses to make death an absolute evil: he preaches a **free death**, chosen at the right hour, that crowns existence instead of merely undergoing it. To die one's own death, at the summit, is to make one's end an act and a fulfillment, in the amor fati that says yes even to the eternal return.

*Sources : Thus Spoke Zarathustra, On Free Death · Twilight of the Idols*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Eudaimonism 70% · Hedonism 20% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 10%

Nietzsche mocks the pleasure-happiness of the utilitarians as much as the morality of duty: the good is neither enjoyment nor disinterested virtue. It is the **enhancement of life**, the flourishing of power and self-overcoming, the great health of one who dares to create himself and to say yes to existence. A transvalued eudaimonism, where success is measured by strength and greatness, not by comfort.

> “Man does not strive for happiness; only the Englishman does.” (Twilight of the Idols, Maxims and Arrows, 12)

*Sources : Twilight of the Idols, Maxims and Arrows · The Gay Science, IV-V · Thus Spoke Zarathustra*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 70% · Rationalism 30%

Nietzsche overturns the Platonic hierarchy: it is not reason that would reach the true against deceiving senses, it is the senses that do not lie, and reason that falsifies their testimony of becoming by substituting **being**, unity, the thing. The intelligible back-world, from Plato to Kant, is a fiction born of decadence. But Nietzsche does not lapse into naive empiricism: there is neither brute fact nor immaculate perception, all knowledge is interpretation from a perspective, governed by the needs of life and the will to power. An empiricism of the body and the senses, then, but radicalized into **perspectivism**.

> “There is only a perspectival seeing, only a perspectival knowing.” (On the Genealogy of Morals, III, § 12)

*Sources : Twilight of the Idols, Reason in Philosophy · Beyond Good and Evil, §§ 16-17*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Fecundity and redemption 95% · Abolitionism 5%

For Nietzsche suffering is a condition of growth: what does not kill me makes me stronger, and the discipline of great suffering has produced every elevation of humanity. He explicitly despises moralities that make the abolition of suffering the supreme goal.

> “What does not kill me makes me stronger.” (Twilight of the Idols, "Maxims and Arrows", §8)

*Sources : Beyond Good and Evil, §225 · Twilight of the Idols, "Maxims and Arrows", §8 · The Gay Science, §338*

### [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Amor fati 95% · Revolt 5%

[Eternal recurrence](../glossary/eternal-recurrence.md) works as a test: if a demon told you that you will relive this very life, identically, infinitely, would you curse him, or would it be your greatest joy? To say yes to every instant is [amor fati](../glossary/amor-fati.md): not Schopenhauer's resignation, but the highest affirmation, to love the necessary so much that one wills its eternal return.

> “My formula for greatness in a human being is amor fati: that one wants nothing to be different, not forward, not backward, not in all eternity.” (Ecce Homo, "Why I Am So Clever", §10)

*Sources : Ecce Homo, "Why I Am So Clever", §10 · The Gay Science, §276 and §341*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Atheism 95% · Theism 5%

Nietzsche does not prove God's non-existence: he observes that faith in the moral God has become untenable within our own culture, undermined by the very will to truth it had fostered, and that “we have killed him”. He draws the consequence we dare not look at: deprived of their ground, our highest values totter, this is nihilism, at once the danger and the chance of a creation of new values.

> “God is dead! God remains dead! And we have killed him!” (The Gay Science, §125)

*Sources : The Gay Science, §125 · The Antichrist*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Immanence 95% · Transcendence 5%

Zarathustra exhorts us to remain faithful to the earth and not to believe those who speak of supra-earthly hopes. The whole critique of "hinterworlds" makes Nietzsche the thinker of radical [immanence](../glossary/immanence.md): life must be justified by nothing outside itself, and every [transcendence](../glossary/transcendence.md) is unmasked as a flight from the real.

*Sources : Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Prologue, §3 · Thus Spoke Zarathustra, "On the Afterworldly"*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Relativism 80% · Constructivism 20%

Rather than asking whether moral values are true, Nietzsche asks what they are worth and where they come from: this is the *[genealogical](../works/on-the-genealogy-of-morality-nietzsche.md)* method. It uncovers that the [good / evil](../glossary/master-slave-morality.md) pair was born of the [ressentiment](../glossary/ressentiment.md) of the weak, inverting the aristocratic « good / bad ». Moralities are thus not facts but symptoms of types of life, to be assessed and, where needed, **transvalued**.

> “There are no moral phenomena, only a moral interpretation of phenomena.” (Beyond Good and Evil, §108)

*Sources : On the Genealogy of Morality · Beyond Good and Evil, §108*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Affirmation 85% · Reasoned selection 10% · Extinction and detachment 5%

Against the idea that the living first seek to preserve themselves, Nietzsche holds that every force wills to grow: this is the [will to power](../glossary/will-to-power.md). The drives are therefore not to be extirpated, the “castration” preached by the ascetic ideal, but to be shaped: to **give style** to one's passions, to sublimate them in the service of a more intense life, rather than extinguish them or surrender to them.

*Sources : Twilight of the Idols, "Morality as Anti-Nature" · The Gay Science, §290*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Celebration 90% · Asceticism 10%

The third essay of the *[Genealogy](../works/on-the-genealogy-of-morality-nietzsche.md)* dismantles the ascetic ideal as a symptom of declining life, and Zarathustra rehabilitates the body, the "great reason", against the despisers of the body. Nietzschean celebration, however, aims at health and strength more than enjoyment.

*Sources : On the Genealogy of Morality, III · Thus Spoke Zarathustra, "On the Despisers of the Body"*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Tragic thought 85% · Metaphysical optimism 15%

From *[The Birth of Tragedy](../works/the-birth-of-tragedy-nietzsche.md)* onward, Nietzsche conceives the ground of reality as painful chaos, without providence or pre-established harmony: this is tragic thought. But against Schopenhauer's resigned pessimism, he turns it into the occasion for a Dionysian affirmation, hence a tragic but not extreme position.

*Sources : The Birth of Tragedy · The Gay Science, §370*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Sentimentalism 80% · Intellectualism 20%

His psychology of drives makes affects the real source of thoughts and values: conscious reason is only a late surface of the play of instincts. The position is inferred from this repeated principle rather than from a frontal thesis on the status of emotions.

*Sources : Beyond Good and Evil, §3 and §187 · The Gay Science, §333*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*rejects the alternative*

Nietzsche dissolves the alternative: [free will](../glossary/free-will.md) is a fiction invented to make man punishable, but the "unfree will" is in his eyes another mythology, that of reified cause and effect (Beyond Good and Evil, §21). He shifts the question toward strong and weak wills.

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Existentialism 93% · Essentialism 7%

"Become what you are": there is no fixed human essence to realize, but a task of **self-creation**. The subject, the soul, the substantial "I" are grammatical illusions; what we are is the ever-open work of self-fashioning, of “giving style to one's character”.

> “Become what you are.” (The Gay Science, §270; Ecce Homo, subtitle)

*Sources : The Gay Science, §290 · Ecce Homo, subtitle "How One Becomes What One Is"*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Skepticism 85% · Dogmatism 15%

There is no view from nowhere: all knowledge is the optics of a [will](../glossary/will-to-power.md), an interpretation in the service of some vital interest. The ideal of an « objective », disinterested truth is itself a value, and a rather life-hostile one. Yet this perspectivism is no flat relativism: perspectives can be ranked by their fruitfulness, and it is by multiplying viewpoints that one gains in « objectivity ».

> “There are no facts, only interpretations.” (Posthumous Fragments, autumn 1885-1886, 7[60])

*Sources : Posthumous Fragments, autumn 1885-1886, 7[60] · Beyond Good and Evil, §34*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Pragmatism 80% · Coherence 15% · Correspondence 5%

Already in *[On Truth and Lie in a Nonmoral Sense](../works/on-truth-and-lie-in-a-nonmoral-sense-nietzsche.md)*, truth is defined as a mobile army of forgotten metaphors, a **useful error** without which a species could not live. The value of a judgment is measured by its fruitfulness for life, not by its correspondence to reality: a pragmatist-leaning position.

*Sources : On Truth and Lie in a Nonmoral Sense, §1 · Beyond Good and Evil, §4*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Constructed meaning 75% · Absurdism 25%

The death of God strips existence of any pre-given end and opens the danger of nihilism, which a share of the [absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) reflects. But Nietzsche's answer is the **transvaluation of values** and the creation of meaning through the [will to power](../glossary/will-to-power.md): meaning constructed, not discovered. No cosmic teleology is admitted.

*Sources : The Gay Science, §125 and §301 · Thus Spoke Zarathustra, "On the Thousand and One Goals"*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Ethical egoism 85% · Altruism 15%

Nietzsche treats pity and altruism as values of decadence, hostile to life, and opposes to the morality of devotion the **self-affirmation** of the strong type. The objection against the ideal of self-sacrifice runs through his whole work, even if he advocates not vulgar egoistic calculation but the flourishing of one's own power.

*Sources : The Antichrist, §2 and §7 · On the Genealogy of Morality, Preface, §5*

### [Beauty in life](../axes/aesthetic-life.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Aestheticism 93% · Primacy of the ethical 8%

It is only as an aesthetic phenomenon that existence and the world are eternally justified: the thesis of *[The Birth of Tragedy](../works/the-birth-of-tragedy-nietzsche.md)* subordinates the meaning of life to the aesthetic rather than the moral register. Art, not morality, is the truly metaphysical activity of man.

> “It is only as an aesthetic phenomenon that existence and the world are eternally justified.” (The Birth of Tragedy, §5)

*Sources : The Birth of Tragedy, §5 and §24 · The Birth of Tragedy, "Attempt at a Self-Criticism", §5*

### [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Contingency 88% · Directional meaning 13%

Genealogy is an anti-teleological method: it tracks the low and contingent beginnings of values and unmasks the belief in **moral progress** as an optimistic illusion. History bears no inscribed meaning or direction; it is a play of forces and chances reinterpreted after the fact.

*Sources : On the Genealogy of Morality, II, §12 · Twilight of the Idols, "Skirmishes of an Untimely Man", §44*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Materialism 85% · Idealism 15%

The critique of "hinterworlds" and the call to remain faithful to the earth bring everything back to sensuous [immanence](../glossary/immanence.md): the body, forces, this world. Without subscribing to a mechanistic materialism he also mocks, Nietzsche refuses any true world behind the phenomenon, which places him on the side of a this-worldly ontology rather than idealism.

*Sources : Twilight of the Idols, "How the True World Finally Became a Fable" · Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Prologue, §3*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Bundle theory 75% · Narrativism 15% · Psychological continuity 10%

For Nietzsche the unified "self" is a fiction: the subject is only a **multiplicity of drives** in struggle, a society of instincts whose apparent unity results from a provisional command. This dissolution of the substantial ego brings his psychology close to a bundle theory, inferred from his analyses rather than professed under that name.

*Sources : Beyond Good and Evil, §12 and §19 · Posthumous Fragments, 1885, 40[42]*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue ethics 95% · Consequentialism 5%

Beyond his critique of "morality", Nietzsche's positive ethics prizes traits of character, strength, nobility, the [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md) of the strong, rather than rules or consequences. His [master morality](../glossary/master-slave-morality.md) reads as a **virtue ethics** of power, inferred from his praise of accomplished types.

*Sources : Beyond Good and Evil, §260 · On the Genealogy of Morality, I, §10*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Presentism 70% · Project 30%

[Eternal recurrence](../glossary/eternal-recurrence.md) and [amor fati](../glossary/amor-fati.md) command us to love the moment enough to will its eternal repetition: the weight falls on the fullness of the affirmed present. A share of project remains, since the [overhuman](../glossary/ubermensch.md) is a task to accomplish, but Nietzsche devalues the excess of historical memory that paralyzes action.

*Sources : The Gay Science, §341 · On the Use and Abuse of History for Life, §1*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological pessimism 75% · Anthropological optimism 25%

Man is a rope stretched between beast and [overhuman](../glossary/ubermensch.md), something that shall be overcome: Nietzsche casts a severe gaze on the gregarious, mediocre humanity of his age, marked by [ressentiment](../glossary/ressentiment.md). But this critical diagnosis comes with confidence in the possibility of an elevation, which tempers the pessimism.

> “Man is something that shall be overcome.” (Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Prologue, §3)

*Sources : Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Prologue, §3 and §4 · On the Genealogy of Morality, I, §11*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The lived body 95% · The body as instrument 5%

“There is more reason in your body than in your best wisdom.” For Nietzsche, the conscious self is but an instrument of the bodily self, which thinks, evaluates and wills through us. To despise the body is the resentment of the despisers of the body.

*Sources : Thus Spoke Zarathustra, "On the Despisers of the Body"*

### [Universals](../axes/universals.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Nominalism 90% · Realism about universals 10%

Concepts arise by equating what is not equal: forgetting singular differences, we forge forms and essences where there are only unique, shifting cases. The universal is a useful fiction, not a reality.

*Sources : On Truth and Lie in an Extra-Moral Sense*

## Influenced by

- **[Michel de Montaigne](../philosophers/montaigne.md)** (reinterprets) : [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) hails [Montaigne](../philosophers/montaigne.md) as a free spirit and a model: writing the self, affirming the body and the earth, gaining in the joy of living by his reading alone. He takes up the essay's candor and recasts it within his own genealogical, life-affirming stance.
- **[Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)** (reinterprets) : The influence runs not through [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s own theses but through his character [Callicles](../characters/callicles.md): in the *[Gorgias](../works/gorgias-plato.md)*, Plato puts into his mouth the right of the stronger and the denunciation of the morality of the weak, precisely in order to refute them. [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) reactivates this immoralist position, which scholars liken to his morality of masters, and turns it against the morality of Socratic and Platonic inspiration.
- **[Platonism](../movements/platonism.md)** (reacts against) : [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) defines his task as an “inversion of Platonism”: against [Platonism](../movements/platonism.md) and its “true world” that devalues the sensible, he means to restore innocence to the earth and the body.
- **[Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md)** (reinterprets) : [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) inherits more from Romanticism than he admits: the Dionysian of The Birth of Tragedy, art held to be « the metaphysical activity par excellence », the revolt against Socratic reason and the Enlightenment all come from this source, transmitted through [Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md) and Wagner. But he soon turns these weapons against Romanticism itself, which he denounces as nostalgia, weakness and flight: he gathers its energy in order to reject its melancholy.
- **[Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md)** (reacts against) : As a young man, [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) hails [Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md) as his educator and inherits his tragic reading of existence and the centrality of the will. He then turns the inheritance around: against pessimism and the denial of the will to live, he sets the affirmation of life and the will to power.

## Influence on

- **[Camus](../philosophers/camus.md)** (reinterprets) : [Camus](../philosophers/camus.md) reads [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) as a critical heir: he takes up the diagnosis of nihilism and the love of fate, but turns them into an ethics of measure, the absurd calling for revolt rather than the approval of everything.
- **[Foucault](../philosophers/foucault.md)** (continues) : [Foucault](../philosophers/foucault.md) takes from [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) the genealogical method: the critique of origins and of the universal pretensions of truth and the subject, which he unfolds into an analysis of the relations between knowledge and power.
- **[Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md)** (reinterprets) : [Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md) devotes extensive lecture courses to [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md), whom he reads as the last great metaphysician: the will to power and nihilism become the completion and the destiny of Western metaphysics.
- **[Transhumanism](../movements/transhumanism.md)** (reinterprets) : Transhumanism sometimes claims [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md)'s overman, reinterpreted as a biotechnological posthuman. The lineage is largely an over-reading: the Nietzschean overcoming is spiritual and tragic, not technical, and Nietzsche would likely be deeply critical of this craving for absolute control, in which he would see a refusal of becoming and finitude, a hatred of life contrary to amor fati.
- **[Max Weber](../philosophers/weber.md)** (reinterprets) : [Weber](../philosophers/weber.md), for whom a thinker's honesty is measured by his stance toward [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) and Marx, transposes the genealogical suspicion into sociology: his analysis of values, of ressentiment and of the "disenchantment of the world" owes much to the Nietzschean diagnosis.
