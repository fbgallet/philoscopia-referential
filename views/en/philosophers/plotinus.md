# Plotinus

*204 – 270 · Greek · [Platonism](../movements/platonism.md)*

A Greek-speaking philosopher of late antiquity (c. 204-270), born in Roman Egypt and trained in Alexandria under Ammonius Saccas, Plotinus then taught in Rome, where he gathered a circle of disciples. He is the chief founder of what would be called [Neoplatonism](../movements/platonism.md): in his own eyes not a new system but the faithful unfolding of [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md), whom he systematizes and bends toward mysticism. He wrote only late and without concern for order; his treatises were collected and edited after his death by his pupil Porphyry, who arranged them into six groups of nine, the *[Enneads](../works/enneads-plotinus.md)*. His thought of the three principles, the One, the Intellect and the Soul, would feed Christian theology, above all [Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md), as well as the Jewish and Islamic traditions, down to the Florentine Renaissance.

## Core positions

1. [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md) : **The principle of all is a [One](../glossary/the-one.md) beyond being and beyond thought: nothing can be said of it, one can only ascend back to it.**
2. [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md) : **All things flow from the [One](../glossary/the-one.md) and all things aspire to return to it: the meaning of a life is this ascent back to the source from which it was never wholly cut off.**
3. [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md) : **Evil is not a power: it has no being of its own, it is only [privation](../glossary/privation.md), a lack of good at the lowest degree of emanation.**
4. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **Virtue is only a beginning: the sovereign good is contemplative union with the [One](../glossary/the-one.md), "the flight of the alone to the Alone".**
5. [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : **Your true self has not descended into the body: its highest part abides always in the intelligible, and to know yourself is to awaken to it.**

## Positions

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Transcendence 90% · Immanence 10%

The first principle can be neither a being nor a thought, for every being is already manifold, distinct from what it thinks: to ground multiplicity without belonging to it, there must be an absolutely simple [One](../glossary/the-one.md), "beyond being" and beyond [intellect](../glossary/nous.md), of which nothing can properly be said. Plotinus thus radicalizes [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s Good above essence into a pure transcendence. Yet [emanation](../glossary/emanation.md) tempers the distance: the One overflows without dividing or diminishing itself, and stays present to all that proceeds from it, more inward to the soul than the soul is to itself. Hence a strong transcendence, but not without a graded immanence, which holds the score back from the extreme.

> “It is none of the things of which it is the source; such is its nature that, though nothing can be affirmed of it, not name, not reason, we can yet say something concerning it.”, Enneads, V, 3, 13-14

*Sources : Enneads, VI, 9 (On the Good, or the One) · Enneads, V, 3 and V, 5*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 85% · Atheism 15%

Plotinus's whole metaphysics culminates in a single, absolute principle, the [One](../glossary/the-one.md), source of all that is and term of the soul's return: a first divine principle exists, and atheism is beside the point. But this principle is not the personal, creating God of monotheism: it is impersonal, "beyond being", does not know, does not will, does not create by decision but overflows by the necessity of its superabundance. This metaphysical theism, without personal providence or creation ex nihilo, holds the score back from the extreme while remaining firmly on the side of affirming a divine absolute.

*Sources : Enneads, VI, 9 · Enneads, V, 1 (On the Three Primary Hypostases)*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 70% · Eudaimonism 30%

Happiness lies neither in pleasure nor even in the virtuous life alone: virtue is only a first degree, a purification that detaches the soul from the sensible and makes it like the divine. The true sovereign good is beyond, in the contemplation of the Intellect and then in union with the [One](../glossary/the-one.md), where the soul rejoins its source. Plotinus takes up Greek eudaimonism, the happy life as the activity of our best part, but shifts its summit: blessedness is no longer the excellence of action but a contemplative ascent that passes beyond moral virtue itself. The perfect sage, already united to the intelligible, stays happy even in misfortune, for his true self is not reached by it.

> “This is the life of gods and of godlike and blessed men: a deliverance from all things here below, the flight of the alone to the Alone.”, Enneads, VI, 9, 11

*Sources : Enneads, I, 4 (On Well-Being) · Enneads, I, 2 (On Virtues)*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Idealism 65% · Dualism 35%

The real unfolds as a hierarchy of degrees of being, from the [One](../glossary/the-one.md) down to matter: the more a thing is one and intelligible, the more real it is; the more dispersed in the sensible, the less. The sensible world is therefore not illusory but the lowest degree, a faint image produced by the [Soul](../glossary/world-soul.md) that orders and animates the cosmos. Matter, at the bottom of the scale, is all but nothing, sheer lack. This priority of mind leans strongly toward idealism, but it is a Platonic gradation, not the idealism of a [Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md) for whom to be is to be perceived: a duality of intelligible and sensible persists, hence a residual weight on dualism.

*Sources : Enneads, V, 1 and V, 2 · Enneads, IV, 8 (On the Descent of the Soul into Bodies)*

### [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theodicy 75% · Revolt / anti-theodicy 25%

If all things proceed from a perfectly good principle, whence comes evil? Plotinus refuses to make it a positive principle, a power rivaling the Good: evil has no being of its own, it is [privation](../glossary/privation.md), a lack of good, a defect of being at the lowest degree of the procession. He identifies it with matter as the ultimate limit where the light of the One fades out, not as a force but as an absence. Moral evil is then merely a soul turning away from the higher toward the lower. This dissolution of evil into non-being, which saves the goodness of the whole without denying the reality of our ordeal, is a form of theodicy; it will bequeath to [Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md) the idea of evil as [privatio boni](../glossary/privation.md).

*Sources : Enneads, I, 8 (On What Evils Are and Whence They Come) · Enneads, III, 2-3 (On Providence)*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 100%

The very movement of being is circular: each thing proceeds from the [One](../glossary/the-one.md) by [emanation](../glossary/emanation.md) and then tends to return to it, and the return ([epistrophē](../glossary/epistrophe.md)) is inscribed in the nature of whatever issues from the Good. The meaning of human life is therefore neither to be invented nor absent: it is given by this structure, the soul having consciously to retrace the path to its source. To live fully is to convert one's gaze, to turn away from the outside and climb back toward the origin from which one is never wholly cut off.

*Sources : Enneads, VI, 9 · Enneads, V, 1*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Finalism 100%

[Emanation](../glossary/emanation.md) is not a mechanical production but a derivation oriented toward the good: each [hypostasis](../glossary/hypostasis.md) begets the lower one because the perfect, by superabundance, cannot but radiate, and all that proceeds from it is drawn by the return toward its principle. Causality is therefore fundamentally final, suspended from the Good as its end, the opposite of a universe of blind collisions. Nature itself produces by contemplating, like a sleep of the soul that imitates the intelligible.

*Sources : Enneads, III, 8 (On Nature, Contemplation and the One) · Enneads, V, 4*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Substantialism 100%

The self is not the bodily composite but the soul, an immaterial and immortal substance, and more deeply still the intellect within us. Plotinus presses inwardness to a singular thesis: the highest part of the soul never wholly descends into the body but abides permanently in the intelligible. Our true self is that unaltered summit, of which we have usually lost awareness, absorbed as we are in sensible life. To know oneself is to awaken to this higher identity, more real than the empirical person.

*Sources : Enneads, IV, 8, 8 (the undescended soul) · Enneads, I, 1 (What Is the Living Being, and What Is Man)*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Immortalism 100%

The soul being a simple substance, immaterial and akin to the intelligible, it does not decompose and survives the body's dissolution: Plotinus takes up and develops the [immortality](../axes/death.md) of [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s *[Phaedo](../works/phaedo-plato.md)*. Death is only a stripping away, the liberation of the soul from its bodily envelope, and so nothing to be feared by one who has already learned to live turned upward. The sage rehearses it in this life, by detaching his true self from the body.

*Sources : Enneads, IV, 7 (On the Immortality of the Soul) · Enneads, I, 9 (On Leaving the Body)*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Essentialism 95% · Existentialism 5%

[Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s [Forms](../glossary/theory-of-forms.md) receive in Plotinus a proper home: they are the thoughts of the [Intellect](../glossary/nous.md), which contemplates them eternally in a unity where thinking and being coincide. The essence of each thing is thus its intelligible Form, prior and superior to its sensible existence, which is only a reflection of it. Platonic essentialism is therefore preserved and even reinforced, the intelligible becoming the living content of a divine mind.

*Sources : Enneads, V, 5 (That the Intelligibles Are Not Outside the Intellect) · Enneads, V, 9*

### [Universals](../axes/universals.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Realism about universals 95% · Nominalism 5%

Like every Platonist, Plotinus holds universals to be realities in themselves, more real than individuals: the intelligible Forms exist independently of sensible things, of which they are the models. But he lodges them in the divine [Intellect](../glossary/nous.md) rather than in a separate heaven, which makes them universals ante rem thought by an eternal mind.

*Sources : Enneads, V, 9 (On Intellect, the Forms and Being)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The examined life 95% · Primacy of action 5%

The path to the principle runs not through the outside but through a turning of the soul upon itself: "withdraw into yourself and look". Since the [One](../glossary/the-one.md) is present at our innermost, closer to the soul than the soul is to itself, it is by gathering oneself, by sculpting one's own inner statue, that one rises to it. This absolute primacy of contemplative inwardness over outward action makes the examined life the whole of philosophy, and anticipates Augustinian interiority.

> “Withdraw into yourself and look: and if you do not find yourself beautiful yet, act as does the sculptor of a statue that is to be made beautiful.”, Enneads, I, 6, 9

*Sources : Enneads, I, 6 (On Beauty) · Enneads, VI, 9, 7*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The body as instrument 95% · The lived body 5%

The true self being the soul, the body is only its instrument and provisional envelope, the low place where the soul lingers and risks forgetting itself. Porphyry reports that Plotinus "seemed ashamed to be in a body" and refused to have his portrait made. The body is not the subject we are but that from which we must free ourselves in order to ascend, which places Plotinus at the extreme of the body-as-instrument.

*Sources : Porphyry, Life of Plotinus, 1 · Enneads, IV, 8; I, 1*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Asceticism 93% · Celebration 8%

Bodily pleasures bind the soul to the lowest degree of being and turn it away from its ascent: the first virtue, the "purificatory" one, consists precisely in detaching from them so as to return the soul to itself. Without condemning the body as a positive evil, Plotinus urges a resolute asceticism, the sage holding bodily needs to be mere necessities to be met at a minimum, never ends.

*Sources : Enneads, I, 2 (On Virtues) · Enneads, I, 4*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Extinction and detachment 80% · Reasoned selection 20%

Desire turned toward sensible things disperses the soul and chains it to the manifold; wisdom demands withdrawing it so as to redirect it wholly upward. Plotinus does not abolish the desiring drive but converts it into an [erōs](../glossary/eros.md) for the intelligible and the One: the soul finds rest only by ceasing to desire what is beneath it. The lived tendency is therefore detachment from the sensible, more austere still than in [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md).

*Sources : Enneads, III, 5 (On Love) · Enneads, VI, 7, 22-35*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Intellectualism 85% · Sentimentalism 15%

The passions belong to the compound of soul and body, not to the higher self, which stays unaffected by them: the soul truly itself is not moved, and only its lower part, turned toward the body, suffers. Plotinus therefore holds emotions to be disturbances to be mastered by conversion toward the intelligible, in the intellectualist line, even if his interest bears less on their psychological analysis than on the means of escaping them.

*Sources : Enneads, I, 1, 7-9 · Enneads, III, 6 (On the Impassibility of the Incorporeal)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Rationalism 90% · Empiricism 10%

True knowledge comes not from the senses, which reach only images of the sensible, but from the intellect turned toward the intelligible, which it contemplates from within: the soul recovers the Forms within itself because it proceeds from the [Intellect](../glossary/nous.md). At the summit, the highest knowledge is no longer even discursive but an immediate grasp, indeed a union that surpasses thought. This inward, rational source of knowledge, against all empiricism, places Plotinus firmly on the side of rationalism.

*Sources : Enneads, V, 3 (On the Knowing Hypostases) · Enneads, V, 5*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 80% · Skepticism 20%

Plotinus builds an affirmative, architectured metaphysics, confident of reaching the true through the intellect: there is a science of the intelligible, and the Intellect possesses an infallible knowledge of its objects, which are its own thoughts. This confidence sets him on the side of dogmatism. It is tempered, however, regarding the [One](../glossary/the-one.md), by an almost skeptical reserve: of the supreme principle nothing can properly be said, and discourse approaches it only through negations.

*Sources : Enneads, V, 5 · Enneads, VI, 9, 3-4*

### [Nature of love](../axes/love-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Eros as lack 85% · Agape as gift 15%

Plotinus extends the [erōs](../glossary/eros.md) of [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s *[Symposium](../works/symposium-plato.md)*: love is that drive born of lack which carries the soul from sensible beauty toward intelligible Beauty, and thence toward the [One](../glossary/the-one.md), source of all beauty. It is an ascending love, a desire to be united with that from which one proceeds. A note of gift nonetheless surfaces, for the One itself lacks nothing and overflows by pure superabundance, which reverses the lack at the summit.

*Sources : Enneads, III, 5 (On Love) · Enneads, VI, 7, 31-34*

### [Attention and diversion](../axes/attention.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Recollection 93% · Diversion embraced 8%

The whole Plotinian discipline is a recollection: one must gather the soul dispersed in the sensible manifold, concentrate it and make it attentive to its inner source so as to rise to contemplation. Union with the [One](../glossary/the-one.md) requires the silence of the senses and of discursive thought, a total withdrawal within. It is the very opposite of any fruitful dispersion.

*Sources : Enneads, VI, 9, 7-11 · Enneads, V, 1, 12*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Universalism 100%

Norms are neither conventional nor constructed but grounded in the objective order of being: the good is the [One](../glossary/the-one.md) itself, principle and end of all, and a thing's worth lies in its degree of nearness to it. The hierarchy of virtues mirrors this real scale of being, hence a realist universalism rooted in metaphysics.

*Sources : Enneads, I, 2 (On Virtues) · Enneads, VI, 9*

### [Beauty in life](../axes/aesthetic-life.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Primacy of the ethical 70% · Aestheticism 30%

Beauty has worth only as a degree and a springboard upward: sensible beauty reflects intelligible beauty, which in turn reflects the Good, and love of the beautiful is the first step of the ascent toward the [One](../glossary/the-one.md). The aesthetic life is therefore not an end in itself but stays subordinate to the spiritual end, the beautiful counting only as that which reveals and leads to the principle.

*Sources : Enneads, I, 6 (On Beauty) · Enneads, V, 8 (On the Intelligible Beauty)*

## Influenced by

- **[Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)** (reinterprets) : [Plotinus](../philosophers/plotinus.md) absorbs major pieces of [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) into his Neoplatonism, foremost the Intellect as thought thinking itself, while rejecting the categories as applied to the intelligible.
- **[Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)** (reinterprets) : [Plotinus](../philosophers/plotinus.md) rereads [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) through his texts, six centuries later, and draws from them [Neoplatonism](../movements/platonism.md): a hierarchical metaphysics of the One, the Intellect and the Soul that goes beyond the letter of the dialogues.

## Influence on

- **[Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md)** (reinterprets) : [Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md) receives from the Neoplatonists, [Plotinus](../philosophers/plotinus.md) foremost, a metaphysics of inwardness and of the soul's ascent toward the immaterial, which he reconfigures within Christian theology.
