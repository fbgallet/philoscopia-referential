# Adi Shankara

*700 – 750 · Indian · [Vedānta (Advaita)](../movements/vedanta.md)*

Master of [Advaita Vedānta](../movements/vedanta.md), the non-dualist branch of Vedānta, traditionally placed in India around 700-750 (his dating remains debated). He systematized a reading of the Upaniṣads in which the self ([ātman](../glossary/atman.md)) and the absolute ([brahman](../glossary/brahman.md)) are one and the same reality, against the dualist and ritualist schools of his day. To him we owe great commentaries (bhāṣya) on [Bādarāyaṇa](../philosophers/badarayana.md)'s *[Brahma Sūtras](../works/brahma-sutras-badarayana.md)*, on the principal Upaniṣads and on the Bhagavad Gītā, as well as didactic treatises such as the *[Upadeśasāhasrī](../works/upadesasahasri-shankara.md)*. Heir to [Gauḍapāda](../philosophers/gaudapada.md), he makes the path of knowledge ([jñāna](../glossary/jnana.md)) the heart of his doctrine, and tradition credits him with founding the monasteries (maṭha) that would perpetuate his school. His non-dualism remains to this day the most influential current of Hindu philosophy.

## Core positions

1. [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : **You are neither your body nor your mind: your true self ([ātman](../glossary/atman.md)) is witness-consciousness, and it is identical to the absolute, [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md).**
2. [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md) : **A single reality exists without a second; the world's plurality is but an [appearance](../glossary/maya.md) superimposed on brahman by ignorance, like a snake seen on a rope.**
3. [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md) : **No act and no rite liberates: only [knowledge](../glossary/jnana.md) of the self, by dispelling ignorance, frees, for one does not become brahman, one already is it.**
4. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **The personal God is real for the one who journeys, but the ultimate is a [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md) without attributes, beyond all personhood: two levels of truth, not two gods.**
5. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **The sovereign good, [liberation](../glossary/moksha.md), is not a state to be won but the recognition of what one already is: the very bliss of brahman.**

## Positions

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Idealism 100%

A single reality exists without a second, [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md), pure consciousness: neither matter nor the plurality of things are autonomous substances. The world of objects is not sheer nothingness, but it holds only an empirical reality (vyāvahārika), superimposed on brahman by ignorance, as one sees a snake where there is only a rope. This idealism is sui generis: it does not make the world the content of a finite mind, in Berkeley's manner, but refers it wholly to the one absolute consciousness of which things are merely the appearance. The question is thus not "mind or matter?" but "is the manifold real?", to which Śaṅkara answers by non-duality.

> “Brahman is real, the world is appearance, and the self is nothing other than brahman.” (verse attributed to Śaṅkara (Brahmajñānāvalīmālā))

*Sources : Commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, I.1.1-4 · Upadeśasāhasrī*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Substantialism 100%

The true self, the [ātman](../glossary/atman.md), is neither the body, nor the mind, nor the changing sequence of psychic states: it is pure consciousness, the permanent ever-present witness that every experience presupposes. It cannot be denied without being confirmed, for the one who would deny it would still be it. Śaṅkara thus takes the exact opposite of the bundle thesis: where one would find only a flux of impressions without a bearer, as Hume would hold, or before him the Buddhist [not-self](../glossary/anatta.md) (anātman), he maintains an irreducible subject. Yet this permanent core is not one individual soul among others: it is identical in all and identical to [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md), which radicalizes substantialism while depersonalizing it.

*Sources : Upadeśasāhasrī, II (prose part), on "thou art that" · Commentary on the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 80% · Atheism 20%

Śaṅkara distinguishes two levels of the absolute: a personal God, creator and object of devotion (saguṇa brahman, Īśvara), real at the empirical level, and, at the ultimate level, a [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md) without attributes ([nirguṇa](../glossary/nirguna-brahman.md)), beyond all personhood and all relation. With respect to our axis this is not an atheism: the absolute is indeed the ground of everything. But neither is it the theism of a sovereign creator distinct from the world, for the personal God is himself a conventional figure that ultimate knowledge passes through. The tension between devotion and liberating knowledge is thus dissolved by the hierarchy of the two truths: God for the one who journeys, the impersonal for the one who knows, hence a theist but restrained position.

*Sources : Commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, I.1 · Commentary on the Bhagavad Gītā*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Transcendence 80% · Immanence 20%

[Brahman](../glossary/brahman.md) is at once the most intimate, present in the heart of each as one's own self, and the absolute that radically exceeds the sensible world: the latter holds only an empirical reality, taken as [illusion](../glossary/maya.md) (māyā) in the light of the ultimate. The balance tips toward transcendence, not in the sense of a separate backworld, but because truth is not found in the given: it requires passing through the appearance of the manifold to reach the one real. Immanence is real, but it is the immanence of an absolute that, in itself, depends on nothing worldly.

*Sources : Commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, II.1 · Upadeśasāhasrī*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Rationalism 70% · Empiricism 30%

On brahman, which is neither perceived nor inferred from the world, the only valid source is revelation (śruti), the word of the Upaniṣads: sense experience gives only the manifold, and reasoning alone cannot establish the absolute. But Śaṅkara is no fideist: reason (yukti) is indispensable to interpret Scripture, resolve its apparent contradictions and refute rival schools, and the teaching saves only when it becomes direct experience of the self. The primary material is thus not empirical but scriptural and rational, which places him on the side of reason without making him a pure rationalist, the authority of revealed testimony remaining first.

*Sources : Commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, I.1.3-4 · Upadeśasāhasrī*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Liberating knowledge (vidyā) is not a probable opinion but a definitive certainty which, once ignorance is removed, can no longer be contradicted, as the rope once recognized dispels the snake forever. Doubt belongs to the domain of ignorance, not to the knowledge of the self, which is immediate and self-evident. Śaṅkara argues moreover as a dogmatist against the skeptics and rival schools, taking it as settled that an ultimate truth is accessible and demonstrable.

*Sources : Upadeśasāhasrī · Commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, II.2*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Eudaimonism 60% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 40%

The sovereign good is [liberation](../glossary/moksha.md) (mokṣa), understood as the recognition of the identity of the self and the absolute: not a pleasure, not even a virtue for its own sake, but the achievement of what one already is. This liberation is described as bliss (ānanda), a beatitude that is the very nature of [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md): we are close to a eudaimonism, the sovereign good being the realized fullness of being. A perfectionist strand remains, since the path demands discipline, renunciation and purification of the mind, but these means do not create liberation: they remove what was veiling it.

*Sources : Commentary on the Taittirīya Upaniṣad (on ānanda) · Upadeśasāhasrī*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 85% · Reasoned selection 15%

Desire for objects binds one to [saṃsāra](../glossary/samsara.md), the cycle of rebirths: it arises from the ignorance that takes the self for the body and chases after what it lacks. Among the conditions of knowledge, Śaṅkara places renunciation (vairāgya) of the fruits of action, here and hereafter, and the ardent quest for liberation alone. The slope is clearly that of detachment: not because pleasure would be culpable, but because every desire turned toward the manifold misses the one real and the only stable good. A sorting remains in the background, the desire for liberation (mumukṣutva) being itself cultivated, but as a desire meant to extinguish once its end is reached.

*Sources : Commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, I.1.1 (the fourfold prerequisite) · Commentary on the Bhagavad Gītā*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 85% · Constructed meaning 15%

Existence belongs to an order whose end is fixed in advance: to leave the cycle of rebirths and recover the identity of the self and [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md). Meaning is not an arbitrary creation of the individual but the recognition of a truth already there, veiled by ignorance; every life is oriented, whether it knows it or not, toward this liberation. A measure of personal journeying remains, since it falls to each to walk the path, but the end is not invented: it is discovered.

*Sources : Commentary on the Bhagavad Gītā · Upadeśasāhasrī*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Immortalism 90% · Death is nothing 10%

The self ([ātman](../glossary/atman.md)) is never born and never dies: death touches only the body-mind compound, not the consciousness that witnesses it. In this respect Śaṅkara belongs to immortalism, the present life being a passage toward liberation. But the encoding calls for a nuance: what is immortal is not the individual soul, which unravels with its rebirths, but the impersonal self identical in all. For the one who has realized this identity, death is no longer even an event to fear, which adds a coloring close to "death is nothing": it can subtract nothing from what, in essence, was never in time.

> “It is never born and never dies; unborn, abiding, eternal, it is not slain when the body is slain.” (Bhagavad Gītā, II.20, as commented by Śaṅkara)

*Sources : Commentary on the Bhagavad Gītā, II.11-30 · Commentary on the Kaṭha Upaniṣad*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 75% · Fecundity and redemption 25%

Suffering belongs to [saṃsāra](../glossary/samsara.md) and to the ignorance that identifies the self with the body: it has no redemptive value of its own, it is an evil bound to the empirical condition whose root is [error](../glossary/avidya.md) (avidyā). The aim is not to assume or sanctify it but to undo it at its source, by dispelling ignorance through knowledge: the realized self, an impassive witness, is no longer reached by it. The position thus meets the idea of an abolition of suffering, by way of knowledge rather than mere relief.

*Sources : Commentary on the Bhagavad Gītā · Upadeśasāhasrī*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Intellectualism 80% · Sentimentalism 20%

Liberation is reached through knowledge (jñāna), not through emotion or devotion alone: it is ignorance, an error of the intellect, that enslaves, and a right knowing that frees. The preparatory discipline includes tranquillity (śama) and mastery of the passions, which trouble the discernment between the real and the apparent. Affect is thus neither the access route to truth nor the spring of liberation, which leans toward an intellectualism, without emotions being denied at the empirical level.

*Sources : Commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, I.1.1 · Upadeśasāhasrī*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Asceticism 90% · Celebration 10%

Renunciation (saṃnyāsa) of the world and its enjoyments is held to be the condition most favorable to liberating knowledge: Śaṅkara's ideal sage is the wandering ascetic, detached from bodily pleasures as from every worldly good. Sensible pleasures bind one to the body-mind compound that the path invites one precisely to transcend. It is a pronounced asceticism, which does not hold the body to be evil in itself but subordinates it entirely to the quest for liberation.

*Sources : Upadeśasāhasrī · Commentary on the Bhagavad Gītā*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The examined life 90% · Primacy of action 10%

Against the ritualists (Mīmāṃsā), for whom the performance of prescribed acts is the way, Śaṅkara holds that action does not liberate: it stays within the field of ignorance and yields only transient fruits. Only the turn inward, the inquiry into "who am I?" and the knowledge of the self, leads to liberation. The discipline consists of hearing the teaching (śravaṇa), reflection (manana) and meditation (nididhyāsana): a patient examination turned toward the witness of all experience, the opposite of a primacy of outward action.

*Sources : Commentary on the Brahma Sūtras, I.1 (knowledge vs action) · Upadeśasāhasrī*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 85% · Existentialism 15%

There is in each a determinate, eternal essence, the self ([ātman](../glossary/atman.md)), which does not make itself by its choices but is from all eternity what it is, identical to [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md). Empirical existence, with its history and acts, adds nothing to this nature: it only veils it. This is a strong essentialism, the exact opposite of the idea that existence would precede essence, since the task is not to create oneself but to recognize what one has always been.

*Sources : Upadeśasāhasrī · Commentary on the Bhagavad Gītā, II*

### [Can a machine think](../axes/machine-mind.md)

*not applicable*

An anachronistic question: Śaṅkara's corpus contains nothing that would allow positioning him on machine thought without hazardous extrapolation. At most one may note that witness-consciousness, irreducible to any compound, is in principle not a process one could manufacture.

## Influenced by

- **[Bādarāyaṇa](../philosophers/badarayana.md)** (transmits) : [Bādarāyaṇa](../philosophers/badarayana.md)'s *[Brahma Sūtras](../works/brahma-sutras-badarayana.md)* are the base text that [Śaṅkara](../philosophers/shankara.md) comments on: he transmits them by reading them non-dualistically (his Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya), grounding [Advaita](../movements/vedanta.md) on that authority and anchoring there the identity of the self and [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md).
- **[Gauḍapāda](../philosophers/gaudapada.md)** (continues) : [Śaṅkara](../philosophers/shankara.md) systematises [Advaita Vedānta](../movements/vedanta.md) by leaning explicitly on [Gauḍapāda](../philosophers/gaudapada.md), whose doctrine of the non-duality of the self and the absolute he carries on.
