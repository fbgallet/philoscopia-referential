# René Descartes

*1596 – 1650 · French · [Rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md)*

French mathematician, physicist and philosopher, schooled by the Jesuits at La Flèche and long settled in the Dutch Republic, where he published most of his work away from the university authorities. Regarded as the father of modern philosophy and founder of continental [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md), he breaks with the Aristotelian scholasticism inherited from the Middle Ages and undertakes, in the *[Discourse on Method](../works/meditations-descartes.md)* and then the *[Meditations on First Philosophy](../works/meditations-descartes.md)*, to refound the whole of knowledge on a single method modelled on mathematics. The inventor of analytic geometry, he paves the way for the mechanistic physics that would culminate in Newton. His thought at once drew debate and objection, from [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) to [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md), and still frames modern disputes about mind, body and knowledge.

## Core positions

1. [Nature of consciousness](../axes/consciousness-nature.md) : **Mind and body are two really distinct substances: I can conceive the thinking thing with nothing corporeal, so my essence is to think, not to be extended.**
2. [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : **I can doubt everything except that I doubt: “I think, therefore I am” withstands the most [radical doubt](../glossary/hyperbolic-doubt.md) and becomes the indubitable ground of all knowledge.**
3. [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : **The senses deceive: only reason, by the intuition of simple natures and deduction, reaches the true, starting from [innate ideas](../glossary/innate-ideas.md) that experience did not put in us.**
4. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **A perfect God exists and cannot deceive me: his veracity alone guarantees that my clear and distinct ideas are true, and so lifts doubt.**
5. [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md) : **Science should no longer contemplate nature but transform it: by knowing the forces of bodies, man makes himself “as master and possessor” of the material world.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 70% · Abolitionism 30%

Close in this to the Stoics, Descartes holds that adversity is the occasion to test one's own strength: [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md), the firm resolve to use one's free will well, yields an **inner contentment** that nothing external can remove, and that arises precisely from the effort to do well despite the ordeal. Suffering, mastered by judgment, thus becomes matter for a legitimate self-satisfaction, not a pure evil.

*Sources : The Passions of the Soul, arts. 147-148 and 190 · Letters to Princess Elisabeth*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 55% · Constructed meaning 45%

Descartes believes in a provident God and in the immortality of the soul, which set life within a willed order and promise it an end; but he banishes final causes from knowledge, judging divine wisdom impenetrable. Accessible meaning then shifts toward the human: the right use of [free will](../glossary/free-will.md), the perfection of reason and the contentment of [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md), a work to accomplish more than a purpose to contemplate.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, IV · The Passions of the Soul, arts. 153-156*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Immortalism 90% · Death is nothing 5% · Being-toward-death 5%

Cartesian dualism really separates the soul, a thinking substance, from the body, an extended substance: nothing in the nature of the mind depends on the body, whose death therefore cannot reach it. Descartes presents it as an explicit aim of the Meditations to demonstrate, against the libertines, the immortality of the soul. Death is but the breakdown of a machine, the soul surviving it.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, Dedicatory Letter and VI*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Reasoned selection 40% · Affirmation 30% · Extinction and detachment 30%

Descartes holds the passions to be useful when well governed: the point is neither to suppress nor to follow them, but to make the soul their master through judgment and a firm will. Stoic in inspiration, his provisional morality bounds the will to what depends on us and counsels changing one's desires rather than the order of the world. Yet he joins to this the affirmation of a desire guided by reason: virtue lies in resolutely doing what one judges best, the source of an inner contentment even in failure. Detachment, sorting and affirmation balance out in him.

> “To strive always to master myself rather than fortune, and to change my desires rather than the order of the world.”, Discourse on Method, III

*Sources : Discourse on Method, III · The Passions of the Soul, arts. 144-148 and 211-212*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dogmatism 90% · Skepticism 10%

Against [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md), Descartes turns doubt against itself: he drives it to the point of [hyperbole](../glossary/hyperbolic-doubt.md), supposing an **evil genius** who deceives him about everything, to see whether anything withstands it. Yet the very act of doubting presupposes a subject who doubts: I can call everything into doubt except that I am thinking, and therefore that I am. The cogito is no dogma laid down in advance but a certainty won through doubt itself, an indubitable foothold from which the whole of knowledge can be rebuilt. The conclusion is thus firmly [dogmatic](../glossary/dogmatism.md): full certainty is attainable, and skepticism refutes itself when pushed to its limit.

> “I think, therefore I am.”, Discourse on Method, IV

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, I and II · Discourse on Method, IV*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Rationalism 95% · Empiricism 5%

Since the senses deceive us and yield only confused qualities, they cannot ground a certain science: only the understanding, which conceives geometrical extension clearly beneath the sensory appearances (the analysis of the **piece of wax**), reaches the nature of things. Descartes infers that the founding concepts, the idea of God, of extension, the mathematical truths, cannot come from experience: they are [innate ideas](../glossary/innate-ideas.md) placed in the understanding. All science then reduces to the intuition of simple natures and the deduction that links them, on the model of mathematics, which sharply separates him from the coming empiricism, for which nothing is in the mind that was not first in the senses.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, III and V · Rules for the Direction of the Mind, III*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Free will 90% · Hard determinism 10%

Descartes makes the will the infinite faculty of the soul, that in which we most resemble God: it is the will, not the limited understanding, that can affirm or deny anything. This freedom of [free will](../glossary/free-will.md) is the most immediate experience we have of ourselves.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, IV · The Passions of the Soul, arts. 41 and 152*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dualism 90% · Materialism 5% · Idealism 5%

Reality divides into two irreducible substances: the thinking thing (res cogitans), whose essence is thought, and the extended thing (res extensa), whose essence is geometrical extension. This is Cartesian **dualism**, which grounds the possibility of an entirely material physics and an immaterial soul.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, VI · Principles of Philosophy, I, arts. 51-54*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Substantialism 90% · Psychological continuity 10%

The self is a substance, a thinking thing, whose existence is grasped immediately in the cogito and whose identity depends on neither body nor memory. This is a **substantialism** of the subject, which also secures the soul's immortality.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, II · Discourse on Method, IV*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Mechanism 85% · Finalism 15%

Material nature is fully explained by the laws of motion and the shape of parts: the bodies of living beings, and animals themselves, are **machines**, devoid of soul. Descartes banishes the search for final causes from physics, reserving them to God, hence a residual share of theological **finalism**.

*Sources : Discourse on Method, V · Principles of Philosophy, I, art. 28 and II*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 95% · Atheism 5%

The cogito at first secures only my own existence: so long as an **evil genius** could deceive me even in my self-evident truths, doubt is not defeated. Proving God therefore becomes the keystone of the system. Descartes offers two proofs: the idea of infinity within me, which cannot arise from a finite being, demands an infinite cause (Meditation III); and existence belongs to the essence of the perfect being as the equality of its angles belongs to the triangle, the **ontological** proof (Meditation V). Now a perfect God cannot be a deceiver, deception betraying a defect: his veracity therefore guarantees that what I conceive clearly and distinctly is true. Far from inherited theism, the position is the keystone that lifts doubt and grounds the whole of science.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, III and V · Discourse on Method, IV*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Correspondence 85% · Coherence 15%

The criterion of truth is evidence: what I conceive through clear and distinct ideas is true, and this adequacy of the idea to its object is guaranteed by divine veracity. The Cartesian conception thus belongs to the correspondence theory, with an internal demand for coherence in the chaining of reasons.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, III and IV · Principles of Philosophy, I, art. 45*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Mastery 85% · Stewardship 10% · Member among others 5%

Once material nature is nothing but extension governed by the laws of motion, knowing the forces of bodies as one knows a mechanism allows them to be applied to every use, medicine first of all. Descartes replaces the **speculative philosophy** of the Schools, which contemplates essences, with a **practical philosophy** that transforms: the end of knowledge is no longer to understand a given order but to harness it. Nature ceases to be a community or a mother and becomes a domain to be possessed, a programme that inaugurates the modern technological relation to the world.

> “Instead of that speculative philosophy taught in the schools, we may find a practical one […] and thus render ourselves, as it were, masters and possessors of nature.”, Discourse on Method, VI

*Sources : Discourse on Method, VI*

### [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Techno-optimism 85% · Techno-criticism 15%

For Descartes, the new science has a practical end: to yield techniques, mechanical arts, and a medicine that would prolong life and relieve the human condition. This optimism in the transformative power of applied knowledge anticipates techno-optimism, though it never forms an explicit thesis about technology as such.

*Sources : Discourse on Method, VI*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Intellectualism 80% · Sentimentalism 20%

In *[The Passions of the Soul](../works/the-passions-of-the-soul-descartes.md)*, the passions are motions of the soul caused by the body's spirits: they are neither to be suppressed nor blindly followed, but governed by reason and a firm will. Mastery lies in an enlightened use of judgment, which leans toward **intellectualism** without denying the worth of well-ordered passions.

*Sources : The Passions of the Soul, arts. 27-50 and 211-212*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Scientism 80% · Epistemic pluralism 20%

Descartes conceives a unified knowledge: all the sciences are but one single human wisdom, which he pictures as a tree whose root is metaphysics, whose trunk is physics, and whose branches are the applied sciences. This methodic unity of knowledge leans toward scientism, even though its root remains metaphysical.

*Sources : Principles of Philosophy, Preface · Rules for the Direction of the Mind, I*

Descartes holds animals to be machines and devises a language test to tell man from automaton, yet his dualism places thought outside all mechanism: applying the functionalism / biological-naturalism axis would require an anachronistic extrapolation too strong to be positioned.

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The examined life 90% · Primacy of action 10%

The whole Cartesian procedure is a withdrawal inward: it is by turning away from the senses and the world, in the solitude of meditation, that the mind grasps itself as a thing that thinks. The cogito is won through methodic introspection, the reflective examination of one's own thoughts becoming the ground of all certainty.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, I and II · Discourse on Method, IV*

### [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Anthropocentrism 100%

Deprived of thought and therefore of soul, animals are for Descartes mere **automata**, the famous **beast-machine**: they neither feel nor suffer in the proper sense, their cries being only mechanical effects. Moral consideration is thereby restricted to thinking beings alone, a strict **anthropocentrism**.

*Sources : Discourse on Method, V · Letter to the Marquess of Newcastle, 23 November 1646*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Scientific realism 93% · Constructivism 8%

Clear and distinct ideas reach the real as it is: guaranteed by the veracity of a non-deceiving God, they are not constructions of the mind but the true apprehension of the nature of things, such as the geometrical extension of the material world. This is a strong, metaphysically grounded **scientific realism**.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, V and VI · Principles of Philosophy, I, art. 45 and II, art. 4*

### [Wholes and parts](../axes/reduction-emergence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Reductionism 93% · Emergentism 8%

The living body, the human body included, is fully explained as a **machine**: circulation, digestion, and the motion of the limbs reduce to the sole laws of shape and motion, with no superadded vital principle required. Life is **reduced** to mechanism, the soul belonging to another order entirely.

*Sources : Discourse on Method, V · Treatise on Man*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Harmony 85% · Separate domains 10% · Fideism 5%

Dedicating his Meditations to the Sorbonne, Descartes argues that the existence of God and the immortality of the soul, truths of faith, can be demonstrated by natural reason. Faith and reason thus agree: far from contradicting each other, philosophy comes to confirm and rationally ground the teachings of religion.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, Dedicatory Letter to the Sorbonne · Discourse on Method, IV*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Essentialism 93% · Existentialism 8%

Each substance has a principal attribute that constitutes its essence: thought for the mind, extension for the body. This determination of a fixed nature, prior and defining, amounts to a pronounced **essentialism**, the self's very existence being known through its essence as a thinking thing.

*Sources : Principles of Philosophy, I, art. 53 · Meditations on First Philosophy, II and VI*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eudaimonism 60% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 30% · Hedonism 10%

Descartes develops no systematic ethics, but the **provisional morality** and the analysis of [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md) in *[The Passions of the Soul](../works/the-passions-of-the-soul-descartes.md)* locate the sovereign good in the right use of [free will](../glossary/free-will.md) and firmness of judgment, the source of a stable inner contentment close to a self-mastery [eudaimonism](../glossary/eudaimonism.md).

*Sources : Discourse on Method, III · The Passions of the Soul, arts. 153-156 and 212*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Virtue ethics 70% · Deontologism 20% · Consequentialism 5% · Ethics of care 5%

The morality of *[The Passions of the Soul](../works/the-passions-of-the-soul-descartes.md)* rests on a master virtue, [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md): to know that nothing truly belongs to us but the free disposal of our will, and to feel within ourselves a firm resolution to use it well. The right is decided less by rule or consequence than by the quality of the one who acts: generosity is like the key to all the other virtues and the general remedy against the disorders of the passions, which anchors Descartes in a virtue ethics, with a share of duty in the firmness of judgment.

> “Generosity, which is as it were the key to all the other virtues.”, The Passions of the Soul, art. 161

*Sources : The Passions of the Soul, arts. 153-156 and 161 · Letter to Princess Elisabeth, 18 May 1645*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Altruism 65% · Ethical egoism 35%

Cartesian [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md) unites a legitimate self-esteem, grounded in the right use of [free will](../glossary/free-will.md), with esteem for others: recognizing in every person this same power of willing well, the generous are naturally inclined to do good toward others and to value nothing more highly than being useful to them. Self-concern is thus not displaced but reconciled with benevolence, which leans moderately toward others without lapsing into self-forgetfulness.

*Sources : The Passions of the Soul, arts. 153-156 · Letter to Princess Elisabeth, 6 October 1645*

### [Forgiveness](../axes/forgiveness.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Unconditionalism 70% · Conditionalism 30%

From [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md) follows a readiness to excuse rather than to despise: the generous, Descartes writes, can hold no one in contempt, and being inclined to excuse the faults of others, they believe that whoever commits them does so rather through lack of knowledge than through lack of goodwill. Must forgiveness be made to depend on the wrongdoer's repentance? This principled leniency, prior to any reparation, leans toward a clemency close to the unconditional.

*Sources : The Passions of the Soul, arts. 154 and 187*

### [Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Foundationalism 90% · Coherentism 10%

The project of the Meditations on First Philosophy is expressly foundationalist: to tear down every doubtful opinion and rebuild knowledge on a single unshakeable first principle, the “cogito”, whose certainty then supports the whole edifice. Knowledge rests on indubitable foundations, not on the mere coherence of a web of beliefs.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, I-II*

### [Nature of consciousness](../axes/consciousness-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Substance dualism 90% · Property dualism 10%

The argument rests on a principle: what I clearly and distinctly conceive as able to exist apart really exists apart, since God could create it so. Now I can conceive my mind, pure thinking thing (res cogitans), with nothing corporeal attributed to it, and body, pure extension (res extensa), with no thought. My essence is therefore to think, and the mind is really distinct from the body, a substance of another nature than the brain. This **substance dualism** breaks with the Aristotelian soul, form of a body, and at one stroke frees matter for a purely mechanistic physics; yet it bequeaths the problem, which Descartes will struggle to solve, of their union and interaction.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, VI*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The body as instrument 90% · The lived body 10%

The true self is the thinking thing; the body is a machine, distinct from the soul, to which the soul is joined. Descartes nonetheless acknowledges a substantial union of soul and body and rejects the image of the pilot in his ship, which tempers the pure instrumentalism somewhat.

*Sources : Meditations on First Philosophy, VI; Letters to Elisabeth*

### [Nature of love](../axes/love-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eros as lack 50% · Philia (friendship) 30% · Agape as gift 20%

In *[The Passions of the Soul](../works/the-passions-of-the-soul-descartes.md)*, love is a passion, an emotion of the soul caused by the body's spirits, that inclines the will to join itself to objects which appear suitable to it. Descartes distinguishes two loves: the love of concupiscence, which desires for oneself what one lacks, and the love of benevolence, which wills the good of the beloved. To the passion undergone is added [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md), the firm disposition to make good use of one's free will, to esteem others and to unite with them, which governs and ennobles love instead of merely suffering it.

*Sources : The Passions of the Soul, arts. 79-83 (love); arts. 153-156 (generosity)*

## Influenced by

- **[Anselm of Canterbury](../philosophers/anselm.md)** (reinterprets) : [Anselm](../philosophers/anselm.md)'s ontological argument resurfaces, through scholastic relays, in [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s Fifth Meditation, which derives God's existence from his essence, with no acknowledged borrowing.
- **[Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md)** (reinterprets) : [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s “cogito” has a famous Augustinian precedent, the “si fallor, sum”: [Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md) bequeaths him the self-certainty drawn from doubt itself and the turn toward interiority, which Descartes recasts as the foundation of knowledge, acknowledging no borrowing.
- **[Skepticism](../movements/skepticism.md)** (reacts against) : [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) takes up the arguments of [Skepticism](../movements/skepticism.md) (the illusion of the senses, the dream, the evil genius) but reverses their use: radical doubt becomes a method for reaching an indubitable certainty that overcomes it.

## Influence on

- **[Husserl](../philosophers/husserl.md)** (reinterprets) : [Husserl](../philosophers/husserl.md) takes up from [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) the quest for an apodictic ground rooted in the ego, the return to the “I think” as the indubitable soil of knowledge. But he rejects its decisive step: the ego of the reduction is no longer a thinking substance cut off from the world, it is the transcendental ego whose analysis unfolds intentional life.
- **[Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md)** (reinterprets) : [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md) starts from the Cartesian problems of substance and mechanism, but contests [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s physics of extension to ground a dynamics of monads, points of force without parts.
- **[Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md)** (reinterprets) : [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) takes up the demonstrative rigour and method of [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md), but overturns his metaphysics: instead of two substances and an external God, a single infinite substance of which everything is a mode.
- **[Transhumanism](../movements/transhumanism.md)** (continues) : The Cartesian formula of man as "master and possessor of nature" gives transhumanism its motto, from which it erases the cautious "as if": the mastery sought is no longer analogical but literal, and extends to human nature itself.
- **[Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md)** (reacts against) : In the Third Objections to the Meditations, [Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md) directly contests [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md): the thing that thinks may well be corporeal. On this central point of dualism, the confrontation crystallises his materialism against the Cartesian immaterial soul.
