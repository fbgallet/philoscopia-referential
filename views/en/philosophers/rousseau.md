# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

*1712 – 1778 · Genevan · [The Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md)*

Born in Geneva in 1712, a self-taught man who became one of the greatest writers of the century, Jean-Jacques Rousseau holds a singular place within the [Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md): a philosopher of the contract and of popular sovereignty, at first a contributor to the Encyclopédie, he nonetheless fell out with [Voltaire](../philosophers/voltaire.md), [Diderot](../philosophers/diderot.md) and [d'Alembert](../philosophers/d-alembert.md). His first Discourse argues that the sciences and arts have corrupted morals, and his whole work opposes to civilization the goodness of natural man. Through the popular sovereignty of the Social Contract, the education according to nature of Émile and the religion of the heart of the Savoyard vicar, he marks revolutionary thought and, by his cult of feeling and nature, heralds [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md). [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), who called him « the Newton of the moral world », owes him his morality of autonomy.

## Core positions

1. [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md) : **« Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains »: only a pact in which each, uniting with all, obeys only himself can found a legitimate authority.**
2. [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md) : **Man is naturally good; it is property, comparison and society that have corrupted him.**
3. [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md) : **The progress of the sciences and arts has not improved morals, it has corrupted them: civilization does not mean moral improvement.**
4. [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : **Conscience is a feeling, not a calculation: it is the heart, and pity, that tell us the good before cold reason.**
5. [Political liberty](../axes/political-liberty.md) : **To be free is not to do what one wants, it is to obey only the laws one has prescribed to oneself: liberty is autonomy.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 65% · Abolitionism 35%

Rousseau does not value suffering for its own sake, but he grants it a fruitfulness: pity, the first natural movement, is born of another's suffering felt as our own, and grounds all humanity. Ordeal and melancholy deepen the feeling soul, which heralds the Romantic valuing of painful sentiment, far from the worldly person's insensibility.

*Sources : Discourse on the Origin of Inequality · Emile, IV*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 55% · Constructed meaning 45%

The Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar affirms a good God, a providence and an immortal soul, and makes conscience the infallible voice of nature within us: a given order, with which the upright life accords. But the most intimate meaning Rousseau finds in the **sentiment of existence** and the authenticity of the self, recovered far from social artifice, a properly human and inward share.

*Sources : Emile, IV (Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar) · Reveries of the Solitary Walker*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Contractualism 95% · Organic community 5%

Since no one has natural authority over his fellow and force creates no right, the legitimate order can rest only on a convention. Rousseau seeks the form of association in which each, giving himself to all, gives himself to no one, and « still obeys only himself »: through each person's alienation to the [general will](../glossary/volonte-generale.md), the subject remains free in obeying a law of which he is co-author. Against [Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md), the pact institutes not a master but a sovereign body politic.

> “To find a form of association by which each, uniting with all, still obeys only himself and remains as free as before.”, The Social Contract, I, 6

*Sources : Rousseau, The Social Contract, books I-II*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Anthropological optimism 85% · Anthropological pessimism 15%

« Man is naturally good »: his vices lie not in his nature but in the history that has drawn him from it. In the state of nature, self-love and [pity](../glossary/pitie.md) reign, not wickedness; it is property, comparison and [amour-propre](../glossary/amour-de-soi-et-amour-propre.md) that breed inequality and domination. Rousseau thus overturns the dogma of original sin and shifts the fault from nature to society: it is not men who must first be corrected, but institutions.

> “Everything is good as it leaves the hands of the Author of things; everything degenerates in the hands of man.”, Emile, book I

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Origin of Inequality · Rousseau, Emile, book I*

### [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Techno-criticism 75% · Techno-optimism 25%

Crowned in 1750 for defending the paradox that the revival of the sciences and arts has corrupted morals, Rousseau strikes at the Enlightenment's cardinal hope: the progress of knowledge and the arts does not bring moral progress, it erodes it. Luxury and the arts polish appearances while softening souls and masking vices beneath civil exteriors. This is no praise of ignorance, but a refusal of the century's central equation between civilization and the improvement of man.

> “Our souls have grown corrupt in proportion as our sciences and arts have advanced toward perfection.”, Discourse on the Sciences and Arts (1750)

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Sciences and Arts*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The primacy of feeling 85% · The primacy of reason 15%

Against the intellectualism that makes reason the judge of the good, Rousseau lodges morality in a feeling: conscience, a « divine instinct », makes us love the good before we understand it, and [pity](../glossary/pitie.md), the natural reluctance to see suffering, grounds sociability before any calculation. The heart precedes and overflows reason. This rehabilitation of feeling, unfolded in Julie, or the New Heloise, opens the way to [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md).

> “Conscience! conscience! divine instinct, immortal and heavenly voice.”, Emile, Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar

*Sources : Rousseau, Emile, Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar · Rousseau, Julie, or the New Heloise*

### [Political liberty](../axes/political-liberty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Positive liberty 80% · Republican non-domination 15% · Negative liberty 5%

Liberty is not the absence of hindrance or the right to do anything, but obedience to the law one has prescribed to oneself: a positive liberty, the autonomy of the citizen. Civil man exchanges his natural independence for moral liberty, the only kind that truly makes him master of himself. This is why whoever refuses to obey the [general will](../glossary/volonte-generale.md), Rousseau writes in a formidable phrase, shall be « forced to be free ».

> “The impulse of mere appetite is slavery, while obedience to a law which we prescribe to ourselves is liberty.”, The Social Contract, I, 8

*Sources : Rousseau, The Social Contract, I, 7-8*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Egalitarianism 85% · Libertarianism 15%

The second Discourse makes inequality the major political evil: born of property, it warps self-love into [amour-propre](../glossary/amour-de-soi-et-amour-propre.md) and institutes domination. There is no real liberty where gaps of fortune allow some to buy and others to sell themselves. A resolutely egalitarian stance, which sets him apart from Lockean property liberalism and will inspire revolutionary thought.

> “That no citizen be rich enough to buy another, and none poor enough to be forced to sell himself.”, The Social Contract, II, 11

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Origin of Inequality · Rousseau, The Social Contract, II, 11*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Affirmation 60% · Reasoned selection 35% · Extinction and detachment 5%

Far from Stoic or Buddhist extinction, Rousseau takes desire and imagination to be the very source of the feeling of existence: one enjoys less what one possesses than what one hopes for, and happiness lies in the striving, not in full satisfaction. Yet this affirmation is not without sorting: one must follow the simple desires born of self-love and [pity](../glossary/pitie.md), and beware of those insatiable ones that [amour-propre](../glossary/amour-de-soi-et-amour-propre.md) inflates through comparison.

> “Woe to him who has nothing left to desire! He loses, as it were, all that he possesses.”, Julie, or the New Heloise, VI

*Sources : Rousseau, Julie, or the New Heloise, VI · Rousseau, Emile, book II*

### [Who should decide](../axes/who-decides.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Radical democracy 95% · Epistocracy 5%

Sovereignty resides in the people; it is inalienable and indivisible, and therefore cannot be represented. Rousseau pushes democracy to direct sovereignty, the assembled people themselves making the law, against any epistocracy as against purely representative government. Representation, in his eyes, dispossesses the citizen of the very liberty it claims to serve.

> “The instant a people gives itself representatives, it is no longer free; it no longer exists.”, The Social Contract, III, 15

*Sources : Rousseau, The Social Contract, III, 15*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Holism 75% · Individualism 25%

The pact does not juxtapose individuals: it transforms them into a moral and collective body, a « common self » endowed with a will of its own. The citizen draws his being and his liberty from this whole, to which he alienates himself entirely. Against the individualism of the Enlightenment, Rousseau leans holist, without dissolving the individual, since the end remains his liberty.

*Sources : Rousseau, The Social Contract, I, 6*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Conservatism 57% · Progressivism 43%

Rousseau is neither a progressive who expects salvation from civilization, nor a conservative who venerates established tradition. He rejects the century's central idea that the progress of enlightenment carries that of morals, and takes natural man as a critical yardstick for the present; yet he calls for a radical political refounding, not the maintenance of the old order. Hence a position drawn back from Enlightenment progressivism, without tipping into conservatism.

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Sciences and Arts · Rousseau, The Social Contract*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Epistemic pluralism 65% · Scientism 35%

If science increases knowledge, it is for Rousseau neither the model of good living nor the path of virtue: erudition and the arts often spring from idleness and vanity, and flatter [amour-propre](../glossary/amour-de-soi-et-amour-propre.md) more than they elevate the soul. The knowledge of the heart and of conscience matters more than that of the academies, hence a clear challenge to the authority the century grants to science.

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Sciences and Arts*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Naturalism 70% · Culturalism 30%

Nature, for Rousseau, is a norm: natural man, his simple needs and spontaneous feelings serve as the measure for judging social man and his artifices. If education and institutions shape man, they do so by drawing him from a primal goodness that must be recovered, not surpassed. Hence a naturalism in which culture is suspected of denaturing rather than fulfilling.

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Origin of Inequality · Rousseau, Emile*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Existentialism 65% · Essentialism 35%

Man differs from the animal less by understanding than by [perfectibility](../glossary/perfectibilite.md), that faculty of transforming oneself indefinitely which leaves him with no nature fixed in advance. What man is, he becomes through his history; his very nature is to be undetermined, which distantly heralds the idea that existence precedes essence.

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Origin of Inequality*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The examined life 85% · Primacy of action 15%

No one has pushed the exploration of the inner self further: the Confessions inaugurate the autobiography of the singular subject, and the Reveries make self-examination and inner feeling the place of truth. Against the primacy of public action, Rousseau prizes the return upon oneself, solitude and the listening to conscience.

*Sources : Rousseau, The Confessions · Rousseau, Reveries of the Solitary Walker*

### [Beauty in life](../axes/aesthetic-life.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Primacy of the ethical 75% · Aestheticism 25%

Though Rousseau exalts feeling and nature, he does not make art the highest place of existence: in the Letter to d'Alembert, he denounces the theatre that softens morals and flatters the passions. Ethics takes precedence over aesthetics, virtue over taste. In this he already differs from the aesthete Romantics he nonetheless inspires.

*Sources : Rousseau, Letter to d'Alembert on the Theatre*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Member among others 50% · Stewardship 35% · Mastery 15%

Against the Baconian project of making oneself master and possessor of nature, Rousseau sets the harmony of man with a natural order whose loss makes his unhappiness. Nature is not a material to exploit but a model and a refuge; in it man is first of all a living being among others, not a sovereign.

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Origin of Inequality · Rousseau, Reveries of the Solitary Walker*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Theism 75% · Atheism 25%

The Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar affirms a God, author of an order of the world that the heart recognizes, and the immortality of the soul. But this theism is that of a natural religion, or [deism](../glossary/deisme.md): Rousseau rejects the proofs of theology as well as revealed dogmas, and parts clearly from the atheist materialism of a [d'Holbach](../philosophers/holbach.md).

*Sources : Rousseau, Emile, Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Fideism 40% · Harmony 35% · Separate domains 20% · Conflict 5%

The vicar's faith rests neither on the authority of the Church nor on a demonstration, but on inner feeling: it is the heart that attests to God where reason wavers. This religion of conscience, midway between fideism and a harmony of faith with natural reason, heralds the religious feeling of the Romantics and of Schleiermacher.

*Sources : Rousseau, Emile, Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue ethics 45% · Ethics of care 30% · Deontologism 20% · Consequentialism 5%

The foundation of morality is neither the calculation of consequences nor an abstract rule, but two natural movements of the heart: well-ordered self-love and [pity](../glossary/pitie.md), the immediate feeling that makes us suffer at another's harm. Conscience, the instinct for the good, precedes reason. This is close to an ethics of virtue and of care, where feeling, rather than law, grounds obligation.

*Sources : Rousseau, Emile, Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar · Rousseau, Discourse on the Origin of Inequality*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Altruism 70% · Ethical egoism 30%

To the egoism that society manufactures through [amour-propre](../glossary/amour-de-soi-et-amour-propre.md), Rousseau opposes [pity](../glossary/pitie.md), prior to all reflection, which tempers self-love by identification with the suffering being. Man is not naturally inclined to harm; it is social denaturing that sets him against others. A stance leaning toward altruism, grounded in a feeling rather than in a duty.

*Sources : Rousseau, Discourse on the Origin of Inequality*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Living in the present 60% · Project 20% · Memory and transmission 20%

In the Reveries, Rousseau describes those moments when, with neither past nor future, the soul suffices to itself in the pure « feeling of existence ». Happiness lies neither in the project nor in memory, but in full presence to the moment and to nature. This prizing of the lived present will inspire the Romantic sensibility.

*Sources : Rousseau, Reveries of the Solitary Walker, Fifth Walk*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Universalism 55% · Constructivism 25% · Relativism 20%

Conscience, the « infallible judge of good and evil », speaks in every man: its verdicts depend not on local customs but on a feeling common to the species. Rousseau thus maintains a moral universalism, even though, as legislator for Poland or Corsica, he acknowledges that institutions must be fitted to the particular genius of each people.

*Sources : Rousseau, Emile, Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar · Rousseau, Considerations on the Government of Poland*

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of truth 50% · Vital illusions 50%

It is not truth that corrupts but presumption: we go astray not through what we do not know, but through what we think we know. Émile's education therefore delays the truths that cannot yet be either understood or verified, religion, metaphysics, conventions, and admits knowledge only in the measure of its present usefulness and of the pupil's experience; ignorance there is a lesser evil than error, and conscience a surer guide than reasoning.

*Sources : Emile, or On Education, II-III (1762) · Emile, IV (Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar)*

## Influenced by

- **[Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md)** (reacts against) : [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) reverses [Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md)'s [state of nature](../glossary/etat-de-nature.md): far from a « war of all against all », it is peaceful, and man within it is naturally good, moved by self-love and [pity](../glossary/pitie.md), not by fear and pride. Hobbes, Rousseau charges, projected onto natural man the passions of already-social man; it is not nature but society that makes man wicked.

## Influence on

- **[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)** (continues) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) acknowledges owing to [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) that he learned to honour mankind: he takes up the dignity of the person and moral autonomy, shifting the centre of philosophy from knowledge toward practical freedom.
- **[Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md)** (continues) : [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md) recognizes [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) as its precursor: it takes up and radicalizes his rehabilitation of feeling against reason, his cult of nature and of the authenticity of the self, and his critique of civilization. The Confessions and the Reveries inaugurate the Romantic exploration of inwardness, and the « religion of the heart » of the Savoyard vicar heralds the religious feeling of the Romantics.
