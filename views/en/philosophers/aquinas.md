# Thomas Aquinas

*1225 – 1274 · Italian · [Aristotelianism](../movements/aristotelianism.md)*

Born at the castle of Roccasecca in the kingdom of Sicily, Thomas entered while still young the Dominican order, a mendicant order devoted to preaching and study, against the will of his family, who held him captive for a year. At Paris and then Cologne he was the pupil of [Albert the Great](../philosophers/albert-the-great.md), who passed on to him a taste for the natural sciences and for the [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) rediscovered through the Arab commentators [Avicenna](../philosophers/avicenna.md) and [Averroes](../philosophers/averroes.md). A master of theology at the University of Paris at the very moment when Aristotelianism was alarming the Church authorities, he set out, in the *[Summa Theologiae](../works/summa-theologiae-aquinas.md)* and the *[Summa contra Gentiles](../works/summa-contra-gentiles-aquinas.md)*, to show that philosophical reason and revealed faith, far from contradicting each other, agree. His thought, first contested and then partly condemned in 1277, became the framework of Scholasticism and, since Leo XIII, the reference philosophy of the Catholic Church.

## Core positions

1. [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md) : **[Grace](../glossary/grace.md) does not destroy nature but perfects it: reason and faith, coming from the same God, cannot contradict each other and work together.**
2. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **God's existence is not a mere article of faith: it is demonstrated by five ways that, starting from the sensible world, rise to a first necessary term.**
3. [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md) : **There is a [natural law](../glossary/natural-law.md), the human reason's participation in God's [eternal law](../glossary/eternal-law.md), which grounds an objective and universal morality accessible to all.**
4. [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : **The soul is not a pilot lodged in the body but its form: the human being is a single substance, the unity of body and soul, not two juxtaposed things.**
5. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **No finite good satisfies the human being's desire: perfect beatitude can consist only in the [vision](../glossary/beatific-vision.md) of God, which nature calls for without being able to reach it alone.**

## Positions

### [Violence](../axes/violence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Just war 100%

The Summa systematizes **just war** under three conditions: the authority of the sovereign (no private person may declare war), a just cause (punishing a fault, repelling injustice), and a rightful intention (aiming at the good and at peace, not vengeance or greed). Force is not evil in itself: it is an instrument of justice when these conditions are met, like the sword entrusted to public power.

> “For a war to be just, three things are necessary: the authority of the sovereign, a just cause, and a rightful intention.”, Summa Theologiae, II-II, q. 40, a. 1

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, II-II, q. 40 · Summa Theologiae, II-II, q. 64, a. 7 (self-defense, double effect)*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Harmony 90% · Fideism 5% · Separate domains 5%

Can reason and faith contradict each other? Thomas answers no, and his argument rests on a thesis about their shared source: one and the same God is the author of nature, which gives reason its light, and of Revelation; one truth therefore cannot belie another. Against the Averroist doctrine of double truth, which reserved an independent domain for philosophy, he distinguishes without opposing: reason establishes by itself the preambles of faith (the existence of God, the immortality of the soul), while the properly revealed mysteries, such as the Trinity, surpass reason without contradicting it. Where an argument seems to refute the faith, it is because it is badly conducted, and philosophy itself can expose the flaw.

*Sources : Summa contra Gentiles, I, 3-9 · Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 1, a. 8*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 98% · Atheism 3%

Thomas rejects both fideism, which would treat God's existence as a mere matter of faith, and [Anselm](../philosophers/anselm.md)'s ontological argument, which claimed to deduce it from the concept alone: for a finite mind, God's existence is not self-evident, it is demonstrated from effects. Hence the [Five Ways](../glossary/five-ways.md), which all start from a fact of experience (motion, causation, contingency, degrees of perfection, the ordering of ends) and rise, to avoid an infinite regress, to a first term: an unmoved mover, an uncaused cause, a necessary being. Theism is thus held to be rationally established, and not merely believed.

> “Whatever is moved is moved by another; we must therefore arrive at a first mover moved by nothing, and this everyone understands to be God.”, Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 2, a. 3 (the first way)

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 2, a. 3 (the Five Ways) · Summa contra Gentiles, I, 13*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 60% · Eudaimonism 35% · Hedonism 5%

Taking up [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s [eudaimonism](../glossary/eudaimonism.md), Thomas grants that every action aims at a final end, happiness; but he shows that no finite good, neither wealth, nor honour, nor pleasure, nor even philosophical contemplation, can satisfy the human being's natural desire, which is stilled only in the universal good. Now God alone is that good without limit: perfect beatitude can therefore consist only in the [beatific vision](../glossary/beatific-vision.md), the contemplation of the divine essence, unreachable in this life and received by grace. Greek eudaimonism is thus preserved yet surpassed, the sovereign good being referred to a perfecting that nature calls for without being able to attain it on its own.

> “Ultimate and perfect beatitude can lie only in the vision of the divine essence.”, Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 3, a. 8

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 1-5*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Universalism 90% · Constructivism 10%

Moral norms are for Thomas neither variable conventions nor arbitrary divine decrees: they have an objective ground in the [natural law](../glossary/natural-law.md), the rational creature's participation in the [eternal law](../glossary/eternal-law.md) by which God orders all things to their end. Every human being has access to it through reason, starting from the first precept "do good and avoid evil", from which the fundamental inclinations follow (to preserve one's life, to live in society, to know the truth). Morality is therefore universal and knowable by all, believers or not, which places Thomas on the side of realist universalism; a slight reservation lies in the fact that the secondary precepts admit of variable determinations according to circumstances.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 91 and q. 94*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 70% · Deontologism 25% · Consequentialism 5%

The criterion of the right lies for Thomas in virtue, a stable disposition that perfects the agent: he takes up [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s four cardinal virtues (prudence, justice, courage, temperance) and orders them to the final end. But virtue ethics is articulated with the natural law, which supplies the objective rule that [prudence](../glossary/phronesis.md) applies to the singular case, hence a deontological strand: the virtuous agent is the one whose dispositions agree with the rational order of ends. At the summit, the virtues acquired by habit are crowned by the theological virtues, faith, hope and charity, infused by grace, which orient the human being toward an end that surpasses his nature.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 55-67; IIa-IIae*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Essentialism 95% · Existentialism 5%

Thomas takes up Aristotelian [hylomorphism](../glossary/hylomorphism.md), every sensible being being a compound of matter and form, but adds a decisive distinction: in every creature, the essence (what the thing is) is really distinct from the [esse](../glossary/esse.md) (the act of existing). An essence does not possess existence of itself, it receives it; in God alone is the essence to exist, he being “subsistent being itself”. The nature of things is therefore genuinely real and defined, yet radically dependent, hanging on the act of being that God communicates to it. An essentialism, but reordered by the primacy of existence as act.

*Sources : On Being and Essence (De ente et essentia), 4-5 · Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 3, a. 4*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Substantialism 70% · Narrativism 20% · Psychological continuity 10%

Against the [Platonic](../philosophers/plato.md) dualism that makes the soul a pilot lodged in the body, Thomas holds the soul to be the form of the body in the Aristotelian sense: the human being is not a soul using a body but a substantial unity, a single being whose soul is the formal principle. The self is therefore a substance, but a hylomorphic substance, not a pure spirit. The difficulty proper to Christianity, personal immortality, is resolved by making the human soul a form indeed united to the body yet subsistent, capable of operations (intellection) that do not depend on an organ, and thus able to survive death while awaiting the resurrection.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 75-76*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The lived body 55% · The body as instrument 45%

From the substantial union of soul and body follows a refusal of the body-as-instrument: the body is neither a prison nor a tool the soul would make use of, it enters into the very definition of the human being, who thinks only from the senses and the image (phantasm). Without going so far as phenomenology's lived body, Thomas occupies the median position of a close union in which the soul needs the body to exercise its own operations.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 76, a. 1; q. 84, a. 7*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Finalism 85% · Mechanism 10% · Vitalism 5%

Thomas inherits [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s four [causes](../glossary/four-causes.md) and the primacy of the final cause: every agent acts for the sake of an end, and it is this orientation that makes nature intelligible. But he radicalizes finalism by referring it to God: the order of ends observable in the world, which culminates in the fifth way, presupposes an intelligence directing the beings devoid of knowledge toward their goal, as the arrow is directed by the archer. Nature is therefore not a blind mechanism but a willed order, in which each thing tends by its form toward the perfection that belongs to it.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 2, a. 3 (the fifth way); q. 44, a. 4*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dualism 70% · Materialism 15% · Idealism 15%

Thomas is neither a materialist, the real including spiritual substances (God, the angels, the intellective soul), nor an idealist, the sensible world having a consistency of its own. His position approaches a moderate dualism: the human creature is composed of a material body and a subsistent spiritual soul, but this dualism is tempered by the hylomorphic unity, which refuses to treat the human being as two juxtaposed substances, as [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) later would.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 75-76*

### [Universals](../axes/universals.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Realism about universals 70% · Nominalism 30%

On the quarrel of universals, Thomas holds a moderate realism that claims to reconcile [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) and [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md): the universal exists in three ways, as an idea in the divine intellect before the thing, as a common form in the individuals, and as an abstract concept in our mind after the thing. He thus refuses both the Platonic realism of separate forms and the nominalism that would see in them only names: the universal is really grounded in things, yet exists as a universal only in the intellect that abstracts it.

*Sources : On Being and Essence (De ente et essentia), 3-4*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 65% · Rationalism 35%

Thomas refuses [innatism](../glossary/innate-ideas.md) and holds, following [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md), that all knowledge begins with the senses, the intellect being at first like a [blank slate](../glossary/tabula-rasa.md) on which nothing is written. But the empiricism is tempered: the [agent intellect](../glossary/agent-intellect.md) does not passively receive the sensible, it abstracts from it the intelligible form, drawing the universal out of the perceived particular. Knowledge therefore starts from experience without reducing to it, which keeps Thomas on the empirical side without making him a strict empiricist, the activity of the mind having its irreducible share.

> “There is nothing in the intellect that was not first in the senses.”, Disputed Questions on Truth, q. 2, a. 3, arg. 19

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 84-85*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Correspondence 90% · Coherence 5% · Pragmatism 5%

Thomas gives its classic formula to the correspondence conception: truth is the adequation of thing and intellect, the agreement of thought with what is. But he grounds it theologically: things are true because they conform to the idea God has of them, and our knowledge is true when it is regulated by them. Truth is therefore first in the intellect, divine then human, and the correspondence between mind and the real itself has its origin in God.

> “Truth is the adequation of thing and intellect.”, Disputed Questions on Truth, q. 1, a. 1

*Sources : Disputed Questions on Truth, q. 1, a. 1-2*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Confident in reason's power to demonstrate (God's existence, the principles of morality), Thomas takes the side of [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md): science proceeds from self-evident first principles to certain conclusions, and systematic doubt has no place. This assurance is nonetheless tempered by an awareness of the limits of the finite mind, which does not grasp God's essence and must receive by faith what exceeds its reach.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 1, a. 2; q. 2*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Free will 75% · Hard determinism 25%

Thomas holds a real [free will](../glossary/free-will.md), which he grounds in the intellect: because the will is moved by the good as reason presents it, and no finite good imposes itself on it absolutely, the human being deliberates and freely chooses among the means. But this freedom is not absolute: the will is necessarily borne toward the good in general, it does not choose itself out of nothing, and above all, without [grace](../glossary/grace.md), it cannot orient itself toward its supernatural end. Hence a measured libertarian position, articulating the creature's freedom with divine motion rather than opposing them.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 83; Ia-IIae, q. 109-114 (grace)*

### [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theodicy 45% · Free-will defence 45% · Revolt / anti-theodicy 10%

How can a good God allow evil to exist? Thomas takes from [Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md) the thesis that evil is not a thing nor a substance created by God, but a privation, a lack of being or of good that ought to be there, as blindness is the absence of sight. God therefore does not create evil; he permits physical evil for the sake of a greater good in the order of the whole, and moral evil out of respect for free will, without which there would be neither virtue nor merit. The profile thus blends theodicy (evil ordered to the good of the whole) and the free-will defence inherited from Augustine.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 48-49 · Summa contra Gentiles, III, 71*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Transcendence 93% · Immanence 7%

God is for Thomas radically [transcendent](../glossary/transcendence.md), distinct from the world he creates out of nothing, and his essence surpasses everything we can conceive: by reason we know of him only what he is not (negative theology). Yet transcendence is not sheer separation, for God is also intimately present to every thing as the cause that gives it being at each instant. This articulation is expressed by the [analogy of being](../glossary/analogy-of-being.md): the perfections of creatures (goodness, wisdom) are found in God, but in an eminent degree and in a mode that escapes us, neither purely identical (univocity) nor purely alien (equivocity).

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 3 (divine simplicity); q. 13 (the divine names)*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Immortalism 100%

Death separates the soul from the body, but the intellective soul, because it subsists in itself, does not perish: it survives awaiting the resurrection of bodies, where the human being will once again be complete. Death is therefore neither sheer nothingness nor the ultimate horizon, but a passage toward eternal life and, for the just, toward the vision of God. This is a properly Christian immortalism, joining the immortality of the soul to the resurrection of the flesh.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 75, a. 6; Suppl., q. 75*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 95% · Constructed meaning 5%

The meaning of life is for Thomas given, inscribed in a [providence](../glossary/providence.md) that orders every being to its end: the human being is created to know and love God, and all his existence finds its direction in this return toward its origin. A slight constructive part remains, since it falls to each one to cooperate freely with this end through his acts, but the fundamental orientation is neither invented nor absurd, it is received from a willed order.

*Sources : Summa contra Gentiles, III, 25 and 37*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The principle of hope 95% · Lucidity without hope 5%

Hope is for Thomas one of the three theological virtues: stretched toward the arduous but possible future good that is beatitude, it relies on the help of divine grace. Far from a lucidity without hope, Christian existence is oriented by the confident expectation of an end that surpasses the powers of nature, which places Thomas at the extreme of the pole of hope.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, IIa-IIae, q. 17-22*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Reasoned selection 70% · Extinction and detachment 20% · Affirmation 10%

Thomas does not condemn desire: the passions and appetites are good in themselves, willed by God, and the virtue of temperance does not extinguish them but orders them according to reason, after the manner of the Aristotelian [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md). Reasoned selection therefore prevails, but it is ordered to an ultimate end: every finite desire is only the sign of a natural desire for God, alone able to still it, which inflects the balance without lapsing into detachment.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 22-48 (the passions); IIa-IIae, q. 141*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Sentimentalism 65% · Intellectualism 35%

Against the Stoic ideal of extinguishing the passions, Thomas grants them a properly moral value: integrated into virtue and ordered by reason, compassion, fear or just anger perfect the good act instead of disturbing it. The virtuous person is not the impassive one but the one whose affects follow right judgment, which leans moderately toward a rehabilitation of the emotions without giving up the primacy of reason.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 24 (the goodness of the passions)*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Organic community 93% · Contractualism 7%

Taking up [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s political animal, Thomas holds society to be natural and not born of a pact: the human being is led by his nature to live in community, since alone he can neither provide for his needs nor develop his reason. The city is therefore not an artifice born of fear, as the contractualists will hold, but the fulfilment of an inclination inscribed in human nature and willed by God, ordered to the common good.

*Sources : On Kingship (De regno), I, 1 · Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 96, a. 4*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Authority 75% · Anarchism 25%

Political authority is for Thomas legitimate and natural, since even a state of innocence would have required a direction of the whole toward the common good. But this authority is not absolute: it is bound to serve the common good and remains subject to the natural law; an unjust law, contrary to the good or to the divine law, does not bind in conscience and is no longer a law but a kind of violence. Hence a position favourable to authority, yet bounded by a higher norm that judges it.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 96, a. 4 (the unjust law)*

### [Ideal and reality](../axes/political-ideal.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Political realism 70% · Utopianism 30%

Following [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) rather than [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s ideal city, Thomas reasons about actual regimes and holds tempered monarchy to be the best government, provided it serves the common good and is restrained from becoming tyranny. This prudent realism, attentive to circumstances and to the means of preventing the abuse of power, distances him from utopianism without carrying him to Machiavellian cynicism.

*Sources : On Kingship (De regno), I, 1-6*

### [Nature of rights](../axes/rights-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Natural rights 85% · Legal positivism 15%

The Thomist doctrine of [natural law](../glossary/natural-law.md) is the medieval ancestor of natural rights: there is a just that does not depend on the legislator's will but on the nature ordered by God, and human law only determines or applies this prior norm. Positive law derives its validity from its conformity to natural law; it is not held to be a sheer creation of the sovereign, which places Thomas on the side of natural rights against the legal positivism to come.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 95, a. 2*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Libertarianism 60% · Egalitarianism 40%

Thomas takes up Aristotelian distributive justice, proportioned to merit and rank, but tempers it with the universal destination of goods: private property is legitimate for use and administration, not absolute, and in case of extreme necessity the poor person who takes what he needs to survive does not steal, since then all things are common. This limitation of the right of property in favour of the needy qualifies the meritocratic leaning toward a requirement of sufficiency.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, IIa-IIae, q. 61 (distributive justice); q. 66, a. 7 (theft in necessity)*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Equity-phronesis 80% · Legalism 20%

Taking up [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s equity (epieikeia), Thomas holds that the law, by its generality, can fail in the singular case where its literal application would contradict the legislator's intention and the common good: one must then depart from it to follow what justice and the common good require. The rule is therefore not a letter to be applied mechanically but the expression of a reasonable intention that prudence interprets, which leans toward the spirit against legalism.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, IIa-IIae, q. 120 (epieikeia)*

### [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Anthropocentrism 90% · Pathocentrism 10%

Thomas takes up the Aristotelian hierarchy of living beings, the lower creatures being ordered to the use of the human being, alone made in God's image and endowed with an immortal rational soul. Charity is owed to persons, not to beasts, and cruelty toward animals is blameworthy only indirectly, because it hardens the heart. This is a marked anthropocentrism, tempered by the idea that every creature, insofar as it is, is good and reflects its Creator.

*Sources : Summa contra Gentiles, III, 112*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Mastery 60% · Stewardship 30% · Member among others 10%

From the ordering of creatures to the human being follows a vision in which nature is entrusted to his use, but this use is framed: the human being is not an absolute owner, he administers a creation that remains God's work and bears its trace everywhere. The position leans toward mastery, but a responsible mastery, closer to stewardship than to the Promethean exploitation modernity will claim.

*Sources : Summa contra Gentiles, III, 112; Summa Theologiae, Ia, q. 96*

### [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Necessity, leisure as the end 50% · Fulfillment 40% · Alienation 10%

Work belongs first to the necessity of providing for life, but Thomas, heir to the monastic rule, recognizes in it a dignity that Greek antiquity denied it: it disciplines the body, wards off idleness and makes almsgiving possible. It remains nonetheless ordered to a higher end, the contemplative leisure (otium) in which the mind turns toward God, which stays superior to the active life. Hence a weight shared between necessity and a form of fulfilment.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, IIa-IIae, q. 187, a. 3; q. 182 (active and contemplative life)*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Partialism 80% · Impartialism 20%

Charity itself is ordered, Thomas argues: we ought to love all human beings, but not all equally, for the order of nature requires that we first help those close to us, parents and fellow citizens, to whom we are more tightly bound. This hierarchy of duties, grounded in real proximity, places Thomas on the side of partialism, without thereby excluding the universality in principle of love of neighbour.

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, IIa-IIae, q. 26 (the order of charity)*

### [Nature of love](../axes/love-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Agape as gift 55% · Philia (friendship) 35% · Eros as lack 10%

The supreme love is for Thomas charity (caritas), the Latin rendering of [agape](../glossary/agape.md): a love of benevolence that wills the other's good and descends from God toward the creature rather than rising from lack toward the good, as Platonic [eros](../glossary/eros.md) does. But Thomas gives it a precise and decisive definition: charity is a [friendship](../glossary/philia.md) (amicitia) of the human being with God, [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s very category taken up and transposed: founded on the sharing of beatitude, it supposes, like every friendship, mutual benevolence and a communion. The gift remains primary, since it is God who loves first and makes this friendship possible by grace, yet it takes the reciprocal form of philia, not that of a one-way gift; the old upward thrust of desire toward the good nonetheless persists as a residual share.

> “Charity is a kind of friendship between man and God.”, Summa Theologiae, IIa-IIae, q. 23, a. 1

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, IIa-IIae, q. 23 (charity as friendship)*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Asceticism 50% · Celebration 50%

Against the contempt for the body found in certain traditions, Thomas holds bodily pleasures to be good in themselves, willed by God and necessary to life: pleasure completes the natural operation. But the virtue of temperance submits them to reason and to the final end, neither condemning nor exalting them. Hence a position of balance, equidistant from asceticism and celebration, faithful to the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md).

*Sources : Summa Theologiae, Ia-IIae, q. 34 (the goodness of pleasures)*

## Influenced by

- **[Albert the Great](../philosophers/albert-the-great.md)** (continues) : [Thomas Aquinas](../philosophers/aquinas.md)'s master at Cologne, [Albert the Great](../philosophers/albert-the-great.md) introduces him to the systematic study of Aristotle and to reconciling natural philosophy with faith.
- **[Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)** (synthesizes) : [Aquinas](../philosophers/aquinas.md) integrates the physics, psychology and metaphysics of [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md), "the Philosopher", into a vast synthesis with Christian doctrine, making Aristotelian reason the instrument of theology.
