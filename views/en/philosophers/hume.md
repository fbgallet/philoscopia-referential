# David Hume

*1711 – 1776 · Scottish · [Empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md), [Skepticism](../movements/skepticism.md)*

Scottish philosopher, historian and essayist, a leading figure of the Edinburgh Enlightenment and of British [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md), following [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) and Berkeley. Refused a university chair on suspicion of irreligion, he lived by his pen, from a youthful *[system](../works/treatise-of-human-nature-hume.md)* that at first fell dead-born to a celebrated *[Enquiry](../works/enquiry-concerning-human-understanding-hume.md)*, the Essays and a monumental History of England. His project: to apply Newton's experimental method to the human mind, testing how far observation, without metaphysical hypotheses, can carry the analysis of knowledge, the passions and morality. Reckoned a scandalous sceptic in his lifetime, he became the goad of modern philosophy: it was he, [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) would say, who roused him from his “dogmatic slumber”.

## Core positions

1. [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : **We never perceive the necessary connection between cause and effect, only their constant conjunction: causation is but a habit of the mind, and nothing rationally grounds [induction](../glossary/induction.md).**
2. [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : **Reason can never by itself move us to act or set an end: it is and ought only to be the servant of the passions, computing nothing but their means.**
3. [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : **When I look within, I never catch a self but always some perception: the self is only a “bundle of perceptions” in flux, with no substance and no real [identity](../glossary/personal-identity.md).**
4. [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md) : **From what is one cannot derive what ought to be: value is not read off the facts but lies in the spectator's sentiment of approval.**
5. [Ethics of belief](../axes/belief-ethics.md) : **One must measure one's assent to the strength of the evidence: no testimony can establish a miracle, for the witness being mistaken is always less improbable than a violation of the laws of nature.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Abolitionism 85% · Fecundity and redemption 15%

For Hume, suffering is an evil that sympathy and benevolence move us to relieve: virtue is measured by its usefulness and agreeableness. So he ranks the “monkish virtues”, fasting, penance, mortification, among the vices, for they add to the world only suffering while producing nothing good: no fruitfulness in sought-out pain.

*Sources : An Enquiry concerning the Principles of Morals, IX*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 85% · Teleology and providence 10% · Absurdism 5%

Hume ruins the argument from design and judges providence unknowable: no meaning is legible in the order of things. But he draws from this neither anguish nor revolt: meaning is found at human scale, in the pleasures of sociable life, friendship, activity and useful virtue. An immanent, serene significance that does without a beyond.

*Sources : Dialogues concerning Natural Religion · An Enquiry concerning the Principles of Morals*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Death is nothing 85% · Immortalism 10% · Revolt / refusal 5%

Hume refutes the immortality of the soul on every ground, metaphysical, moral and physical: nothing warrants the belief that consciousness survives the dissolution of the body. He faces his own end, moreover, with a cheerful serenity, without fear or hope of a beyond. Death is the natural terminus, and to dread it makes no more sense than to regret not having existed before being born.

*Sources : Of the Immortality of the Soul*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eudaimonism 55% · Hedonism 40% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 5%

Hume sketches a sociable, temperate good life: a blend of delicate pleasures, activity, friendship and benevolence. Virtue itself has worth only through what is useful and agreeable, to oneself and to others; the “monkish virtues”, fasting and mortification, are rejected as useless and so vicious. Neither rigorism nor pure hedonism, but a natural human flourishing.

*Sources : An Enquiry concerning the Principles of Morals, IX · A Treatise of Human Nature, III*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Affirmation 55% · Reasoned selection 40% · Extinction and detachment 5%

Hume overturns the classical primacy of reason over desire: the passions are the legitimate springs of all action, which reason serves without being able to oppose them, only the calm passions tempering the violent ones. No asceticism, then, but the acceptance of desires as the primary data of life, ordered from within rather than repressed.

> “Reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions.” (A Treatise of Human Nature, II, 3, 3)

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, II, 3, 3*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Existentialism 60% · Essentialism 40%

Hume dissolves fixed essences: we know no real essence of substances, and the **self** itself is but a bundle of perceptions in flux, with no permanent core. What we become is forged by custom and habit more than by an assigned nature, without amounting for all that to a free self-fashioning, the human being remaining largely shaped.

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, I, 4, 6 · An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 98% · Rationalism 3%

Perceptions fall into two classes set apart by their force alone: impressions, vivid and original (sensations, passions), and ideas, their faint copies in thought. From this Hume draws both a principle and an instrument: every simple idea derives from a prior impression, so an idea answering to no impression is empty of meaning. More radical than [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)'s [blank slate](../glossary/tabula-rasa.md), this test does not merely reject [innate ideas](../glossary/innate-ideas.md): it becomes an engine for dissolving the pseudo-concepts of metaphysics (substance, necessary connection), which one need only challenge to produce their source impression. Empiricism is thus turned into a critical weapon against the [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md) of [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md).

> “All our ideas are nothing but copies of our impressions, or, in other words, it is impossible for us to think of anything which we have not antecedently felt.” (Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. II)

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. II (of the origin of ideas) · A Treatise of Human Nature, I, part I, sect. 1*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Mechanism 100%

Every idea must derive from an impression: so Hume asks which impression yields the necessary connection between a cause and its effect, and finds none. In objects, experience gives only succession and constant conjunction; never the bond that would oblige one to follow the other. Necessity is therefore not in things: it is a habit (custom) of the mind, an expectation projected by repetition. Hence the problem of [induction](../glossary/induction.md): if nothing lets us perceive the causal link, no inference from past to future is rationally grounded. It is this analysis, by undermining causation as objective necessity, that would rouse [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) from his dogmatic slumber. [Finality](../axes/causality.md) and vital force, answering to no impression, fall with it.

> “After a repetition of similar instances, the mind is carried by habit, upon the appearance of one event, to expect its usual attendant... This connexion, therefore, which we feel in the mind, is the impression from which we form the idea of power or necessary connexion.” (Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. VII)

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. VII (of the idea of necessary connexion) · A Treatise of Human Nature, I, part III, sect. 6 and 14*

This axis contrasts conceptions of causation in Nature (mechanism, finalism, vitalism). Hume defends no vitalist or finalist physics: his critique is epistemological, denying that we perceive any necessary connection. Lacking any finality or vital force, the fitting pole is mechanism, by default rather than from a committed metaphysics of matter.

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Skepticism 85% · Dogmatism 15%

The problem of [induction](../glossary/induction.md) brings Hume to the edge of [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md): if no reasoning justifies expecting the future to resemble the past, our most assured beliefs rest on custom, an instinct, not on reason. But the argument does not end in Pyrrhonian paralysis, and that is its point: nature is stronger than doubt, compelling us to believe and act the moment we leave the study. Hume therefore opposes to thoroughgoing [Pyrrhonism](../glossary/skepticism.md), untenable in life, a “mitigated” (academical) skepticism, a moderate reserve that confines inquiry to what experience can reach.

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. IV-V and sect. XII (of the academical or sceptical philosophy) · A Treatise of Human Nature, I, part IV, sect. 1-2*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Bundle theory 100%

The same test applies to the self: if it existed, there would be an impression that gives it. But introspection never yields a permanent subject, only some particular perception, heat, light, pleasure or pain, at once replaced by another. Hume infers that there is no thinking substance beneath the perceptions: the self is but a “bundle of perceptions” in perpetual flux. The [personal identity](../glossary/personal-identity.md) we ascribe to it is a fiction of the imagination, mistaking the resemblance and continuity of its contents for a real identity. The thesis targets [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) and every soul-substance; it is the archetype of the bundle theory.

> “For my part, when I enter most intimately into what I call myself, I always stumble on some particular perception or other... I never can catch myself at any time without a perception.” (A Treatise of Human Nature, I, IV, 6)

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, I, part IV, sect. 6 (of personal identity)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Sentimentalism 95% · Intellectualism 5%

Reason has only two offices: to relate ideas (the demonstrative) or to infer matters of fact (the causal); yet neither act, by itself, moves the will. Hume concludes that reason can neither produce nor prevent an action, and so can never oppose a passion: only a contrary passion can. Reason settles the means, but ends come from sentiment; it is not contrary to reason to prefer one's own ruin to the slightest unease of another, for a desire is neither true nor false. Overturning the Socratic and Stoic intellectualism that made passions errors of judgement, Hume defends a sentimentalism in which reason is subordinate to affect in everything bearing on action and moral judgement.

> “Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions, and can never pretend to any other office than to serve and obey them.” (A Treatise of Human Nature, II, III, 3)

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, II, part III, sect. 3 (of the influencing motives of the will)*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Ethics of care 60% · Consequentialism 20% · Virtue ethics 20%

The distinction of good and evil arises from a sentiment of approval or disapproval, not from a deduction of reason: morality is felt more than judged. This sentiment is rooted in sympathy, the capacity to share others' affects, which makes us approve qualities useful and agreeable to oneself and to others. Hence an ethics of care and sentiment, with a virtue component (qualities of character) and a consequentialist concern for utility.

> “Morality is more properly felt than judg'd of.” (A Treatise of Human Nature, III, I, 2)

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals, sect. I and IX (moral sentiment, utility) · A Treatise of Human Nature, III, part I, sect. 1-2 (morals not derived from reason)*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Constructivism 45% · Relativism 35% · Universalism 20%

Hume spots an illicit leap in moral treatises: their authors slip without warning from propositions joined by “is” to propositions joined by “ought”, though a new ought cannot be deduced from any set of purely descriptive premises (Hume's guillotine). No relation of fact, and so no act of reason alone, can ground an obligation: value is not a quality of things but lies in the spectator's sentiment of approval. Morality thereby loses its realist foundation, against the rationalist moral realism that thought to read the good off being. Yet this sentiment, common to all human beings and regulated by shared utility, lends judgements a near-universal stability: the position is broadly constructivist, with a relativist share, without lapsing into arbitrariness.

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, III, I, 1 (the is-ought passage) · Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals, sect. IX and Appendix I*

### [Ethics of belief](../axes/belief-ethics.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Evidentialism 100%

Since every factual belief rests on experience, its firmness should be measured by the evidence: to believe beyond what the evidence warrants is an epistemic fault. Hume turns this into a weapon against miracles: a miracle is by definition a violation of the laws of nature, and those laws are established by uniform experience, hence by the fullest proof there is. The weight of contrary experience is therefore maximal; no testimony can overcome it, unless its falsehood would be more miraculous still than the reported fact, which never occurs. The argument does not deny the supernatural a priori: it shows that a mind ruled by evidence cannot assent to it.

> “A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence.” (Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. X)

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. X (of miracles)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Atheism 78% · Theism 22%

In the Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion, Hume dismantles the argument from design: the order of the world warrants no secure inference to an infinite, wise and good creator, above all given evil. Without professing firm atheism (he allows a vague “cause or causes” of order), he empties theism of any determinate content: the position leans clearly toward agnosticism and atheism.

*Sources : Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion (esp. parts V, VIII and XI) · The Natural History of Religion*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Hard determinism 75% · Free will 25%

Hume proposes a reconciliation (the “reconciling project”) between liberty and necessity: liberty is not the absence of causes, but the power to act according to the determinations of one's will, free from external constraint. The [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md) of human actions is even required for moral responsibility, since it is character that accounts for acts: classical [compatibilism](../glossary/compatibilism.md).

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. VIII (of liberty and necessity) · A Treatise of Human Nature, II, part III, sect. 1-2*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Conflict 50% · Separate domains 40% · Fideism 10%

Hume sets religious belief against the [demand for evidence](../axes/belief-ethics.md): no religion grounded in reason holds up, faith always exceeding the available evidence (hence the ironic fideist twist when he concludes that only a continued miracle, faith itself, makes one believe Christianity). Religious belief thus belongs to another register than knowledge, and where it claims to demonstrate (natural theology) it conflicts with rigorous reason.

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. X, conclusion (on faith) · Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion, part XII*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Correspondence 60% · Coherence 30% · Pragmatism 10%

Hume distinguishes two objects of inquiry: relations of ideas (mathematical, certain and demonstrable) and matters of fact, whose truth lies in their conformity to what experience reveals of the real. Factual truth is thus a matter of correspondence with experience, while the truth of relations of ideas is a matter of internal coherence, hence the split weights.

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. IV, part I (relations of ideas and matters of fact)*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dualism 40% · Materialism 35% · Idealism 25%

Hume suspends every strong thesis about the ultimate nature of the real: he treats as a fiction both the belief in the continued, independent existence of bodies and the belief in a spiritual substance, without denying either in ordinary life. This [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) about substance, material or immaterial, forbids any clear classification and yields near-balanced weights, with a slight tilt toward the dualist doubt inherited from his examination of perceptions.

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, I, part IV, sect. 2 (of scepticism with regard to the senses) · A Treatise of Human Nature, I, part IV, sect. 5 (of the immateriality of the soul)*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Naturalism 85% · Culturalism 15%

Hume aims to ground a “science of human nature” on the experimental method: the same springs (passions, habit, sympathy) govern human beings in all times and places, and history is a store of observations on constant features. This uniformity of human nature, indispensable to moral science, reflects a marked naturalism, even as he acknowledges the weight of mores and custom.

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, introduction (the project of a science of man) · Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. VIII, part I (the uniformity of human actions)*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Partialism 80% · Impartialism 20%

Sympathy, the source of moral approval, is by nature unequal: we feel more keenly for those near us than for the distant. Hume acknowledges this “limited sympathy” as a primary fact of human nature; the rules of justice and institutions serve precisely to correct the effects of this partiality, without abolishing it. The leaning is thus partialist, tempered by an artificial corrective.

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, III, part II, sect. 1-2 (the origin of justice, limited sympathy)*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Organic community 75% · Contractualism 25%

In the essay “Of the Original Contract”, Hume criticizes the fiction of a founding social contract: no real government rests on explicit consent, and obedience is explained by habit, utility and common interest, not by an original promise. Political order is a customary fact gradually instituted, which distances him from abstract contractualism without making him an organicist.

*Sources : Essays, Moral, Political, and Literary, "Of the Original Contract"*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · extrapolated · minor*

Conservatism 70% · Progressivism 30%

Holding the established order to be a product of custom and tested utility, Hume distrusts reforms driven by abstract principles and enthusiasms (religious or factious enthusiasm). Without being a reactionary, he prizes the stability of institutions and prudence: a rather conservative sensibility, wary of abrupt change.

*Sources : Essays, Moral, Political, and Literary (esp. "Of the Original Contract" and "Of the Rise and Progress of the Arts and Sciences")*

### [Beauty](../axes/beauty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Subjective beauty 85% · Objective beauty 15%

Beauty is no quality of things but exists in the mind that contemplates them: it reduces to a sentiment of pleasure, like moral worth. Hume does not infer sheer arbitrariness: a “standard of taste” emerges from the verdict of experienced, delicate critics, which tempers the subjectivism without overturning it.

> “Beauty is no quality in things themselves: it exists merely in the mind which contemplates them; and each mind perceives a different beauty.” (Essays, "Of the Standard of Taste")

*Sources : Essays, "Of the Standard of Taste"*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Altruism 65% · Ethical egoism 35%

Hume rejects the psychological egoism that would reduce all motivation to self-interest: there exists a genuine disinterested benevolence and real sympathy, the sources of moral approval. Interest is therefore not the only spring, though it plays a large part (utility, the origin of justice), hence a moderately altruistic position.

*Sources : Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals, Appendix II (of self-love)*

### [Can a machine think](../axes/machine-mind.md)

*not applicable*

The functionalism / biological-naturalism opposition about mind presupposes 20th-century cognitive science and artificial intelligence. Hume theorizes the mind as a bundle of perceptions associated by laws (resemblance, contiguity, causation), but the question whether a machine could think is foreign to him.

### [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Argument from evil (atheism) 90% · Revolt / anti-theodicy 10%

In the Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion, Philo revives Epicurus's old questions: faced with the world's mass of suffering, divine goodness and omnipotence cannot be inferred from nature. Evil is the reef on which natural theology runs aground.

*Sources : Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion, X-XI*

### [Universals](../axes/universals.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Nominalism 88% · Realism about universals 13%

Following Berkeley, Hume denies that abstract general ideas exist: an idea is always particular, but a general word ties it to others that resemble it and can recall them. Universals are a matter of words and habit, not of realities.

*Sources : A Treatise of Human Nature, I, I, 7*

## Influenced by

- **[George Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md)** (reinterprets) : [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) inherits from [Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md) the critique of abstract ideas and the recentring of all knowledge on perceptions, which he extends from matter to the mind itself.
- **[Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)** (reinterprets) : [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) pushes Locke's analysis of ideas drawn from experience to its skeptical conclusions: an associationist theory of the mind and the dissolution of personal identity and of necessary causation.

## Influence on

- **[Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md)** (transmits) : [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md) reports that on reading [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) the scales fell from his eyes: there he finds psychological naturalism and the idea that utility is the measure of good, which he erects into the principle of [utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md).
- **[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)** (reacts against) : [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) draws a skeptical conclusion from empiricism: no impression delivers the necessary connection between a cause and its effect, only habit does. [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) says this woke him from his “dogmatic slumber”, and he builds critical philosophy against that skepticism, lodging causality in the a priori conditions of experience rather than in things themselves.
- **[Popper](../philosophers/popper.md)** (reacts against) : [Popper](../philosophers/popper.md) takes [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md)'s problem of induction, that no accumulation of observations justifies a law, to be insoluble, and sidesteps it: science does not verify, it refutes; a theory counts only if it is falsifiable.
