# Immanuel Kant

*1724 – 1804 · Prussian*

German Enlightenment philosopher, a professor at Königsberg he never left, a pivotal figure of modern philosophy standing between the [rationalist legacy](../movements/rationalism.md) of [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md) and the [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) of [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), who he says “woke him from his dogmatic slumber”. After a long "pre-critical" period he published late, near sixty, the three Critiques, in which he sets out to bring reason itself before a tribunal, to measure what it can know, what it ought to do and what it may hope. His work commands the whole of German idealism that followed ([Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md)) and remains, from neo-Kantianism to [Rawls](../philosophers/rawls.md), a living reference for ethics and political philosophy.

## Core positions

1. [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md) : **We never know things as they are in themselves, only phenomena: it is the mind that, through its a priori forms, constitutes the object of knowledge.**
2. [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : **All knowledge begins with experience but does not all derive from it: without concepts intuitions are blind, without intuitions concepts are empty.**
3. [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : **An action has moral worth only when done from duty, never from its consequences: act only on a maxim you can will as a [universal law](../glossary/categorical-imperative.md).**
4. [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : **As a phenomenon the human being is fully [determined](../glossary/determinism.md), but as a [thing in itself](../glossary/thing-in-itself.md) he is [free](../glossary/free-will.md): "you ought" implies "you can".**
5. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **No theoretical proof of God holds, yet morality requires him: I had to deny knowledge to make room for rational faith.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 55% · Abolitionism 45%

Kant does not make the absence of suffering the end of morality: what has unconditional worth is the good will, independent of pleasure and pain. Suffering is thus morally indifferent as to the worth of the person, and ordeal can even display moral firmness. But this detachment is bordered by a **duty of beneficence** that commands relieving the suffering of others.

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, I-II · Metaphysics of Morals, Doctrine of Virtue*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 55% · Constructed meaning 45%

The meaning of life, for Kant, lies in the moral vocation: to become worthy of happiness by fulfilling the law that reason gives itself, which belongs to **autonomy**, not to a received end. But the realization of the highest good exceeds our powers and postulates a moral world-order guaranteed by God: a rationally hoped-for providence thus borders autonomy with a teleological horizon.

*Sources : Critique of Practical Reason, Dialectic · Idea for a Universal History with a Cosmopolitan Aim*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Immortalism 80% · Death is nothing 10% · Being-toward-death 10%

Theoretical reason cannot prove that the soul survives the body, but practical reason postulates it: the moral law commands a perfection that no finite life completes, which requires an indefinite progress, hence the immortality of the soul. An immortality not demonstrated but postulated, a rational belief resting on morality.

*Sources : Critique of Practical Reason, Dialectic, II*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Extinction and detachment 50% · Reasoned selection 40% · Affirmation 10%

Inclinations are not evil in themselves, but they cannot ground moral worth: an action has value only when done from duty, independently of any inclination. This **moral disinterestedness** requires that the will make itself autonomous with regard to desire, acting against it where need be; without preaching the extinction of inclinations, Kant keeps a distance from them that leans toward detachment more than toward their eudaimonist sorting.

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, I-II · Critique of Practical Reason*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 75% · Existentialism 25%

Kant grants the human being a rational nature that is not chosen: it is humanity as an end in itself, the source of dignity and the ground of the moral law. This rational essence is given and universal; but moral character is won through freedom, which tempers the essentialism without abolishing it.

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, II · Anthropology from a Pragmatic Point of View*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dogmatism 70% · Skepticism 30%

Roused from his **dogmatic slumber** by [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), Kant rejects both opposing paths: [dogmatic](../glossary/dogmatism.md) metaphysics, which claims to know the unconditioned, God, the soul, the world as a totality, loses itself in the **antinomies**, where reason contradicts itself; yet Humean [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md), dissolving necessity into habit, is just as untenable. The critical path settles it: there is indeed an **apodictic** certainty, that of synthetic a priori judgments, but it holds only of appearances, objects as they appear to us under the forms of the subject, not of things in themselves. A necessary and universal certainty, then, but paid for with an impassable limit.

> “I freely admit that it was the objection of David Hume that, many years ago, first interrupted my dogmatic slumber.” (Prolegomena, Preface)

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Prefaces and Transcendental Dialectic · Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 50% · Rationalism 50%

The dispute between [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md) and [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) is insoluble so long as one term is asked to carry everything: experience alone never yields necessity ([Hume](../philosophers/hume.md)'s objection about causation), reason alone runs idle. Kant's answer is the synthetic a priori judgment: there are a priori forms of sensibility and understanding that structure any possible experience, so that knowledge begins with experience without deriving wholly from it. This is the “Copernican turn” applied to knowledge, and the reason the axis settles at the centre.

> “Thoughts without content are empty, intuitions without concepts are blind.” (Critique of Pure Reason, A51/B75)

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Introduction · Critique of Pure Reason, A51/B75*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Deontologism 90% · Virtue ethics 10%

Nothing is good without qualification except a **good will**: neither talents nor happiness, which can serve evil. If moral worth lies neither in the object willed nor in the consequences, it can lie only in the form of the maxim, its fitness to hold as a law for all. From this the [categorical imperative](../glossary/categorical-imperative.md) is derived: act only on a maxim I can will to be universal. This is a deontology that refuses, against [utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md), to measure duty by outcome; the *[Doctrine of Virtue](../works/metaphysics-of-morals-kant.md)* reintroduces the virtues, but as derived from duty, hence a small residual weight on that pole.

> “Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law.” (Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, section II)

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, sections I and II · Metaphysics of Morals, Doctrine of Virtue*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Universalism 90% · Constructivism 10%

The **moral law** holds universally for every rational being, independently of cultures and inclinations: this is the most assertive universalism in the tradition. A slight constructivist weight is warranted by **autonomy**: the law is not discovered in the world, reason gives it to itself.

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, section II · Critique of Practical Reason*

### [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Truthful rigorism 95% · Benevolent lying permitted 5%

Against Benjamin Constant, Kant maintains that truthfulness is an **unconditional duty**, even toward the murderer asking where one's friend is hiding: lying would destroy the foundation of all right. This is the textbook case of truth-telling rigorism.

*Sources : On a Supposed Right to Lie from Philanthropy*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Free will 85% · Hard determinism 15%

The third antinomy sets a seamless [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md) against the demand for a free causality, and both theses seem equally provable. Kant resolves it through the [phenomenon / thing-in-itself](../glossary/thing-in-itself.md) distinction: determinism governs the human being as phenomenon, yet nothing forbids thinking him a [free](../glossary/free-will.md) cause at the noumenal level. The moral law requires it: "you ought" implies "you can", so freedom is a fact of practical reason. Hence the explicit rejection of [compatibilism](../glossary/compatibilism.md) (a “wretched subterfuge”); but since freedom stays noumenal, never observed in the world, the score stops short of the extreme.

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Third Antinomy · Critique of Practical Reason*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 70% · Atheism 30%

Since all knowledge is bounded to phenomena, God, who is not one, escapes proof: Kant dismantles the proofs of God's existence one by one (ontological, cosmological, physico-theological), the first failing in its leap from concept to existence. Yet what is barred to knowledge is required by morality: without God and immortality, the accord of virtue and happiness (the **highest good**) would stay unthinkable. God thus returns as a postulate of practical reason, an object of rational faith and not of knowledge, which warrants a moderate rather than an assertive theism.

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, "Transcendental Ideal" · Critique of Practical Reason, Dialectic*

### [Beauty](../axes/beauty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Objective beauty 50% · Subjective beauty 50%

The **judgment of taste** is subjective (it rests on no concept of the object) yet legitimately claims universality. This is exactly the axis's named median position, subjective universality.

> “The beautiful is that which pleases universally without a concept.” (Critique of the Power of Judgment, §9)

*Sources : Critique of the Power of Judgment, §6-§9*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Cosmopolitanism 90% · Communitarianism and patriotism 10%

Kant defends **cosmopolitan right** and the idea of a federation of free states as the horizon of history: every human being has a right of universal hospitality as a **citizen of the earth**. The nation state remains a necessary mediation, which qualifies the score.

*Sources : Perpetual Peace · Idea for a Universal History with a Cosmopolitan Purpose*

### [The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Retributivism 80% · Deterrence 10% · Rehabilitation 10%

Punishment is demanded by justice itself, as **retribution** proportionate to the crime (juridical law of retaliation), never as a mere means of social utility: punishing someone to deter others would treat him as a means. This is classical retributivism.

*Sources : Metaphysics of Morals, Doctrine of Right, §49 E*

### [Can a machine think](../axes/machine-mind.md)

*positioned · extrapolated · minor*

Biological naturalism 75% · Functionalism 25%

A modern question, foreign to the corpus: one may nevertheless conjecture that Kant would refuse to ascribe thought to pure mechanism, since thinking requires the **spontaneity of apperception**, and he opposes the self-organizing organism to the watch, a mere machine. A speculative reading, to be presented as such.

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Transcendental Deduction (B131-B132) · Critique of the Power of Judgment, §65*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Idealism 70% · Dualism 30%

If the mind conformed to objects, we could never know anything of them a priori; reverse the hypothesis, as Copernicus set the observer at the centre, and let objects conform to our cognition. Then space, time and the categories are not features of things but the forms of the subject under which alone an object can appear to us. The consequence is transcendental idealism: we know only phenomena, never the [thing in itself](../glossary/thing-in-itself.md). This boundary saves science (its laws hold necessarily for every phenomenon) without collapsing into Berkeley's idealism, since the thing in itself remains; the phenomenon / noumenon dualism thus kept rules out the fully idealist pole.

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Transcendental Aesthetic · Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics, §13*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Substantialism 40% · Psychological continuity 30% · Narrativism 30%

The "I think" of transcendental apperception accompanies all my representations as the formal unity of the subject, yet Kant refuses, against the **paralogisms**, to infer from it a known soul-substance: the noumenal self is postulated without being an object of knowledge. The weighting thus blends a regulative substantialism with the continuity of unified consciousness, the bundle pole being excluded.

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Transcendental Deduction (B131-B135) · Critique of Pure Reason, Paralogisms of Pure Reason*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mechanism 75% · Finalism 25%

Causality is an **a priori category** of the understanding that structures every phenomenon by mechanical law: known nature is a [deterministic](../glossary/determinism.md) chain of cause and effect. The third Critique adds **purposiveness**, but Kant declares it merely regulative, a principle for judging living things rather than a mechanism of the world, hence a minority finalist weight.

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Second Analogy of Experience · Critique of the Power of Judgment, §§61-68*

### [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Directional meaning 85% · Contingency 15%

Kant reads history as the unfolding of a **plan of nature**: through the "unsocial sociability" of human beings, the species advances toward a perfect civil constitution and a peaceful federation of peoples. This is a directional history, conceived as a regulative idea rather than as sheer contingency.

*Sources : Idea for a Universal History with a Cosmopolitan Purpose*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Progressivism 85% · Conservatism 15%

**Enlightenment** is the human being's emergence from his self-incurred immaturity. Kant makes the **free public use of reason** the engine of a gradual progress of humanity, placing him clearly on the progressist side.

> “Sapere aude! Have courage to use your own understanding: this is the motto of the Enlightenment.” (An Answer to the Question: What Is Enlightenment?)

*Sources : An Answer to the Question: What Is Enlightenment?*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The principle of hope 85% · Lucidity without hope 15%

"What may I hope?" is one of the three cardinal questions of philosophy. The **postulates of practical reason**, freedom, immortality of the soul and God, ground a rational hope that the **highest good**, the accord of virtue and happiness, is possible: an avowed principle of hope.

> “Two things fill the mind with ever new and increasing admiration: the starry heavens above me and the moral law within me.” (Critique of Practical Reason, Conclusion)

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Canon of Pure Reason · Critique of Practical Reason, Dialectic*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Impartialism 93% · Partialism 8%

A maxim has moral worth only if I can will it to become a **universal law** holding for every rational being, with no exception in my favour or for those close to me. The [categorical imperative](../glossary/categorical-imperative.md) excludes by construction any privilege granted to oneself or one's own, hence an almost pure impartialism.

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, section II*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Legalism 90% · Equity-phronesis 10%

Duty imposes itself as a **strict law** that reason gives to itself, and Kant draws from it a famous **rigorism**: the prohibition of lying admits no exception, even to save a life. The slightest yielding to circumstances would ruin the unconditionality of the **moral law**, placing Kant firmly on the legalist side.

*Sources : On a Supposed Right to Lie from Philanthropy · Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, section II*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Contractualism 85% · Organic community 15%

The civil condition rests on an **original contract**, not as a historical fact but as an idea of reason: the criterion of a just law is that it could have received the assent of the whole people. The legitimacy of power is grounded on this rational agreement, an avowed contractualism.

*Sources : On the Common Saying: That May Be Correct in Theory, but It Is of No Use in Practice · Metaphysics of Morals, Doctrine of Right*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 80% · Eudaimonism 20%

Nothing is good without limitation except a **good will**: morality, not happiness, is the supreme good and the condition of all worth. The complete highest good does include happiness, but only proportioned to virtue and as crowning it, hence a minority [eudaimonist](../glossary/eudaimonism.md) weight and the exclusion of [hedonism](../glossary/hedonism.md).

> “Nothing in the world, indeed nothing even beyond the world, can possibly be conceived which could be called good without qualification except a good will.” (Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, section I)

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, section I · Critique of Practical Reason, Dialectic, II*

### [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Maximalism 80% · Minimalism 20%

The moral demand is high: always act from duty and not from mere conformity, subjecting all one's maxims to the universal law. Kant tempers this by distinguishing **perfect duties**, strict and without exception, from **imperfect duties** such as beneficence or the cultivation of one's talents, which leave latitude to judgment, holding back from an extreme maximalism.

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, section II · Metaphysics of Morals, Doctrine of Virtue*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Altruism 80% · Ethical egoism 20%

An action has moral worth only when done from duty and not from self-interested inclination: **respect for the law** takes precedence over all self-love. Kant does not, however, preach pure self-abnegation, since each has an **indirect duty** toward his own happiness, placing the score on the altruist side without absolutising it.

*Sources : Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals, section I*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Harmony 55% · Separate domains 30% · Fideism 15%

Theoretical reason can neither prove nor refute God, but practical reason postulates a **rational faith** attuned to morality. Faith and knowledge thus occupy distinct domains while harmonising under the primacy of the practical, with a fideist nuance since faith remains a need of reason rather than a matter of proof.

> “I had to deny knowledge in order to make room for faith.” (Critique of Pure Reason, Preface to the Second Edition (B XXX))

*Sources : Critique of Pure Reason, Preface to the Second Edition (B XXX) · Religion within the Bounds of Bare Reason*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Authority 80% · Anarchism 20%

Kant asserts a duty to obey the established power and denies any **right of resistance** or revolution, even against an unjust sovereign, since overturning the constitution would ruin the rule of law itself. The citizen retains the freedom to criticise publicly in writing, but must obey.

> “Argue as much as you will and about what you will, but obey!” (An Answer to the Question: What Is Enlightenment?)

*Sources : Metaphysics of Morals, Doctrine of Right, §49 · An Answer to the Question: What Is Enlightenment?*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological pessimism 70% · Anthropological optimism 30%

Kant maintains a [radical evil](../glossary/radical-evil.md): there is in human nature a propensity to subordinate the moral law to self-love, and "unsocial sociability" mixes in each a tendency to oppose others. This diagnosis, tempered by the predisposition to good and by perfectibility, leans toward a moderate anthropological pessimism.

*Sources : Religion within the Bounds of Bare Reason, Part One · Idea for a Universal History with a Cosmopolitan Purpose, Fourth Proposition*

### [Nature of rights](../axes/rights-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Natural rights 85% · Political constructivism 15%

Right is derived a priori from reason, not from utility nor from positive law alone: every human being possesses, by virtue of their humanity, an innate right to freedom, grounded in the dignity of the person as an end in itself. A rational version of natural rights.

*Sources : The Metaphysics of Morals, Doctrine of Right*

## Influenced by

- **[George Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md)** (reacts against) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) defines his transcendental idealism by demarcating it from what he calls [Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md)'s dogmatic idealism, which denies mind-independent matter. The Refutation of Idealism in the *[Critique of Pure Reason](../works/critique-of-pure-reason-kant.md)* primarily targets [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s problematic idealism; the opposition to Berkeley, however, is explicit and central to Kant's self-positioning.
- **[Empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md)** (synthesizes) : Against dogmatism, [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) retains from [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) that all knowledge begins with experience; his Critique synthesises the two traditions: without intuition concepts are empty, without concepts intuitions are blind.
- **[The Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md)** (synthesizes) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) brings the Enlightenment to its philosophical completion: he overcomes the quarrel of [empiricism and rationalism](../axes/knowledge-source.md) in his criticism, and makes the autonomy of reason the heart of his morality and of his very definition of Enlightenment, « man's emergence from his immaturity ».
- **[Hume](../philosophers/hume.md)** (reacts against) : [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) draws a skeptical conclusion from empiricism: no impression delivers the necessary connection between a cause and its effect, only habit does. [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) says this woke him from his “dogmatic slumber”, and he builds critical philosophy against that skepticism, lodging causality in the a priori conditions of experience rather than in things themselves.
- **[Rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md)** (synthesizes) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) takes from [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md) the demand for a priori knowledge, universal and necessary, but confines it to the conditions of possible experience: pure concepts know nothing beyond appearances.
- **[Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md)** (continues) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) acknowledges owing to [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) that he learned to honour mankind: he takes up the dignity of the person and moral autonomy, shifting the centre of philosophy from knowledge toward practical freedom.

## Influence on

- **[Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md)** (reinterprets) : [Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md) transforms [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)'s criticism into absolute idealism: he contests the fixity of the Kantian oppositions, appearance and thing-in-itself, by absorbing them into the dialectical movement of spirit.
- **[Charles Sanders Peirce](../philosophers/peirce.md)** (reinterprets) : [Peirce](../philosophers/peirce.md) reads [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) closely and works through him at length: he recasts the table of categories and the architectonic of the first *[Critique](../works/critique-of-pure-reason-kant.md)* into his own triad (Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness) and his realism about universals. Kant remains the philosopher from whom he thinks, even where he corrects him.
- **[Rawls](../philosophers/rawls.md)** (continues) : [Rawls](../philosophers/rawls.md) claims a political revival of [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)'s moral constructivism: the original position and the veil of ignorance recast the Kantian demand to treat each person as an end and to ground universalisable principles.
- **[Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md)** (reinterprets) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)'s third Critique arms Romanticism: his theory of [genius](../glossary/genie.md), to whom « nature gives the rule to art », his analysis of the [sublime](../glossary/sublime.md) and his productive imagination become the bedrock of Romantic aesthetics. Romanticism also extends the momentum of post-Kantian idealism, while overstepping the limit Kant set to reason: it claims to reach, through art and intuition, the very absolute Kant declared unknowable.
- **[Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md)** (reinterprets) : [Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md) adopts the Kantian division between appearance and thing-in-itself, but identifies the latter with the will: a blind, endless striving of which the world of representations is only the manifestation.
