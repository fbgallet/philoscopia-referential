# Nāgārjuna

*150 – 250 · Indian · [Buddhism](../movements/buddhism.md)*

Indian thinker of Mahāyāna [Buddhism](../movements/buddhism.md), active around 150-250 CE (dates uncertain), regarded as the founder of the [Madhyamaka (the Middle Way)](../glossary/the-middle-way.md) school, one of the two great philosophical currents of the Mahāyāna. His masterwork, the Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way (Mūlamadhyamakakārikā), mounts a tight, chapter-by-chapter critique of the categories that the earlier Buddhist schools, the Abhidharma above all, held to be the ultimate constituents of the real. Where the [Buddha](../philosophers/buddha.md) aimed chiefly at release from [dis-ease (duḥkha)](../glossary/dukkha.md), Nāgārjuna draws out its logical and metaphysical consequences, at the risk of seeming to dissolve everything, hence the skeptical objection answered in the Vigrahavyāvartanī (The Dispeller of Disputes). Commented on by Candrakīrti and Bhāviveka, he became a central reference for Tibetan Buddhism, where Madhyamaka crowns the philosophical curriculum, and for East Asian Buddhism, whose Sanlun (the "Three Treatises") school carried his thought into China.

## Core positions

1. [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md) : **Nothing has an [inherent existence (svabhāva)](../glossary/svabhava.md): everything is [empty (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md), and emptiness itself is no hidden essence but is empty in turn.**
2. [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : **To be empty and to arise dependently are one and the same: [dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda)](../glossary/pratityasamutpada.md) stands in the place of any essence.**
3. [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md) : **There are [two truths](../glossary/two-truths.md): without conventional language the ultimate emptiness cannot be taught, and whoever confounds the two registers mistakes both.**
4. [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md) : **Between saṃsāra and [nirvāṇa](../glossary/nirvana.md) there is no difference at all: liberation is no elsewhere, but the right seeing of this conditioned world.**

## Positions

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Ethics of care 50% · Virtue ethics 40% · Consequentialism 10%

Nagarjuna's Mahayana places at the summit the ideal of the bodhisattva, who renounces his own extinction until all beings are delivered: universal compassion, inseparable from the wisdom of emptiness, becomes the spring of the whole of ethics. A boundless concern for others, borne by virtue, prevails over any rule or calculation.

*Sources : Precious Garland (Ratnāvalī) · Shantideva, Bodhicaryāvatāra*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Preparing for the passage 40% · Freeing ourselves from the fear 30% · Living in view of one's death 30%

Nagarjuna takes up the Buddhist view of death as a passage within the cycle of rebirths, but reads it in the light of emptiness: birth and death are themselves empty of inherent nature. Death is therefore neither an absolute terminus nor the survival of a soul, but a conditioned transition, one stage among others, until wisdom undoes the cycle. A balanced position, without the pole of refusal.

*Sources : Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way (Mūlamadhyamakakārikā), XXV*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 70% · Eudaimonism 25% · Hedonism 5%

The highest good remains, for Nagarjuna, liberation (nirvana), but understood in the light of emptiness: to see that samsara and nirvana are equally empty is to cease grasping and to be freed. The bodhisattva path adds to this compassion for all beings. A spiritual perfection through wisdom, beyond pleasure as much as beyond mere worldly virtue.

*Sources : Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way (Mūlamadhyamakakārikā), XXV · Precious Garland (Ratnāvalī)*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*not applicable*

The antinomy of free will and determinism presupposes agents and causes endowed with inherent existence (svabhava), which Madhyamaka holds to be empty. Conventionally, karma and action operate; ultimately, neither an autonomous will nor a rigid causal chain is real. To settle on a pole would reify what the doctrine of the two truths dissolves.

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*not applicable*

Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka does not sit on the empiricism / rationalism axis: it calls into question the means of knowledge (pramāṇa) themselves, denying that any, perception or reasoning, is self-established. Conventional knowledge rests on ordinary usage and experience, but ultimate truth proceeds from a dialectical analysis that dissolves every positive thesis, epistemology included. Forcing a single source would betray the doctrine of the two truths.

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Existentialism 80% · Essentialism 20%

The argument is a reductio on the very notion of essence: an [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md) (svabhāva, the essence by which a thing would be of itself, independently of everything else) would, by definition, have to be unproduced and unchanging; yet everything we meet arises from causes and conditions, and so changes. An essence that came to be would be fabricated, which contradicts its concept. Nāgārjuna thus turns the Abhidharma's own analysis against it: the substantialist schools resolved things into ultimate factors (dharmas) endowed with inherent existence; he shows that these factors, being themselves conditioned, are in turn [empty (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md). It must be distinguished from existentialism: the thing is empty of essence not so that it may freely create itself, but because the notion of a fixed essence is incoherent.

> “It is not tenable that inherent existence should arise from causes and conditions: an inherent existence arisen from causes and conditions would be something fabricated.”, Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, XV, 1-2

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. XV (the examination of svabhāva) · Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, ch. XXIV (the examination of the Noble Truths)*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Bundle theory 100%

Classical Buddhist analysis dissolved the person into aggregates (body, sensations, perceptions, formations, consciousness), but treated those aggregates as the ultimate elements of the real. Nāgārjuna objects that the critique stopped too soon: applied to the relation of fire and fuel, his examination shows that the subject and its constituents are neither identical nor separate, and so lack [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md). [Not-self (anattā)](../glossary/anatta.md) then holds of the aggregates themselves, in turn [empty (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md): the self is a pure bundle of dependent processes, with no ultimate term to bear it, bundle theory carried to its limit.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. XVIII (the examination of self and phenomena) · Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, ch. X (the examination of fire and fuel)*

At the limit, the emptiness of emptiness (śūnyatā is itself not an inherent nature) forbids reifying even the bundle into an ultimate thesis: the bundle holds at the conventional level, not as a final ontological word.

### [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*contextualist*

The method of the [tetralemma](../glossary/catuskoti.md) (catuṣkoṭi, which examines the four cases: it is, it is not, both, neither) refuses the metaphysical options rather than settling them. [Emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md) is neither materialism, nor idealism, nor dualism: positing anything as real by [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md) falls back into the refuted error. Nāgārjuna keeps to the middle, affirming no ultimate nature of the real.

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*contextualist*

In the Vigrahavyāvartanī (The Dispeller of Disputes), Nāgārjuna meets the skeptical objection: “if you have no thesis, you can refute nothing”. He owns precisely that he has no thesis of his own to establish, his [emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md) being not one more dogmatic position but the dissolving of them all. This is neither the dogmatism of a possessed truth nor the skepticism of suspended judgment, but a third way that undoes the opposition itself.

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Pragmatism 50% · Coherence 40% · Correspondence 10%

The distinction answers a formidable objection: if everything is empty, then suffering, the path, and the Buddha's teaching are empty too, and emptiness seems to wreck everything. Nāgārjuna meets it by distinguishing two registers of truth, not two worlds: at the [conventional](../glossary/two-truths.md) level, language and ordinary practices are fully valid; at the ultimate level, nothing has [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md). Far from excluding each other, they are mutually conditioning: the ultimate can only be pointed to by means of the conventional, and whoever takes emptiness for an ultimate thesis about the real “is lost, as one is by a wrongly grasped snake”. Conventional truth thus holds by its coherence of usage and its efficacy on the path, not by a correspondence to essences.

> “The Dharma taught by the buddhas rests on two truths: the conventional, worldly truth and the truth in the ultimate sense. Without relying on the conventional, the ultimate is not taught; without reaching the ultimate, nirvāṇa is not attained.”, Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, XXIV, 8-10

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), XXIV, 8-10 (the two truths)*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Mechanism 60% · Vitalism 40%

How is a thing produced? The work opens on this problem and attacks it through its four possible branches: a thing can arise neither from itself (it would already be there), nor from another endowed with [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md) (two separate essences could not condition one another), nor from both, nor without a cause. If real causation presupposes terms with inherent existence, it is incoherent. The original step is not to abolish causation but to identify it with emptiness: precisely because things arise in dependence, they have no inherent existence. [Dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda)](../glossary/pratityasamutpada.md) and [emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md) are then two names for one fact, with no agent and no design, which excludes finalism.

> “Never and nowhere does anything exist that is arisen from itself, from another, from both, or without a cause.”, Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, I, 1

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. I (the examination of conditions)*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Immanence 98% · Transcendence 3%

The argument draws out the consequence of universal emptiness: if saṃsāra (the cycle of conditioned rebirths) and [nirvāṇa (extinction)](../glossary/nirvana.md) each had an [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md), a gulf would separate them and one could never pass from the one to the other. But both are equally [empty (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md), and so lack the essential boundary that would set them in opposition. The metaphysical leap toward a beyond collapses: nirvāṇa is no other place to be reached, it is saṃsāra rightly seen, stripped of the grasping that made it suffering. Hence a radical immanence, with no backworld, which sets Madhyamaka apart from conceptions of salvation as escape out of the world.

> “There is no difference at all between saṃsāra and nirvāṇa; there is no difference at all between nirvāṇa and saṃsāra.”, Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, XXV, 19

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. XXV (the examination of nirvāṇa)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Atheism 80% · Theism 20%

An heir of [Buddhism](../movements/buddhism.md), Nāgārjuna acknowledges no creator God: his critique of any production by a cause with [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md) undercuts in advance the idea of a self-positing first principle. Liberation comes from no grace but from understanding [emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md). This is a non-theism, held without militant polemic against belief.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. I (the examination of conditions, against a self-sufficient cause)*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Extinction and detachment 90% · Reasoned selection 10%

Nāgārjuna takes up the Buddhist diagnosis: [craving (taṇhā, the grasping thirst that clings)](../glossary/tanha.md) and appropriation feed the cycle of rebirth. But he illuminates its root: one clings because one ascribes to objects an [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md); seeing their [emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md) loosens attachment at its source. The path remains one of detachment, here grounded in metaphysical lucidity.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. XXIII (the examination of errors) · Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, ch. XXVI (the examination of the twelve links of existence)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Abolitionism 93% · Fecundity and redemption 7%

The whole project aims at the cessation of suffering and of conditioned rebirth: Nāgārjuna grants suffering no redemptive value, it being an effect of ignorance and grasping, to be dissolved by seeing [emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md). It is in grasping the absence of [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md) that the mental fabrication (prapañca, conceptual proliferation) sustaining dis-ease comes to rest: a clearly abolitionist leaning.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), XVIII, 5 (the cessation of conceptual proliferation) · Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, ch. XXVI (the examination of the twelve links)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of reason 80% · The primacy of feeling 20%

For Nāgārjuna, afflictive affects rest on an intellectual mistake: believing that things have an [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md) and clinging to it. It is therefore right understanding, the seeing of [emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md), that undoes the disturbing emotion at its root. Transformation proceeds through knowledge, which aligns with a therapeutic intellectualism, without denying the role of meditative practice.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. XXIII (the examination of errors and mental defilements)*

### [Attention and diversion](../axes/attention.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Recollection 85% · Diversion embraced 15%

Nāgārjuna inherits the Buddhist discipline of recollection: liberation presupposes a gathered mind, able to sustain the analysis until the conceptual proliferation (prapañca) is stilled. Seeing [emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md) is not a scattering but a penetrating, continuous attention turned upon the conditioned arising of phenomena, hence a clear leaning toward recollection.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), XVIII, 5 (the stilling of proliferation)*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Constructivism 93% · Scientific realism 7%

No description grasps a reality endowed with [inherent existence](../glossary/svabhava.md), since there is none: the categories and distinctions of knowledge belong to conventional truth, dependent and useful constructions, never the mirror of essences in themselves. Our statements order an [empty (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md) world rather than reflecting independent structures: a pronounced epistemic constructivism, at the conventional level.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), XXIV, 8-10 (the two truths) · Vigrahavyāvartanī (The Dispeller of Disputes), on the means of knowledge (pramāṇa)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The examined life 80% · Primacy of action 20%

The path is first a labor of analysis and vision: examining phenomena so as to loosen grasping and still the conceptual proliferation (prapañca). Transformation turns on right inner understanding rather than on action upon the outer world, even though the Mahāyāna's compassion (karuṇā) orients it toward others.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. XVIII (the examination of self and phenomena)*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 80% · Constructed meaning 10% · Absurdism 10%

Like all of [Buddhism](../movements/buddhism.md), Madhyamaka orders existence toward an assignable end: liberation from suffering through the understanding of [emptiness (śūnyatā)](../glossary/sunyata.md). This end is set not by a creator but discovered as the nature of things, hence a teleological structure, which the identity of saṃsāra and [nirvāṇa](../glossary/nirvana.md) brings back entirely into this very world.

*Sources : Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way), ch. XXV (the examination of nirvāṇa)*

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of truth 75% · Vital illusions 25%

The distinction of the [two truths](../glossary/two-truths.md) governs the edifice: conventional truth is indispensable for teaching and for reaching the ultimate, emptiness, but neither of the two may be reified. Whoever scorns the conventional loses the path, whoever stops at it loses the goal.

*Sources : Root Verses on the Middle Way (Mūlamadhyamakakārikā), XXIV*
