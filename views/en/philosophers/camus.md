# Albert Camus

*1913 – 1960 · French*

French writer, journalist and philosopher born in colonial Algeria in 1913, into a poor pied-noir family. A reporter in Algiers and then a Resistance member in Paris, where he edited the clandestine paper Combat, he approached philosophy as a novelist and playwright as much as an essayist, with *[The Stranger](../works/the-stranger-camus.md)* and *[The Myth of Sisyphus](../works/the-myth-of-sisyphus-camus.md)* (1942), then *[The Plague](../works/the-plague-camus.md)* (1947) and *[The Rebel](../works/the-rebel-camus.md)* (1951). His constant question is that of meaning and of how to act in a world that gives no answer to our demand. This last essay, with its critique of revolutions waged in the name of History, triggered his famous break with [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md) and the [existentialists](../movements/existentialism.md), whose label he always refused. Nobel laureate in literature in 1957 and killed in a car crash in 1960, he remains a major reference for a Mediterranean philosophy of measure, attentive to limits.

## Core positions

1. [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md) : **[The absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) is neither in the world nor in me, but in the divorce between my demand for meaning and the world's silence: no given meaning fills it.**
2. [Hope](../axes/hope.md) : **One must live the absurd without appeal: suicide and the leap of faith alike evade it, and every hope in an elsewhere betrays lucidity.**
3. [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md) : **I rebel, therefore we are: to say no to the intolerable reveals a value common to all men and grounds solidarity.**
4. [Ideal and reality](../axes/political-ideal.md) : **[Revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) carries its own measure: the revolution that sacrifices the living present to History disowns itself and ends in terror.**
5. [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md) : **There is no world beyond: the only sacred is here below, in the sun, the sea and the earth, and every value is taken in the [immanence](../glossary/immanence.md) of the present.**

## Positions

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue ethics 40% · Ethics of care 30% · Deontologism 20% · Consequentialism 10%

Camus's ethics is born of revolt: from the refusal of the absurd arises a value, human solidarity, and a measure. “I revolt, therefore we are”: in rising up, the rebel affirms a common dignity and limits not to be crossed, even within revolt. Neither a calculation of consequences nor an abstract law, but a virtuous fidelity to the human and a concern for others.

*Sources : The Rebel*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 75% · Hard determinism 25%

Stripped of God and of an eternal future, the absurd man no longer has a destined life to fulfill: this ruin of higher ends sets him free, not with a demonstrated metaphysical freedom, but with the lived freedom of one who expects nothing more from a beyond. His lucid revolt takes up the present and multiplies experiences, without denying that everything around him has its causes: freedom is a way of living the absurd, not a thesis about the will.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus, Absurd Freedom · The Rebel*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Skepticism 75% · Dogmatism 25%

The human mind demands unity, clarity, ultimate certainties; the world answers with an unreasonable silence. From this divorce springs [the absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md): the absolute truths reason craves lie beyond its reach, and any thought that promises them, religious or systematic, performs a **leap** away from the evidence. Yet Camus guards against wholesale [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md), which refutes itself in asserting itself: at least two things remain certain, the absurd itself and the bare fact of existing. Lucidity holds to this middle ground, refusing both metaphysical hope and the surrender of knowing.

> “I do not know whether this world has a meaning that transcends it. But I know that I do not know that meaning and that it is impossible for me just now to know it.”, The Myth of Sisyphus

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus, An Absurd Reasoning*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Absurdism 80% · Constructed meaning 20%

Two terms are equally certain for Camus: the human mind demands unity and meaning, and the world stays silent before that demand. [The absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) lodges in neither term but in their **divorce**, in the confrontation itself: it is therefore neither a property of the world nor a defect in man, but a relation that cannot be dissolved without suppressing one of its terms. So meaning is neither given in advance nor purely absent: no transcendent meaning fills the divorce, yet the man who holds it without fleeing forges values in the present, hence a share of constructed meaning.

> “One must imagine Sisyphus happy.”, The Myth of Sisyphus (1942)

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942) · The Rebel (1951)*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Tragic thought 90% · Metaphysical optimism 10%

The world has neither providence nor hidden harmony: this is a tragic outlook, in which man faces an indifferent universe that does not answer his demand. Camus nonetheless keeps this lucidity clear of despair, which makes it an accepted tragic stance rather than a nihilism.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942) · Nuptials (1939)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Atheism 90% · Theism 10%

Camus resolutely thinks a world without God: [the absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) presupposes precisely that no [transcendence](../glossary/transcendence.md) guarantees meaning. His atheism nevertheless stays free of militant [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md), attentive to the scandal of evil more than to any metaphysical proof, hence a strong but not absolute score.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942) · The Plague (1947), Paneloux's sermon*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Immanence 100%

Deprived of any world beyond, man has only this world, and it is enough: Camus refuses to depreciate the sensible for the sake of an elsewhere that does not exist. Against a long tradition that makes the earth a place of exile and defers true life to the beyond, he sets a pagan fidelity to the tangible present: the warmth of the sun, the salt of the sea, the stones of the ruins of Tipasa are the only truth on offer, and the only sacred is that one. This rootedness in Mediterranean [immanence](../glossary/immanence.md) is not a mere metaphysical thesis about the absence of God but an orientation of value: every measure of the good is taken here below, in the happiness of existing, not in any [transcendence](../glossary/transcendence.md).

*Sources : Nuptials (1939) · Summer (1954)*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Lucidity without hope 90% · The principle of hope 10%

If [the absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) arises from the confrontation between man and the world, keeping it alive requires holding both terms in view: anything that removes one is an evasion. Suicide abolishes the consciousness that demands; hope, religious or ideological, abolishes the silence by lending it a hidden meaning. Camus therefore ranks the faith of Kierkegaard or Shestov with philosophical suicide: to leap toward a consoling [beyond](../glossary/transcendence.md) is to betray the very lucidity through which the absurd was recognized. The only consistent stance is to live without appeal, expecting nothing from any elsewhere, which is not despair but the refusal of a deceptive hope.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942)*

### [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Revolt 90% · Amor fati 10%

The slave who says no to his master refuses more than an order: by drawing the line he will no longer let be crossed, he asserts that something in him is worth defending, and worth it for all. Camus draws from this gesture a decisive argument: the rebel's no reveals a value exceeding the individual, so that [revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) carries us from the solitary acknowledgment of the absurd to a solidarity, as the “cogito” carried Descartes from doubt to certainty. The refusal of what humiliates, not consent to the given, thus becomes the principle of a worthy existence, and the immanent ground of a morality without God.

> “I rebel—therefore we exist.”, The Rebel (1951)

*Sources : The Rebel (1951)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Abolitionism 95% · Fecundity and redemption 5%

The agony of an innocent child is the limit case that ruins every **theodicy**: no higher order, no final harmony can offset such suffering without becoming complicity with the torturer. Camus dramatizes the argument in *[The Plague](../works/the-plague-camus.md)* by opposing two answers: Paneloux's sermon, which urges loving what one cannot understand and accepting suffering as a willed trial, and Rieux's refusal, who declines to love a creation in which children are tortured and chooses instead to heal. Suffering therefore has no redemptive fecundity: the only fitting stance is to fight it, which makes the scandal of evil not a mystery to venerate but a call to [revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) and to care.

*Sources : The Plague (1947)*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Altruism 85% · Ethical egoism 15%

Because the value disclosed in [revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) is never that of one man alone but the one he shares with all the humiliated, the good of others ceases to be external to my own good: standing in solidarity is no sacrifice of my interest but the consequence of what revolt has taught me about myself. Thus Rieux can heal the plague-stricken without hoping for any salvation, out of “plain decency”: not by calculation, nor by abstract duty, but by faithfulness to this common value. Camus thereby grounds an altruism without God and without transcendence, anchored in the sole experience of the shared condition.

*Sources : The Rebel (1951) · The Plague (1947)*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Living in the present 80% · Project 10% · Memory and transmission 10%

Deprived of transcendent meaning, absurd man bets on the quantity of experiences rather than their quality or any future project: to exhaust the present, to multiply lived moments. This is the **presentism** exalted through Don Juan and the actor in *[The Myth of Sisyphus](../works/the-myth-of-sisyphus-camus.md)*.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942), "Don Juanism"*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Hedonism 50% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 30% · Eudaimonism 20%

Camus cultivates a sensual, carnal relation to the world, the happiness of flesh, sun and sea, close to a pagan [hedonism](../glossary/hedonism.md) affirmed in *[Nuptials](../works/nuptials-camus.md)*. But this happiness comes with a demand for lucidity and dignity, hence a measure of moral perfectionism that keeps it from being reduced to pleasure alone.

*Sources : Nuptials (1939) · Summer (1954)*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Existentialism 80% · Essentialism 20%

Lacking a divine order or a given nature, man is defined by what he makes of the absurd: he has no prior essence and constitutes himself in [revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) and action. This nearness to existential thought stays tempered by Camus's refusal of an absolutely founding freedom, unlike the existentialists.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942) · The Rebel (1951)*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Universalism 60% · Constructivism 30% · Relativism 10%

[Revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) posits a near-universal limit, a "no" that every man opposes to humiliation and murder, which brings Camus close to a de facto universalism. Yet this value is not deduced from a nature or an absolute: it emerges from the shared experience of revolt, hence a constructive share.

*Sources : The Rebel (1951)*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Refusing death 65% · Freeing ourselves from the fear 20% · Living in view of one's death 15%

Death is the scandal par excellence: the horizon that reveals [the absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) and ruins all hope, but which Camus refuses to greet with serenity. To resignation as much as to the hope of a beyond, he opposes revolt: to die unreconciled, lucid and in revolt, with no appeal and no consolation. It is in this obstinate refusal, not in acceptance, that the absurd man asserts his greatness.

> “What matters is to die unreconciled and not of one's own free will.”, The Myth of Sisyphus, 'Absurd Freedom'

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus · The Rebel*

### [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Contingency 85% · Directional meaning 15%

In *[The Rebel](../works/the-rebel-camus.md)*, Camus denounces the ideologies that sacrifice the living to a necessary meaning of History, and sets against them a [revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) faithful to the present and to limits. He refuses any historical providence, which places him on the side of contingency, against revolutionary messianism.

*Sources : The Rebel (1951)*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Celebration 95% · Asceticism 5%

Far from any asceticism, Camus celebrates the sensual happiness of the body, close to a [hedonism](../glossary/hedonism.md) of the present moment: the warmth of the sun, the salt of the sea, the embrace of the Algerian earth are lived as a nuptial union with the world. Carnal pleasure is no weakness to be corrected but a tangible truth, the only one offered to man with no beyond.

*Sources : Nuptials (1939) · Summer (1954)*

### [Ideal and reality](../axes/political-ideal.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Political realism 85% · Utopianism 15%

[Revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) carries its own measure within it: it arms itself with a value (the dignity owed to every man) that it cannot trample without disowning itself. From this Camus draws his political verdict: the revolution that, in the name of a radiant future or a necessary meaning of History, sacrifices the living present, turns revolt against its own principle and leads logically to state terror. Against this excess he sets the “thought at the meridian”, a politics of limits and of the possible that holds freedom and justice together without sacrificing one to the other. This critique of Soviet communism, written at the height of the Cold War, earned him his resounding break with [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md).

*Sources : The Rebel (1951)*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Conflict 80% · Separate domains 20%

The reason that has acknowledged [the absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) incurs a debt of lucidity: it cannot, without contradicting itself, turn its own failure to find meaning into proof of a hidden meaning. This is Camus's objection to the religious thinkers of existence, Kierkegaard and Shestov: they describe the absurd admirably, then deify it, erecting reason's scandal into a reason to believe. This leap out of the evidence is an abdication, not a solution. Faith and reason therefore stand here in open conflict: the former eases the anguish only by conjuring away what the latter has seen, and only a reason that consents to its own limits, without filling them by [transcendence](../glossary/transcendence.md), stays honest.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942), "Philosophical Suicide"*

### [Beauty in life](../axes/aesthetic-life.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Aestheticism 70% · Primacy of the ethical 30%

For Camus, to create is one of the highest ways of living [the absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md): the work of art, faithful to the sensible world without claiming to explain it, is a form of [revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) and beauty that justifies existence. This value granted to creation leans toward aestheticism, without erasing the ethical demand of a revolt in solidarity.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942), "Absurd Creation" · The Rebel (1951), "Rebellion and Art"*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Affirmation 80% · Reasoned selection 20%

Absurd man does not repress his desires but seeks to exhaust the possible, to multiply and intensify experiences for want of a meaning that would rank them. This affirmation of desire, close to a [hedonism](../glossary/hedonism.md) of intensity embodied by Don Juan, sets him clearly apart from detachment, even if lucidity tempers the pure pursuit of quantity.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942), "Don Juanism"*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Impartialism 80% · Partialism 20%

The solidarity born of [revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) holds for every humiliated man, with no privilege of proximity or belonging: the formula "I rebel, therefore we are" extends the value to all men. Rieux heals the plague-stricken indiscriminately, which leans toward impartialism, tempered by Camus's carnal rootedness in a beloved land.

*Sources : The Rebel (1951) · The Plague (1947)*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological pessimism 60% · Anthropological optimism 40%

Camus believes neither in man's natural goodness nor in his radical corruption: his thought of measure sets a human limit against the **excess** that, in the name of an absolute, ends by justifying murder. This sense of the human leaning toward excess keeps him from anthropological optimism, without tipping into pessimism.

*Sources : The Rebel (1951), "Thought at the Meridian"*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Primacy of action 55% · The examined life 45%

[The absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) is first uncovered in a lucid consciousness that faces its condition squarely, which presupposes an inner examination. But Camus refuses to stop there: [revolt](../glossary/revolt.md) and action, to heal, to create, to resist, are the living answer to the absurd, hence a near-median balance between the examined life and the primacy of action.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942) · The Plague (1947)*

In The Plague, before a child's agony, Rieux refuses to love a creation in which children are tortured. No harmony, no salvation justifies this suffering: a flat refusal of all theodicy, the revolt against an order that would demand it.

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The primacy of truth 90% · Vital illusions 10%

Keeping the [absurd](../glossary/the-absurd.md) alive requires rigging neither of its two terms: any consolation that lends the world a hidden meaning is an evasion, a philosophical suicide. Lucidity is not sadness but the honour of living: to see clearly and live all the same, without appeal, is the only greatness within reach.

*Sources : The Myth of Sisyphus (1942)*

## Influenced by

- **[Kierkegaard](../philosophers/kierkegaard.md)** (reacts against) : In The Myth of Sisyphus, [Camus](../philosophers/camus.md) calls [Kierkegaard](../philosophers/kierkegaard.md)'s leap of faith “philosophical suicide”: the absurd defines itself against that religious escape, as a demand for lucidity without consolation.
- **[Marxism](../movements/marxism.md)** (reacts against) : The Rebel is written against [Marxism](../movements/marxism.md) as a religion of History: [Camus](../philosophers/camus.md) refuses that the revolutionary end justify terror and sets revolt, which posits a limit, against the revolution that abolishes it.
- **[Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md)** (reinterprets) : [Camus](../philosophers/camus.md) reads [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) as a critical heir: he takes up the diagnosis of nihilism and the love of fate, but turns them into an ethics of measure, the absurd calling for revolt rather than the approval of everything.
