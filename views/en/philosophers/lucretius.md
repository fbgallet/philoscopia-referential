# Titus Lucretius Carus

*99 BCE – 55 BCE · Roman · [Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md)*

A Roman poet and philosopher of the first century BCE, of whom almost nothing is known except that, amid the crisis of the Republic, he composed the *[De rerum natura](../works/on-the-nature-of-things-lucretius.md)*, a vast didactic poem in six books that is the fullest ancient exposition of Epicureanism to have reached us. In it he renders into Latin verse the physics of [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md), inherited from the atomism of Democritus: there are only atoms and void, nature was made neither by the gods nor for men, and his avowed aim is therapeutic, to free humanity from the fear of the gods and of death that poisons life. A fervent disciple rather than an inventor of doctrine, Lucretius nonetheless gives Epicureanism a voice of its own: the dark grandeur of the materialist [sublime](../glossary/sublime.md), an uncompromising polemic against religion as a source of evils, and a natural history of humankind in book V. Nearly lost, his poem was recovered in 1417 by Poggio Bracciolini and became one of the chief sources of modern materialism and free thought, from Gassendi to Montaigne and the [Enlightenment](../glossary/lumieres.md).

## Core positions

1. [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md) : **There exist only atoms and void: nothing comes from nothing or returns to it, and all the real, down to the soul, is but matter in motion.**
2. [Death](../axes/death.md) : **[Death](../axes/death.md) is nothing to us: the soul dissolves with the body, and to fear the nothingness after death is as vain as to weep over the one before birth.**
3. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **The gods exist, at peace in the [intermundia](../glossary/intermundia.md), but govern nothing: “so much wrong could religion prompt”, and it is from religion, not the gods, that we must be freed.**
4. [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : **An atom can swerve from its fall without cause, the [clinamen](../glossary/clinamen.md): this grain of indeterminacy breaks the chain of fate and saves the “will wrested from the fates”.**
5. [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : **Nature pursues no end: no organ was made in order to serve, use comes after being, and the world, too flawed, was not made for us.**

## Positions

### [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Materialism 100%

Everything begins from two principles that Lucretius establishes before all else: nothing comes from nothing, nothing returns to nothing. Were it otherwise, anything could spring from anything, with no seed and no season; yet experience shows fixed natures and ordered cycles, so the real reduces to first bodies, the atoms, and the void that lets them move. Outside this pair nothing exists in itself: soul and mind themselves are aggregates of very fine atoms, and there is no room for any immaterial substance. This thoroughgoing materialism, inherited from Democritus and [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md), Lucretius unfolds into a complete system and makes visible, from the speck of dust dancing in a sunbeam to the infinity of worlds.

> “Nothing is ever created out of nothing by divine power.”, On the Nature of Things, I, 150

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, I, 146-264 (nothing from nothing, nothing to nothing) · On the Nature of Things, I, 418-448 (the all is body and void)*

### [Wholes and parts](../axes/reduction-emergence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Reductionism 95% · Emergentism 5%

Sensible qualities are not primary: colour, heat, taste, even life and sensation, arise from the arrangement, motion and collisions of atoms that themselves have neither colour nor heat. The whole therefore adds nothing to its parts: it is only atoms variously disposed, and it is their configuration that explains the world's variety. This bottom-up explanation, in which the higher reduces entirely to the meeting of the smallest, is a [reductionism](../axes/reduction-emergence.md) that continues the line of Democritus.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, II, 730-864 (atoms are colourless; colour arises from their arrangement) · On the Nature of Things, II, 865-990 (the sentient arises from the non-sentient)*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Mechanism 100%

Nature pursues no end and was ordered by no design: things come to be through the merely mechanical encounter of atoms over infinite time, in which every possible combination has formed and only those able to endure have lasted. Hence a famous reversal of finalism: “no bodily member was made for our use”; the order is the reverse, the organ exists first and the use comes after. To seek a purpose in the eye, the hand or the stars is therefore an illusion, and Lucretius mocks those who believe the world made for men. This is a thoroughgoing [mechanism](../axes/causality.md), where only the swerve of the [clinamen](../glossary/clinamen.md) introduces play without reintroducing any end.

> “Nothing was born in the body that we might use it: it is the thing born that creates the use.”, On the Nature of Things, IV, 833-835

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, IV, 823-857 (nothing in the body was made in order to serve) · On the Nature of Things, V, 156-234 (the world was not made for men)*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Free will 80% · Hard determinism 20%

If atoms did nothing but fall straight down through the void by their own weight, they would never meet, and necessity would chain every cause to another without end: neither the world-making collisions, nor above all that “will wrested from the fates” which every living thing feels as it surges forward, would be possible. Lucretius therefore posits the [clinamen](../glossary/clinamen.md), the slight swerve, at an undetermined time and place, by which an atom deviates from its fall, breaking the chain of [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md). His is the text that transmits the argument to us: this grain of [indeterminacy](../glossary/indeterminism.md) at the root of matter grounds, against the physicists' fate, the [free will](../glossary/free-will.md) of which we have inner evidence.

> “Whence comes this will wrested from the fates, by which we move wherever pleasure leads each on? The swerve breaks the decrees of fate.”, On the Nature of Things, II, 251-260

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, II, 216-293 (the swerve and the will wrested from the fates)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 70% · Atheism 30%

Lucretius does not deny that the gods exist: made of subtle atoms, they lead an immortal and perfectly peaceful life in the [intermundia](../glossary/intermundia.md), with no care for ours. But he draws a radical consequence: a blessed being has neither anger nor favour, so no [providence](../glossary/providence.md) governs the world, no god made it or rewards and punishes. The proof lies in the very spectacle of nature, too full of flaws to be the work of a divine power. This position is therefore not atheism, of which he was accused, but a theology without [providence](../glossary/providence.md): the gods neither create nor govern anything, and its edge is polemical: “so much wrong could religion prompt”, it is religion, not impiety, that is the true evil to be fought.

> “So much wrong could religion prompt.”, On the Nature of Things, I, 101

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, I, 62-101 (religion as source of crimes, the sacrifice of Iphigenia) · On the Nature of Things, V, 146-234 (the gods of the intermundia; the world too flawed to be divine)*

### [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Evil refutes God 85% · Unjustifiable evil 15%

Lucretius does not reason about a good and all-powerful God, but turns the argument from design on its head: if the world had been made for us by gods, it would not be so “gravely flawed”. Yet the greater part of the globe is devoured by mountains, forests and seas, the climate afflicts men, the soil resists their toil, wild beasts and diseases strike them, and the newborn is cast naked and wailing on the shore of life. So many defects prove that nature is the work of no providence: this is less a theodicy than a [proof from evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md) of the absence of divine design.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, V, 195-234 (a world so flawed cannot be the work of the gods)*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Freeing ourselves from the fear 100%

Since the soul is corporeal and is born, grows and ages with the body, it dissolves with it: all feeling then ceases, and a subject who is no more can suffer no evil. “Death is therefore nothing to us”, a conclusion Epicurus already held; but Lucretius devotes the whole of book III to it and makes of it a long consolation, multiplying proofs of the soul's mortality and the symmetry argument: we do not grieve over the nothingness before our birth, so why dread the one after our death, which resembles it in all things? To wish to survive is to contradict oneself, for the dead will have neither lack nor regret. The consolation widens into a rejoinder spoken by nature herself, who reproaches the living for their ingratitude and their thirst to endure.

> “Death, then, is nothing to us and touches us not at all, since the nature of the mind is held to be mortal.”, On the Nature of Things, III, 830-831

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, III, 417-829 (the mortal soul; death is nothing to us) · On the Nature of Things, III, 830-1094 (the symmetry argument; the diatribe of Nature)*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Bundle theory 100%

The mind (animus, the seat of thought) and the vital breath (anima, spread through the whole body) are not a simple or immortal substance but a compound of very small, very mobile atoms mingled with the flesh. Born with the body, this bundle comes apart when the assemblage breaks up: the self has no identity beyond its material composition, and there can be no transmigration. Lucretius adds an argument of his own: even were our atoms to gather again after ages, it would not be “us”, for the thread of memory would be severed. This is a bundle theory.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, III, 94-322 (animus and anima, the nature of the soul) · On the Nature of Things, III, 843-861 (were our atoms to reassemble, it would no longer be us)*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Immanence 95% · Transcendence 5%

Nothing exceeds nature: there is no world of Ideas, no overhanging providence, no beyond in which to survive. Everything, from the gods to the soul, belongs to the single universe of atoms and void. The poem turns the gaze not toward a higher order but toward the infinite depth of this world, where thought, having crossed the “flaming ramparts of the world”, ranges through immensity meeting no bound and no outside. This is a thoroughgoing [immanence](../axes/transcendence.md).

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, I, 62-79 (the mind crosses the flaming ramparts of the world) · On the Nature of Things, II, 1048-1089 (the infinity of worlds)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 95% · Rationalism 5%

All knowledge goes back to the senses, and a reason claiming to refute them would saw off the branch that bears it: for the very notions it uses are drawn from the senses, and no criterion surer than they can be set against them. Lucretius develops the Epicurean doctrine of the [simulacra](../glossary/simulacra.md), those thin films of atoms that bodies emit and that, striking the organs, produce perception; he defends at length the truth of the senses against illusions, which he charges to judgment and not to sensation. This is a radical [empiricism](../axes/knowledge-source.md), set against the doubt of the skeptics and Platonic innatism.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, IV, 26-238 (the simulacra and perception) · On the Nature of Things, IV, 469-521 (whoever refutes the senses has nothing surer to set against them)*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 80% · Skepticism 20%

Against the skeptics who suspend all assent, Lucretius holds the senses to be a secure criterion and rests the whole of physics on their testimony: whoever declares them uncertain thereby ruins the reason that contests them, and will no longer be able even to tell true from false. Sensation taken in itself never deceives; error arises only from the opinion added to it. This confidence in a firm ground of knowledge is a [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md), sensualist though it be.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, IV, 469-521 (refutation of skepticism)*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Naturalism 70% · Culturalism 30%

Book V offers a natural history of humankind with no golden age and no fall: the first men, more robust, lived scattered like beasts, without fire, language or laws, harder and rougher than we are. It was need, use and the imitation of nature that, little by little, gave rise to fire, huts, articulate speech, the arts and cities. Man therefore has no fixed nature stamped in advance: he carries a common natural ground, but his customs and skills are the slow work of experience. The weight remains on the natural continuity of the human animal, hence a naturalist leaning tempered by this cultural genesis.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, V, 925-1027 (the first men, savage and scattered) · On the Nature of Things, V, 1028-1457 (the birth of language, fire, the arts and cities)*

### [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Contingency 65% · Directional meaning 35%

The history of mankind follows no plan and no providence: it is need, the chance of discoveries and groping imitation that, with no overall direction, led from the savage to the cities. The arts progress by trial and use, but this progress is ambivalent, for it also brings war, gold and the ambitions that disturb repose. Lacking any assigned meaning or aimed-at term, history therefore belongs to [contingency](../axes/history-meaning.md), without lapsing for all that into a programmed decline.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, V, 1105-1160 (kings, property, ambition) and 1448-1457 (the slow progress of the arts)*

### [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Techno-criticism 60% · Techno-optimism 40%

The arts and inventions, from metalworking to weaving and seafaring, were born of need and experience, and Lucretius describes them without any nostalgia for the savage state. But he sees in them no march toward happiness: the discovery of gold and metals armed war, and luxury inflames the empty desires that nature does not demand. Material progress leaves untouched, indeed worsens, the trouble of soul that philosophy alone soothes, hence a clear reservation about what techniques truly bring.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, V, 1241-1457 (metals, war, luxury and the ambivalent progress of the arts)*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Contractualism 85% · Organic community 15%

Right and laws descend neither from the gods nor from a natural order: weary of the violence of private vengeance, men of their own accord consented to neighbourly pacts so as no longer to harm one another, and from that agreement justice was born. Lucretius thus gives, before the term existed, a contractual genesis of civil order, in which the just is a convention of common advantage and not a transcendent command, extending the “compact of utility” of [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md). This is a naturalist contractualism.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, V, 1011-1027 (neighbourly pacts) and 1136-1160 (the founding of law)*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Hedonism 90% · Eudaimonism 10%

The highest good is pleasure understood as peace, not as enjoyment: the end of the whole of physics is [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md), the state in which, the fears of the gods and of death dispelled, nothing more troubles the soul. Nature, says Lucretius, asks for only one thing, a body free of pain and a mind freed of care and dread. This definition of the sovereign good is taken over from [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md); Lucretius makes it the avowed aim of his poem and the famous image of the sage who, from the shore, watches the shipwrecked without peril.

> “Nature cries aloud for nothing else but that pain be kept away from the body, and that the mind enjoy pleasant sensation, free of care and fear.”, On the Nature of Things, II, 16-19

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, II, 1-61 (the proem: suave mari magno; nature asks for little)*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Reasoned selection 85% · Extinction and detachment 10% · Affirmation 5%

Disturbance comes from boundless desires that nature does not demand: the greed for wealth and honours, fanned by the fear of death, throws men into an endless race. The cure lies in knowing the limit nature sets to pleasure, little being enough to keep pain away; beyond that, empty desire only feeds anxiety. Taking up the Epicurean sorting of desires, Lucretius makes of it the spring of a biting critique of Roman ambition and luxury. This is a [reasoned selection](../axes/desire.md) of desire according to nature.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, II, 1-61 (empty terrors and measureless desires) · On the Nature of Things, III, 59-93 (greed and ambition born of the fear of death)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 93% · Fecundity and redemption 7%

Suffering has no redemptive value: it is the very evil that the whole poem seeks to disarm by tracing it back to its causes, the false opinion about the gods and about death. The great suffering, for Lucretius, is not so much bodily pain as the terror of the soul, which no ordeal sanctifies and which knowledge of nature must dissolve. The leaning is clearly abolitionist, without bodily pain itself receiving the slightest meaning.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, III, 1053-1075 (the malaise that drives men to flee)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of reason 75% · The primacy of feeling 25%

The passions that gnaw at the soul, dread of the underworld, thirst for honours, the fury of love, arise from false opinions about the nature of things: it is therefore knowledge, not exhortation, that calms them. “This terror of the mind, this darkness, can be dispelled neither by the sun nor by daylight, but only by the aspect and the law of nature.” The therapy proceeds through the light of physical knowledge, which marks a clear intellectualist leaning, attested too by the famous demystifying analysis of erotic passion in book IV.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, II, 55-61 and III, 87-93 (terror dispelled by the law of nature) · On the Nature of Things, IV, 1058-1287 (the analysis of erotic passion)*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Metaphysical optimism 75% · Tragic thought 25%

The universe infinite in atoms and worlds is neither good nor evil, nor made for us: indifferent, it offers neither a consoling providence nor a tragic hostility. Once the fears of the gods and of death are dispelled, nature suffices for happiness, the good being easy to attain. Yet this therapeutic optimism keeps company with an acute sense of the world's fragility, itself doomed to come undone, which holds back any cosmic enthusiasm: a sober optimism, without the tragic but without providence.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, II, 1090-1174 (nature without gods; the declining world)*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 65% · Absurdism 30% · Teleology and providence 5%

No god assigns life an end and nature was not made for us: meaning is therefore not inscribed in the order of things. But Lucretius does not conclude to a desperate absurd: once fear is removed, life finds its sufficiency in the stable pleasure that nature places within our reach, and the poem itself, devoted to enlightening men, bears witness to a meaning to be built here below. The absurdist share lies in the absence of cosmic finality, but the stress falls on a human meaning built by wisdom.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, V, 156-234 (the world not made for men) and III, 1-30 (the praise of Epicurus, liberator)*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Lucidity without hope 75% · The principle of hope 25%

Hope, for Lucretius, is above all the hope of a survival of the soul, and it is this that must be destroyed: as long as one awaits a beyond or fears the punishments of the underworld, one does not live the present. His therapy is a lucidity without illusion, which removes the object of hope as of fear and restores man to the peaceful finitude of this life. The leaning is clearly on the side of lucidity, happiness being won in the present and not in expectation.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, III, 978-1023 (the punishments of the underworld, allegories of present anxieties)*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Consequentialism 70% · Virtue ethics 30%

The criterion of action remains that of Epicurus: the contribution to pleasure understood as freedom from disturbance, measured by the reckoning of the good and ill each choice brings. Lucretius scarcely theorizes ethics for its own sake, but the whole of his poetic morality aims at this outcome, ataraxia, and makes of prudence enlightened by knowledge of nature the virtue that leads to it. The structure is hedonist and consequentialist, with a place for practical wisdom.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, II, 7-19 (the true good: a body free of pain, a mind free of fear)*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Communitarianism and patriotism 65% · Cosmopolitanism 35%

The pursuit of honours and power, which throws men against one another, is ranked among the empty desires born of the fear of death: better “to obey and keep quiet than to wish to rule affairs by command and to hold kingdoms”. Like the Epicureanism of which he is the herald, Lucretius turns men away from civic ambition and public life toward the security of withdrawal, which leans toward a restricted community rather than an active cosmopolitanism.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, V, 1120-1135 (better to obey in peace than to reign)*

### [Can a machine think](../axes/machine-mind.md)

The question of machine minds presupposes debates foreign to antiquity; Lucretius holds no position on this contemporary problem.

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The primacy of truth 65% · Vital illusions 35%

Knowing the causes frees us from religio and from the fear of death: that is the Epicurean core. But Lucretius adds an avowal about manner: as the physician rims the cup of wormwood with honey, he coats an austere doctrine with poetry, sweetness in the service of truth, never in its place.

*Sources : On the Nature of Things, I and IV*

## Influenced by

- **[Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md)** (transmits) : Three centuries after [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md), [Lucretius](../philosophers/lucretius.md) transmits to the Roman world, in a great poem, his atomist physics and his ethics of release from the fear of the gods and of death.

## Influence on

- **[Michel de Montaigne](../philosophers/montaigne.md)** (continues) : [Montaigne](../philosophers/montaigne.md) read and annotated *[On the Nature of Things](../works/on-the-nature-of-things-lucretius.md)*, which he laces with quotations throughout the *[Essays](../works/essays-montaigne.md)*. His uptake of [Lucretius](../philosophers/lucretius.md)'s naturalism is selective: he draws above all on death, the bodily condition and the refusal of dogmatism, rather than on a system. The reading is direct, yet the filiation stays indirect, made of literary borrowings without acknowledged mastership.
