# George Berkeley

*1685 – 1753 · Irish · [Empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md)*

Anglican bishop and Anglo-Irish philosopher, the third great name of British [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) alongside [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) and [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md). Born near Kilkenny and educated at Trinity College, Dublin, he published his decisive works while still young, the *[Principles of Human Knowledge](../works/principles-of-human-knowledge-berkeley.md)* and the *[Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous](../works/three-dialogues-berkeley.md)*, before long travels, a failed plan for a missionary college in Bermuda, a stay in Rhode Island, and finally the bishopric of Cloyne. Taking up Locke's empiricism in order to radicalise it, he drew an unexpected conclusion: if every idea comes from the senses, the idea of a matter existing outside any mind is an empty word. Hence his [immaterialism](../glossary/immaterialism.md), long caricatured as a denial of the world, which Samuel Johnson thought to refute by kicking a stone, and which [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) and then [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) had to confront at close quarters.

## Core positions

1. [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md) : **There is no matter: “to be is to be perceived”, and bodies are only ordered collections of ideas in minds.**
2. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **If things exist when no human perceives them, an infinite mind perceives them all: the world is the language by which God speaks to us.**
3. [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : **It is matter that breeds scepticism: remove that veil between us and things, and perception becomes secure knowledge again.**
4. [Universals](../axes/universals.md) : **One cannot form the idea of a triangle that is no particular triangle: [abstract general ideas](../glossary/abstract-general-ideas.md) are a fiction, and it is this fiction that forged the empty concept of matter.**
5. [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : **It is by keeping faith with experience alone, against [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) himself, that one discovers nothing exists outside minds and their ideas.**

## Positions

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Idealism 100%

Berkeley starts from a simple empiricist remark: whatever I have an idea of, I have it through the senses, hence as a perception. But to posit, behind my perceptions, a matter that would exist without being perceived is to posit the idea of a thing of which, by definition, I can have no idea: a contradiction. He thus turns [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)'s empiricism against matter itself: what we call bodies is only an ordered collection of sensible ideas, and for them to exist is to be perceived. What remains is that which perceives, the mind, the one genuine substance. Far from denying the world, he claims to save it from scepticism by removing the veil of an unknowable matter set between us and it.

> “Their being is to be perceived; nor is it possible they should have any existence out of the minds which perceive them.” (Principles of Human Knowledge, §3)

*Sources : Principles of Human Knowledge, §§3-7 · Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous, I*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 95% · Rationalism 5%

All knowledge arises from experience: for Berkeley there are only ideas received through the senses or reflected upon, and no [innate ideas](../glossary/innate-ideas.md). This is [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)'s strict empiricism, but carried further than Locke took it: Berkeley refuses even the remnant of a beyond-experience that Locke had let stand, matter as an unknown substratum. The sensible is given in full, with nothing behind it, which paradoxically leads this uncompromising empiricism to an anti-materialist metaphysics: it is by keeping faith with experience alone that one discovers there are only minds and ideas.

*Sources : Principles of Human Knowledge, Introduction and §§1-2*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 98% · Atheism 3%

Immaterialism makes God indispensable, and Berkeley turns this into a new proof of his existence. My sensible ideas do not depend on my will: I do not open my eyes without seeing what forces itself on me, in a constant order I did not make. If to exist, for things, is to be perceived, and if they persist when no human perceives them, then there must be an infinite mind that perceives them all and sustains them: God. The world becomes the language God speaks to us, his ideas imprinted on us according to regular laws. A theism, then, deeply affirmed and of a rare kind, where God is not only creator but continual perceiver, guarantor of the existence and the order of the real.

*Sources : Principles of Human Knowledge, §§25-33 and §§146-149 · Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous, II*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Berkeley takes scepticism to be the disease to cure, and immaterialism to be its remedy. Modern doubt, he says, springs entirely from matter: once one posits real things hidden behind our ideas, one can never know whether our ideas resemble them, hence the veil of perception into which the sceptic rushes. Remove that unknowable matter, and the gap vanishes: things are no more than what we perceive, immediately and with certainty. Far from the rationalist dogmatism of [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md), who grounds certainty on reason, Berkeley relocates it in sensible perception itself, made secure because there is no longer anything outside it to miss.

*Sources : Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous, Preface and III · Principles of Human Knowledge, §§86-88*

### [Universals](../axes/universals.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Nominalism 90% · Realism about universals 10%

The introduction to the *[Principles](../works/principles-of-human-knowledge-berkeley.md)* opens with a polemic against [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)'s [abstract general ideas](../glossary/abstract-general-ideas.md): one cannot, Berkeley argues, form the idea of a triangle that is neither equilateral nor scalene, nor of a colour that is no particular colour. Every idea is particular; a word becomes general not because it signifies an abstract essence but because a particular sign stands indifferently for all the individuals resembling it. This is an outright nominalism, and it is not incidental: for Berkeley it is precisely the belief in abstractions that had made it possible to forge the empty concept of matter, the idea of a thing stripped of every perceived quality.

*Sources : Principles of Human Knowledge, Introduction, §§6-20*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Substantialism 85% · Psychological continuity 10% · Bundle theory 5%

If bodies are passive, made of inert ideas, then that which perceives and wills must be of a wholly different nature: an active, simple and indivisible substance, which Berkeley calls spirit or soul. The self is therefore not one idea among ideas but that which has them and acts upon them, known not by perception but by a [notion](../glossary/notion.md) we have of our own activity. Here is a sharp break with [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), who in introspection will find only a [bundle](../glossary/personal-identity.md) of perceptions with no subject: for Berkeley, to remove the perceiving subject would be to remove that without which no idea would exist at all. The self as substance is saved at the very point where matter as substance is denied.

*Sources : Principles of Human Knowledge, §§2, 27 and 139-142 · Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous, III*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Transcendence 85% · Immanence 15%

The real does not stand on its own: both its existence and its order rest at every instant on a mind that surpasses and sustains it. God is not an immanent force diffused through nature, in the manner of [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), but a personal mind, distinct from the world, of which bodies are only the sensible discourse. The sacred, for Berkeley, thus lies on the side of what transcends perceived nature, even as God makes himself everywhere present by speaking to us ceaselessly through it.

*Sources : Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous, II · Principles of Human Knowledge, §§146-149*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Harmony 80% · Fideism 15% · Conflict 5%

A bishop as much as a philosopher, Berkeley sees no opposition between well-conducted reason and faith: his immaterialism presents itself as a rational demonstration that issues in the existence of God and defends religion against the free-thinkers. Reason, far from threatening faith, would be its natural ally once freed from matter, the mother of scepticism and irreligion. This confidence in the agreement of reason and revelation ranges him on the side of harmony, in the line of an [Aquinas](../philosophers/aquinas.md), rather than of a fideism of the leap.

*Sources : Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous, Preface · Principles of Human Knowledge, §§133-156*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Finalism 90% · Mechanism 10%

For Berkeley sensible ideas are utterly inert: an idea can produce nothing, so no body is ever a true cause. The only real causality is that of a will, and we experience it solely in the activity of a mind. The regularity of nature is therefore not a mechanical chain of impacts but the constant order by which God, the one cause, produces our ideas in us: what we take for natural causes are merely the regular signs of effects to come. Nature thus receives a finalist reading, governed by the ends of an ordering mind, against the mechanism of [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md), for whom matter acts by its motion alone.

*Sources : Principles of Human Knowledge, §§25-33 and §§60-66*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructivism 75% · Scientific realism 25%

In De Motu, Berkeley anticipates scientific [instrumentalism](../glossary/instrumentalism.md): the force, attraction and absolute space of Newton's mechanics are not hidden realities that physics would uncover but convenient terms, calculational fictions that let us predict and order phenomena. The physicist does not unveil real causes; he establishes rules of use among signs. This distrust of science's claim to describe a mind-independent structure of the world removes him from scientific realism, without amounting to a social constructivism: it is idealism that gives it its colour.

*Sources : De Motu (On Motion), §§1-6 and 35-42*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The body as instrument 75% · The lived body 25%

My own body does not escape the general rule: it is, like any body, a collection of sensible ideas that I perceive. What is me is never this perceived body but the active mind that perceives and moves it. The body thus stays on the side of the object, instrument and sensible appearance rather than subject of my experience, which places Berkeley near the body-as-instrument, even though he developed no philosophy of the lived body.

*Sources : Principles of Human Knowledge, §§135-141*

## Influenced by

- **[Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)** (reinterprets) : [Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md) accepts the [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) of [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md), for whom every idea comes from experience, but radicalises it into immaterialism: denying material substance, admitting only perceived ideas and the minds that perceive them.

## Influence on

- **[Hume](../philosophers/hume.md)** (reinterprets) : [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) inherits from [Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md) the critique of abstract ideas and the recentring of all knowledge on perceptions, which he extends from matter to the mind itself.
- **[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)** (reacts against) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) defines his transcendental idealism by demarcating it from what he calls [Berkeley](../philosophers/berkeley.md)'s dogmatic idealism, which denies mind-independent matter. The Refutation of Idealism in the *[Critique of Pure Reason](../works/critique-of-pure-reason-kant.md)* primarily targets [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s problematic idealism; the opposition to Berkeley, however, is explicit and central to Kant's self-positioning.
