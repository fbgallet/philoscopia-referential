# Marcus Aurelius

*121 – 180 · Roman · [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md)*

Roman emperor from 161 to 180 and the last of the « good emperors » of the Antonine dynasty, Marcus Aurelius was adopted by Antoninus Pius and governed an empire strained by wars on the Danube and by a great plague. A [Stoic](../movements/stoicism.md), he was decisively shaped by reading the *[Discourses](../works/discourses-epictetus.md)* of [Epictetus](../philosophers/epictetus.md), passed to him by his teacher Rusticus, and he inherits the Roman Stoa of [Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md). His *[Meditations](../works/meditations-marcus-aurelius.md)*, a notebook written in Greek over the course of his campaigns and never meant for publication, are not a treatise but a series of exhortations addressed to himself, to lead himself back each day to a life according to [reason](../glossary/logos.md). Become one of the great classics of spiritual life, they offer the rare testimony of a man of power striving, in daily exercise, to remain just and serene.

## Core positions

1. [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md) : **You need no retreat in the country or by the sea: at any hour you can withdraw into yourself, for nowhere is there a calmer retreat than one's own [soul](../glossary/hegemonikon.md).**
2. [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md) : **No one ever loses any life but the one he lives at this instant: inhabit this present alone, do its one task well, for the most lasting fame, seen from above, is only a breath.**
3. [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md) : **Whether the world is [providence](../glossary/providence.md) or a mere swirl of atoms, your duty is unchanged; but everything in me attests that a [reason](../glossary/logos.md) orders the whole, and to live well is to fall in with this nature.**
4. [Death](../axes/death.md) : **Keep death daily before your eyes: a mere transformation of nature, it holds nothing terrible, and it is death that gives the present act its worth, to be done as if it were the last.**
5. [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : **At dawn, tell yourself you will meet the ungrateful, the violent, the deceitful: they err for want of telling good from evil, they are your kin through [reason](../glossary/logos.md), and none can truly harm you, nor, therefore, anger you.**

## Positions

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The examined life 80% · Primacy of action 20%

Since disturbance comes not from things but from the judgment we pass on them, the place where calm is always to be found is no place in the world but the [ruling faculty](../glossary/hegemonikon.md), the part of oneself nothing external can reach. Marcus makes of it a citadel into which one may always withdraw, returning to one's [impressions](../glossary/phantasia.md) to set them back in order. Yet this retreat is no flight from action: the notebook of the *[Meditations](../works/meditations-marcus-aurelius.md)* is that very exercise, left at once to return to one's task as a man and an emperor.

> “Men seek retreats for themselves, in the country, by the sea, in the hills; but nowhere can a man withdraw into more quiet than into his own soul.”, Meditations, IV, 3

*Sources : Meditations, IV, 3 · Meditations, VII, 59 · Meditations, VIII, 48*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Living in the present 85% · Project 10% · Memory and transmission 5%

One can be stripped neither of the past, which is no more, nor of the future, which is not yet: what we lose in dying is only ever that indivisible point, the present instant, the same for the man who dies old and the man who dies young. From this Marcus draws the deflation of posthumous glory, since those who might remember me will die in their turn, and the concentration on the task at hand, to be carried out as if it were the last. This meditation on transience, more insistent in him than in any Stoic, summons us not to enjoy the moment but to inhabit it worthily.

> “No one loses any other life than the one he now lives, nor lives any other than the one he now loses.”, Meditations, II, 14

*Sources : Meditations, II, 14 · Meditations, III, 10 · Meditations, XII, 26*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 85% · Absurdism 10% · Constructed meaning 5%

The cosmos is a whole ordered by the [logos](../glossary/logos.md), and man, a fragment of that reason, holds his place and function within it: to live well is to fall in with it. Yet Marcus introduces an un-Stoic note, the « providence or atoms » alternative he keeps turning over: either the world is [providence](../glossary/providence.md), or it is no more than a swirl of atoms, and in either case the wise man must keep his soul upright. He always comes down on the side of rational order, but this recurring hypothesis betrays a restless lucidity that sets him apart from the doctrinal serenity of [Epictetus](../philosophers/epictetus.md).

*Sources : Meditations, IV, 4 · Meditations, IX, 28 · Meditations, XII, 14*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Freeing ourselves from the fear 60% · Living in view of one's death 35% · Preparing for the passage 5%

If everything in the cosmos arises from change and returns to it, death is but one transformation among others, the dissolution of the elements back into the whole they came from: nothing in keeping with [nature](../glossary/logos.md) can be an evil. But Marcus does not merely defuse its terror; he keeps it daily before his eyes to govern present action, so that an acute awareness of death, more than a consolation, becomes the spur to a life rightly lived. His own note is the image of the ripe fruit: to leave life as the olive falls, blessing the earth that bore it.

> “Pass out of life as a ripe olive falls, blessing the nature that produced it and giving thanks to the tree on which it grew.”, Meditations, IV, 48

*Sources : Meditations, II, 17 · Meditations, IV, 48 · Meditations, IX, 3*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The primacy of reason 93% · The primacy of feeling 8%

Like every Stoic, Marcus holds a passion to be an [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) given to a false judgment, hence a correctable error and no fatality; but he turns it above all into the instrument of an ethics of gentleness. The reasoning he urges on himself against anger is tight: the one who wrongs me is mistaken about good and evil, and no one errs willingly; he is my kinsman, born of the same [reason](../glossary/logos.md); he cannot harm me in the one thing that is mine, my [judgment](../glossary/prohairesis.md); and to resent him would add his wrong to my own. Understanding another's ignorance dissolves resentment before it forms.

> “Begin each day by telling yourself: today I shall meet the meddler, the ungrateful, the insolent; but none can bind me to what is base, nor make me angry at a kinsman.”, Meditations, II, 1

*Sources : Meditations, II, 1 · Meditations, XI, 18 · Meditations, IX, 42*

### [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

What is up to us 95% · Prometheanism 5%

Taking up the [dichotomy](../glossary/dichotomy-of-control.md) of [Epictetus](../philosophers/epictetus.md), Marcus continually brings his mind back to the one masterable domain: external things do not touch the soul, which is disturbed only by the judgment it adds to them; remove that judgment, drop the « I am harmed », and the harm itself is gone.

*Sources : Meditations, V, 19 · Meditations, VI, 52 · Meditations, VIII, 47*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 90% · Eudaimonism 10%

The only good lies in virtue, that is, in the rightness of the [ruling faculty](../glossary/hegemonikon.md); health, honours and pleasures are mere [indifferents](../glossary/adiaphora.md) that no good use turns into goods. Nothing, he repeats, equals the justice, truth and temperance of a soul.

*Sources : Meditations, II, 5 · Meditations, III, 6 · Meditations, VI, 41*

### [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Amor fati 95% · Revolt 5%

If the world is a single living being run through by one [reason](../glossary/logos.md), no event is isolated or accidental: what befalls me was, from all eternity, woven together with me into the fabric of the whole (the [sympatheia](../glossary/sympatheia.md), the cosmic sympathy of things with one another). Marcus goes beyond resignation: not to endure what happens but to love it, since to wish it otherwise would be to demand that the entire universe be otherwise. Revolt is then not so much forbidden as absurd, the part being unable to accuse the whole from which it issues: this is [love of fate](../glossary/amor-fati.md) as the highest form of consent.

> “All that is in tune with you, O Universe, is in tune with me: nothing that comes in season for you is too early or too late for me.”, Meditations, IV, 23

*Sources : Meditations, IV, 23 · Meditations, V, 8 · Meditations, X, 5*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Altruism 83% · Ethical egoism 18%

From the rational and social nature of man, Marcus deduces that my good and the community's are one: endowed with [reason](../glossary/logos.md) like every human being, I am to the city of the world what the limb is to the body, and the limb that severs itself is maimed. Stoic [appropriation](../glossary/oikeiosis.md) takes a singular turn here, for it is an emperor who reminds himself at the threshold of each day that his own task is the just act done for the common good: virtue is no solitary perfection but is turned wholly toward cooperation.

> “What is not good for the swarm is not good for the bee either.”, Meditations, VI, 54

*Sources : Meditations, V, 1 · Meditations, VI, 54 · Meditations, VII, 13*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 70% · Reasoned selection 30%

Desire fixed on what is not up to us tears the soul from its nature and gives it over to disturbance; it must be withdrawn from externals and consent given to the course of things. A reasoned [selection](../glossary/adiaphora.md) of the preferable remains, so that the point is less to extinguish all desire than to redirect it toward the one true good.

*Sources : Meditations, II, 16 · Meditations, III, 6 · Meditations, XI, 16*

### [Attention and diversion](../axes/attention.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Recollection 93% · Diversion embraced 8%

The fundamental exercise is [prosochē](../glossary/prosoche.md), the vigilant attention turned at every moment on one's judgments and impulses: to watch the [impression](../glossary/phantasia.md) as it arises is to recollect oneself upon the [ruling faculty](../glossary/hegemonikon.md) rather than be scattered along the stream of impressions.

*Sources : Meditations, III, 4 · Meditations, IV, 22 · Meditations, VII, 54*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 90% · Deontologism 10%

Moral worth lies wholly in excellence of character and rightness of intention, not in consequences nor in an external rule: a [virtue ethics](../axes/ethics-foundation.md), with a thin deontological strand in the idea of faithfully discharging the function attached to one's roles.

*Sources : Meditations, V, 1 · Meditations, III, 6 · Meditations, XI, 1*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Cosmopolitanism 90% · Communitarianism and patriotism 10%

From the [reason](../glossary/logos.md) common to all men, Marcus concludes to a single city of the world of which each is a citizen before belonging to his homeland. The formula is his own: a citizen of Rome as Antoninus, but a citizen of the world as a man, the first belonging subordinate to the second.

> “As Antoninus, my city and homeland is Rome; as a man, it is the world.”, Meditations, VI, 44

*Sources : Meditations, VI, 44 · Meditations, III, 11 · Meditations, IV, 4*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 85% · Atheism 15%

Marcus affirms providential gods identified with the immanent [reason](../glossary/logos.md) of the cosmos, a pantheist rather than personal theism. The « or else atoms » hypothesis he always keeps open does not overturn this conviction but strips it of any dogmatic claim: he believes in [providence](../glossary/providence.md) without being able to prove it.

*Sources : Meditations, II, 11 · Meditations, XII, 28 · Meditations, VI, 44*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Universalism 90% · Constructivism 10%

One law of [reason](../glossary/logos.md) runs through all rational beings: good and evil hold neither of cities nor of conventions but of the common nature. One cosmos, one law, one shared reason, he repeats, ground a realist moral universalism.

*Sources : Meditations, IV, 4 · Meditations, VII, 9 · Meditations, X, 6*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Holism 85% · Individualism 15%

Rational beings are the limbs of one body, not mere parts set side by side: the individual is fulfilled only in working for the whole, and the antisocial act cuts itself off from nature. A holist view, in which the common good is no constraint but the completion of the individual's nature.

*Sources : Meditations, VII, 13 · Meditations, II, 1 · Meditations, IX, 23*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The body as instrument 85% · The lived body 15%

Marcus demeans the body with a famous bluntness: he decomposes it into its elements, a little flesh, blood and bone, reduces food and the union of bodies to their organic processes, and sets against this perishable matter the [ruling faculty](../glossary/hegemonikon.md) alone. The body is but an [instrument](../glossary/adiaphora.md) lent to the soul, which places Marcus squarely on the body-as-instrument side, without yet despising it into a mortifying asceticism.

> “What I am is only a little flesh, a little breath, and the ruling part.”, Meditations, II, 2

*Sources : Meditations, II, 2 · Meditations, VI, 13 · Meditations, VIII, 24*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Hard determinism 55% · Free will 45%

Everything is linked by [fate](../glossary/determinism.md) and [providence](../glossary/providence.md), yet the soul keeps the power to judge and to consent: no event can compel the [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) of the [ruling faculty](../glossary/hegemonikon.md). This is Stoic [compatibilism](../glossary/compatibilism.md), cosmic necessity leaving inner freedom intact; the position leans only slightly toward determinism, since the web of causes enfolds all the rest.

*Sources : Meditations, VIII, 47 · Meditations, XII, 10 · Meditations, V, 10*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Substantialism 60% · Bundle theory 20% · Psychological continuity 10% · Narrativism 10%

Marcus identifies the true self not with the body or the breath but with the [ruling faculty](../glossary/hegemonikon.md), the intelligence that judges and governs. This rational soul, a fragment of the divine, makes him chiefly a substantialist about the self; yet his insistence on perpetual flux, where the body is a river and memory a vapour, blends in a note of impermanence close to the bundle conception.

*Sources : Meditations, II, 2 · Meditations, III, 5 · Meditations, V, 11*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 70% · Abolitionism 30%

Hardship is neither to be fled nor wept over: the soul turns into material for virtue what claimed to thwart it, and the impediment to action becomes the action itself. The stance sits between Stoic [indifference](../glossary/adiaphora.md), which counts pain as nothing, and the active fecundity of adversity, which makes it the occasion of progress.

> “What stands in the way of action advances action. What blocks the road becomes the road.”, Meditations, V, 20

*Sources : Meditations, IV, 1 · Meditations, V, 20*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Impartialism 50% · Partialism 50%

Cosmopolitanism grounds an equal regard for every rational being; but Marcus thinks this concern in circles, from kin to city to humankind, each carrying its own duties. He thus stands on the median position of concentric circles, neither pure impartialism nor avowed partiality.

*Sources : Meditations, III, 11 · Meditations, VII, 13*

### [Forgiveness](../axes/forgiveness.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Unconditionalism 65% · Conditionalism 35%

The wrongdoer acts in ignorance: one cannot resent him as a free culprit, but should instruct him, or bear with him and let it pass. This inclination to excuse, grounded in the involuntary fault, leans toward a generous remission, without reaching a doctrine of unconditional forgiveness.

*Sources : Meditations, II, 1 · Meditations, IX, 42 · Meditations, XI, 18*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Lucidity without hope 80% · The principle of hope 20%

Hope and fear go hand in hand, both turned toward a future not up to us: Marcus prescribes himself to expect nothing of it and to hold to the consented present. Let nothing in the future trouble you, he writes, for you will meet it, if you must, with the same reason as today: a serene lucidity rather than a principle of hope.

*Sources : Meditations, VII, 8 · Meditations, XII, 1*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Asceticism 78% · Celebration 22%

To break the objects of desire into their parts dissolves their prestige: this dish is only an animal's carcass, this wine grape juice, the union of bodies a mere rubbing. Pleasure being an [indifferent](../glossary/adiaphora.md), one should use it soberly and without enslavement to it, a measured asceticism rather than mortification.

*Sources : Meditations, VI, 13 · Meditations, III, 2*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Metaphysical optimism 75% · Tragic thought 25%

The providential world is fundamentally good and ordered, which places Marcus on the side of metaphysical optimism: all that happens befits the whole. But his unceasing contemplation of vanity, flux and oblivion lends that order a melancholy hue which holds the position well short of a [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md)'s serene optimism.

*Sources : Meditations, II, 3 · Meditations, IV, 23 · Meditations, IV, 32*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Immanence 90% · Transcendence 10%

The divine is no beyond: [reason](../glossary/logos.md) runs through and animates the cosmos, and the human soul is a fragment broken off from it. A markedly [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md) conception of the sacred, with no world behind the world.

*Sources : Meditations, XII, 26 · Meditations, VIII, 54*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Finalism 70% · Mechanism 30%

Fate is the rational chaining of causes directed toward the good of the whole: causality is fundamentally [finalist](../axes/causality.md), providential. The recurring atoms hypothesis nonetheless introduces a share of sheer mechanism, an alternative Marcus holds to be thinkable.

*Sources : Meditations, IV, 4 · Meditations, IX, 1 · Meditations, IX, 28*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Naturalism 80% · Culturalism 20%

Man is by nature a being of [reason](../glossary/logos.md) made for society; virtue fulfils that nature, and vice is but a straying from it, not an acquired second nature. A naturalist position, in which living according to nature and living according to reason coincide.

*Sources : Meditations, VIII, 12 · Meditations, V, 1 · Meditations, IV, 29*

### [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Evil has its reasons 100%

What seems an evil has its place in the providential order or serves as a trial of virtue: nothing befalls a man that he is not, by nature, fitted to bear. A Stoic [theodicy](../axes/problem-of-evil.md), rounded out by charging moral evil to ignorance rather than malice.

*Sources : Meditations, II, 11 · Meditations, IV, 8 · Meditations, V, 18*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 78% · Skepticism 22%

As a Stoic, Marcus takes it as settled that a true knowledge of the good and of nature is possible, and in that sense he is a [dogmatist](../glossary/dogmatism.md), not a [sceptic](../glossary/skepticism.md). But his refrain that « everything is judgment » (the value we add to things) and his tone of humility temper the doctrine: he asserts a system less than he exhorts himself to see straight.

*Sources : Meditations, IV, 3 · Meditations, VIII, 47 · Meditations, XII, 8*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 80% · Existentialism 20%

Each thing has its own nature and end: man's is to live according to [reason](../glossary/logos.md), and all morality consists in fulfilling that received essence. This Stoic [essentialism](../axes/essence.md) sets a definition of man before his existence, against any idea of a freedom that would create itself without a model.

*Sources : Meditations, V, 1 · Meditations, VIII, 12 · Meditations, X, 2*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological optimism 50% · Anthropological pessimism 50%

Marcus oscillates: the *[morning meditation](../works/meditations-marcus-aurelius.md)* forecasts meddling, ungrateful and violent men, an unillusioned look at conduct; yet he holds their fault to be a correctable ignorance, not a malice of nature. This tension places him at the median point, where human nature is neither good nor evil but educable.

*Sources : Meditations, II, 1 · Meditations, IX, 42 · Meditations, XI, 18*

### [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Maximalism 85% · Minimalism 15%

The demand is unrelenting: to stop discoursing on what the good man should be, and to be one, now, without delay or excuse. This call to an effort of every instant, in which each act engages the whole of virtue, is a moral maximalism, tempered only by the [limit of what is up to us](../glossary/dichotomy-of-control.md).

> “Waste no more time arguing what a good man should be. Be one.”, Meditations, X, 16

*Sources : Meditations, V, 1 · Meditations, X, 16 · Meditations, XII, 1*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Member among others 70% · Mastery 15% · Stewardship 15%

Man is no empire within an empire but a part of the cosmic whole, akin to all the beings that issue from it: being a part, he says, I shall be discontented with nothing the whole assigns me. More than master or steward of the world, Marcus sees himself as one member among others of a single great living being.

*Sources : Meditations, X, 6 · Meditations, IX, 9 · Meditations, IV, 40*

### [The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Rehabilitation 70% · Deterrence 20% · Restorative justice 10%

Faced with the wrongdoer, Marcus's first move is to instruct him and show him his error, for he acts in ignorance of the good; failing that, to bear with him. Punishment, where he contemplates it, thus aims at correcting the strayed far more than at requiting his fault, in the spirit of rehabilitation.

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Authority 60% · Anarchism 40%

Marcus praises in his adoptive father Antoninus a measured power, respectful of the laws and of the freedom of the governed, and forms the idea of a state administered by equality and freedom of speech. Holder of supreme authority, he conceives its exercise as a regulated service rather than a domination, which inclines him toward a [constitutionalism](../axes/authority.md) that tempers authority by law.
