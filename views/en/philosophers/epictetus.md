# Epictetus

*50 – 135 · Greek · [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md)*

[Stoic](../movements/stoicism.md) philosopher of the imperial era, born a slave in Phrygia and later freed in Rome. Trained under the Stoic Musonius Rufus, he first taught in Rome, from which the emperor Domitian banished him along with the other philosophers, then founded a celebrated school at Nicopolis in Epirus. He wrote nothing: his oral teaching reached us through the notes of his pupil Arrian, who composed the *[Discourses](../works/discourses-epictetus.md)* and the *[Enchiridion](../works/enchiridion-epictetus.md)*. This ethics of the good life deeply shaped Marcus Aurelius, who names him among his teachers, and today inspires cognitive behavioral therapy.

## Core positions

1. [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md) : **First divide your concerns in two: [what is up to you](../glossary/dichotomy-of-control.md) and what is not, for all unhappiness comes from having confused the two.**
2. [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : **It is not things that disturb us but our [judgments](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) about them: change the judgment and the passion falls away.**
3. [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : **My body may be chained, never my [will](../glossary/prohairesis.md): no one, not even Zeus, can compel my [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md).**
4. [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : **Desire only what is up to you: fixed on anything else, desire dooms you to miss your aim and to suffer what you flee.**
5. [Attention and diversion](../axes/attention.md) : **To every [impression](../glossary/phantasia.md) say "let me see who you are": wisdom is an attention kept at every instant, never relaxed.**

## Positions

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 80% · Existentialism 20%

For Epictetus, the human being has a determinate nature, reason, a fragment of the divine logos, which sets him apart from the animal and assigns his proper function. To live well is not to invent oneself but to fulfill this nature: to act as a rational being, in accordance with what we are by constitution.

*Sources : Discourses, I, 6 and II, 9 · Enchiridion*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 65% · Rationalism 35%

Epictetus' whole discipline bears on impressions (phantasiai): they impose themselves on us from outside, sensory material we do not choose, but the sage's proper work is their use and the assent one grants or withholds. The source of the material is empirical; right knowledge depends on the correctness of the judgment that sorts it, which keeps Epictetus on the side of experience without abolishing the share of reason.

*Sources : Discourses, I, 27 and II, 18 · Enchiridion, 1 and 5*

### [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

What is up to us 95% · Prometheanism 5%

The root of all unhappiness, Epictetus argues, is to expect one's happiness from things that are not in our power. Hence the opening move of the *[Enchiridion](../works/enchiridion-epictetus.md)*: sorting our concerns into two classes by the single criterion of mastery, what is wholly up to us (judgments, desires, aversions) and what is not (body, goods, reputation). The [dichotomy](../glossary/dichotomy-of-control.md) is not one thesis among others but the operation that conditions everything that follows: all disturbance arises from having claimed a hold on what eludes us. Where the older Stoics dwelt on cosmic fate, Epictetus brings the division down to the concrete use, here and now, of what is properly mine.

> “Some things are within our power, while others are not.” (Enchiridion, §1)

*Sources : Enchiridion, §1 · Discourses, I.1*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Free will 50% · Hard determinism 50%

A former slave, Epictetus relocates the whole question of freedom outside chains and masters: my body may be fettered, I may be jailed or exiled, but no one, not even Zeus, can compel my [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md). Real freedom lodges in the one place nothing external can reach, the [prohairesis](../glossary/prohairesis.md), the faculty of judging and willing. Hence a sharp [compatibilist](../glossary/compatibilism.md) stance: the course of the world obeys fate, yet the use I make of my impressions stays inalienably mine. Against the common notion that measures freedom by the absence of obstacles, he holds that a man in chains can be free and a tyrant a slave to his desires.

*Sources : Discourses, I.1 · Discourses, I.17*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 80% · Eudaimonism 20%

The good resides exclusively in virtue, that is, in the right use of [impressions](../glossary/phantasia.md) and of the will: this is Stoic moral perfectionism. A [eudaimonist](../glossary/eudaimonism.md) dimension remains, since this virtue is also the path to serenity ([ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md)), but pleasure is never the criterion.

*Sources : Discourses, I.29 · Enchiridion, §8*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 80% · Reasoned selection 20%

Desire and aversion aim at attaining or avoiding: directed at what is not up to us, they doom us to miss our aim and to suffer what we flee, hence to unhappiness. The first of the three disciplines, that of desire, is to withdraw them from everything external so as to desire only what is in our power, until progress makes desire safe. For the beginner Epictetus goes so far as to recommend suspending desire altogether and using only aversion, and that solely toward what depends on us. A reasoned sorting of the preferred [indifferents](../glossary/adiaphora.md) remains in the background, but the lived tendency is detachment from all that fortune can take back.

> “Do not ask that things happen as you wish, but wish them to happen as they do, and your life will go well.” (Enchiridion, §8)

*Sources : Enchiridion, §2 · Enchiridion, §8*

### [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Amor fati 95% · Revolt 5%

Loving acceptance of what happens is a central prescription: to will that events be as they are, not as we would have them. This is the Stoic matrix of [amor fati](../glossary/amor-fati.md).

*Sources : Enchiridion, §8 · Discourses, II.14*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Intellectualism 90% · Sentimentalism 10%

Every passion contains a value judgment about some external thing, of the form "this is bad": it is that judgment, not the event, that constitutes the disturbance. The decisive step is to charge the passion to opinion rather than to the thing: if an emotion rests on a judgment, and a judgment can be revised, then no passion is suffered as a fate, all are correctable by reason. Epictetus draws an unusual ethical corollary: never to blame others or circumstances for our distress, but the use we make of our [impressions](../glossary/phantasia.md). Anger, grief and fear are not alien forces to be subdued but hasty assents to be withdrawn.

> “Men are disturbed not by things, but by the opinions about things.” (Enchiridion, §5)

*Sources : Enchiridion, §5*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 80% · Constructed meaning 20%

The world is ordered by a rational [providence](../glossary/providence.md) and every being has an assigned role in it, which reason allows us to recognize and fulfill. A constructive part remains, since it falls to each of us to play the received role well, like an actor in a play.

*Sources : Discourses, I.16 · Enchiridion, §17*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 90% · Atheism 10%

Epictetus constantly affirms the existence of a providential god, the immanent reason of the world, whom he celebrates in a genuine hymn of gratitude. This theism is pantheistic and cosmic rather than personal, which holds the score back from the extreme.

*Sources : Discourses, I.16 · Discourses, I.6*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Cosmopolitanism 85% · Communitarianism and patriotism 15%

Taking up Cynic and Stoic cosmopolitanism, Epictetus invites us to call ourselves citizens of the world and sons of Zeus rather than citizens of a particular city. Local attachments remain as roles to honor, hence a strong but not extreme position.

*Sources : Discourses, I.9 · Discourses, II.10*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 90% · Deontologism 10%

For Epictetus the only good is virtue, understood as the right use of [impressions](../glossary/phantasia.md): moral worth lies entirely in the state of the character and of the [prohairesis](../glossary/prohairesis.md), not in consequences nor in an external rule. It is a virtue ethics in the strict sense, with a thin deontological strand in the idea of faithfully discharging the duties attached to one's roles.

*Sources : Discourses, I.29 · Enchiridion, §30*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Like every Stoic, Epictetus is [dogmatic](../glossary/dogmatism.md) against Academic [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md): a criterion of truth exists, the [cognitive impression](../glossary/kataleptic-impression.md) (phantasia katalēptikē), and the [wise person](../glossary/the-sage.md) (sophos) can grant or withhold [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) advisedly. He openly mocks those who claim nothing can be asserted.

*Sources : Discourses, I.5 (Against the Academics) · Discourses, I.27*

### [Attention and diversion](../axes/attention.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Recollection 93% · Diversion embraced 8%

If passion comes from a hasty [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) granted to an [impression](../glossary/phantasia.md), then everything is decided at the moment the impression appears, not afterward. Hence the need for continuous attention to oneself, prosochē: relentlessly questioning each impression, "wait for me a little, let me see who you are", so as never to assent mechanically. This discipline of vigilance, a standing recollection upon the [ruling faculty](../glossary/hegemonikon.md), is what Epictetus puts in place of a morality of rules: not a code to apply but a scrutiny of every second, the opposite of the dispersion in which the soul follows its impressions unfiltered.

*Sources : Discourses, IV.12 (On attention) · Enchiridion, §4*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Altruism 70% · Ethical egoism 30%

The Stoic doctrine of [oikeiōsis](../glossary/oikeiosis.md) widens concern for oneself to all rational beings, making another's good a part of my own. Epictetus does not oppose self-interest and the common good: playing one's social roles well is to serve the whole, hence an altruistic position that yet involves no self-sacrifice.

*Sources : Discourses, II.10 (On our stations) · Discourses, I.19*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Impartialism 70% · Partialism 30%

Stoic cosmopolitanism grounds equal regard for every rational being, a child of the same Zeus. Yet Epictetus also stresses the particular duties attached to roles (father, son, citizen), which tempers the impartialism: an impartiality of principle lived through concrete attachments.

*Sources : Discourses, II.10 · Discourses, III.24*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Universalism 90% · Constructivism 10%

Morality rests on nature: the same [logos](../glossary/logos.md) dwells in all human beings, and to live according to nature, that is, according to reason, is a universal and non-conventional norm. This is a realist universalism, grounded in the divine law of the world.

*Sources : Discourses, I.11 · Discourses, II.16*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 80% · Abolitionism 20%

Hardship has the value of training: difficulties are the opponents [providence](../glossary/providence.md) sends us to reveal and strengthen our character, as for the athlete. Suffering, rightly used, is fruitful.

> “It is difficulties that show what men are.” (Discourses, I.24)

*Sources : Discourses, I.24 (How to struggle against difficulties) · Discourses, I.6*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Lucidity without hope 80% · The principle of hope 20%

Hope and fear both bear on the future, which is not up to us: Epictetus teaches us to desire only what is in our power and to welcome the rest, without hanging happiness on the anxious expectation of an uncertain future. This is a serene lucidity rather than a principle of hope.

*Sources : Enchiridion, §2 · Discourses, II.16*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Asceticism 80% · Celebration 20%

The body and pleasure are [indifferents](../glossary/adiaphora.md): one should use external goods with measure, attach to them only as one uses a passing inn, and defer pleasure to make sure one remains its master. A moderate asceticism, with no absolute condemnation of pleasure.

*Sources : Enchiridion, §11 and §34 · Enchiridion, §33*

### [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Truthful rigorism 85% · Benevolent lying permitted 15%

The one making progress must be of unfailing integrity, matching deeds to judgments and not adorning oneself with what one is not. This demand for inner consistency leans clearly toward a rigorism of truthfulness.

> “On no occasion call yourself a philosopher: be one.” (Enchiridion, §46)

*Sources : Enchiridion, §46 · Enchiridion, §48*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Death is nothing 70% · Immortalism 20% · Being-toward-death 10%

Death "is nothing terrible", but is the opinion about it that makes it so: it is a natural event, the return of the elements to the whole, to be kept daily before one's eyes without fear. The tone meets the Epicurean serenity toward death, with a Stoic cosmic coloring rather than any genuine personal immortality.

*Sources : Enchiridion, §5 and §21 · Discourses, III.24*
