# Jean-Paul Sartre

*1905 – 1980 · French · [Existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md), [Phenomenology](../movements/phenomenology.md)*

A French philosopher, novelist and playwright, the central figure of atheistic [existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md) and of postwar intellectual life. Steeped in the [phenomenology](../movements/phenomenology.md) of Husserl and Heidegger, he built in Being and Nothingness (1943) a philosophy of consciousness and freedom, which he extended into an ethics of commitment. Companion of [Simone de Beauvoir](../philosophers/beauvoir.md) and editor of Les Temps modernes, he embodied the engaged intellectual: the Resistance, anti-colonialism, his refusal of the Nobel Prize in 1964, then his move toward [Marxism](../movements/marxism.md).

## Core positions

1. [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md) : **In man, existence precedes essence: he first arises with no given nature, then defines himself through his acts.**
2. [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : **Man is “condemned to be free”: neither past nor situation determines him, and no circumstance excuses him.**
3. [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : **Consciousness is no thing: it is a nothingness separated from itself, which is never what it is and is what it is not.**
4. [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md) : **One can lie to oneself: [bad faith](../glossary/bad-faith.md) is treating oneself as a thing to flee one's freedom, against the demand for [authenticity](../glossary/authenticity.md).**
5. [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md) : **In choosing myself, I choose man: creating one's values with no model commits the whole of humanity.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 70% · Fecundity and redemption 30%

Sartre refuses any redemptive justification of suffering: without God or providence, no **theodicy** redeems it, and to lend it a hidden meaning would be a flight into bad faith. Suffering is a contingent fact that freedom must assume and, as far as possible, transform through action; it has only the meaning we give it, not a fruitfulness inscribed in the order of things.

*Sources : Being and Nothingness · Existentialism Is a Humanism*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Death is nothing 75% · Revolt / refusal 15% · Immortalism 10%

Sartre explicitly rejects Heidegger's being-toward-death: far from being my ownmost possibility, which would give my life meaning, death is an absurd, external fact, the sheer suppression of my possibilities. It is neither awaited nor assumed as mine, it hands me over to the gaze of others. Meaning comes from freedom in progress, never from death, which is less to be tamed than to be kept from governing life.

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, IV (My Death)*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eudaimonism 55% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 30% · Hedonism 15%

Lacking given values, the good is not received but made: it is the authentic existence that assumes its freedom and its project without taking refuge in bad faith or in a reassuring essence. The successful life is the one that lucidly realizes itself in action and engagement, more than a codified virtue or a pursued pleasure.

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism · Being and Nothingness, IV*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Affirmation 55% · Reasoned selection 30% · Extinction and detachment 15%

For Sartre, desire is not an accident to be corrected but the way the for-itself, a lack of being, throws itself toward the world and toward others: freedom is experienced in desiring engagement, to be assumed rather than fled. The analysis of Being and Nothingness nonetheless marks it with a tragic note, the desire to coincide at last with oneself being a “useless passion”.

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, IV*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 60% · Rationalism 40%

Sartre's phenomenological ontology starts from the lived experience of consciousness, anguish, nausea, the look of the other, rather than from rational deductions or pure sensory facts. Consciousness is from the outset an intentional relation to the concrete world, and it is by describing it that one knows it. But the pre-reflective cogito remains a Cartesian-type foothold, which keeps Sartre near the median, leaning slightly toward described experience.

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, Introduction · The Imaginary*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 60% · Skepticism 40%

Against all relativism, Sartre insists that there must be an absolute starting point, and that there is none other than the cogito: consciousness grasping itself, immediate and within everyone's reach. But this certainty is narrow: it yields the subject's existence, not an essence, a human nature or values, all of which are wanting, since **existence precedes essence**. Foundationalist on this single foothold yet stripped of any other given certainty, Sartre stands near the divide, on the side of assurance through the cogito, on the side of vertigo for all the rest.

> “There can be no truth other, at the starting point, than this: I think, therefore I am; that is the absolute truth of consciousness attaining itself.” (Existentialism Is a Humanism)

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism · Being and Nothingness, Introduction (the pre-reflective cogito)*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Existentialism 100%

For a manufactured object, the paper-knife, essence (the artisan's concept) precedes existence; tradition thought of man likewise, created according to a **human nature** conceived by God. Remove the creator, and the order reverses for man alone: he exists first, with no prior definition, and it is from being nothing at the outset that he draws the full responsibility of making himself.

> “Man first exists, encounters himself, surges up in the world, and defines himself afterward. If man as the existentialist conceives him is indefinable, it is because at first he is nothing. He will not be anything until later, and then he will be what he makes of himself.” (Existentialism Is a Humanism)

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism (1946) · Being and Nothingness, Introduction and Part 1 (the for-itself)*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Free will 95% · Hard determinism 5%

Freedom is not a property man has but the mode of being of consciousness: the for-itself nihilates every situation, which therefore never determines it and takes on meaning only in the light of its project. Even obstacles, even facticity (body, past, situation), are limits only for a freedom that aims at them; “condemned” man is, because he did not choose to be free and yet cannot but choose.

> “Man is condemned to be free. Condemned, because he did not create himself, yet nonetheless free, because once thrown into the world, he is responsible for everything he does.” (Existentialism Is a Humanism)

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, Part 4, ch. 1 (being and doing: freedom) · Existentialism Is a Humanism (man is condemned to be free)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Atheism 100%

Sartre is resolutely atheist: his [existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md) defines itself as the attempt to draw all the consequences of a coherent atheist position. If God does not exist, there is no divine craftsman to have conceived an **essence** of man, and so no value inscribed in an intelligible heaven: everything must be invented. The absence of God is not lamented, it is the assumed starting point.

> “Existentialism is nothing else but an attempt to draw all the consequences of a coherent atheist position.” (Existentialism Is a Humanism)

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism (atheistic existentialism) · Being and Nothingness, conclusion (the idea of God, in-itself-for-itself, is contradictory)*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Immanence 100%

With no God and no supra-sensible order, there is no backworld where values or meaning are deposited in advance: everything plays out in the immanence of the world into which man is thrown and which he must shape. Meaning and values do not descend from a beyond, they arise from the project of finite consciousness, here below. The position is therefore clearly immanentist.

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism (no a priori values, abandonment) · Being and Nothingness (transcendence as the for-itself's structure toward the world, not toward a beyond)*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Constructed meaning 85% · Absurdism 15%

No teleology presides over existence: being is first of all contingent, de trop (superfluous), without reason to be, as the nausea of the hero Roquentin reveals. Meaning is therefore neither given nor discovered but **constructed** through each person's choices and commitments, which invent their own values. A measure of absurdity remains, as the backdrop of this radical contingency, but it is not, as in [Camus](../philosophers/camus.md), the last word.

*Sources : Nausea (1938), the experience of contingency · Existentialism Is a Humanism (man invents his values)*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Narrativism 75% · Psychological continuity 25%

All consciousness is consciousness of something: it is therefore no substance, no thing-self lodged within it, but a presence to itself separated from itself by a nothingness. It is this gap, not an ego (against [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) and Husserl), that makes the for-itself “never what it is and is what it is not”: the condition of its freedom and its temporality.

> “Consciousness is a being such that in its being its being is in question insofar as this being implies a being other than itself: it is never what it is and is what it is not.” (Being and Nothingness, Introduction)

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, Part 1, ch. 1 (the for-itself is not what it is) · Being and Nothingness, Part 2 (the transcendent ego; cf. The Transcendence of the Ego)*

### [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Truthful rigorism 90% · Benevolent lying permitted 10%

[Bad faith](../glossary/bad-faith.md) poses a puzzle: to lie one must know the truth one hides, yet here deceiver and deceived are one and the same. Sartre resolves it through the non-coincidence of the for-itself: not being a thing, I can flee myself by playing at being one, like the café waiter who makes himself wholly a « café waiter ». To fail [authenticity](../glossary/authenticity.md) in this way is to refuse one's own freedom.

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, Part 1, ch. 2 (bad faith, the café waiter)*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Constructivism 80% · Relativism 20%

With no values inscribed in an intelligible heaven, man **creates** them through his choices; but Sartre refuses to conclude that this is mere arbitrariness. To choose is to hold what one chooses as good, and so to propose it as an image of man: “in choosing himself, he chooses all men”. The creation of values thus carries a near-universal responsibility, without resorting, as [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) does, to a law of reason.

> “When we say that man chooses himself, we mean that each of us chooses himself, but by that we also mean that in choosing himself he chooses all men.” (Existentialism Is a Humanism)

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism (to choose is to choose for all)*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue ethics 70% · Consequentialism 15% · Deontologism 15%

Sartre rejects any morality of a priori rules: to the young man torn between staying with his mother and joining the Resistance, no doctrine, neither Christian love nor Kantian duty, dictates the answer: no general ethics can show what is to be done. What remains is the primacy of the agent who makes himself through his acts, close to a virtue ethics refounded on freedom and authenticity, without a table of values or a reckoning of utility.

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism (the example of the young man, moral invention)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Sentimentalism 65% · Intellectualism 35%

In his Sketch for a Theory of the Emotions, Sartre takes emotion to be a meaningful conduct, not a mere undergone disturbance: it is a **magical transformation of the world** by which consciousness, unable to act really on a situation, metamorphoses it to make it bearable. Emotion thus belongs to intentionality and meaning rather than to pure intellectualizable mechanism; but since it remains a second-order conduct, degraded relative to lucid action, the sentimentalist leaning stays moderate.

*Sources : Sketch for a Theory of the Emotions (1939)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Primacy of action 70% · The examined life 30%

Consciousness is wholly turned outward: there is no inner life folded upon itself, for consciousness is intentional, always consciousness of something, and the ego itself is a transcendent object, in the world. Man reveals himself not through introspection but through his acts and his engagement. The leaning thus runs toward the primacy of action, even if reflective lucidity remains required.

> “Man is nothing other than the series of his acts, he is nothing other than his life.” (Existentialism Is a Humanism)

*Sources : The Transcendence of the Ego (1936) · Existentialism Is a Humanism (man is the sum of his acts)*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Project 80% · Presentism 15% · Memory and transmission 5%

The for-itself is stretched toward the future: it defines itself by the project, that toward which it throws itself forward, and is never fixed in its past, which it must always take up and re-signify. To live is to pro-ject oneself; the present itself has meaning only in relation to what one makes oneself be. The orientation is therefore resolutely that of the project, not of memory nor of the bare instant.

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, Part 2, ch. 2 (temporality, the ecstasis of the future)*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Tragic thought 85% · Metaphysical optimism 15%

Existence is contingent and without justification: being-in-itself is de trop (superfluous), gratuitous, and consciousness arises in a world that promises it nothing. The human condition is marked by anguish, abandonment and despair, and by the failure of every craving for the absolute (man is a useless passion). This tragic tone of finitude leans clearly toward tragic thought, without however reaching a defeatist pessimism, since freedom remains a foothold.

> “Man is a useless passion.” (Being and Nothingness, conclusion)

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, conclusion (man is a useless passion) · Nausea (contingency, being superfluous)*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Culturalism 100%

Sartre denies any **human nature**: lacking a God to conceive it, there is no common essence fixing in advance what humans are. He does distinguish a **human condition**, the set of a priori limits of existence (being in the world, laboring there, dying there among others), which is not a given nature but the common frame within which each person makes himself. The position is therefore strongly culturalist, man being what he makes himself, not what he is by nature.

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism (there is no human nature, but a human condition)*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dualism 50% · Materialism 40% · Idealism 10%

The ontology of Being and Nothingness sets two regions of being against each other: the in-itself, full, opaque, what it is, and the for-itself, consciousness, a nothingness that brings forth the world and its meanings. This is not a substance dualism in [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s manner, since the for-itself is no thinking thing but a fissure in being; nor is it a materialism, consciousness not being reducible to the in-itself. The position stands between a basis of brute being and the irreducibility of conscious upsurge, hence the shared weights.

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, Introduction (the in-itself and the for-itself)*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Altruism 70% · Ethical egoism 30%

To will one's own freedom is, by the same movement, to will that of others. The engaged Sartre, drawing ever closer to Marxism after 1945, makes solidarity and the struggle against oppression an extension of this demand. The position leans toward an altruism of responsibility, without giving up each person's grounding in his own free project.

> “I cannot take my freedom as an end unless I take that of others equally as an end.” (Existentialism Is a Humanism)

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism (willing the freedom of others) · Critique of Dialectical Reason (1960), commitment and collective praxis*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Holism 55% · Individualism 45%

The early Sartre starts from the individual freedom of consciousness, irreducible, which anchors his thought firmly on the side of the individual. But the later Sartre, of the Critique of Dialectical Reason, articulates this freedom with matter and history: the individual makes himself within collective wholes (the group-in-fusion, the practico-inert) that condition him in return. The position thus hovers near a balance, shifted slightly toward the collective by this evolution.

*Sources : Critique of Dialectical Reason (1960), the group-in-fusion and the practico-inert*

### [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Directional meaning 70% · Contingency 30%

The later Sartre attempts a synthesis of existentialism and [Marxism](../movements/marxism.md): history is not pure contingency but the dialectical process by which men make their history out of material conditions they did not choose. He nonetheless maintains the irreducibility of free praxis against any rigid historical determinism, hence a moderate directional orientation, with no guaranteed teleology.

*Sources : Search for a Method (1957) and Critique of Dialectical Reason (1960)*

### [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Alienation 60% · Fulfillment 25% · Necessity, leisure as the end 15%

Under the influence of [Marxism](../movements/marxism.md), Sartre analyzes labor in scarcity as the site where human praxis congeals into the practico-inert, turns back against those who produced it and breeds **alienation**. Yet labor remains the mode through which man transforms the world and objectifies himself in it, which leaves it a measure of possible fulfillment, beyond its dominant alienated figure.

*Sources : Critique of Dialectical Reason (1960), scarcity, the practico-inert, alienation*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Lucidity without hope 80% · The principle of hope 20%

Existentialism assumes despair in a technical sense: one reckons only on what depends on one's own will, without leaning on the hope of outside rescue, of Providence or of a guaranteed meaning to history. To act without hope is not resignation but lucidity: action is worth something in itself, not through the expectation of an assured outcome. The leaning thus runs toward a lucidity without hope, rather than toward a principle of hope.

> “Whatever the things I want, there will always be this possibility that things come about otherwise than I want: one must act without hope.” (Existentialism Is a Humanism)

*Sources : Existentialism Is a Humanism (anguish, abandonment, despair)*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anarchism 80% · Authority 20%

Sartre, an engaged and dissenting intellectual, refuses the constituted authorities that fix man in a role or deny his freedom: he declines the Nobel Prize in 1964 so as not to be institutionalized, supports anti-colonial struggles and the revolt of 1968. Without theorizing a systematic anarchism, his constant distrust of the state, of institutions and of all alienating authority leans clearly toward that pole.

*Sources : Refusal of the Nobel Prize in Literature (1964); political commitments (anti-colonialism, May 1968)*

### [Can a machine think](../axes/machine-mind.md)

*not applicable*

The question of machine minds (artificial intelligence) is not addressed by Sartre, whose analysis of consciousness as intentional for-itself predates the contemporary debate on functionalism.

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The lived body 85% · The body as instrument 15%

I do not have a body like an object: I exist my body. In Being and Nothingness, the body is the point of view I am upon the world, and the body-for-others makes me feel my objectness under the gaze. The body is lived, not an instrument.

*Sources : Being and Nothingness, Part III, "The Body"*

### [Social identity](../axes/social-identity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Social construction 85% · Social realism 15%

Existence precedes essence: no nature fixes in advance what one is. The Jew, Sartre shows, is in part made by the situation and the antisemite's gaze. Social identities are constructed, not given.

*Sources : Anti-Semite and Jew; Existentialism Is a Humanism*

## Influenced by

- **[Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md)** (reinterprets) : [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md) existentialises [Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md)'s analyses, being-in-the-world, facticity, anxiety, within a framework centred on freedom and project, which Heidegger would reject as a humanist misreading.
- **[Husserl](../philosophers/husserl.md)** (reinterprets) : [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md) takes from [Husserl](../philosophers/husserl.md) intentionality, all consciousness is consciousness of something, but radicalises it: consciousness, emptied of all substantial content, is nothing but nihilation and freedom.
- **[Kierkegaard](../philosophers/kierkegaard.md)** (continues) : [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md) takes from [Kierkegaard](../philosophers/kierkegaard.md) the themes of anxiety, choice and existential singularity, secularising them: existence precedes essence, without the leap of faith that had crowned them.

## Influence on

- **[Beauvoir](../philosophers/beauvoir.md)** (reinterprets) : In constant dialogue with [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md), [Beauvoir](../philosophers/beauvoir.md) transforms the categories of freedom and situation into an ethics of ambiguity and an analysis of gendered oppression, where existentialism had stayed abstract.
