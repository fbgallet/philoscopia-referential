# Lucius Annaeus Seneca

*4 BCE – 65 · Roman · [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md)*

A leading figure of imperial [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md), born at Córdoba around 4 BCE, Seneca was a senator, playwright and wealthy man of affairs before becoming tutor and then minister to Nero, who eventually ordered him to take his own life. Heir to the [Stoa](../movements/stoicism.md) of Zeno and Chrysippus, he shifts philosophy from theory toward the cure of the soul: his *[dialogues](../works/on-providence-seneca.md)*, and above all his *[Letters to Lucilius](../works/letters-to-lucilius-seneca.md)*, are spiritual exercises meant to transform their reader. Against the charge that he preaches wisdom from amid luxury and power, he owns the gap between the ideal sage and the one still making progress he calls himself. A model of moral prose, his thought fed the Church Fathers, the Renaissance essayists and modern neo-Stoicism.

## Core positions

1. [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md) : **Count as yours only what no fortune can take from you: your judgements and your desires depend on you, the rest was merely lent.**
2. [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md) : **Adversity is not an evil but the trial that forges virtue: the god does not spare those he loves, he tests them.**
3. [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md) : **Life is not short, we make it so: long enough for whoever knows how to use it, it slips away from whoever keeps deferring it to tomorrow.**
4. [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : **Anger is not to be moderated but forestalled: being an [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) of reason to a false judgement, it must be cut off at the threshold, not metered out.**
5. [Death](../axes/death.md) : **To rehearse dying each day is the path to freedom: whoever no longer fears death can no longer be coerced by anything.**

## Positions

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 80% · Existentialism 20%

Seneca shares the Stoic essentialism: the human being possesses a rational nature, a spark of the logos, and wisdom consists in bringing it to completion. Nature has placed the seeds of virtue in everyone; to develop them, not to create oneself from nothing, is the task of existence.

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, 76 and 120*

### [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

What is up to us 95% · Prometheanism 5%

Misfortune comes not from things but from our grip on them: only what we wrongly count as ours can be torn away. From this Seneca draws the [dichotomy of control](../glossary/dichotomy-of-control.md): to count as one's own only one's judgements and desires, surrendering fortune to the chance that gives and reclaims it. Less a speculative thesis than a practical directive, it makes [the wise man](../glossary/the-sage.md) invulnerable by shrinking the surface he offers to blows: what he carries within, no fortune can reach.

> “The wise man loses nothing through any loss, for he carries all his goods within himself.”, On the Constancy of the Wise Man, V

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, IX (the wise man is self-sufficient) · On the Constancy of the Wise Man, V-VI*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 65% · Eudaimonism 35%

The sovereign good is virtue, the only thing truly good; happiness ([the happy life](../glossary/eudaimonism.md)) follows from it, never the reverse. External goods are [indifferents](../glossary/adiaphora.md): neither wealth, nor health, nor pleasure belongs to the good. Moral perfection thus clearly outweighs any flourishing received from fortune.

> “The highest good is a mind that despises the gifts of chance and takes joy in virtue.”, On the Happy Life, IV

*Sources : On the Happy Life, IV-VII (virtue alone is good) · Letters to Lucilius, LXXVI (the only good is the honourable)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Intellectualism 95% · Sentimentalism 5%

If a passion such as anger is an [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) of reason to the idea that one has been wronged and must take revenge, then it is no alien force besieging the soul but the soul itself going astray. Hence the consequence Seneca turns against the [Peripatetics](../philosophers/aristotle.md): a passion cannot be moderated, for once assent is given reason has already wholly given way; it must be forestalled at the threshold by refusing the first movement, aiming at [apatheia](../glossary/apatheia.md) (the absence of passions) rather than at a mean. This [intellectualism](../axes/emotions.md) makes the therapy of the affects a discipline of judgement.

> “It is easier to banish dangerous passions than to govern them.”, On Anger, I, 7

*Sources : On Anger, I, 8 and II, 1-4 (anger as judgement and assent) · Letters to Lucilius, CXVI (should the passions be moderated or rooted out?)*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 70% · Reasoned selection 30%

Happiness comes from limiting desire to necessary and natural goods. Seneca preaches detachment from fortune and superfluous goods, while granting a [reasoned selection](../glossary/adiaphora.md) of preferred indifferents, which may be welcomed without attachment.

> “It is not the man who has little, but the one who craves more, that is poor.”, Letters to Lucilius, II

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, II (only the one who craves more is poor) · Letters to Lucilius, XVI and CX (natural and necessary desires)*

### [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Amor fati 98% · Revolt 3%

Since fate will come to pass regardless, the one thing in our power is to walk with it or be dragged behind it: the same course of things leads us, but our assent decides whether it is a yoke or freedom. Seneca thus pushes Stoicism past mere resignation: not to endure what happens but to will it, because [providence](../glossary/providence.md) has ordered it for the good of the whole. This [love of fate](../glossary/amor-fati.md) turns necessity into an object of desire, and the second grief, that of struggling against the inevitable, is the only one we can spare ourselves.

> “Fate leads the willing and drags the unwilling.”, Letters to Lucilius, CVII

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, CVII (ducunt volentem fata, quoting Cleanthes) · On the Happy Life, XV (to obey God is freedom)*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Death is nothing 85% · Being-toward-death 15%

Since death is but the natural term of a loan repaid to nature, the evil lies not in it but in the fear of it, a fear that enslaves a whole life to its shadow. Seneca then turns that fear into an instrument of freedom: whoever rehearses dying each day, and holds the door of voluntary exit open, can no longer be coerced by anything or anyone. The meditation on death is thus not morbid but emancipatory, and it matters less for an afterlife, which he does not expect, than for restoring to the present its full worth.

> “He who has learned to die has unlearned slavery.”, Letters to Lucilius, XXVI

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, XXVI and LXX (rehearsing death) · Letters to Lucilius, IV (to rehearse death is to rehearse freedom)*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Presentism 85% · Project 10% · Memory and transmission 5%

The common lament over the shortness of life rests, according to *[On the Shortness of Life](../works/on-the-shortness-of-life-seneca.md)*, on an accounting error: we have not been given little time, we waste a great deal of it. The argument shifts the problem from fate to use: the preoccupied, slaves to a thousand engagements, have never lived, for they keep deferring their life to later. Only the present is properly ours, the past being fixed and the future uncertain; to claim one's time as one defends a possession is to stop squandering it and to begin, at last, to live.

> “It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste much of it.”, On the Shortness of Life, I

*Sources : On the Shortness of Life, I-III and XIV (we squander time) · Letters to Lucilius, I (claim your own time for yourself)*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Hard determinism 75% · Free will 25%

Everything is governed by the chain of fate and the necessity of [providence](../glossary/providence.md). Freedom does not lie in escaping it but in consenting to it through reason, a Stoic [compatibilism](../glossary/compatibilism.md): cosmic necessity leaves the inner freedom of assent intact.

> “The maker and ruler of all things himself wrote the decrees of fate, but he follows them: he ordained once, he obeys forever.”, On Providence, V

*Sources : On Providence, V (God himself is bound by the necessity he once laid down) · Natural Questions, II, 35-38 (fate and the order of causes)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 80% · Atheism 20%

God is not a personal being separate from the world but the immanent reason that orders the whole, identifiable with fate, [providence](../glossary/providence.md) and nature. A Stoic pantheist theism, affirming a providential divinity while merging it with the order of the world.

> “Would you call it fate? You will not be wrong. Would you call it providence? You will speak rightly. Would you call it nature? You will not sin.”, Natural Questions, II, 45

*Sources : Natural Questions, II, 45 (the names of God: fate, providence, nature) · Letters to Lucilius, XLI (God is near you, within you)*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Immanence 90% · Transcendence 10%

The divine is not a beyond but an inner presence. The divine reason ([logos](../glossary/logos.md)) pervades all of nature and the human soul is a fragment of it, a markedly immanent conception of the sacred.

> “A sacred spirit dwells within us, the observer and guardian of our good and our evil.”, Letters to Lucilius, XLI

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, XLI (a sacred spirit dwells within us)*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 85% · Constructed meaning 15%

The world is a cosmos ordered by a providential reason ([logos](../glossary/logos.md)), and the meaning of human life is to live in accordance with that rational nature. Man's end, as a fragment of the divine whole, is inscribed in the order of things: a cosmic teleology grounds ethics.

*Sources : On the Happy Life, III (living according to nature) · On Providence, I and V (the providential order of the world)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Fecundity and redemption 93% · Abolitionism 8%

To the problem of the suffering of the just (why does [providence](../glossary/providence.md) let misfortune strike good people?), Seneca answers by denying the premise: what looks like an evil is none, for virtue is proved and forged only under trial, like the soldier at drill or the pilot in the storm. Adversity then ceases to be something to endure and becomes something to desire: the god does not spare those he loves but tests them, and the good man must wrestle misfortune as an athlete wrestles a worthy opponent. Suffering is neither absurd nor redemptive in itself: it is the occasion without which greatness of soul would lie unused.

> “Misfortune is virtue's opportunity.”, On Providence, IV

*Sources : On Providence, II-IV (adversity as the exercise of virtue)*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Lucidity without hope 80% · The principle of hope 20%

Citing Hecato, Seneca ties hope to fear as two correlated affects turned toward the future. [The sage](../glossary/the-sage.md) lives in the present and in accepted necessity rather than in the anxious expectation of a better lot, leaning toward lucidity without hope, though without nihilism.

> “Cease to hope and you will cease to fear... the two march in step: anxiety follows hope.”, Letters to Lucilius, V

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, V (cease to hope and you will cease to fear) · Letters to Lucilius, XIII (we suffer more in imagination than in reality)*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Cosmopolitanism 93% · Communitarianism and patriotism 7%

Seneca distinguishes two commonwealths: the narrow one of our birth, and the great city common to gods and men, measured by the course of the sun. Every human is our fellow citizen by virtue of shared reason, nature having made us kin: a [cosmopolitanism](../axes/belonging.md) grounded in Stoic [appropriation](../glossary/oikeiosis.md).

> “We are members of one great body... nature made us kin, begetting us from the same principles and for the same ends.”, Letters to Lucilius, XCV

*Sources : On the Tranquillity of the Mind, IV (the two commonwealths) · Letters to Lucilius, XCV, 52-53 (nature made us kin)*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 90% · Deontologism 10%

Seneca's ethics is regulated by virtue and the character of the [sage](../glossary/the-sage.md), not by a reckoning of consequences nor by an imperative rule. To live according to nature and reason, cultivating courage, temperance and justice: this is a [virtue ethics](../axes/ethics-foundation.md), where the good is the excellence of the soul.

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, LXXVI and CXX (virtue, the only good and the end of life) · On the Happy Life, VIII-IX (virtue suffices for happiness)*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Asceticism 85% · Celebration 15%

Pleasure is not the good: to confuse the happy life with a life of pleasure is to sink man to the level of beasts. Seneca commends a sober life, the voluntary endurance of hunger and cold, and holds bodily pleasure to be an [indifferent](../glossary/adiaphora.md) not to be sought for its own sake, without lapsing into outright mortification.

*Sources : On the Happy Life, X-XV (against equating happiness with pleasure) · Letters to Lucilius, XVIII and CXXIII (rehearsing frugality)*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Naturalism 85% · Culturalism 15%

Man is by nature a being of reason, made to live according to the [logos](../glossary/logos.md) and for the society of his fellows: virtue fulfils that nature, it does not thwart it. Vices and passions stem from a reason gone astray rather than from a cultural second nature, a naturalist position.

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, LXXVI (man's proper good is reason) · On Anger, II, 31 (man is born for mutual aid)*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Altruism 75% · Ethical egoism 25%

Stoic [appropriation](../glossary/oikeiosis.md) extends self-concern to all humans: born for mutual aid, we owe others kindness, clemency and help. [Beneficence](../glossary/generosity.md) (On Benefits) and gentleness toward humankind draw Seneca toward altruism, without denying that virtue remains first a perfecting of oneself.

*Sources : On Benefits, I, 1-4 (the practice of disinterested giving) · On Anger, II, 31 (we are born for mutual aid)*

### [The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Rehabilitation 55% · Deterrence 45%

In On Clemency, addressed to Nero, and in *[On Anger](../works/on-anger-seneca.md)*, Seneca rejects vengeance: one punishes not because a wrong has been done but so that none be done again, looking to the future, not the past. Punishment aims at correcting the offender and deterring others, never at satisfying a retributive anger.

> “No wise man punishes because a wrong has been done, but so that no wrong be done again.”, On Anger, I, 19 (citing Plato)

*Sources : On Anger, I, 16 and 19 (punishing to correct, not to avenge) · On Clemency, I (the clemency of the prince)*

### [Forgiveness](../axes/forgiveness.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Unconditionalism 75% · Conditionalism 25%

Clemency, the prince's master virtue, disposes one to gentleness in setting penalties and to indulgence toward another's fault, from greatness of soul rather than from the offender's desert. This inclination to excuse and to understand human failings leans toward an unconditional remission, grounded in the magnanimity of the [sage](../glossary/the-sage.md).

*Sources : On Clemency, II, 3-7 (clemency, the soul's inclination to gentleness) · On Anger, II, 28-34 (being indulgent, for we all err)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The examined life 85% · Primacy of action 15%

Seneca practises and prescribes a daily examination of conscience: each evening, to summon one's soul before itself to judge the day's deeds. Philosophy is first a care of the self, a reflexive return upon the inner life, even if it does not exclude public action.

> “Every day I plead my cause before myself... What fault of yours have you cured today?”, On Anger, III, 36

*Sources : On Anger, III, 36 (the evening examination of conscience) · Letters to Lucilius, XVI and XXVIII (philosophy as care of the soul)*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Metaphysical optimism 90% · Tragic thought 10%

The universe is governed by a rational and benevolent [providence](../glossary/providence.md): all that happens conduces to the good of the whole, and what seems an evil is but a trial or an appearance. This trust in the divine order of the world is a metaphysical optimism, far from any tragic vision of an indifferent cosmos.

*Sources : On Providence, I-II (nothing evil can befall a good man)*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 70% · Skepticism 30%

As a Stoic, Seneca takes it as settled that a true knowledge of the world and of the good is possible: nature is rational and wisdom is attainable. Yet he claims freedom from the schools, judging opinions for himself and binding himself to no master, which tempers the Stoic dogmatism with an eclectic mind.

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, XII and XLV (judging for oneself, swearing by no master)*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Holism 70% · Individualism 30%

Man is born for community and mutual aid: we are the limbs of one great body, and the individual good is fulfilled in service to the human whole. Without dissolving the individual into the city, Seneca subordinates philosophical retreat to a duty of common usefulness, leaning toward a holist view of society.

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, XCV (the limbs of one body) · On Leisure, III-VI (the sage must serve the great commonwealth)*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Materialism 80% · Dualism 15% · Idealism 5%

The Stoic physics Seneca expounds in the Natural Questions is corporealist: only what acts or is acted upon exists, hence only bodies. The active principle itself, the divine reason ([logos](../glossary/logos.md)), is a material breath (pneuma) diffused through the world, placing his metaphysics on the materialist side, with only a slight surface soul-body dualism.

*Sources : Natural Questions, II (the corporeal nature of the active principle) · Letters to Lucilius, CVI and CXVII (only bodies exist)*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Finalism 80% · Mechanism 20%

The whole universe is ordered by a [providence](../glossary/providence.md) acting toward an end, the good of the whole: nature does nothing at random and fate is the reasoned chain of causes toward that end. This is a providential [finalism](../axes/causality.md), where the mechanical necessity of causes serves a rational design.

*Sources : On Providence, I and V (the purposive order of the world) · Natural Questions, II, 45 (providence and fate)*

### [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Maximalism 85% · Minimalism 15%

The ideal of the [sage](../glossary/the-sage.md) is so lofty as to be almost unattainable: it demands an effort of every instant, a never-slackening progress (prokopê) toward moral perfection. Seneca, aware of his own shortcomings, nonetheless upholds a maximalist demand for a thorough transformation of the self.

*Sources : Letters to Lucilius, V and LXXV (unceasing moral progress) · On the Happy Life, XVII-XVIII (Seneca admits he is no sage)*

## Influenced by

- **[Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md)** (reinterprets) : Across the rivalry of the schools, [Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md) quotes and appropriates [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) as a moral authority in his Letters, on death, friendship and the right measure of desire.

## Influence on

- **[Michel de Montaigne](../philosophers/montaigne.md)** (continues) : [Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md), alongside Plutarch, is the chief moral source of the Essays: [Montaigne](../philosophers/montaigne.md) continues the [Stoic](../movements/stoicism.md) meditation on death, constancy and self-examination. He then loosens it toward a more natural, fluctuating wisdom, faithful to flux rather than to the sage's firmness.
