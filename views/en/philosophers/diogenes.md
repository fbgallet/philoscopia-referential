# Diogenes of Sinope, the Cynic

*412 BCE – 323 BCE · Greek · [Cynicism](../movements/cynicism.md)*

The most famous figure of [Cynicism](../movements/cynicism.md), Diogenes was born in Sinope, on the Black Sea, from which he was banished, it is said, over a matter of debased coinage, before leading in Athens and then Corinth the life of a philosopher-beggar. He traces back to Socrates through Antisthenes, whose teaching he radicalised into provocation: he lived in a jar, owning only a cloak, a satchel and a staff, and made public scandal his method. Plato is said to have called him "a Socrates gone mad". He left no certain writings, and all our knowledge of him passes through the anecdotes (chreiai) reported three centuries later by Diogenes Laertius in Book VI of his Lives of Eminent Philosophers, which leaves the line between the man and the legend uncertain. Through his pupil Crates, who taught Zeno of Citium, his ethics of virtue and his cosmopolitanism passed into [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md).

## Core positions

1. [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md) : **Whoever desires nothing external depends on no one: [self-sufficiency](../glossary/autarkeia.md) makes the most destitute of men freer than the mightiest of kings.**
2. [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md) : **Almost everything men believe they owe to nature is mere [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md), groundless convention: to live according to nature is to strip away that artifice down to real need alone.**
3. [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md) : **To speak the truth without circumlocution, even to the powerful, and to transgress accepted modesties without blushing: [frankness](../glossary/parrhesia.md) proves by deed that social shame has no natural ground.**
4. [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md) : **No city has any natural claim on me: I am citizen of no particular homeland but a [kosmopolitēs](../glossary/cosmopolitanism.md), a citizen of the whole world.**
5. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **Virtue alone is a good, for it alone cannot be taken from us; and it is a strength won through [training](../glossary/askesis.md) and proven by deeds, not a piece of knowledge.**

## Positions

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Naturalism 95% · Culturalism 5%

Diogenes' whole gesture rests on a cut between [physis](../glossary/physis-nomos.md) (nature), which sets man simple needs and one common lot, and the [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md) (convention) of cities, which heaps on wealth, rank and modesties with no grounding in things. Man makes himself wretched precisely where he takes convention for nature, toiling for factitious goods and blushing at acts no animal deems shameful. To live according to nature is thus to undo this varnish: not an idealised return to a primal goodness, but a stripping away that brings conduct back to real need alone. Nature here is not a teleological norm but a negative criterion, what remains once artifice is removed.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 70-71*

### [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

What is up to us 98% · Prometheanism 3%

One is free only in proportion to what one can do without: every need is a hold that the world, fortune or the powerful have on us. Hence [autarkeia](../glossary/autarkeia.md) (self-sufficiency) as the goal, living so that nothing external is necessary and no one therefore holds anything we depend on. When Alexander, master of the known world, offered to grant him any wish, Diogenes asked only that he stand out of his sunlight: the most destitute of men, if he desires nothing, is more invulnerable than the mightiest of kings, who remains at the mercy of what he owns. The Stoics would later take up this operation as the [dichotomy](../axes/control-scope.md) of control, but Diogenes carries it out through deeds and destitution rather than inner judgment, without turning it into a settled thesis.

> “"Ask of me any boon you like." "Stand out of my sunlight."” (Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 38)

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 38*

### [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Truthful rigorism 98% · Benevolent lying permitted 3%

If social shame is only [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md), to keep it quiet is to become complicit in the collective lie on which conventions rest. Hence two bound demands. [Parrhēsia](../glossary/parrhesia.md) (frank speech) requires telling the truth without circumlocution or flattery, even to the faces of the powerful: asked what is the finest thing among men, Diogenes answered, frank speech. [Anaideia](../glossary/anaideia.md) (deliberate shamelessness) is its proof by deed: to perform in public, without blushing, what accepted modesty forbids is to demonstrate experimentally that this modesty had no natural ground. Cynic frankness is thus no gratuitous coarseness but a method of refutation that takes the body and the scandal as its arguments.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 69*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Cosmopolitanism 98% · Communitarianism and patriotism 3%

If the borders of cities are only [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md), convention without grounding in nature, none can command the allegiance of a rational being: a city has no more natural claim on me than the accident of my birth. Hence the word [kosmopolitēs](../glossary/cosmopolitanism.md) that Diogenes is the first to take for himself, a citizen of the world: not love of an enlarged homeland, but the refusal to acknowledge any, all of humanity taken as the only horizon. The provocation strikes at Greek civic pride, which counted the exile as fallen: the banished man of Sinope turns his condition into a claim. The Stoics would inherit the word, but back it with a cosmic city ordered by the [logos](../glossary/logos.md); in Diogenes it stays first of all a gesture of refusal, with no cosmology.

> “When asked where he came from, he said: "I am a citizen of the world."” (Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 63)

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 63*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 80% · Eudaimonism 20%

Whatever can be misused, or lost to a stroke of fortune, cannot be a true good: neither health, nor wealth, nor pleasure passes the test. Only virtue deserves the name, because it alone always benefits and cannot be taken away; once acquired it suffices for happiness. But Diogenes hardens the Socratic legacy on one decisive point: virtue is not knowledge but a strength, won through [training](../glossary/askesis.md) and proven by deeds, not by definition, against the intellectualism that would make the good a science teachable in words. A thin eudaimonist strand remains, since this life according to nature is also presented as the happiest.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 11 and 104-105*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Asceticism 85% · Celebration 15%

Diogenes' asceticism is radical: through [askēsis](../glossary/askesis.md) (training), he makes do with water, bread and a coarse cloak, hardening himself to cold and hunger. Seeing a child drink from its cupped hands, he threw away his bowl, judging that a child had outdone him in simplicity. Yet the stance is not a pure asceticism that despises the body, and this is its own nuance: whatever accords with nature is never shameful, and Diogenes performs the crudest bodily functions in public. Luxury and refinement, on the other hand, are rejected as forms of bondage, not because pleasure would be bad, but because factitious need enslaves. Hence an asceticism of the superfluous rather than a condemnation of the body.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 37 and 22-23*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 85% · Reasoned selection 15%

The less one desires, the freer one is: the desire for external things is the very chain by which the world holds us. [Askēsis](../glossary/askesis.md) consists in training oneself to need nothing, so that nothing can be lacking or enslaving. Diogenes does not aim at a reasoned sorting of pleasures in the manner of Epicurus, but at a reduction of desire to the strictly natural, detached from all the rest. The tendency is toward the extinction of factitious desires, not their affirmation.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 105*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Primacy of action 93% · The examined life 7%

Since philosophy's sole object is to live well, and living well is a matter of conduct, physics, logic and learned disputation are wasted time: one does not become temperate by defining temperance. Hence the primacy of [praxis](../glossary/praxis.md) over theory, which is not lack of culture but consistency. A thesis is refuted by an act as much as by an argument: hearing motion denied, Diogenes, for all reply, stood up and walked. The gesture serves as demonstration, and the sage's life, exposed in public, stands in for a treatise.

> “When someone argued that there is no such thing as motion, he got up and walked about.” (Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 39)

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 39 and 70-71*

### [Beauty in life](../axes/aesthetic-life.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Primacy of the ethical 90% · Aestheticism 10%

Whatever does not serve virtue, fine arts, refinements, elegance, is held to be social vanity: the only work to be shaped is one's own conduct. Diogenes lives not for beauty but for rectitude, and mocks distinguished culture as a mask of [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md). The form of life takes clear precedence over aesthetic form, which places him firmly on the side of the primacy of the ethical, even if his provocative existence has, by ricochet, something of a performance about it.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 24 and 27-28*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Altruism 75% · Ethical egoism 25%

The end Diogenes pursues is first his own [self-sufficiency](../glossary/autarkeia.md): he preaches not devotion to others but self-liberation from need and opinion. Yet his frank speech means to be a service to others, whom he claims to treat as a physician treats the sick soul of the city, and his cosmopolitanism refuses to rank human beings. The self-concern here is thus less ethical than methodical: making oneself independent so as to be able to tell everyone a useful truth, while expecting nothing from them.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 30 and 63*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Anarchism 90% · Authority 10%

If institutions, magistracies and civic honours are only [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md), then no established authority holds a natural title to command: Diogenes despises the powerful, mocks the assemblies, and recognises order in nature alone. The distrust comes with no political project to replace what it rejects: by analogy one may read in it an anarchism of the self-sufficient individual rather than a doctrine of the city, and the position stays inferred from anecdotes more than asserted as a thesis. His lamp in broad daylight, searching for a man, is aimed first at the figures of power and reputation.

> “He lit a lamp in broad daylight and said: "I am looking for a man."” (Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 41)

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 41*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Universalism 80% · Relativism 10% · Constructivism 10%

By opposing nature to [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md), Diogenes presupposes a criterion of the good independent of conventions and valid for all: what accords with nature is good everywhere, what is merely local custom does not bind. He therefore judges customs in the name of a universal natural norm, and this is what licenses the cosmopolitanism. But the weapon is double-edged: the same critique of [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md) feeds a de facto relativism, since Diogenes delights in stressing how modesties and prohibitions vary from one people to another, proof that none derives from nature. The relativist strand stays secondary, put to the service of a normative naturalism.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 70-73*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 95% · Consequentialism 5%

Moral worth lies entirely in the state of the character, free and self-sufficient: this is a virtue ethics in the strict sense, where neither an external rule nor a calculation of consequences grounds the good. Virtue is not taught in words but acquired through exercise ([askēsis](../glossary/askesis.md)), like an art. Diogenes goes further than ordinary virtue ethics: he makes it a bodily discipline, a training in hardness and want, closer to an athlete's gymnastics than to a sage's deliberation.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 70-71*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 65% · Hard determinism 35%

Diogenes makes freedom (eleutheria) the supreme good, but shifts it entirely to the practical side: to be free is not to settle a metaphysical question about the will, it is to make oneself independent of desires, opinion and the blows of fortune through stripping away. The question of free will does not occupy him; his freedom is an existential conquest, the self-sufficiency of the sage who needs nothing. The positioning therefore stays cautious and inferred, the corpus offering no explicit thesis on determinism.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 71*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 70% · Existentialism 30%

Diogenes presupposes a natural human condition, made of simple needs and shameless like that of the animals, which civilization has covered over and which must be recovered: nature is a norm to be uncovered, not an identity to be invented. But this nature remains for him a negative criterion, what subsists once artifice is removed, without the metaphysics of a fixed essence; hence a moderate naturalist position rather than an emphatic essentialism.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 70-71*

### [Hierarchy of tastes](../axes/taste-hierarchy.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Egalitarianism of taste 90% · Elitism of taste 10%

Diogenes mocks refinement, luxury arts and distinguished culture as so many vanities: what passes for good taste is mere [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md), a status marker detached from natural need. Before a temple's gilded offerings, before its columns and statues, he sees only pretension. The stance is egalitarian, debunking the imposture behind hierarchies of taste.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 24 and 60*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 90% · Abolitionism 10%

Painful effort (ponos) is the very means of virtue: Diogenes trains himself to cold, hunger and destitution to harden himself and become free, embracing snow-covered statues in winter and rolling his jar over burning sand. Suffering voluntarily chosen is no evil but a gymnastics of the soul that forges [self-sufficiency](../glossary/autarkeia.md) and frees one from the blows of fortune. It is hardship sought, not undergone, that makes the sage.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 23 and 71*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Death is nothing 85% · Immortalism 10% · Being-toward-death 5%

Diogenes flaunts a provocative indifference toward death: he asks, it is said, that his corpse be thrown over the wall to the beasts, and mocks the care of burial, since he will no longer be there to feel anything. The fear of dying belongs to the empty conventions he sets out precisely to undo; death is nothing that should trouble the self-sufficient sage. The serenity meets the Epicurean tone, with no promise of personal immortality.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 79*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Atheism 65% · Theism 35%

Diogenes professes no theoretical atheism but a practical irreligion: he mocks rites, mysteries and oracles, and holds the city's religious beliefs and fears to be vain. Seeing the votive offerings of shipwreck survivors, he remarked that they would be far more numerous if those who had perished had dedicated theirs too. Indifferent to the gods of cult, he reduces the divine to nature and to virtue, which sets him apart from theism without making him a militant atheist.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 24, 42 and 59*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 75% · Rationalism 25%

Heir to Antisthenes and his **nominalism**, Diogenes recognises only sensible individuals and holds Plato's general Ideas to be empty words: "I can see a table and a cup, ran this line of thought, but not tablehood or cuphood". The knowledge that counts is taken from experience and concrete practice, not from the contemplation of intelligible essences. It is a nominalist empiricism, more polemical than systematic.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 53*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Impartialism 70% · Partialism 30%

Diogenes' cosmopolitanism denies any natural privilege of one's near ones: homeland, family and city being only [nomos](../glossary/physis-nomos.md), they ground no particular obligation that nature would recognise. Every rational being counts equally, beyond the arbitrariness of birth. The position leans toward impartiality, but stays inferred: Diogenes does not theorise duties, he dissolves conventional loyalties more than he builds a positive impartial ethics.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 72*

### [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Necessity, leisure as the end 60% · Alienation 25% · Fulfillment 15%

The only labour worth anything, for Diogenes, is the effort on oneself (ponos), the training in virtue; paid work, the amassing of wealth and the race for useful occupations belong to a conventional servitude that chains one to factitious needs. An avowed beggar, he holds the sage's studious leisure to be the end, and busy activity a means at best tolerated: one works only so as to be able to stop depending. Worth lies not in production but in the freedom won by doing without it.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 49 and 56*

## Influenced by

- **[Antisthenes](../philosophers/antisthenes.md)** (continues) : [Diogenes](../philosophers/diogenes.md) takes up the ideal of self-sufficiency of [Antisthenes](../philosophers/antisthenes.md) and pushes it to the extreme, into a provocative practice of destitution and freedom. The direct teacher-pupil filiation is traditional but debated.
- **[Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)** (reacts against) : [Diogenes](../philosophers/diogenes.md) defines his Cynicism against [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md): he mocks the [theory of Forms](../glossary/theory-of-forms.md) ("I see the cup, but not cupness") and "Plato's man", which he ridicules with a plucked chicken, opposing to the idealist's abstractions a nominalist, bodily naturalism.
