# Source of knowledge

**Where does our knowledge come from: sensory experience or reason?**

*Truth · descriptive (theory) · bipolar with median · core axis*

Does everything come from the senses, which nonetheless deceive us at times, or does the mind hold truths no experience could supply? Resting knowledge on experience alone deprives it of certainty; on reason alone, it cuts it off from the real world. To take a position is to say what to rely on, in the last resort, in order to know.

## Poles

### Empiricism

All knowledge derives from sensory experience: the mind starts as a blank slate that the senses gradually fill. Even our most abstract ideas can ultimately be traced back to received impressions.

*[Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) · [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md)*

### Rationalism

Reason has resources of its own, independent of experience: innate ideas, necessary truths, first principles. The senses can deceive us; only reason grounds genuinely certain knowledge.

*[Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)*

### Criticism (median position)

Knowledge comes neither from experience alone nor from reason alone, but from their collaboration. The senses supply the matter, the mind the forms that order it. Without concepts, perceptions would be blind; without perceptions, concepts would be empty.

## Live problems

- **Innate or learned?** : Are we born with an empty mind, a blank page the senses fill in, or do we already possess, before any experience, ideas or structures that experience merely awakens? → [Molyneux's problem](../thought-experiments/molyneux-problem.md)
- **Knowledge by intuition** : Some knowledge seems to come all at once, neither by reasoning nor through the senses: the self-evidence of a principle, the sudden grasp of an essence, the certainty of the heart. Is it a higher form of reason, a kind of experience, or an illusion that borrows the name of knowledge?
- **Where does the idea of cause come from?** : Empiricism would trace every idea back to a sense impression. But when one ball strikes another, we see only two motions in succession, never the force or the necessary link between them: where, then, does the idea of cause come from?
- **Does the mind give form to experience?** : What if the mind did not merely receive the world but gave it form? Space, time, causality would then be less things observed than frames we impose on every possible experience.
- **Emotions, a source of knowledge?** : Beyond the senses and reason, do our emotions let us know something of the world and of ourselves, or do they only cloud judgment?
- **Do the senses deceive us?** : If the senses can deceive us to the very root, as in dreams, can solid knowledge still be made to rest on them?

## Grounding relations

- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Empiricism* grounds *[Scientific method](../axes/scientific-method.md) : Proof by experience* : If all knowledge comes from experience, then it falls to experience to prove: science starts from facts and confirms its laws through the methodical observation of regularities. Empiricism thus grounds the trust in experimental proof, from Bacon to [Mill](../philosophers/mill.md), for whom the accumulation of observations establishes our knowledge of nature.
- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Empiricism* grounds *[The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : Hedonism* : If all knowledge, including knowledge of the good, comes from experience, then it is in felt pleasure and pain that what is worthwhile is read, for they are the only values given directly to the senses. Empiricism grounds hedonism: [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md) makes pleasure and pain the 'sovereign masters' by which every good is measured.
- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Empiricism* grounds *[Universals](../axes/universals.md) : Nominalism* : If nothing enters the mind that has not first passed through the senses, and the senses deliver only individuals, then general properties are not realities but names. Empiricism inclines toward nominalism: for [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), the general idea is merely a term attached to particular impressions that resemble one another.
- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Empiricism* grounds *[Place of science](../axes/science-place.md) : Scientism* : If only controlled experience yields genuine knowledge, then science becomes the only legitimate path to knowledge, and religious or metaphysical explanations are merely superseded stages. Empiricism feeds scientism: [Comte](../philosophers/comte.md) makes the positive state the culmination of the human mind's evolution.
- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Empiricism* grounds *[Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Skepticism* : Pressed to its limit, [empiricism](../axes/knowledge-source.md) leads to doubt: if all knowledge comes from the senses, nothing guarantees the truths that outrun them, such as the necessity of a cause or the regularity of the future. This is [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md)'s road, from sensationism to [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md). The link stays contested: a moderate empiricism, in Locke, is in no way skeptical.
- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Empiricism* grounds *[Beauty](../axes/beauty.md) : Subjective beauty* : If all knowledge comes from sense experience, beauty is not a property of things but the pleasure they arouse in us. Empiricism grounds subjective beauty: [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) holds that beauty is no quality in things themselves, but a sentiment in the mind that contemplates them.
- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Rationalism* grounds *[Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Dogmatism* : If [reason](../axes/knowledge-source.md) commands first principles and [innate ideas](../glossary/innate-ideas.md) independent of the deceptive senses, then a certain and definitive truth lies within its reach. Rationalism thus feeds [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md): it is by seeking a foundation reason could not revoke that Descartes believes he closes off doubt.
- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Rationalism* grounds *[Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md) : Foundationalism* : If properly guided reason can reach certain truths, knowledge must rest on basic beliefs, immediately evident, from which all the rest is deduced. Rationalism grounds foundationalism: [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) seeks in the cogito the indubitable fixed point on which to rebuild all knowledge.
