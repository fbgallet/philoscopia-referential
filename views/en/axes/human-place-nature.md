# Humanity's place in nature

**What place should human beings hold in nature: master, guardian, or simply one member among others?**

*The world · value · triangular*

Are we the owners of a nature to exploit, the caretakers responsible for a garden entrusted to us, or one species among others with no special right? Depending on the answer, what we allow ourselves to do to forests, animals, and the climate no longer has the same limits. To take a position is to set what nature can legitimately expect of us.

## Poles

### Mastery

Through knowledge and technology, human beings are called to make themselves as masters and possessors of nature, to relieve their condition and improve life. Nature is a resource to be understood and transformed in the service of humanity.

*[Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) · [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md)*

### Stewardship

Humanity's technological power confers an unprecedented responsibility: to preserve nature and the conditions of an authentically human life. Humans are neither owners of the world nor mere elements of it, but its guardians.

*[Jonas](../philosophers/jonas.md)*

### Member among others

Human beings hold no privileged position in nature: they are one living being among others, embedded in a web of interdependence. All forms of life have intrinsic value, independent of their usefulness to us.

*[Næss](../philosophers/naess.md)*

## Live problems

- **The last man** : If the last human being could, as he died, wipe out all remaining life with no witness, would he do wrong? Does nature have worth only for us, or does it have worth in itself, independent of any human gaze? → [The last man](../thought-experiments/last-man.md)
- **A creature apart?** : Is the human a being apart, held by reason and spirit above nature and bearing a singular dignity, or a living creature sprung from it, continuous with the animal and subject like it to its laws? The posture one adopts turns on this.
- **The domination of nature** : To command nature by understanding it has pushed back hunger and disease; but does the will to dominate it not turn into the domination of humankind, reducing the world to a mere stock on call? Does mastery emancipate, or does it impoverish both nature and ourselves?
- **Sanctuary or garden** : Should nature be left wild and untouched, preserving the part we did not make, or is the ideal a tended, gardened nature that human care completes rather than violates? And is there any "untouched" nature left to save at all?
- **Should we follow nature?** : Is nature a wise order to imitate and respect, or a blind power that crushes and starves with no regard for the good? Does calling something natural tell us anything at all about its worth?

## Grounding relations

- *[Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : Finalism* grounds *[Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md) : Stewardship* : If nature and the living have their own ends and a worth that our power can destroy, then that very power obliges us to preserve them. Finalism grounds stewardship: [Jonas](../philosophers/jonas.md) draws from nature's vulnerability an unprecedented responsibility, that of keeping the world for those to come.
- *[Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : Mechanism* grounds *[Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md) : Mastery* : If nature is but a vast mechanism governed by laws, then to know it is to be able to master it and put it at our service. Mechanism grounds the ideal of mastery: [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) charges science with making us "as masters and possessors of nature", and Bacon makes knowledge a power.
- *[Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md) : Naturalism* grounds *[Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md) : Member among others* : If the human being is a natural being among the living, with no privileged position, then they are not the master of nature but a member of a web of interdependence. Naturalism grounds the idea of the human as one member among others: from Darwinian continuity to deep ecology, every form of life has a worth of its own, independent of its usefulness to us.
