# The meaning of punishment

**Why punish: to repay the wrong, to deter, or to rehabilitate?**

*Others · value · categorical*

Punishing to make the guilty pay can be mere vengeance dressed as justice; punishing only to deter or for efficiency can sacrifice the offender to the common good. Depending on the answer, the penalty looks toward a past to settle or a future to protect. To take a position is to say what we are really doing when we punish.

## Poles

### Retributivism

We punish because a wrong has been committed, and the penalty must be proportionate to that wrong. Punishment does justice to the crime itself and respects the offender as a responsible being, regardless of any social utility.

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)*

### Deterrence

Punishment is justified only by its effects: preventing future crimes by deterring the offender and others. It must be certain, swift, and just severe enough to discourage, without needless cruelty.

*[Beccaria](../philosophers/beccaria.md) · [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md)*

### Rehabilitation

The purpose of punishment is to transform the offender and prepare their return to society. Rather than inflicting suffering, the aim is to understand the causes of crime and reintegrate the person as a full member of the community.

*[Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)*

### Restorative justice

Punishment should first of all repair the harm and restore what the crime has broken: acknowledge the victim, hold the offender accountable to them, and re-establish the order and bonds of the community. Justice aims less to inflict suffering than to repair, reconcile, and remake the social fabric.

- Classical root: Aristotle's corrective justice, restoring the balance broken by the wrong.

*Zehr · Braithwaite*

## Live problems

- **Punishing an innocent to keep the peace?** : If punishment is worth only its effects, sacrificing an innocent to avert a greater harm sometimes seems required: but is condemning one who did nothing still punishment, or the very negation of justice? → [Punishing an innocent to keep the peace?](../thought-experiments/scapegoat-punishment.md)
- **Making an example of an offender** : Punishing to deter is using one man to frighten others: does only the penalty deserved for what he did treat him as a responsible person, rather than as a means?
- **Heal or punish** : Treating crime as a sickness to cure seems more humane than inflicting suffering; but does reshaping someone « for their own good », without limit or their consent, respect them less than a deserved penalty?
- **Do we deserve our punishment?** : Punishing as deserved presupposes the offender could have acted otherwise; if their act flows from their causes, their nature and their circumstances, does the very idea of desert still hold?
- **The right measure of the penalty** : Retributivism measures the penalty by the gravity of the wrong (eye for an eye), deterrence by what it takes to discourage: two measures that can demand far more, or far less, than each other.
- **Settle the debt or mend the bond** : Is the crime a debt to the order, settled by making the offender suffer, or a rupture between persons, repaired only by acknowledging the victim and restoring the bond? And how would added suffering pay for a wrong?
- **Punishment or vengeance** : Is the penalty handed down by the institution an impartial, measured justice, different in kind from revenge, or only revenge made public, orderly, and deferred?
- **Where does the right to punish come from?** : What authorizes the state to inflict on a person what no private individual could: a power ceded by the social contract, or the moral order itself?
- **The death penalty** : Is killing the criminal the only penalty matching the worst crimes (Kant), or a punishment that deters no better than another, forecloses any return, and lowers the society that inflicts it (Beccaria, Camus)?
- **Do we need prisons?** : Does incarceration truly reform the offender, or does the whole penal apparatus do little more than produce and manage delinquency, in the service of an order and a power?
- **Justice or mercy** : Must the deserved penalty always be inflicted, or can clemency, pardon, and forgiveness legitimately set it aside without betraying justice?

## Grounding relations

- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Hard determinism* grounds *[The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md) : Rehabilitation* : If the offender could not have acted otherwise, punishing him in the name of desert becomes unjustifiable. [Determinism](../glossary/determinism.md) thus steers sanction toward rehabilitation, which reshapes the causes of behaviour rather than avenging a free fault.
- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will* grounds *[The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md) : Retributivism* : If the agent could have acted otherwise, they are the true author of their wrong and bear its merit or demerit. [Free will](../glossary/free-will.md) grounds penal retributivism: punishment is owed in proportion to the fault, because it was freely committed. [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) makes it a demand of justice, not a mere means of deterrence.

## Tensions

- With [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : If our acts are fully determined, no one is truly their free author; yet punishing to “give what is deserved” presupposes exactly that responsibility. Holding both creates a tension: retributivism demands a desert that strict determinism removes.
