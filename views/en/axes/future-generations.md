# Future generations

**Do we have duties toward future generations?**

*The world · value · bipolar*

Can we owe anything to beings who do not yet exist, who claim nothing, and whom our choices will bring into being as they will be? Making room for them can weigh heavily on the living; ignoring them means mortgaging a world we will never see. To take a position is to say how far our responsibility extends in time.

## Poles

### Responsibility principle

Our power to act now extends to the living conditions of generations to come: we have a duty to act so that the effects of our actions remain compatible with the permanence of genuinely human life on Earth. The distant future enters the scope of morality.

*[Jonas](../philosophers/jonas.md)*

### Priority to the present

Our obligations are owed first to the people who exist here and now: their needs are real and urgent, whereas the interests of future individuals remain hypothetical and uncertain. Sacrificing the present to a distant future would be unjust to the living.

## Live problems

- **The world after** : Our choices impoverish the distant world, but also decide who will be born into it: those people would not exist under a different policy. Can we harm them, if none of them is worse off than they would otherwise have been, having never existed otherwise? → [The world after](../thought-experiments/non-identity.md)
- **A right with no bearer?** : A right seems to require a bearer who exists; future beings do not yet exist, claim nothing, can do nothing for us. Can there be an obligation with no reciprocity and no one to hold it, or does our duty toward them rest on something other than a right?
- **To bring into being, or only not to harm?** : Do we have a duty to bring future people into being, or only, if they are to exist, not to harm them? Nothing seems to require us to create happy beings, whereas creating miserable ones would be a wrong; and seeking to maximise total happiness leads to preferring a multitude of barely-good lives to a small flourishing humanity.
- **Is a distant good worth less?** : Should a benefit or harm to future people count for less simply because it lies far off in time, as the economic reckoning that discounts the future does, or is that preference for the present a moral error, a mere failure of imagination? A very small annual rate is enough to make a distant catastrophe almost weightless today.
- **A fair share across the ages** : Grant that we owe the future something: what is the fair division of resources and burdens across generations, how much must each age save, and who bears the cost of averting a distant harm, such as the climate burden?
