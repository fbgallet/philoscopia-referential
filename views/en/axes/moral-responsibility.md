# Moral responsibility

**Should we assign blame and credit?**

*Others · value · ordered categorical*

Praising a child, resenting a friend, taking pride in a success, blaming ourselves for a fault: we spend our days assigning blame and credit. Yet what each of us is, talents as much as leanings, was largely made without us, and one can admire a skill while knowing its acquisition owed nothing to the will. Whether our acts are determined is a neighbouring question, with its own axis; what is decided here is an attitude: to take merit and fault seriously, to keep them as tools, or to let go of them. To take a position is to say what we owe one another in praise, reproach, and forgiveness, and what would become of our bonds if we stopped keeping accounts.

## Poles

### Giving each their due

Blame and merit should be assigned, for they are real: when an act is voluntary, done knowingly and without constraint, praise and blame are owed, not merely useful. To refuse to assign them would be to see no longer actions but events, and to treat no one as the author of their life.

- Two justifications share the pole: merit is real, grounded in freedom (Kant), or the practice of assigning it stands on its own, woven into our reactive attitudes with no need of metaphysics (P.F. Strawson). The quarrel plays out in the axis's problems.

*[Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) · [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)*

### Correct, not retaliate

We should keep responding to acts, blaming, sanctioning, praising, not because it is deserved at bottom, but because it works: understanding does not rule out acting. We give up resentment, not response; the question is no longer "is it deserved?" but "what will make things better?".

- Contemporary versions: sanction as quarantine (Pereboom, protecting without desert) or as the shaping of conduct (Schlick). Spinoza gives the classic form: understanding causes dissolves resentment, not the firmness of the city.

*[Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md)*

### Keeping no accounts

We should let go of assignment itself: no one is the ultimate author of what they are, and our acts issue from us as fruit from a tree. In place of reproach and pride come other responses: to understand, to pity, to rejoice or to laugh, as one responds to the beauty of a gesture or the sorrow of a shipwreck, without seeking a culprit. This is not indifference: it is the end of the tribunal, not the end of feeling.

- Galen Strawson gives the logical form: to be responsible for one's acts, one would have to be responsible for what one is, and so on to infinity.
- The feelings that replace them vary: compassion for the ignorant or the damaged (already in Socrates, no one is wicked willingly), sorrow without resentment (Pereboom), Nietzschean gaiety before the innocence of becoming.

*[Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md)*

## Live problems

- **The lottery of merit** : Two identical reckless drivers, a child crosses in front of only one: chance makes one a killer and the other lucky. And what we praise, talents, character, was no more chosen: if luck pervades everything, what remains of merit properly one's own?
- **Responsible for one's character?** : The drunk man no longer controls himself, but he made himself so, glass after glass: for Aristotle, we impute the disposition one has let grow. Yet to be accountable for one's character, would one not have to have chosen it, and so on to infinity?
- **Does understanding all excuse all?** : If every act has its causes, the excuse valid for one, the tumour, the constraint, should hold for all, and no one would be to blame. Unless the excuse precisely distinguishes between causes, and understanding is not excusing.
- **Can we give up resentment?** : Would a world cured of resentment be gentler or less human? To give up holding things against people, is that an attainable wisdom, or the end of person-to-person relations?
- **From blame to punishment** : If no one deserves at bottom, retribution collapses, but is it to be mourned? Protecting, deterring, and repairing may suffice to ground punishment, without ever speaking of a debt.
- **Real merit or necessary practice?** : Does assigning blame presuppose a merit grounded in metaphysics, or does the practice carry itself, no discovery about determinism able to make us treat persons as objects?

## Grounding relations

- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Hard determinism* grounds *[Moral responsibility](../axes/moral-responsibility.md) : Keeping no accounts* : If every act follows from prior causes, no one is the ultimate author of what they do, and imputation loses its ground: this is the path leading from [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) to Nietzsche toward the innocence of becoming. The link is not necessary: Spinoza himself keeps sanction as an instrument, and P.F. Strawson holds our reactive attitudes to be independent of the determinist thesis.
- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will* grounds *[Moral responsibility](../axes/moral-responsibility.md) : Giving each their due* : If the will is genuinely free, if the agent could have acted otherwise, then their acts are properly their own and praise as much as blame is deserved. [Free will](../glossary/free-will.md) grounds desert in the strong sense: for [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), only a transcendental freedom makes the moral law binding and the fault imputable.
