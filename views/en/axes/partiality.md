# Partiality

**Is it right to give moral priority to those close to us?**

*Others · value · bipolar with median · core axis*

We feel we owe more to our children, our friends, our kin than to strangers; yet morality seems to demand equal regard for all. Favouring our own can slide into injustice; treating everyone alike can become inhuman. To take a position is to say whether loving one's own and being just pull the same way.

## Poles

### Impartialism

Morality demands that we consider every person equally: a stranger's interests count as much as those of someone close to us. Favoring our own is a bias that moral reason must correct.

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) · [Singer](../philosophers/singer.md)*

### Partialism

Our particular bonds, family, friends, community, ground legitimate special obligations. A morality that required us to treat loved ones like strangers would miss what gives moral life its meaning: concrete care for those who depend on us.

*Gilligan · [Confucius](../philosophers/confucius.md)*

### Concentric circles (median position)

Our concern for others spreads out in circles: intense for those close to us, it reaches, fading as it goes, all the way to strangers. Far from contradicting impartiality, this priority given to our own can follow from it: it is with them that we know and can do the most, and a world where each tends first to their own takes better care of everyone. The moral task is not to abolish these circles but to widen outward the care we feel within.
