# Freedom of the will

**Are we genuinely free in our choices, or are they determined?**

*The self · descriptive (theory) · bipolar with median · core axis*

We deliberate as if the future were open, yet our choices have causes. If everything were settled in advance, what would remain of merit, fault, responsibility? Our whole relation to ourselves and to others is at stake.

## Poles

### Free will

The will has the power to determine itself: faced with an alternative, I could have chosen otherwise. This radical freedom grounds personal responsibility and dignity, even if it can weigh on us like a burden.

*[Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) · [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md) · [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)*

### Hard determinism

Our choices result from causes beyond our grasp: nature, history, the unconscious. The feeling of freedom comes from our ignorance of what determines us, like a thrown stone believing itself free to fly.

*[Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) · [Freud](../philosophers/freud.md)*

### Compatibilism (median position)

Compatibilism gives up free will in the strong sense, the power to have chosen otherwise when faced with an alternative, but conceives a freedom within necessity itself. To be free is not to escape causation but to be unconstrained from without: I act freely when my act flows from my own desires and reasons, not from a force that overrides me. Freedom is opposed to external constraint, not to the inner necessity of the agent.

*Common opinion leans toward « Free will ».*

## Live problems

- **Could I have done otherwise?** : A man admits to an offence, but his history and his brain pushed him to it: could he really have acted otherwise, and if not, is he responsible for it?
- **Free without being able to do otherwise?** : Even if I could not have done otherwise, am I free when my act springs from my own desires and reasons, with nothing forcing me against myself?
- **A cause that excuses** : When a specific cause, here a brain tumor, lies at the origin of an act, we hesitate to hold the agent responsible: but does that cause really differ from the thousand others, heredity, history, brain chemistry, that weigh on every one of our choices?
- **A self that initiates itself** : Is there in us a power able to initiate an action without itself being wholly caused, or is every power to act merely one link in the chain of causes?
- **Who decides within me?** : Am I transparent to myself when I choose, or do forces I am unaware of decide in my place, under the appearance of a free choice?
- **The choice within the order of causes** : A choice is an event in the physical world: does it belong to the same chain of causes as everything else, so that it was in principle predictable?
- **Is a reason a cause?** : When I act for a reason, does that reason explain my act the way a cause would, or are there two irreducible ways of accounting for an action?
- **Necessity or chance** : If our choices are not determined, are they any freer for it, or merely left to chance? Does freedom come down to the absence of a cause?
- **Determinism or fatalism?** : If everything has a cause, are my efforts in vain, since the outcome would be settled either way, or do they on the contrary count, being themselves causes among others?
- **Escaping the causes, or understanding them?** : Is being free to depend on no cause, or to understand what determines us, to the point of acting in accord with that necessity rather than undergoing it?
- **All decided, and yet free?** : Some religions affirm both that everything is in God's hands, predestination or fate, and that we remain free and responsible: can these two beliefs hold together, or does one ruin the other?
- **Doing as one pleases, or governing oneself?** : Is being free to do what one feels like in the moment, or to govern oneself, at the risk that whoever follows their every desire becomes its slave?
- **Inner freedom or outer freedom?** : What weighs most in our freedom: our inner dispositions, or the outer constraints of power and society? And does one make the other possible: can one be free in spirit under oppression, or free outwardly without being free within?

## Grounding relations

- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Hard determinism* grounds *[Moral responsibility](../axes/moral-responsibility.md) : Keeping no accounts* : If every act follows from prior causes, no one is the ultimate author of what they do, and imputation loses its ground: this is the path leading from [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) to Nietzsche toward the innocence of becoming. The link is not necessary: Spinoza himself keeps sanction as an instrument, and P.F. Strawson holds our reactive attitudes to be independent of the determinist thesis.
- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Hard determinism* grounds *[The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md) : Rehabilitation* : If the offender could not have acted otherwise, punishing him in the name of desert becomes unjustifiable. [Determinism](../glossary/determinism.md) thus steers sanction toward rehabilitation, which reshapes the causes of behaviour rather than avenging a free fault.
- *[Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md) : Existentialism* grounds *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will* : If existence precedes essence, no given nature settles my choices in advance: I am what I make of myself, and therefore radically free. Existentialism grounds [free will](../glossary/free-will.md), to the point of making freedom the very condition of the human being. Invoking a character or a nature to excuse oneself is then a matter of [bad faith](../glossary/bad-faith.md), according to [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md).
- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will* grounds *[Moral responsibility](../axes/moral-responsibility.md) : Giving each their due* : If the will is genuinely free, if the agent could have acted otherwise, then their acts are properly their own and praise as much as blame is deserved. [Free will](../glossary/free-will.md) grounds desert in the strong sense: for [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), only a transcendental freedom makes the moral law binding and the fault imputable.
- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will* grounds *[The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md) : Retributivism* : If the agent could have acted otherwise, they are the true author of their wrong and bear its merit or demerit. [Free will](../glossary/free-will.md) grounds penal retributivism: punishment is owed in proportion to the fault, because it was freely committed. [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) makes it a demand of justice, not a mere means of deterrence.
- *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will* grounds *[The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md) : Evil has its reasons* : If the will is genuinely free and this freedom is of inestimable worth, moral evil can be understood as the price of creatures able to choose. [Free will](../glossary/free-will.md) thus grounds one of the great justifications of evil: for [Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md), the fault lies with humanity, not with God.
- *[Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : Mechanism* grounds *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Hard determinism* : If every physical event is wholly produced by prior causes, then our choices, as events in this world, are too. Mechanism extended to the mind thus grounds [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md) and leaves [free will](../glossary/free-will.md) without an object.
- *[The enigma of consciousness](../axes/consciousness-nature.md) : Substance dualism* grounds *[Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will* : If the mind does not belong to the mechanical order of matter, the will escapes the determinism of physical causes and can determine itself. Substance dualism thus supports [free will](../glossary/free-will.md), by removing decision from the chain of natural causes.

## Tensions

- With [The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md) : If our acts are fully determined, no one is truly their free author; yet punishing to “give what is deserved” presupposes exactly that responsibility. Holding both creates a tension: retributivism demands a desert that strict determinism removes.
