# The enigma of consciousness

**Can a science of the brain fully explain lived experience?**

*The world · descriptive (theory) · categorical*

Pain, the red I see, the taste of coffee: cognitive science describes ever more finely the brain mechanisms that accompany them, but does it explain what it is like to undergo them? To take a position is to say whether lived experience is a mechanism like any other, an illusion of introspection, or the sign that the mind exceeds anything neuroscience will ever grasp.

## Poles

### Reductive physicalism

Consciousness is nothing but the brain's activity: mental states are physical states that science will one day identify. There is no residual mystery, only complexity still poorly understood.

*Smart · Place*

### Substance dualism

The brain will never fully explain experience, because consciousness is not its product: it is a reality of another nature, the soul, which could in principle exist apart. This is the spontaneous view of many, and that of many religions.

*[Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) · Swinburne*

### Property dualism

The brain is physical, but it has irreducible properties: what it is like to see red or to suffer. This qualitative feel cannot be reduced to neurons, and this is the "hard problem" of consciousness.

*Chalmers*

### Panpsychism

Experience did not arise by miracle in a dead universe: a form of inwardness is a fundamental property of matter, present everywhere in varying degrees, of which our consciousness would be the most elaborate form.

- Galen Strawson frames his view as a "real physicalism", not as adding mind to otherwise inert matter.

*Strawson · Goff*

### Illusionism

The qualitative feel we think we grasp by introspection is an illusion the brain makes about itself. There are no qualia to explain: only mechanisms that make us believe there are.

- Dennett rejects qualia as ordinarily conceived, not the existence of conscious life.

*Dennett · Frankish*
