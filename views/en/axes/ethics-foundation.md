# Criterion of right action

**What makes an action morally good?**

*Others · value · categorical · core axis*

One and the same action can be approved or condemned depending on whether we look at its results, the rule it follows, the character it reveals, or the care it shows toward others. To take a position is to choose which of these compasses to follow when they do not point the same way.

## Poles

### Consequentialism

An action is good if it produces the best possible consequences, for instance the greatest happiness for the greatest number. What ultimately matters is neither intentions nor rules, but the actual effects of our choices on the world.

*[Mill](../philosophers/mill.md) · [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md)*

### Deontologism

An action is good if it conforms to duty, that is, to principles valid for everyone, regardless of its consequences. Some acts, such as lying or treating others as mere means, remain forbidden even when they would be useful.

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)*

### Virtue ethics

The fundamental moral question is not "what should I do?" but "who should I be?". Acting well means acting as a virtuous person would: courageous, just, temperate, practically wise. Morality is a matter of character and education, not merely of rules or calculations.

*[Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)*

### Ethics of care

Morality is rooted in concrete attentiveness to vulnerable people and in the caring relationships that bind us to one another. Rather than abstract, impartial principles, it values responsiveness, responsibility toward others, and sensitivity to particular situations.

*[Gilligan](../philosophers/gilligan.md) · Noddings*

## Live problems

- **The consequences or the forbidden act** : Should an act be judged by its consequences alone, even if that means sacrificing one to save five, or do some acts stay forbidden whatever the cost? → [The trolley problem](../thought-experiments/trolley-problem.md)
- **Saving a loved one rather than a stranger** : We save the one we love without hesitating; but is what makes that act good a calculation, a rule that permits it, good character, or the bond itself, which no morality should require us to justify? → [Saving a loved one rather than a stranger](../thought-experiments/williams-rescue.md)
- **Being good or acting rightly** : Is morality a matter of acts to be judged one by one against rules, or first of all a matter of a person's character, of who one is and how one has learned to see situations?
- **Can one learn to be good?** : Can goodness be taught, like a body of knowledge, or is it acquired through habit? And how is it that we sometimes know the good yet fail to do it?
- **Are there absolutely forbidden acts?** : Do some acts, such as lying or letting an innocent die, stay forbidden even when they would avert a disaster, or does everything give way before the scale of the consequences?
- **The part luck plays** : We judge the reckless driver who ran over a child more harshly than the equally reckless one who happened to meet none: does the moral worth of an act depend on results we do not control?
- **Rivals or facets** : Consequences, duty, virtue, care: are these rival theories of which only one can be true, or the facets of a single morality that nothing compels us to unify?
- **Is the moral standpoint impartial?** : Is judging morally a matter of rising to principles that hold for everyone regardless of persons, or is concrete attentiveness to those close to us the very heart of morality?
- **Where does morality get its authority?** : What makes a moral criterion binding on us: reason, nature, a shared sentiment, society, or God? And if nothing grounds it, does morality still have any hold?
- **A morality too demanding?** : If only the greatest good counts, morality seems to demand sacrificing everything for others without limit: can it really ask that, or must it leave room for our own lives?
- **The rule or the situation** : Is acting well a matter of applying principles fixed in advance, or of judging each situation in its singularity, at the risk that rule and case never quite coincide?

## Grounding relations

- *[Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md) : Essentialism* grounds *[Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : Virtue ethics* : If the human being has a nature and a function of its own, acting well means excelling in the fulfilment of that function, that is, being virtuous. Essentialism grounds virtue ethics: [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) defines the human good by the excellent activity of reason, a matter of character more than of rules.
- *[Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md) : Universalism* grounds *[Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : Deontologism* : If there are objective norms valid for every rational being, the criterion of right action is conformity to duty, not the calculation of effects. Moral universalism grounds deontologism: [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) derives from reason a [categorical imperative](../glossary/categorical-imperative.md) that holds unconditionally, regardless of consequences.
