# Law and conscience

**Can it be right to disobey the law?**

*Others · value · ordered categorical · core axis*

Any law may one day clash with conscience: an iniquitous decree, an order one cannot follow without betraying oneself. To obey nonetheless is to protect what makes common life possible; to disobey is to affirm that above the laws there is something else, at the risk that each person will follow only their own judgment. From Antigone to the great disobediences of history, the question returns with each generation. To take a position is to say what we owe the law when it commands what conscience refuses.

## Poles

### Obedience at all costs

The law binds even when it displeases: it is everyone's obedience that makes common life possible, and whoever exempts themselves in the name of conscience undoes what protects all. One contests, one pleads, one seeks to change the law by the means it provides; one does not break it. Enduring a bad law remains a lesser evil than ruining the authority of all laws.

- Kant hardens the position: no right of resistance, ever; but full freedom of the pen, the citizen obeys and criticizes publicly.
- Hobbes himself keeps one limit: the subject owes everything to the sovereign except the surrender of self-defence, for no one is bound to obey an order to kill or incriminate themselves. Obedience is near-total, not absolute.

*[Socrates](../philosophers/socrates.md) · [Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md)*

### Civil disobedience

When a law commands the unacceptable, conscience has the right, sometimes the duty, to disobey: openly, without violence, accepting the penalty. This disobedience in broad daylight is not a betrayal of the law but an appeal addressed to it: it aims to awaken the community's sense of justice, not to escape the common rule.

- Rawls gives it its theory: in a nearly just society, a public and nonviolent breach whose legal consequences one accepts, addressed to the majority's sense of justice and reserved for grave injustice.
- King: to break an unjust law while accepting prison is to show the highest respect for law.
- Publicity and acceptance of the penalty mark above all the model of Rawls and King; Thoreau's conscientious objection, an individual refusal to pay tax, is its most solitary form.

*[Thoreau](../philosophers/thoreau.md) · [Gandhi](../philosophers/gandhi.md)*

### Resistance to oppression

When power itself betrays its reason for being, violates the rights it was meant to protect, and governs against those it was meant to serve, the obligation ceases: there is no longer a law to respect, only a force one has the right to resist. Resistance is then not a crime against order: it is power that broke the pact first.

- The monarchomachs and the right-of-resistance tradition made it, before Locke, a regulated recourse against the tyrant, not a license for permanent revolt.

*[Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)*

*Common opinion leans toward « Civil disobedience ».*

## Live problems

- **Persuade or obey** : Condemned unjustly, Socrates refuses to escape: the Laws raised him, he accepted their benefits his whole life, and he may persuade them but not betray them. Does the injustice of a verdict permit breaking with what made us?
- **The unwritten laws** : Antigone buries her brother against the city's decree, in the name of laws no one has written: if there is a right above the laws, is a law that violates it still a law, or merely an order?
- **Why obey at all?** : No one signed a contract, and staying in a country is not consenting: one might as well say, with Hume, that one consents to the captain by remaining aboard on the open sea. If neither consent, nor fairness, nor utility grounds a general duty to obey, is political obligation an illusion?
- **Unjust according to whom?** : A law never arrives labeled unjust: someone judges it so. If each conscience decides which laws bind it, what remains of the law shared by all; and if none may, how would injustice ever be denounced?
- **How far may one resist?** : From the strike to sabotage, from tax refusal to armed struggle: does just disobedience have internal limits, publicity, nonviolence, or does extreme oppression lift every bound?

## Grounding relations

- *[Nature of rights](../axes/rights-nature.md) : Natural rights* grounds *[Law and conscience](../axes/political-obligation.md) : Resistance to oppression* : If rights are natural, prior to any law, a power that violates them no longer has title to obedience: the right of resistance follows directly from natural rights. This is [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)'s argument, for whom the people always retain the power to remove a government that betrays its mission of protecting life, liberty, and property.
