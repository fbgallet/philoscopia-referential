# Justification of belief

**What makes a belief justified?**

*Truth · descriptive (theory) · triangular*

From pseudo-sciences to rumours, countless beliefs present themselves as justified: what remains is to know what that warrant rests on, first certainties, the agreement of the whole, or methods that have proven themselves. Depending on the answer, we draw the line between knowledge and what merely looks like it in a different place.

## Poles

### Foundationalism

Knowledge rests on basic beliefs, immediately certain, that need no others to justify themselves: everything else is built upon them, like a building on its foundations.

*[Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)*

### Coherentism

No belief is justified on its own: it is justified by its place in a coherent whole, where each supports the others. An idea holds if it fits without contradiction into the entire web of our experience, with no ultimate foundation.

*Quine · Davidson*

### Reliabilism

A belief is justified if it is produced by a reliable process, perception, memory, sound reasoning, even if the one who holds it could not prove it. What matters is not being able to justify it, but having been formed in the right way.

*[Goldman](../philosophers/goldman.md)*

## Live problems

- **Should you believe it?** : Faced with a claim we cannot check for ourselves, what would make believing it justified: being able to trace it to first-hand evidence, its coherence with all we already know, or the reliability of the source it comes from?
- **The chain of reasons** : Every belief rests on a reason, which rests on another: either the chain stops at beliefs that justify themselves, or it closes into a circle where each supports the others, or it runs off to infinity. These three exits are exactly the three ways of answering "what justifies a belief?".
- **Are there first evidences?** : Foundationalism assumes beliefs that justify themselves, a perception or an evidence so immediate it needs no other. But do we ever perceive anything pure? Even "I see red" already draws on concepts, memory, learning: if there is no truly first given, what does the whole edifice rest on?
- **Coherent, yet false?** : A set of beliefs can be perfectly coherent and yet wholly cut off from reality: a well-built tale, a systematic delusion, a conspiracy theory that explains everything and absorbs every objection. If coherence is not enough to touch the world, must beliefs not also answer to something outside, to the experience that contradicts them?
- **Must you be able to justify yourself?** : To be justified, must one be able to give one's reasons, or is it enough that the belief was well produced? An expert "senses" the right diagnosis without being able to explain it; conversely, we would hesitate to say he knows, the one whose hunches hit the mark through a power he knows nothing of. Does justification lie in the reasons one can give, or in the reliability of the process, even when invisible to the one who exercises it?
- **True by luck** : The classic definition holds knowledge to be a belief that is both true and justified. Yet one can believe something for good reasons, and have it be true, without thereby knowing it: the stopped clock we glance at just at the hour it still shows, the rock we take from afar for a sheep while a real one grazes behind it. The belief is true and justified, but it hits the mark by accident. Must we then redefine what knowledge is, or tighten what we are entitled to demand of a justification, until it links belief to truth otherwise than by luck?
- **Have we the right to believe?** : Beyond what makes a belief justified, is it permissible to believe without sufficient evidence, or is it a fault, toward oneself as toward others? The question of warrant extends into that of the duty of belief.
- **The post-truth age** : The whole question of justification presupposes that there is a truth to reach. But when "alternative facts" and indifference to truth take hold, is it truth itself that wavers, or only our honesty in seeking it?

## Grounding relations

- *[Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md) : Coherence* grounds *[Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md) : Coherentism* : If a statement is true only by its non-contradictory place within an overall system, then a belief is justified only by its coherence with the entire web of the others. The coherence conception of truth supports coherentism: there is no ultimate foundation, but beliefs that support one another, from the Hegelian whole ([Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md)) to Quine's "web".
- *[Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Rationalism* grounds *[Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md) : Foundationalism* : If properly guided reason can reach certain truths, knowledge must rest on basic beliefs, immediately evident, from which all the rest is deduced. Rationalism grounds foundationalism: [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) seeks in the cogito the indubitable fixed point on which to rebuild all knowledge.
