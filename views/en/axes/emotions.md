# Status of emotions

**Are emotions an obstacle to thought or a source of knowledge?**

*The world · descriptive (theory) · bipolar with median*

Should we distrust what we feel, a disturbance that clouds judgement, hear in it a fine perception of what matters, or learn to train it? To take a position is to say how much credit to give our emotions when they contradict cold reason, and whether wisdom lies in silencing them, following them, or shaping them.

## Poles

### The primacy of reason

Emotions cloud judgment: hasty assents or confused stirrings, they distort more often than they reveal what matters. Only reason, by examining them, can sort out what truth they carry.

- Descartes is more nuanced than Stoicism: the passions are good by nature and useful to life, but the soul must regulate them through knowledge rather than extinguish them, which already inclines him toward the middle way.

*[the Stoics](../movements/stoicism.md) · [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)*

### The primacy of feeling

Emotions are not the enemy of thought: they orient us, motivate us, and inform us about what matters. Reason alone is powerless to judge and to act without the help of feeling.

- Nussbaum qualifies the pole: emotions are intelligent value judgments, hence assessable and trainable, which brings her closer to the middle way than to a mere primacy of feeling.

*[Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) · [Nussbaum](../philosophers/nussbaum.md)*

### Educating the emotions (median position)

Emotions are neither a mere disturbance nor an oracle: they are trainable dispositions. Ill-tuned, they lead us astray; shaped by habit and reason, they become an accurate perception, sensing danger, injustice, or grief at the right time and in the right measure. Wisdom lies neither in extirpating them nor in following them blindly, but in tuning them: this is Aristotle's path, for whom the well-raised person feels the right emotion toward the right person, to the right degree.

## Live problems

- **Is an emotion a judgment?** : Is the fear that grips me a thought appraising a danger, as the Stoics and then contemporary cognitivism hold, or the feeling of a bodily change triggered by perception, as William James would have it? What judges can be argued with and corrected; what shakes us can only be tamed.

## Grounding relations

- *[Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : The primacy of reason* grounds *[Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Reasoned selection* : If the passions are not blind forces but judgments about what is worth pursuing, then reason can examine and sort them. To hold desire to be a revisable judgment grounds reasoned selection: one satisfies the desires that withstand examination and dismisses those resting only on a confused opinion. This is the shared move of [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) and the Stoics.
- *[Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : The primacy of feeling* grounds *[Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Affirmation* : If our affects are not obstacles to reason but a way of sensing what matters, desire ceases to be an impulse to correct: it reveals what carries us. To hold emotions to be a source of value rather than a disturbance inclines one to affirm one's desires rather than master them, from Romanticism to a sensitive reading of [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md).
