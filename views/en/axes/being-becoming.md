# Being and becoming

**Is the real, at bottom, what endures or what becomes?**

*The world · descriptive (theory) · bipolar with median*

Everything we see is born, changes, and perishes; and yet we look everywhere for what endures: stable laws, identical things, a self that stays the same. Is the ground of the real an immutable being of which change is only the surface, or a flux in which stable things are merely temporary slowings? On the answer hangs even the way we understand the world: through fixed forms or through processes.

## Poles

### Permanence of being

What really is can neither come to be nor perish: change, whereby a thing is and then is not, contradicts being itself. Sensible becoming is appearance, the opinion of mortals or a world of shadows; thought reaches the true only by rising to what endures, immutable Being or the eternal Forms.

- Plato does not abolish becoming: the changing sensible keeps its consistency as a moving image of eternity; only genuine knowledge requires the immutable.

*[Parmenides](../philosophers/parmenides.md) · [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)*

### Becoming and flux

You cannot step into the same river twice: the real is process, flow, continuous creation, and stability is only a slow rhythm or a construct of the mind. Things are not substances that endure but events in the making; to grasp the world is to follow its movement rather than freeze it into concepts.

- Thinking flux is not denying all stability: Heraclitus' logos governs becoming, and Whitehead's "societies" are enduring patterns; stability there is real, but derivative of process.

*[Heraclitus](../philosophers/heraclitus.md) · [Bergson](../philosophers/bergson.md) · [Whitehead](../philosophers/whitehead.md)*

### Hylomorphism (Aristotle) (median position)

Change is neither an illusion nor the dissolution of all things: it is the passage from potentiality to actuality within a substance that persists. The form endures while the matter is transformed: the seed becomes a tree without ceasing to be that living thing. Being and becoming do not exclude each other; becoming is the way beings realize themselves. Aristotle is the emblem of this middle way, but any thought of substance persisting through change inhabits it.
