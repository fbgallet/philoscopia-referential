# Nature of the self

**What makes me remain myself through time?**

*The self · descriptive (theory) · categorical*

What makes me the same person as the child in the old photographs, or as the one I will be in thirty years? If nothing stable endures, promises, remorse, and pride in a past self lose their footing; if a core remains, no change ever truly remakes me. To take a position is to say what exactly we are holding onto when we say "me."

## Poles

### Substantialism

The self is a substance: a thinking thing, distinct from the body, that remains identical beneath the flow of thoughts and experiences. This permanent core is what each of us refers to when saying "I".

*[Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)*

### Psychological continuity

What constitutes personal identity is not a substance but the continuous chain of mental states: memory, character, intentions. I am the same person as yesterday because my memories and psychological traits connect to it without interruption.

*[Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) · [Parfit](../philosophers/parfit.md)*

### Narrativism

The self is built like a story: we become who we are by telling and reinterpreting our own history. Identity is neither given nor illusory; it is the ever-resumed work of a life woven into a plot.

*[Ricoeur](../philosophers/ricoeur.md)*

### Bundle theory

There is no permanent self: only a bundle of perceptions, thoughts and sensations following one another. The "I" is a convenient fiction, and letting go of it can be a liberation.

*[Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) · [Buddhism](../movements/buddhism.md)*

## Live problems

- **The teletransporter** : If you are scanned here, your body destroyed, and an exact copy with all your memories rebuilt on Mars, is it you who arrives, or did you die, replaced by a double who believes he is you? → [The teletransporter](../thought-experiments/teletransporter.md)
- **One self or many?** : Am I a unified subject who says "I", or an assembly of voices and forces sometimes in conflict, part of which escapes me and acts without my knowing?
- **Letting go of the self** : Is the "I" a convenient fiction, mistaken for a thing? And would letting go of it be a loss, or rather a deliverance?
- **Is the self made alone?** : Does one become oneself through introspection alone, as a self-sufficient subject, or through the gaze and recognition of others, without whom the "I" would not take shape?
- **The self or the body?** : Am I a thinking thing distinct from my body, or am I this very body, so that my identity would hang first of all on it?
- **A given self or a self to make?** : Do I have a nature that precedes me and that I have to discover, or do I become what I choose to be, with no model fixed in advance?

Bundle theory grounds presentism: following [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), letting go of a substantial self invites us to seize the day rather than project ourselves forward or dwell on regret.
- *[Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : Bundle theory* grounds *[Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Extinction and detachment* : If there is no substantial self but a mere flux of states (the [anatta](../glossary/anatta.md) thesis), then desire, which clings to the world in the name of a self to be satisfied, rests on an illusion. To recognize the absence of a self undoes attachment at the root: detachment is no longer an effort of will, but the consequence of a correct view.
- *[Nature of consciousness](../axes/consciousness-nature.md) : Substance dualism* grounds *[Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : Substantialism* : If the mind is a substance distinct from the body, the self that says "I" is this thinking thing, identical to itself beneath the flow of experiences. Substance dualism grounds substantialism: [personal identity](../glossary/personal-identity.md) rests on the permanence of the soul, not on memory or narrative. This is [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)' position.
- *[Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : Substantialism* grounds *[Interiority](../axes/interiority.md) : The examined life* : If there exists in each of us a substantial self, a soul distinct from the flow of appearances, then knowing oneself becomes the highest task. Substantialism grounds the examined life: from Socrates, for whom an unexamined life is not worth living, to [Augustine](../philosophers/augustine.md), who turns inward to find truth there.
- *[Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : Substantialism* grounds *[Death](../axes/death.md) : Immortalism* : If the self is a simple, immaterial substance distinct from the body, nothing requires it to perish when the body decomposes. Substantialism opens the way to the immortality of the soul, as in [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s argument deriving the soul's indestructibility from its simplicity.
