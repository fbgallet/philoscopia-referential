# The source of morality

**What gives morality its authority?**

*Others · descriptive (theory) · categorical · core axis*

Everyone feels that some things are simply not done, but where does that demand get its force? If the good is grounded in God, a society that loses its faith risks losing its morality too, and the line ascribed to Dostoevsky looms: if God does not exist, everything is permitted. If morality rests on reason, on feeling, or on an inherited agreement, it survives secularization, but it remains to be explained why it still binds those who no longer want to play along. To take a position is to say what really upholds our duties, and what would be left of them the day that support gives way.

## Poles

### Grounded in God

The good owes its authority to God: it is because a good God wills, commands, or embodies the good that our duties bind absolutely. Reason can read the moral law, but it does not ground it; without this guarantor, only preferences and calculations would remain, with nothing that truly forbids.

- The pole houses an internal quarrel: is the good good because God wills it (voluntarism, Ockham), or does God will it because it is good? Thomas Aquinas opens a middle path: the good is rooted in God's very nature, neither arbitrary decree nor external standard, and his law remains readable by natural reason.

*[Thomas Aquinas](../philosophers/aquinas.md) · [Dostoevsky](../philosophers/dostoevsky.md)*

### The law of reason

Moral obligation holds for every rational being: a demand that reason discovers or gives itself, like a truth that depends on no decree. Believer or not, whoever reasons runs up against the same duties; it is the universality of reason, not a higher will, that gives morality its force.

- Leibniz, a theist, lodges the good in the eternal truths of the divine understanding, prior to any will: a limit case that grounds morality in reason while locating it in God.

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) · [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md)*

### Moral sentiment

Morality is rooted in a natural sensibility: sympathy, pity, revulsion at the sight of suffering. Reason clarifies, compares, and corrects, but it does not create obligation; the heart is what first tells good from evil, and a shared human nature explains why moralities, beneath their variations, resemble one another so much.

- Mencius gives its classic Chinese version: four innate sprouts (compassion, shame, deference, a sense of right) that moral cultivation makes grow.
- In Hume, sympathy grounds common morality, but justice arises from a useful convention: the sentiment pole communicates here with the convention pole.

*[Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) · [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md)*

### A tacit convention

Morality rests on a tacit agreement, sedimented by a society's history: a tradition of shared judgments about what is done and what is not. Neither the law of a pure reason nor a mere impulse of the heart, this convention holds because we renew it together; its authority is real, but immanent, situated, and therefore revisable.

- Two readings share the pole: an affirmative one (Xunzi: the rites, an artifice of the sages, civilize and genuinely bind) and a debunking one (Nietzsche: genealogy reveals a fabrication and deposes the absolute; Mackie: our moral judgments rest on an error).

*[Xunzi](../philosophers/xunzi.md) · [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md)*

## Live problems

- **The Euthyphro dilemma** : Is the good good because God wills it, or does God will it because it is good? One branch makes morality arbitrary, the other seems to make it independent of God; theologians have been seeking a way between the two ever since.
- **Without God, is everything permitted?** : If no transcendent guarantor upholds our duties, do they keep their binding force, or do they become choices that can always be undone? Secularized societies put the phrase to the test every day.
- **Omnipotence and the good** : If God is all-powerful, does the good depend on his will to the point that he could have commanded otherwise? Descartes ventures it for the eternal truths, Ockham for certain precepts of the moral law, and arbitrariness looms.
- **Can a feeling bind?** : Observing that nature made us compassionate states a fact; concluding that we ought to be so crosses the step from is to ought. If morality is only a feeling, what do we say to someone who does not feel it?
- **The force of custom** : Can a rule that rests only on our agreement bind someone who withdraws from it? Custom binds as long as it is shared, but the reformer who contests it seems to appeal to something beyond it.
- **Does explaining morality destroy it?** : If we can tell how our values were fabricated (history, evolution, ressentiment), does their claim to the absolute collapse, or does a norm's origin say nothing about its validity?
