# The point of philosophy

**What is philosophy for?**

*Truth · value · categorical · core axis*

Anyone who opens a book of philosophy expects something from it: finally understanding the world, changing society, learning to live better, or shedding badly posed questions — when a widespread opinion does not simply deem it useless, superseded by the sciences. Philosophers themselves have never stopped quarreling over their own vocation; this site, which invites you to build your own worldview, itself takes a stand in that debate.

## Poles

### Understanding the world

Philosophy fulfills its vocation by understanding: grasping the order of things, making the real intelligible, is the highest activity we are capable of, and it need serve nothing beyond itself. Knowledge here is not a means but the end: thought comes to embrace what is, as the owl of Minerva takes flight at dusk.

*[Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) · [Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md)*

### Changing the world

Interpreting the world is not enough: the point is to change it. A philosophy that leaves things as they are betrays its task; to think is to criticize injustice and domination, to inform collective action, to work for emancipation. The worth of an idea is measured by what it changes in the world.

*[Marx](../philosophers/marx.md)*

### Transforming oneself

Philosophy is an exercise before it is a discourse: a way of life that works on our desires, fears, and judgments so as to transform the one who practices it. "Empty is the word of the philosopher that heals no human suffering": its worth is that of medicine for the soul and an art of living, not of a system.

*[Hadot](../philosophers/hadot.md) · [the Stoics](../movements/stoicism.md) · [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md)*

### Clarifying thought

Philosophy delivers neither truths about the world nor recipes for living: it teaches us how to think. Its task is to examine critically every belief, every claim, every concept, to test their grounds and untangle confusions; at its sharpest, it shows that many of the great problems are knots in language, to be dissolved rather than solved. Clarity is not a preliminary here: it is the very goal.

*[Wittgenstein](../philosophers/wittgenstein.md)*

## Live problems

- **What is the point?** : Is philosophy's uselessness its very dignity, the one free knowledge pursued for nothing beyond itself, or a real defect next to the sciences and to action, like Thales falling into a well while watching the stars, to the laughter of the Thracian servant girl?
- **Understanding or transforming** : Can philosophy content itself with understanding, or is all contemplation already complicit, arriving too late like the owl of Minerva, when every thought in fact takes sides for or against an order?
- **Discourse or a way of life** : Is philosophy a body of doctrines, or first of all a way of life made of exercises that transform the one who practices them, since reduced to mere theory?
- **Real problems or confusions** : Are the great philosophical problems real questions, or knots of language to be dissolved rather than solved, at the risk of throwing out the questions that matter?
- **Does philosophy make progress?** : After centuries, philosophy has no settled results like the sciences and the same questions keep returning: a failure to dissolve pseudo-problems, or the mark of questions not meant to be settled once and for all?
- **Philosophy and the sciences** : Is philosophy a proto-science that the sciences dethrone as they mature, or does it ask questions, about values, meaning, its own presuppositions, that no science can settle?
- **For everyone or for the few** : Is philosophy the examination each person owes their life, in the agora with Socrates and under the motto "dare to know," or does it remain, through its difficulty and the leisure it demands, the affair of a few?
