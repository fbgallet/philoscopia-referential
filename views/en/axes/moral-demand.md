# Moral demandingness

**How much does morality demand of us?**

*Others · value · bipolar · core axis*

Is it enough to harm no one, or must we give without respite as long as some misery remains to be relieved? A modest morality leaves us in peace but can cover indifference; a demanding one ennobles, but can leave us guilty of all we failed to do. To take a position is to say where duty ends and the heroism no one can require begins.

## Poles

### Minimalism

Morality demands few things, but it demands them firmly: do not harm others, respect their freedom. As long as I wrong no one, my life is my own business, and no one may impose on me an ideal of saintliness or sacrifice.

*[Mill](../philosophers/mill.md) · [Ogien](../philosophers/ogien.md)*

### Maximalism

Morality demands a great deal: if I can prevent a great harm at a modest cost to myself, I must do so. Our duties do not stop at refraining from harm; they include actively helping those most in need, wherever they are, and may claim a substantial share of our resources.

*[Singer](../philosophers/singer.md) · Effective altruism*

## Live problems

- **The drowning child, the starving child** : Everyone grants that we must save the child drowning before our eyes, even at the cost of ruining our clothes; but if distance makes no difference to the duty, as Singer argues, why does it not extend to everyone a donation would suffice to save?
- **Doing and allowing** : Is letting someone die when we could save them as grave as killing? If the difference dissolves, the duty not to harm swells until it covers all the evil we fail to prevent.
- **Saints and heroes** : The heroic act is admired, never required: there would be a realm beyond duty. But if all the good we can do is owed, are these acts that exceed duty anything but duties we excuse ourselves from?
- **A life of one's own** : If we must give as long as any misery remains to be relieved, what legitimate room is left for my projects, my loved ones, my tastes? Does a morality that absorbs a whole life demand too much, or is it our comfort looking for excuses?
- **Between consenting adults** : If no one is wronged without their consent, does morality have nothing left to say? Mill himself refuses to let anyone sell themselves into slavery: is consent enough to permit everything, or are there wrongs we do to ourselves?
- **Charity or justice** : If distant misery stems from unjust institutions we benefit from, giving is no longer enough: the duty would be to repair and transform the order, not to give alms. Is individual giving a duty, or the alibi of an ongoing injustice?
- **What a human being can bear** : Does a demand beyond our strength breed discouragement and hypocrisy, or must the ideal exceed what we are precisely in order to pull us upward? What morality may ask depends on what human beings can give.
