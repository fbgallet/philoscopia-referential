# The fox's freedom

*Objection to [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md) : What is up to us*

**To want only what depends on you, is that not tailoring your desires to your powerlessness?**

## The argument

1. The sage says he no longer desires anything he does not control. But how did he learn to stop wanting it? Often by persuading himself that it was not worth wanting.
2. It is the gesture of the fox in the fable, who declares the grapes too sour because he cannot reach them. [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) saw in it the mainspring of a morality of resignation: lacking power, one makes a virtue of one's powerlessness.
3. If my serenity requires desiring only what I am sure to obtain, it grows only as what I care about shrinks. It is a real peace, but paid for by an impoverishment of life.

**⇒** So the sage's freedom might be no conquest, but a renunciation in disguise: the calm of one who has ceased to want, for want of power.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The objection hits home: my calm sometimes looks like expectations I have lowered. I must also hold to things that do not depend on me, and act for them.** : You hand back to action a share of what acceptance had set aside: the path of discernment.
- **I keep the Stoic path, but set it apart from self-denial: to accept what I cannot change, without convincing myself that nothing outside is worth desiring.** : You hold acceptance in its exact sense: not shrinking your desires, but not leaving your peace to chance.
- **I refute it: seeing clearly what does not depend on me is not spite. The sage's calm is fuller, not poorer, than the restlessness of one who wants everything.** : You distinguish serenity from bitter renunciation: to give up your grip on the world is not to give up on life.
