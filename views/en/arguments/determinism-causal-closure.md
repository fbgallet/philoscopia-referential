# The causal-closure argument

*Argument for [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Hard determinism*

**Every effect has a cause: why would our decisions make an exception?**

## The argument

1. Everywhere we look, we find causes: on inspection, no phenomenon turns out to have no antecedent. The physical world appears [causally closed](../glossary/causal-closure.md), every effect within it having a cause that is itself physical.
2. A decision is one event among others, carried by a physical brain woven into that same causal fabric.
3. By the [principle of sufficient reason](../glossary/principle-of-sufficient-reason.md), nothing happens without a reason that determines it; to grant the will alone a causeless power would be an exception without justification.

**⇒** Our decisions are therefore [determined](../glossary/determinism.md) by their causes, like any other event.

## Deepenings

### Why would nothing happen without a cause?

1. The principle of sufficient reason states that, for every fact, there is a reason why it is so rather than otherwise.
2. To deny it is not to discover a freedom, it is to give up understanding: an event with no cause at all would be, literally, unintelligible.

### Laplace's demon

1. Laplace imagines an intelligence that would know, at a given instant, all the forces of nature and the position of every being.
2. For such an intelligence, he writes, nothing would be uncertain: the future, like the past, would be present to its eyes.
3. The image is not about what we can predict, but about what strict determinism commits us to: everything, in principle, is already settled.

*Laplace, *A Philosophical Essay on Probabilities*, 1814*
