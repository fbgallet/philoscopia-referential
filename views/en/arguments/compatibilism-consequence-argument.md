# The consequence argument

*Objection to [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Compatibilism*

**If the past and the laws do not depend on us, neither do our acts.**

*[Peter van Inwagen](../philosophers/van-inwagen.md), *An Essay on Free Will*, 1983*

## The argument

1. If [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md) is true, my acts follow necessarily from the state of the world long before my birth and from the laws of nature.
2. But I have no power over that distant past, nor over the laws of nature: none of this is up to me.
3. And what is not up to me, its necessary consequences are not up to me either.

**⇒** So, if everything is determined, my acts are not up to me: redefining freedom does not suffice to make them mine.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The argument lands: if there is determinism, freedom truly vanishes. I grant it, and with it the idea of being the author of my acts.** : You follow the incompatibilist consequence all the way, on the side of [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md): no real power over the course of causes.
- **If freedom truly requires a power over the consequences, then I rather doubt that everything is determined.** : You draw the same consequence the other way: holding to a real freedom, you reject strict determinism.
- **I refute it: the argument trades on "up to me". Acting from my reasons IS a form of power, even if those reasons have a history.** : You reject the transfer of "no power" principle: [compatibilism](../glossary/compatibilism.md) redefines precisely what "power" means.
