# The self-ownership argument

*Argument for [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md) : Libertarianism*

**I own myself: no one has over my body, my time, and my labor a right that I have not granted them. What comes of it is therefore mine.**

*[Nozick](../philosophers/nozick.md), [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md), *[Anarchy, State, and Utopia](../works/anarchy-state-and-utopia-nozick.md)*, 1974*

## The argument

1. Each person owns themselves, their body, their time, their powers; no one else holds a prior right over them, else slavery would be legitimate. It is a [right over oneself](../axes/rights-nature.md) that no society confers and none may withdraw.
2. Now what I produce with what is mine, and what others freely transfer to me in exchange, extends that ownership: the fruit of labor and free exchange belongs to me on the same footing.
3. A distribution is then just according to how it came about, through legitimate acquisitions and transfers, not according to the shape it forms at a given instant: there is no 'right snapshot' of wealth to be enforced.
4. Hence, forcibly taking part of my earnings to redraw that snapshot is to dispose of my labor without my consent, thus to treat me as if I belonged in part to others.

**⇒** So an imposed redistribution is not a fair sharing but a violation of rights: justice bears on the legitimacy of acquisitions, not on equality of outcome.

## Deepenings

### Original acquisition

1. The whole chain of holdings goes back to a first title: how does an unowned thing become legitimately mine?
2. By mixing my labor with it, answers [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md), provided that 'enough, and as good' is left for others. This original title, passed on by free exchange, grounds all the rest.

*[Locke](../philosophers/locke.md), *[Two Treatises of Government](../works/two-treatises-of-government-locke.md)**
