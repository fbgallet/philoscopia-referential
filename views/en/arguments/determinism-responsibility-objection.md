# The responsibility argument

*Objection to [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Hard determinism*

**If everything is determined, no one is responsible for anything.**

## The argument

1. Holding someone responsible, praising or punishing them, presupposes that they could have [acted otherwise](../glossary/alternate-possibilities.md) than they did.
2. Likewise, deliberating makes sense only if the outcome is not already settled as one weighs the pros and cons.
3. A strict determinism removes both presuppositions at once, and with them part of what structures law and moral life.
4. This cost is no refutation, but it weighs: a thesis that dissolves responsibility must pay the price, or show how to save it otherwise.

**⇒** A strict determinism therefore strips responsibility of its usual ground.

## Deepenings

### The testimony of remorse

1. Remorse is not mere regret: it carries the stubborn thought "I could have, and should have, acted otherwise".
2. This moral experience, widely shared and hard to dismiss, gives body to the idea of real alternatives that strict determinism declares illusory.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The objection lands: I grant a real margin of freedom in our choices.** : The moral cost makes you step back: your position moves away from strict determinism.
- **I keep determinism, but I salvage responsibility as a social usefulness, not as free will.** : A [compatibilist](../glossary/compatibilism.md) answer: blame and punishment keep a sense as levers, even without a power to choose otherwise.
- **I refute it: deliberation and responsibility are compatible with causes; they are part of the process.** : You deny that "determined" entails "not responsible": the strong compatibilist option, holding determinism while conceding nothing on morality.
