# The illusion argument

*Objection to [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will*

**This feeling of freedom might be nothing but ignorance of our causes.**

*[Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), *Letter to Schuller*, 1674*

## The argument

1. Every state of the world follows from the prior state according to laws: the [determinist](../glossary/determinism.md) hypothesis, which succeeds everywhere else, from stars to cells.
2. But my brain, which deliberates, is a fragment of that world; its states too therefore follow from prior causes.
3. The lived moment of "choice" is then merely a link in that chain: felt from within, but already under way before I decide it.

**⇒** The feeling of choosing freely is therefore no proof of freedom.

## Deepenings

### Libet's experiments

1. From the 1980s, Libet records a cerebral "readiness potential" that precedes the awareness of having decided by several hundred milliseconds.
2. If the act is initiated before the conscious decision, the feeling of "choosing now" would come after the fact, as a commentary on what is already under way.

*Libet, 1983*

### Spinoza's image of the stone

*The thrown stone that would think its flight free.*

1. Spinoza imagines a thrown stone that, for the span of its flight, were given consciousness and thought. Feeling its motion but unaware of the hand that cast it, it would believe itself free to go where it goes.
2. Such would be our condition: we perceive our desires and our decisions, but not the causes that produced them, and we mistake that ignorance for freedom.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The objection is decisive: my choices result from causes beyond me.** : You let the objection decide: your position slides toward [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md).
- **I keep a freedom, but redefined: acting without constraint, rather than an absolute power to choose otherwise.** : This is the path of [compatibilism](../glossary/compatibilism.md): freedom as absence of constraint, compatible with causes.
- **I refute it: that my choice has causes does not show it was constrained; my deliberation is one of them.** : You distinguish "having a cause" from "being constrained": a caused deliberation can still be yours. The classic reply to determinism.
