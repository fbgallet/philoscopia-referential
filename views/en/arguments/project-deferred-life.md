# We always put off living

*Objection to [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md) : Project*

**By always living for tomorrow, one realizes too late that one has never lived.**

*[Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md), *[On the Shortness of Life](../works/on-the-shortness-of-life-seneca.md)*, 49*

## The argument

1. Suppose that to exist is to project oneself. The danger is only the greater: for the project always pushes life off to the moment when it will be accomplished, and that moment endlessly recedes.
2. [Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md), in *[On the Shortness of Life](../works/on-the-shortness-of-life-seneca.md)*, observed it among the busy men of Rome: wholly given to their undertakings, they lived as though they would live forever, putting off the best until later. They arrange their lives for the far off, and death surprises them in the midst of their preparations, before they have begun to live.
3. This deferral is no accident of the project, it is its slope: the busyness that thinks itself useful is often one more [divertissement](../glossary/divertissement.md), filling the hours so as not to have to ask whether one is living them. We save time everywhere, except at living.

**⇒** So to project oneself without end can be the subtlest way of missing one's life: not out of laziness, but by dint of preparing it instead of living it.

## Deepenings

### The busy, according to Seneca

1. Seneca does not target the idle, but the most active, magistrates, the ambitious, men overwhelmed with affairs, who never have an hour to themselves; he calls them the « occupied ».
2. His reproach is not that they work, but that they never belong to themselves: their time is everyone's, except their own. « Life is long, if you know how to use it », he writes: it is not time that is lacking, it is we who are lacking to ourselves.

*[Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md), *[On the Shortness of Life](../works/on-the-shortness-of-life-seneca.md)**

## Ways out of the objection

- **Seneca is right: by dint of preparing my life, I keep deferring it. The only time I have is now, and it is now that I want to live.** : The objection moves you from the project to the present: no longer to live for tomorrow, but to stop putting off until tomorrow the act of living.
- **I keep projecting myself, but I make myself live along the way: a goal, yes, provided I do not hang all my days on it.** : You keep the project while drawing its venom: to move toward a goal without ceasing to inhabit the journey.
- **To live for a goal is not to defer it: it is the goal that gives each day its direction and its savor. Seneca condemns the busybody, not the one who knows why he acts.** : You distinguish busyness from the project: it is not projecting oneself that makes one miss one's life, it is bustling about without knowing toward what.
