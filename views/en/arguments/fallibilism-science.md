# Science as revisable knowledge

*Argument for [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Fallibilism*

**Our most solid knowledge knows itself to be revisable.**

*[Charles Sanders Peirce](../philosophers/peirce.md)*

## The argument

1. Look at how science proceeds, our most reliable form of knowledge: it lays down no untouchable dogmas, it puts forward theories and exposes them tirelessly to the test of the facts.
2. When a theory fails, it is corrected or replaced: Newton's mechanics, long held to be definitive, gave way to Einstein's, without knowledge collapsing for all that. It progressed.
3. Such knowledge is therefore neither a dogma nor a doubt: it holds its best results to be true as far as can be established, while knowing them **revisable**. Revision is no weakness here, it is its very method.

**⇒** So one can know in earnest without claiming definitive certainties: [fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md) is no soft compromise, it is the very attitude of our surest knowledge.

## Deepenings

### Conjectures and refutations

1. Karl Popper made it the heart of his philosophy: a scientific theory is never proved once and for all, it is offered as a bold conjecture that one then strives to refute.
2. What distinguishes a science from a fixed belief is not that it is certain, it is that it accepts in advance what could bring it down. Its strength lies in its very revisability.

*[Popper](../philosophers/popper.md), *Conjectures and Refutations*, 1963*
