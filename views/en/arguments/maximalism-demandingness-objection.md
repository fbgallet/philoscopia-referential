# A life of one's own

*Objection to [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md) : Maximalism — attacks the position*

**Pushed to its end, the maximalist principle never stops: it devours the projects, the attachments, the very life of the one it claims to make good.**

*[Bernard Williams](../philosophers/williams.md), Susan Wolf, *'Moral Saints'*, 1982*

## The argument

1. The maximalist principle, pushed to its end, never stops: as long as an evil remains preventable at a cost lesser than itself, I must give, my time, my money, my projects, up to the point where I would myself need rescuing.
2. But a human life rests on attachments and projects pursued for their own sake, not to maximize the world's good. To summon them to yield at every instant to the calculus of the greater good is to tear from the agent their very **integrity**.
3. A 'moral saint' who acted only to relieve misery would have an impoverished life, without projects of their own, almost inhuman: not an ideal, but the dissolution of the person into an endless demand.

**⇒** So morality cannot demand everything: there must be a part of life that is my own, failing which duty devours the very person it claims to make good.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: a morality that absorbs a whole life demands too much. Strict duty stops at not harming; the rest is mine.** : You return toward minimalism: supererogation exists, sacrifice is admired, not required.
- **I keep a strong duty of aid, but a bounded one: a fair share, compatible with my projects and my loved ones, not an endless sacrifice.** : A tempered maximalism: aid is a real duty, but morality leaves room for a life of one's own.
- **'Integrity' rings hollow before a dying child: it is often the privileged who invoke their own life so as not to share. The demand seems excessive because the world is.** : The maximalist reply: the agent's comfort does not set the measure of duty; the scale of preventable evil does.
