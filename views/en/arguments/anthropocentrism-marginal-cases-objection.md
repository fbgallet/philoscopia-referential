# The argument from marginal cases

*Objection to [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md) : Anthropocentrism — attacks the reasoning*

**If reason is the criterion, then infants and the severely cognitively impaired do not meet it either, and yet they count fully.**

*[Singer](../philosophers/singer.md), [Ryder](../philosophers/ryder.md), *Animal Liberation*, 1975*

## The argument

1. The argument makes reason, responsible agency, the criterion of full moral status.
2. But many humans do not meet it: infants, people with advanced dementia, the severely cognitively impaired can neither promise nor answer for their acts. We nonetheless grant them full dignity.
3. Either one keeps the criterion and excludes these humans, which no one accepts; or one includes them through what they share with animals, the capacity to suffer and to enjoy, and that criterion then holds for animals too.

**⇒** So reason cannot be the real criterion: what brings a being into the moral circle is not reasoning but the capacity to suffer, and that many animals have.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: if I protect the infant who cannot yet reason, I can no longer exclude the animal that suffers in the name of reason. The criterion is sentience.** : You join [Singer](../philosophers/singer.md): the moral boundary is sentience, and privileging by species is a speciesism.
- **I grant consideration to sentient animals, but I maintain a difference of degree: the person keeps a dignity that sentience alone does not confer.** : A softened anthropocentrism: animals count, but less; the hierarchy remains, without being an exclusion.
- **The infant and the gravely ill belong to a rational species and are held in human bonds: it is as members of the community of persons that they count, not as mere sentient beings.** : The 'species norm' reply: one counts as a member of a rational kind, even when not exercising its reason.
