# Never a mere means

*Argument for [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : Deontologism*

**A person has a dignity, not a price: one cannot treat them as a mere means, whatever good might be got from it.**

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), *[Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals](../works/groundwork-of-the-metaphysics-of-morals-kant.md)**

## The argument

1. There is a difference between things, which have a price and can be replaced, and persons, who have a [dignity](../glossary/categorical-imperative.md): they are worth something as ends in themselves, not for the service got from them.
2. To use someone as a mere means, to sacrifice them, deceive them, instrumentalize them 'for the good', is to deny that dignity, to reduce them to a useful thing. The gain obtained does not redeem that denial.
3. Some acts therefore carry this violation in themselves, independently of their happy outcomes: what makes them unjust is not their balance sheet, but what they do to the person.

**⇒** So the right action is the one that conforms to duty, to principles valid for all and respectful of each as an end; and some prohibitions, such as treating another as a mere means, hold whatever the cost.
