# Preventing harm when one can

*Argument for [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md) : Maximalism*

**If I can prevent a great harm at the cost of a minor sacrifice, I ought to do so, wherever the victim may be: our duties do not stop at refraining from harm.**

*[Singer](../philosophers/singer.md), *'Famine, Affluence, and Morality'*, 1972*

## The argument

1. Suffering and death for want of food, shelter, or care are evils: no one disputes it.
2. Now if we can prevent such an evil without sacrificing anything comparable, a little money, a little comfort, we ought to do so: it is what everyone feels before [the drowning child](../thought-experiments/drowning-child.md). To refuse this principle is to say that a dying child counts for less than our small comforts.
3. This principle knows no border: neither remoteness, nor the fact that others could help, nor the number of victims lessens the obligation; what counts is that I can act, and at what cost.

**⇒** So morality demands far more than 'do no harm': as long as a great evil remains preventable at a modest cost, not acting is consenting to it. Helping the most destitute, wherever they are, is a [duty](../axes/partiality.md), not a favor.

## Deepenings

### Why distance does not count

1. Proximity was never a moral reason, only a practical limit: once, one could neither know nor act at a distance.
2. Now that one can, the excuse falls: a child's death weighs no less for happening ten thousand kilometers away, and a transfer reaches him as surely as an outstretched arm.

*[Singer](../philosophers/singer.md), *'Famine, Affluence, and Morality'*, 1972*
