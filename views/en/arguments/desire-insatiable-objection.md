# The leaky jar

*Objection to [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Affirmation*

**To affirm all your desires is to condemn yourself to never being satisfied.**

*[Plato](../philosophers/plato.md), [Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md)*

## The argument

1. Affirming desire assumes that, by following it, one can reach a kind of fullness: we want, we obtain, we enjoy.
2. But a satisfied desire does not die out, it shifts: no sooner is the wanted thing reached than a new lack arises, and the pleasure of having gives way to the desire for something else. [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) compared the insatiable soul to a leaky jar, filled without ever being full.
3. Whoever says yes to all their desires is therefore not the master of their life, but the servant of the next lack: they run, and what they pursue draws back by just as much.

**⇒** So unreserved affirmation does not lead to the promised fullness, but to a restlessness that never closes.

## Deepenings

### The pendulum of lack and boredom

1. Schopenhauer saw the life of willing as a pendulum: as long as a desire is unmet, we suffer from the lack; once it is met, boredom creeps in, and we must desire anew to escape it.
2. Pleasure, on this view, is never more than a brief respite between two pains, never a state in which one could rest.

*[Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md), *The World as Will and Representation*, 1818*

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: following all my desires leads nowhere. Better to tell apart those that truly fulfill me from those that merely set me off again.** : You move from affirmation to sorting: this is already [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md)'s gesture, separating the desires that bring calm from those that stir us up.
- **I keep affirming my desires, but I admit it: none gives lasting rest. It is the impulse that counts, not the arrival.** : You shift the worth of a life from the goal to the movement: no longer the fulfillment, but the intensity of wanting.
- **What you call restlessness, I call life. A desire that revives another is not a failure, it is the sign of an existence that does not come to a halt.** : This is [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md)'s reply: what wants to grow must want beyond itself; rest, here, would be an impoverishment.
