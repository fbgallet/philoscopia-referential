# The comfort of the middle

*Objection to [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md) : Knowing the difference*

**To call yourself « clear-eyed » about what depends on you, is that not often a license to do little?**

## The argument

1. The path of discernment has common sense on its side: who would refuse to « weigh things up »? It is even the position everyone believes they already hold.
2. But this obviousness is a trap: it is convenient to file under the « impossible » precisely what change would cost us. We often draw the boundary of the possible where our courage stops, not where our power stops.
3. Discernment may then serve as an alibi: it eases the conscience of inaction by naming it clear-sightedness, and of giving up by calling it maturity. The middle way may be nothing but the line of least resistance.

**⇒** So discernment, lacking a demand to hold it firm, can degenerate into complacency: a wisdom of façade for those who want neither truly to struggle nor truly to let go.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The objection hits home: too often I declare « impossible » what would mainly take courage. Better to push action further.** : You correct the boundary upward: when in doubt, presume it depends on you, and try.
- **The other way: if I distrust my own alibis, I also distrust the agitation that thinks itself all-powerful. Better an acceptance owned for what it is.** : You pull the objection the other way: rather than a good-conscience activism, a clear-eyed serenity.
- **I refute it: that it can be misused does not condemn discernment; the remedy is not to pick a camp, but to exercise that judgment with rigor.** : You hold discernment to be a task, not a comfort: asking yourself endlessly whether the « impossible » truly is.
