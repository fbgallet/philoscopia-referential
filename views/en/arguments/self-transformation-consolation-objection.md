# To heal oneself, or to seek the truth?

*Objection to [The point of philosophy](../axes/metaphilosophy.md) : Transforming oneself — attacks the position*

**Making philosophy a medicine for the soul risks reducing it to a consolation, one that prefers inner peace to truth.**

*[Socrates](../philosophers/socrates.md), *Apology of Socrates**

## The argument

1. Philosophy-as-exercise is judged by its effect on the one who practices it: it is worth something if it soothes, heals, helps one live better.
2. But what consoles is not necessarily what is true: a comforting illusion 'heals' better than a bitter truth. To measure philosophy by its benefit is to risk preferring what soothes to what is.
3. And turning toward one's own salvation may turn one away from the world: while one works on one's serenity, injustice remains. The art of living risks being merely a retreat, a care of self that abandons truth and others.

**⇒** So philosophy cannot be reduced to a therapy: the search for truth may disquiet rather than soothe, and to think is not first of all to heal oneself.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: to think is to seek the truth even when it unsettles, not to reassure oneself. Critical rigor comes before comfort.** : You lean toward clarification, or contemplation: philosophy must examine without complacency, not console.
- **I keep philosophy as an art of living, but it is worth something only if it rests on truth, not on convenient consolations.** : A demanding art of living: self-transformation must bend to truth, on pain of being merely a balm.
- **This opposition is false: the Ancients did not seek to lie to themselves but to live according to the true. The exercise transforms precisely because it makes one see things as they are, not to flee but to stop telling oneself stories.** : The defense of the art of living: ancient wisdom unites the true and living well, it does not oppose them.
