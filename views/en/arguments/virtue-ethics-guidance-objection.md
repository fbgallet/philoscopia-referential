# But what exactly should I do?

*Objection to [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : Virtue ethics — attacks the position*

**'Act as the virtuous person would': but who is she, if not the one who acts well? The criterion goes in a circle and never says what to do.**

## The argument

1. Virtue ethics offers this rule: do what the virtuous person would do. But who is this person? The one who does what ought to be done.
2. The criterion goes in a circle: it defines the right action by the virtuous agent, and the virtuous agent by the right action, without ever saying, in the concrete case, what ought to be done.
3. And when equally admirable people judge in opposite ways, whether to lie to save a friend, to fight or to yield, the appeal to character stays silent, where a rule or a calculation do give an answer.

**⇒** So virtue ethics may describe the good agent, but it does not guide action: it lacks the criterion that duty or consequences provide.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The objection lands: I want to know what to do, not only whom to admire. A morality must give a rule or a calculation that decides in the concrete case.** : You ask for a decision procedure, on the side of duty or consequences: exactly what virtue ethics refuses to supply.
- **I keep the primacy of character, but grant that we need some markers: the virtues themselves, to be honest, courageous, just, point a direction, even without a recipe.** : You hold the reply from 'virtue rules': do what is honest, generous, just, markers without a mechanical procedure.
- **I refute it: the absence of a mechanical procedure is not the absence of guidance. Practical wisdom really does guide; it is the dream of a rule that would decide for us that is the illusion.** : You hold the neo-Aristotelian reply: no rule dispenses with judgment; the sage's discernment is no less a guide for not being a recipe.
