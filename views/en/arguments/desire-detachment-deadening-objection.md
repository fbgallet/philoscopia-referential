# To live, or to withdraw from life?

*Objection to [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Extinction and detachment*

**To detach from your desires may be to withdraw from life itself.**

## The argument

1. Detachment promises serenity: to expect nothing more from what does not depend on us is to be no longer wounded by its loss.
2. But what binds us to the world is precisely desire: to love, to hope, to commit, is to hold to things one can lose. To cut away this attachment in order to protect oneself is to risk no longer loving anything enough to suffer from it, hence no longer truly loving.
3. The peace thus gained might then resemble an absence: not the fullness of the sage, but the lukewarmness of one who has put himself out of reach by withdrawing from everything.

**⇒** So detachment, pushed to the end, threatens to confuse serenity with indifference, and liberation with the renunciation of living.

## Deepenings

### Serenity or indifference?

1. The [Stoics](../movements/stoicism.md) distinguished [apatheia](../glossary/apatheia.md), freedom from the troubling passions, from insensibility: the sage is not made of stone, he loves and acts, but without being the slave of what he loves.
2. The objection asks whether this distinction holds in practice: can one really hold to someone while staying ready to lose them unshaken, or does detachment always end up cooling what it is applied to?

## Ways out of the objection

- **Extinguishing everything would be too much. Rather than detach from all, better to choose what I hold to and lighten the rest.** : You move from extinction to sorting, as [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) invited: not renouncing all desire, but keeping the right ones and letting the others go.
- **I keep the path of detachment, but set it apart from indifference: freeing oneself from the grip of desire does not require ceasing to love.** : You hold [apatheia](../glossary/apatheia.md) in its exact sense: not a dry heart, but a heart that loss no longer overthrows.
- **What you call a taste for life is often what keeps us in servitude. To detach is not to stop loving, it is to stop fearing loss.** : You hold to detachment's demand: true freedom begins where I no longer need things to be other than they are.
