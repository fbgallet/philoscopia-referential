# The root of suffering

*Argument for [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Extinction and detachment*

**Desire chains us to what escapes us: to free oneself from it is to dry up the source of torment.**

*[Epictetus](../philosophers/epictetus.md), [The Buddha](../philosophers/buddha.md), *Enchiridion; the four noble truths**

## The argument

1. To desire is to expect something of the world: that this event happen, that this person stay, that this thing come back to us. Yet none of that [depends entirely on us](../axes/control-scope.md).
2. Our suffering arises precisely from this gap: not from things themselves, but from our demand that they be other than they are. [Buddhism](../movements/buddhism.md) calls this thirst [tanha](../glossary/tanha.md), and sees in it the origin of [dukkha](../glossary/dukkha.md); the [Stoics](../movements/stoicism.md) remind us that nothing troubles us but our judgment about what is not ours.
3. If suffering comes from attachment, deliverance comes from loosening its grip: no longer to demand, but to welcome. To stop wanting things to be otherwise is to strip them of all power to wound us.

**⇒** So to quiet one's desires is not to maim oneself, but to set oneself free: it is to take back from the outside the governing of one's own peace.

## Deepenings

### What depends on us

1. [Epictetus](../philosophers/epictetus.md) divides everything in two: what depends on us (our judgments, our desires) and what does not (the body, fortune, the opinion of others); all wisdom lies in desiring only the first. This is the [dichotomy of control](../glossary/dichotomy-of-control.md).
2. [Buddhism](../movements/buddhism.md) goes further: the extinction of thirst is [nirvana](../glossary/nirvana.md) itself, not a dull emptiness, but the end of what was burning us.
