# The only unconditional good

*Argument for [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : Virtue / moral perfectionism*

**Pleasure, wealth, talent can serve the worst ends; only uprightness is good in every case. The true good is the one that cannot be misused.**

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), [the Stoics](../movements/stoicism.md), *[Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals](../works/groundwork-of-the-metaphysics-of-morals-kant.md)**

## The argument

1. Let us review what we take to be goods: intelligence, courage, wealth, power, and even happiness itself. All have this in common, that they can serve evil: the cunning of the deceiver, the cold blood of the criminal, the prosperity of the tyrant make them harmful.
2. A good that, misused, becomes an evil is therefore good only conditionally: it is worth something if the one who uses it wills well. What decides its worth is not the good itself, but the **will** that puts it to use.
3. Now one thing alone stays good whatever the use, because it is precisely the rightness of that use: the **good will**, virtue, willing the just for its own sake. It alone cannot be turned to evil, for it is that through which all the rest becomes good or bad.

**⇒** So virtue alone has unconditional worth; and if the highest good is what is worth something without reservation, then it is moral uprightness, not pleasure or flourishing, that deserves the name.

## Deepenings

### The Stoic argument for the one good

*A true good is what, in every circumstance, benefits and can never harm; virtue alone meets that test.*

1. The [Stoics](../movements/stoicism.md) go further: a true good is what, in every circumstance, benefits the one who has it, and can never harm.
2. Now health can soften, wealth can corrupt, pleasure can enslave: none of these 'goods' benefits for certain. Virtue alone always benefits and never harms, both its holder and those around them.

**⇒** So, by this test, virtue alone is a good; the rest, health, wealth, reputation, is neither good nor bad, but 'indifferent' matter that virtue uses well or badly.
