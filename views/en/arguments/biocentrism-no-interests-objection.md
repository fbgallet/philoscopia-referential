# A good, but for whom?

*Objection to [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md) : Biocentrism — attacks the position*

**A plant has no interests, only needs: without a subject to whom its fate can matter, there is no one to wrong.**

*[Singer](../philosophers/singer.md), *Practical Ethics**

## The argument

1. Biocentrism grants a worth of its own to all that pursues its good, sentient or not.
2. But 'pursuing a good' is said in two senses: a clock 'wants' to tell the time, a virus 'wants' to replicate, without anything mattering to anyone. A genuine interest presupposes a subject to whom its fate can make a difference.
3. Without sentience, there is no point of view from which living or dying is good or bad for the organism: one can destroy it, one does not wrong it; 'respect' has no addressee.

**⇒** So moral worth requires a being able to feel its good: below sentience there are admirable mechanisms, not subjects toward whom we would have duties.

## Ways out of the objection

- **Fair enough: without a being that feels, there is no one to wrong. Only sentience brings one into the circle.** : You return to pathocentrism: the line of the circle is that of beings with a point of view on their life.
- **I place sentience first, but I keep a respect for the living that forbids destroying it without reason.** : A mixed position: suffering obligates first of all, life in general calls for restraint.
- **The difference lies not in feeling but in orientation toward an end: a being that pursues its own unfolding is not a clock, and to scorn it remains a wrong.** : The biocentric reply: having a telos of one's own suffices for worth, independently of consciousness.
