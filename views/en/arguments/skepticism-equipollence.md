# The equipollence argument

*Argument for [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Skepticism*

**To every thesis one can oppose a contrary thesis of equal strength.**

*[Sextus Empiricus](../philosophers/sextus-empiricus.md), *[Outlines of Pyrrhonism](../works/outlines-of-pyrrhonism-sextus-empiricus.md)**

## The argument

1. For each claim I take to be true, I can find contrary reasons of comparable force: the senses vary from one witness to another, and every proof rests on a further proof that is itself open to dispute.
2. But when the case for and against are in balance, nothing justifies leaning to one side rather than the other: that would be to choose without reason.
3. To affirm regardless would be to add a belief the reasons do not support, and so to be mistaken about what one is entitled to take as settled.

**⇒** Therefore the most lucid course is to suspend judgment, wherever no decisive reason settles the matter.

## Deepenings

### Why does the contrary always have "equal force"?

1. The ancient skeptics catalogue "modes" that reopen the debate on any subject: the persistent disagreement among the learned, the variation of perceptions across observers, the fact that every proof demands a proof.
2. As long as these levers remain available, no thesis prevails without a counterweight being available against it: the balance is no accident, it is the rule.
