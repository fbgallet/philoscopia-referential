# The equal worth of each

*Argument for [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md) : Impartialism*

**A stranger's suffering weighs no less than a loved one's.**

## The argument

1. A pain is a pain, whether the one who feels it is my own or a stranger: that they are "mine" is a fact about me, not about the worth of their suffering.
2. To give priority to my own simply because they are mine is a [preference that reason cannot justify](../glossary/impartiality.md), any more than favoring one's nation or one's color.
3. [Singer](../philosophers/singer.md) shows it with a clear case: if I dive in to save the child drowning before me, neither distance nor the absence of a bond changes what I owe to the child dying elsewhere.

**⇒** Morality therefore asks us to count each one equally: partiality toward our own is a leaning, not a just reason.

## Deepenings

### The expanding circle

1. Moral history resembles a circle of consideration that widens: from family to clan, then to the nation, then to humanity, and perhaps one day to animals able to suffer.
2. With each widening, a boundary once thought natural appears, in hindsight, as a mere prejudice.

*[Singer](../philosophers/singer.md), *The Expanding Circle*, 1981*
