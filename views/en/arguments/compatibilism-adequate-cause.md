# Freedom as adequate cause

*Argument for [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Compatibilism*

**We are freer the more we understand what determines us.**

*[Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), *[Ethics](../works/ethics-spinoza.md)*, 1677*

## The argument

1. An effect can depend on me in two ways: either it follows from my nature alone, and I am its **adequate cause**; or its explanation requires outside forces that I undergo, and I am only its partial cause.
2. In the first case I am active, and free to that extent; in the second, passive. Yet most of our desires move us unawares: freedom is therefore not all-or-nothing, but a matter of degree.
3. This degree grows not by escaping causes but by **understanding** them: knowing why I desire what I desire, I act from myself understood, not from an outside that stirs me. No power to do otherwise is required.

**⇒** So one can be wholly determined and yet more or less free, according to whether one acts as an adequate cause: freedom grows with the knowledge of necessity, not with its abolition.

## Deepenings

### Adequate cause, inadequate cause

*The distinction by which Spinoza makes freedom a matter of degree.*

1. I am an adequate cause when the effect is explained by my nature alone; an inadequate, or partial, cause when explaining it requires bringing in something other than me.
2. To be active is to produce effects of which I am the adequate cause; to be passive is to undergo them. All of freedom lies in this passage from passivity to activity.

*[Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), *[Ethics](../works/ethics-spinoza.md)**
