# The moral-responsibility argument

*Argument for [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md) : Free will*

**Without the power to choose otherwise, I would be responsible for nothing.**

## The argument

1. When I deliberate, I treat several courses of action as open: I weigh reasons precisely because I believe the outcome is not yet settled.
2. But holding someone **responsible**, praising or blaming them, presupposes that they could have [acted otherwise](../glossary/alternate-possibilities.md) than they did.
3. Yet we hold that responsibility to be real, in law as in morals; we must therefore grant it its condition, a power to choose otherwise.

**⇒** We therefore have [free will](../glossary/free-will.md), the condition of moral responsibility.

## Deepenings

### Why does responsibility require the power to act otherwise?

1. If only one outcome were possible, blaming an agent for their act would be like blaming a clock for striking: blame addresses an agent only where an alternative was open to them.
2. This is why the law excuses those who acted under absolute constraint: no one is held responsible for what they could not avoid.

### Kant's formula: "you ought, therefore you can"

1. [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) reverses the order: it is not freedom that is proved first, it is moral duty that attests to it.
2. If a moral demand genuinely binds me, it presupposes me able to meet it: the "you ought" implies the "you can", hence a free will.

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), *[Critique of Practical Reason](../works/critique-of-practical-reason-kant.md)*, 1788*
