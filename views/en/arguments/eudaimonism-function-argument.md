# The function argument (ergon)

*Argument for [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : Eudaimonism*

**Just as the good flautist is the one who plays well, the good human being is the one who does well what makes a human: that is where their good lies.**

*[Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md), *[Nicomachean Ethics](../works/nicomachean-ethics-aristotle.md)**

## The argument

1. For a flautist, a sculptor, a craftsman, the good and 'doing well' lie in their proper function, in the work they alone can accomplish. Let us ask the same: does the human being, as a human, have a proper work?
2. Neither mere life, which it shares with plants, nor sensation, which it shares with animals, is proper to it: what is proper is the soul's activity in accordance with reason, the life of a being able to deliberate and give form to its conduct.
3. Now the good of a thing is to perform its proper function excellently. The human good is therefore the exercise of this rational activity in accordance with the excellence proper to it, **virtue**, and this not for a day, but over a whole life.

**⇒** So the highest good is neither pleasure nor virtue taken apart, but [flourishing](../glossary/eudaimonism.md): the whole activity of a human life carried out excellently, with pleasure crowning it rather than being its aim.

## Deepenings

### Why reason, and not life or sensation

1. [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) sets aside nutritive life and sensation not out of contempt, but by method: what defines the proper good of a being is what it shares with no other. Growing, feeding: plants do that; sensing, moving: so do beasts.
2. What remains proper to the human is the part of the soul that possesses reason and the part that can obey it. It is therefore in a life guided by reason, not in what we have in common with the rest of the living, that our fulfillment is at stake.

*[Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md), *[Nicomachean Ethics](../works/nicomachean-ethics-aristotle.md)**
