# The statue of Glaucus

*Objection to [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md) : Anthropological pessimism — attacks the reasoning*

**What the pessimist takes for the nature of man is a work of time and society: like the sea-worn statue of Glaucus, the human soul is so disfigured that it is no longer recognizable.**

*[Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md), *[Discourse on the Origin of Inequality](../works/discourse-on-inequality-rousseau.md)*, 1755*

## The argument

1. The pessimist reads human nature in man as he is before our eyes: rivalry, calculation, greed. From this he concludes that such is our depth.
2. But social man is no longer the original man: on the statue of Glaucus, that sea-god, time and storms had so eaten away the features that it looked less like a god than a wild beast. So too the human soul, altered by social life and the passions it breeds, has changed to the point of being almost unrecognizable.
3. To describe this disfigured figure and call it 'nature' is to take the effect for the origin: the traits the pessimist ascribes to man in himself are the imprint of his history.

**⇒** So the pessimist reasons over a damaged image: one must, beneath the layers deposited by society, recover the man of nature before judging his depth, failing which one takes the disease for health.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: the violence I think 'natural' is the work of society. Beneath that crust, the original man was not evil.** : You join [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md): wickedness is a deposit of history, not the depth of man.
- **I grant that one does not read nature in social man; but nothing says he was good either: say rather undetermined, shapeable.** : You slide toward plasticity: the original man is neither good nor evil, only available to be formed.
- **This intact 'original man' is a fiction: we have never known any man but man in society. What we observe everywhere, for want of another, just is human nature.** : The pessimist reply: the 'pure' man of nature is unobservable; it cannot serve to disqualify what is found everywhere.
