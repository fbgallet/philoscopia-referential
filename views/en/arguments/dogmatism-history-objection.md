# The graveyard of certainties

*Objection to [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Dogmatism*

**So many truths once held as final have ended up falling.**

## The argument

1. In every age, the most rigorous minds held as settled for good truths that what came after overturned: that the Earth stood motionless at the centre of the world, that space and time were the same everywhere and for everyone, that Newton's mechanics had the last word on motion.
2. These convictions were no idle opinions: they rested on the best evidence of their day, and the most learned believed them safe from any revision.
3. But if the best-established certainties of yesterday are the acknowledged errors of today, nothing assures us that ours will meet a different fate: the same reasoning, applied to our own age, should make us cautious.

**⇒** So no truth, however assured, can declare itself definitively safe: it is the nature of knowledge to be revised one day.

## Deepenings

### The pessimistic induction

1. Philosophers of science have given this argument a name: the **pessimistic induction**. Since most theories once held to be true have ended up abandoned, it would be rash to believe ours exempt from that movement.
2. One can resist it by distinguishing, within a superseded theory, what was nonetheless kept: Newton is not wrong everywhere, he remains exact within his domain. Progress would thus preserve a core, without yielding any closed certainty for all that.

*Larry Laudan*

## Ways out of the objection

- **History is final: if our best certainties have always given way in the end, better to trust none of them and suspend judgment.** : You draw a general distrust from the fallibility of the past: your position slides toward [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md).
- **I give up the idea of definitive truths, but I keep solid knowledge, which I hold as the best available and ready to be corrected.** : You keep knowledge while stripping it of its claim to the definitive: this is [fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md).
- **I refute it: these examples come from the natural sciences, which advance by revision. Other truths seem of a different fabric: the cogito, or the truths of logic and arithmetic, hold where physical theories pass away.** : You distinguish empirical knowledge, which is revisable, from truths of reason, which do seem to hold: there would indeed be definitive certainties, but not just any of them.
