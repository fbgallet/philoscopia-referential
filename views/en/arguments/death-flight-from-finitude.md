# The flight objection

*Objection to [Death](../axes/death.md) : Immortalism*

**Hoping to survive might be nothing but a refusal to accept our condition.**

*[Feuerbach](../philosophers/feuerbach.md), [Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md)*

## The argument

1. The hope of another life arises at the sharpest point of the fear of ending: it consoles exactly where death hurts, which should arouse suspicion.
2. Yet nothing in experience confirms this survival: neither the life sciences nor observation show a person without their body. The hope fills a desire, it does not establish a fact.
3. Worse, it can turn us away from the only life we have: by deferring what matters to an uncertain beyond, we risk missing the here and now, the only time given to us.

**⇒** Therefore immortalist hope might be not a truth about death, but a way of not facing it.

## Ways out of the objection

- **That's right: better to face finitude than console myself for it. My only life is this one, and it is for me to answer to it.** : You let the objection decide: giving up the beyond to take on finitude is the path of [being-toward-death](../glossary/being-toward-death.md).
- **I keep a hope, but a lucid one: not a knowledge nor a refuge, rather a meaning I give my life without evading it.** : You distinguish hope from flight: one can hold to a meaning beyond death while fully living the here and now.
- **I refute it: that a belief consoles does not make it false, and the absence of proof is no proof of absence.** : You separate a belief's origin from its truth: the psychological "suspicion" does not refute the hope, it only explains its appeal.
