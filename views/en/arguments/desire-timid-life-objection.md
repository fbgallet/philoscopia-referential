# The shrunken-life objection

*Objection to [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Reasoned selection*

**By wanting only the safe and the simple, one is spared trouble, but also misses greatness.**

## The argument

1. Sorting promises peace by reserving desire for simple and safe objects, those whose satisfaction depends only on us.
2. But what one thus sets aside is not only the superfluous: it is also all that, by nature, exceeds measure and escapes our control, the love that can be lost, the work that can fail, the ambition that can wound. And it is often from there that what gives a life its height comes.
3. Epicurean prudence buys tranquility at the price of intensity: it shelters itself from torment, but also from what is gained only by agreeing to tremble. By measuring one's desires too closely, one risks a life beyond reproach and without radiance.

**⇒** So sorting, in protecting us from trouble, may deprive us of the best: it remains to be seen whether a good life is a life without pain, or a life fully lived.

## Deepenings

### Is pleasure at rest enough?

1. [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) holds the highest pleasure to be a stable state, the absence of pain and trouble, and not the excitement that rises and falls.
2. But one may object, as some did already in antiquity, that a purely « static » pleasure looks all but indistinguishable from the absence of desire, and that a life wanting nothing uncertain has already, out of prudence, given up much living.

## Ways out of the objection

- **By always protecting myself, I miss what would make a life worth it. Better to desire strongly, even at the cost of suffering, than to risk nothing.** : You overturn the criterion: it is no longer the absence of trouble that measures a good life, but the intensity of what one dares to want.
- **I keep the sorting, but make room in it for risk: some uncertain desires are worth the exposure, even at the cost of peace.** : You loosen prudence without abandoning it: sort, yes, but keeping a few desires that exceed measure, because they are worth it.
- **What you call greatness is often mere agitation. The sage does not flee life, he stops hanging it on what he does not control.** : You hold [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md)'s reply: true wealth is to enjoy simply what is here, and it is the limitless, not measure, that impoverishes.
