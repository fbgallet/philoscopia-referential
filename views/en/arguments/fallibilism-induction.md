# The problem of induction

*Objection to [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Fallibilism*

**What guarantees that tomorrow will resemble yesterday?**

*[Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), *[An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding](../works/enquiry-concerning-human-understanding-hume.md)*, 1748*

## The argument

1. The fallibilist trusts the method of the sciences, which generalizes from experience: the sun has always risen, so it will rise; this remedy has cured, so it will cure.
2. But [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) showed it: this passage from past to future cannot be demonstrated. That nature will remain tomorrow what it was yesterday, no experience can establish, since every experience already belongs to the past.
3. Our confidence in [induction](../glossary/induction.md) therefore rests on mere habit, not on proof. The fallibilist thought himself reasonable; he finds that his best-grounded knowledge hangs on an act of faith he cannot justify.

**⇒** So the revisable knowledge the fallibilist vaunts rests, at its base, on a belief that nothing grounds: his assurance was premature.

## Deepenings

### Habit stands in for reason

1. For Hume, it is not reason that makes us expect the future to resemble the past, it is custom: we have so often seen fire burn that we can no longer imagine it otherwise.
2. This habit is useful, vital even, but it proves nothing: it explains why we believe, not why we would be right to believe.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The objection is decisive: if even the regularity of nature cannot be proved, then none of our knowledge is truly grounded. I lean toward doubt.** : You let the problem of induction carry off your confidence: your position slides toward [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md).
- **If knowledge needs a firm ground that experience does not give, then it must be sought elsewhere, on the side of reason.** : You seek a rational foundation beneath empirical knowledge: this is the gesture of [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md), which wants first certainties.
- **I refute it: induction need not be proved to be reasonable. We correct our expectations when they disappoint us, and that is all fallibilism asks.** : You take on induction as a revisable practice rather than a dogma to be proved: the objection leaves [fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md) intact.
