# Evil against God

*Objection to [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : Theism — attacks the position*

**A God both all-powerful and good would not let the innocent suffer; yet the innocent suffer.**

*[Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md), [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), *Dialogues concerning Natural Religion, Part X*, 1779*

## The argument

1. Theism affirms a God who is **all-powerful**, able to prevent every evil, and **perfectly good**, willing to prevent it.
2. Yet evil exists, and not only the kind our freedom explains: the disease that carries off a child, the catastrophe that levels a town, suffering that no visible good requires.
3. If he could prevent it and willed to, there would be none; there is. One of the terms must therefore go: the power, the goodness, or the very existence of this God, unless one can show why a good God would permit that evil.

**⇒** So, as long as the innocent's suffering stands unjustified, it weighs as the strongest reason to doubt the all-powerful and good God.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The objection lands: I cannot reconcile that evil with an all-powerful and good God. My confidence in his existence comes out shaken.** : You give evil the force of an argument: the path of the atheistic argument from evil, from [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) to [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md).
- **I hold to my faith, but the objection binds me: I need an answer, human freedom, a soul formed through trial, or the admission that God's ways exceed us.** : You enter the work of [theodicy](../glossary/theodicy.md): justifying God in the face of evil, or marking the limits of what we can judge of it.
- **I refute it: evil is no proof against God. A free, law-governed world carries that possibility, and we are in no position to judge the whole.** : You hold the line of [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md) and the free-will defense: evil permitted is not evil willed.
