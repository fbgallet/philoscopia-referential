# Undeserved is not up for redistribution

*Objection to [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md) : Egalitarianism — attacks the reasoning*

**That I did not earn my gifts gives no one the right to dispose of them: the absence of desert is not a collective title.**

*[Nozick](../philosophers/nozick.md), *[Anarchy, State, and Utopia](../works/anarchy-state-and-utopia-nozick.md)*, 1974*

## The argument

1. The argument first grants that no one deserves their talents, then concludes that society may redistribute their fruits.
2. But 'not deserving' something does not mean that others have a right to it: from the fact that I do not deserve the color of my eyes or the affection of those close to me, it does not follow that they belong to the collective.
3. Between 'no one deserves their gifts' and 'society may dispose of them', a premise is missing: that each person's talents are a **common asset**. But that is exactly what is in dispute.

**⇒** So the argument leaps from the undeserved to the redistributable: remove the hidden premise of collective ownership of talents, and the natural lottery no longer justifies redistribution.

## Ways out of the objection

- **Fair enough: my gifts may be undeserved, but they are mine; no one has a title to dispose of them without my consent.** : You join [Nozick](../philosophers/nozick.md): justice rests on entitlements, not on some 'due' that the lottery would ground.
- **I still hold that we correct the worst gaps, but not in the name of a collective 'due': rather to guarantee everyone a decent floor.** : You slide toward sufficientarianism: the language of desert gives way to that of the threshold.
- **No one is self-made: my talents are worth something only in a society that trains, demands, and rewards them; their fruits are therefore in part a common work.** : You hold [Rawls](../philosophers/rawls.md)'s reply: talents bear fruit only within a cooperative fabric, which therefore has a say in how the fruit is shared.
