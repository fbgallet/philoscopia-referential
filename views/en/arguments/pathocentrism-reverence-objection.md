# Sentience, an arbitrary boundary?

*Objection to [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md) : Pathocentrism — attacks the position*

**Why suffering, and not life? A tree, a bacterium also pursue their good: is sentience really the right boundary?**

*[Schweitzer](../philosophers/schweitzer.md), Paul Taylor, *Respect for Nature*, 1986*

## The argument

1. Pathocentrism makes the capacity to suffer the threshold of moral status: below it, nothing counts.
2. But every living thing, sentient or not, pursues its own good: a tree grows, repairs itself, resists; it 'wills to live' in its own way, without feeling it.
3. If to count is to have a good that can be served or thwarted, then sentience is only one way among others of having such a good: to keep it alone, is that not a new privilege, that of beings who feel?

**⇒** So the boundary of sentience might be as arbitrary as that of species: either respect goes to all life that pursues its good, or one must say why feeling alone confers a claim.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: every living thing pursues its good; respect is owed to life as such, not only to what suffers.** : You move to biocentrism: with [Schweitzer](../philosophers/schweitzer.md), it is the will-to-live in all life that commands respect.
- **I keep suffering as what weighs most, but I grant non-sentient life a worth, lesser, that calls for consideration.** : A gradation: sentience first, life in general second.
- **A being without sentience has no point of view: nothing is good or bad for it from within. 'Pursuing its good' is there but a metaphor; only what can suffer has real interests.** : The pathocentrist reply: without a subject that feels, there is no one to wrong, hence no obligation.
