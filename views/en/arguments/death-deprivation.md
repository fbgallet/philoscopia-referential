# The deprivation argument

*Objection to [Death](../axes/death.md) : Freeing ourselves from the fear*

**Death is an evil because it deprives us of all that life would have offered us.**

*[Thomas Nagel](../philosophers/nagel.md), *Death (Mortal Questions)*, 1979*

## The argument

1. Not all evils are felt pains: being betrayed without knowing it, or having one's work ruined after death, harms us without our feeling it.
2. But death cuts off everything good we would have lived: it is a deprivation, and a deprivation can be an evil even if no one remains to feel it.
3. The symmetry with the time before birth does not hold: what preceded me could take nothing from me, whereas death interrupts a life already under way, full of projects it reduces to nothing.

**⇒** Therefore Epicurean indifference goes too far: it remains reasonable to regard death as a genuine loss.

## Ways out of the objection

- **That's right: death is a loss, not a nothing. And it is precisely because it deprives that my finite life is so precious.** : You stop neutralizing death: treating it as a deprivation lets finitude give existence its weight, as in [being-toward-death](../glossary/being-toward-death.md).
- **I keep the Epicurean serenity, but without denying the loss: not fearing death does not require calling it indifferent.** : You distinguish two things: the fear, which can be dissolved, and the value of what is lost, which can be acknowledged. Serenity is not blindness.
- **I refute it: with no subject remaining, there is no one for whom the deprivation is an evil. The symmetry holds.** : You hold to the Epicurean requirement: an evil requires someone to undergo it, and death leaves no one. The fear stays groundless.
