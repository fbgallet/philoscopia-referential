# Do not throw out the real questions

*Objection to [The point of philosophy](../axes/metaphilosophy.md) : Clarifying thought — attacks the position*

**By treating the great problems as confusions of language, one risks dismissing the very questions that give philosophy its worth.**

*[Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md), *What Is Metaphysics?*, 1929*

## The argument

1. Clarification holds many philosophical problems to be knots of language, to be dissolved rather than solved: 'whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent' *[(Tractatus)](../works/tractatus-logico-philosophicus-wittgenstein.md)*.
2. But the questions thus consigned to silence, the meaning of life, death, the just, the free, are not mere grammatical mistakes: they are the ones that press upon every existence.
3. Making language clear does not yet teach how to live or what is: in wanting to dissolve too much, one risks keeping only a technique without an object, a logical tidiness that has given up on what mattered.

**⇒** So clarifying cannot be the whole task: one can tidy up language without having answered anything, and the 'pseudo-problems' one sets aside are often the real questions one does not know how to handle.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: the great questions are not pseudo-problems. Philosophy must face meaning, death, the just, not dismiss them.** : You return toward contemplation: there is an order of the real and a meaning to be understood, which language-analysis alone lets slip.
- **I keep the demand for clarity, but as a means: to clarify in order to pose the real questions better, not to be rid of them.** : Clarity in the service of substance: dispelling confusions to free, not to dissolve, the questions that matter.
- **These 'great questions', ill-posed, breed centuries of sterile dispute: to clarify them is often to see that one was asking wrongly, and to make at last tractable what seemed unfathomable. To dissolve a pseudo-problem is not to flee, it is to make progress.** : The defense of clarification: many metaphysical riddles dissolve as soon as one sees the grammatical confusion that feeds them.
