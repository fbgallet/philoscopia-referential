# The whole against the individual

*Objection to [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md) : Ecocentrism — attacks the position*

**If only the whole counts, then the individual, humans included, may be sacrificed in the name of balance: this is an 'environmental fascism'.**

*[Regan](../philosophers/regan.md), Sagoff, *The Case for Animal Rights*, 1983*

## The argument

1. Ecocentrism places worth in the whole, the integrity of the biotic community, of which individuals are but members.
2. But then an individual may be sacrificed whenever it harms the balance: culling en masse the animals of an overabundant species, or even, if the logic holds, treating an overlarge human population as an excess for the 'health' of the whole.
3. Yet we hold that the sentient individual, its suffering, its life, counts for itself, and not merely as a cog in a system: treating beings as mere means in the service of the whole offends what is firmest in morality.

**⇒** So worth cannot reside in the whole alone: an acceptable ethics must first protect sentient individuals, even if that makes ecological integrity only one major good among others, not the supreme good.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: nothing justifies sacrificing a being that suffers in the name of a 'whole'. It is the sentient individual that counts first.** : You return to [Regan](../philosophers/regan.md)'s individualism: the subject-of-a-life has rights that no balance can erase.
- **I keep the health of ecosystems as a major good, but it cannot unconditionally override the life and suffering of individuals.** : A tempered ecocentrism: the whole counts, but under the constraint of not crushing individuals.
- **No individual life survives if the whole collapses: to preserve the biotic community is to protect the very condition of all individuals, present and to come.** : The holist reply: the 'charge of fascism' forgets that outside the whole, no individual stands.
