# The Epicurean measure

*Argument for [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Reasoned selection*

**To sort your desires is to free yourself from those that, by nature, can never satisfy.**

*[Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md), *[Letter to Menoeceus](../works/letter-to-menoeceus-epicurus.md)**

## The argument

1. Our greatest torments do not arise from a lack of bread, but from desires with no natural limit: ever more wealth, power, recognition. As nothing sates them, they never leave us at rest.
2. Now desires can be told apart: some are natural and easy to satisfy, and satisfying them suffices to remove pain; others are empty and measureless, and it is from pursuing them, not from failing to reach them, that trouble comes.
3. By examining one's desires so as to satisfy the first and dismiss the second, one reaches a [pleasure](../axes/sovereign-good.md) that no longer depends on what one does not control: not the excitement of gratification, but [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md), that absence of trouble, stable because it rests on oneself alone.

**⇒** So wisdom is neither to inflame one's desires nor to extinguish them all, but to measure them: a sober contentment, and lasting because it is within our reach.

## Deepenings

### The fourfold remedy

1. The Epicureans summed up their wisdom in four formulas, the [tetrapharmakos](../glossary/tetrapharmakos.md): the gods are not to be feared, death is nothing, happiness is easy to attain, pain is easy to bear.
2. The sorting of desires is its practical heart: it is because happiness rests on little, on simple and true desires, that it is « easy to attain » and beyond the reach of fortune.

*[Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md), *[Letter to Menoeceus](../works/letter-to-menoeceus-epicurus.md)**
