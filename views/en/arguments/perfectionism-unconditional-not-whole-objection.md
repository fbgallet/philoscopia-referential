# Unconditional is not total

*Objection to [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : Virtue / moral perfectionism — attacks the reasoning*

**That virtue is the only good that cannot be misused proves it has a worth apart, not that it is, by itself, the whole good of a life.**

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), *[Critique of Practical Reason](../works/critique-of-practical-reason-kant.md)**

## The argument

1. The argument establishes this: virtue is the only good that stays good under every use, the only one 'without reservation'.
2. But from 'only unconditional good' to 'only good' is a leap: that a thing is the only one worth something in every circumstance does not say it is the only one worth something. Pleasure, health, friendship can be genuine goods, though conditional, that is, good when they do not serve evil.
3. [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) himself sees it: the good will is the unconditional good, but the complete highest good unites virtue and the happiness it deserves, not virtue alone. The unconditional worth of uprightness makes it the condition of the rest, not its replacement.

**⇒** So the argument proves that virtue must command the other goods, not that it abolishes them: the highest good might be not uprightness alone, but the ordered whole of goods under the guidance of uprightness.

## Ways out of the objection

- **Indeed: uprightness is what must command, but a good life also wants happiness, bonds, a work. Virtue orders the goods, it does not replace them.** : You slide toward eudaimonism: virtue as the architectonic part of a complete good, not as the whole good.
- **I keep the absolute primacy of uprightness, but admit that a deserved happiness may legitimately be added: the highest good is virtue first, and the happiness it makes worthy.** : You hold [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)'s complete 'highest good': virtue crowned with the happiness proportioned to it.
- **I refute it: as soon as one adds to virtue other goods as ends, one reopens the door to preferring them to it. For uprightness truly to come first, it must be the only good, the rest being merely its matter.** : You hold the Stoic firmness: any compromise on 'the one good' ends by subordinating virtue to something else.
