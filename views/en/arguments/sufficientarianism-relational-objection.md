# Equality of relations, not just of the basket

*Objection to [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md) : Sufficientarianism — attacks the position*

**One can give everyone the means to live and still have masters and dependents: justice is not just a floor of goods, it is a way of standing as equals.**

*Elizabeth Anderson, *'What Is the Point of Equality?'*, 1999*

## The argument

1. Sufficientarianism measures justice by a basket of goods: does each person have enough? Once the threshold is reached, it declares itself satisfied.
2. But a society can secure the necessities for all while still sorting people into superiors and inferiors: dependent on a boss, a husband, a benefactor whose favor must be begged. The basket is full, the relation remains that of a dependent.
3. But what wounds in inequality is not only the shortfall, it is having to bow one's head, to count for less as a person. The equality that counts is equality of [relations](../axes/political-liberty.md): that no one stands above another.

**⇒** So a floor of sufficiency is not enough: justice also requires that people stand as equals, without domination, which may demand narrowing gaps that sufficiency alone would let run.

## Ways out of the objection

- **Giving 'enough' is not enough if one stays a dependent: a just society also refuses humiliating hierarchies, even at the cost of narrowing the gaps.** : You join Anderson's relational egalitarianism: equality aims first at relations among peers, not at an amount of goods.
- **I keep the threshold as the priority, but add that we must prevent relations of domination, not just fill the shortfalls.** : A sufficientarianism completed by a concern for non-domination: the floor remains, but the standing of persons is watched too.
- **Relations among equals rest on rights, respect, and everyone's voice, not on the size of bank accounts. Guaranteeing all the necessities and equal rights is enough.** : You decouple standing from shares: equal dignity runs through rights and respect, which large gaps in fortune need not necessarily destroy.
