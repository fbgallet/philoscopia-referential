# Owning oneself is not owning the world

*Objection to [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md) : Libertarianism — attacks the reasoning*

**Owning myself does not hand me the Earth: no one produced alone the resources or the cooperation their wealth depends on.**

*G. A. Cohen, *Self-Ownership, Freedom, and Equality*, 1995*

## The argument

1. The argument moves from 'I own myself' to 'I own the fruits of my labor', as if the second followed from the first.
2. But producing always presupposes a matter I did not create, the land, the resources, and a whole world of institutions, language, and inherited knowledge, without which my labor would be worth nothing.
3. To appropriate these common resources without limit is to deprive those who had as good a claim to them as I did: my ownership of myself does not by itself extend to what belonged to no one.

**⇒** So full ownership of what one acquires does not follow from ownership of oneself: part of all wealth traces to a common world, over which the collective retains a legitimate title.

## Ways out of the objection

- **Fair enough: my wealth owes everything to a common world; it is only fair to return a share of it to those who keep that world standing.** : You join the objection of [Rawls](../philosophers/rawls.md) and Cohen: wealth is a social product, whose sharing is a matter of justice.
- **I keep the merit of what I produce, but grant that a share is owed to the common: enough to secure everyone a decent floor.** : You combine self-ownership with a duty of sufficiency, like the 'left-libertarians'.
- **Resources are worth something only through the labor that puts them to work; the one who clears the land creates the value. The 'common share' would stay a dead letter without them.** : You hold the Lockean-Nozickian reply: it is labor that makes value, so the producer keeps the title to it.
