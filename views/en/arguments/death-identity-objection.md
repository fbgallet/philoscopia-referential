# The identity objection

*Objection to [After death](../axes/afterlife.md) : The immortal soul*

**Even if something survives, nothing guarantees it would still be me.**

*[Locke](../philosophers/locke.md), [Paul Ricœur](../philosophers/ricoeur.md)*

## The argument

1. Suppose some part of us escapes death. That part must still be me, and not an impersonal remnant: a survival that would be no one's would console no one.
2. But what makes me myself, whether one seeks it in a body, in the continuity of a memory, or in the story of a life, holds to that very life, to its ties and its history. Strip all that away, and it becomes hard to say in what way what remains would still be the same person.
3. The hope is then caught in a vise: either what survives resembles me enough to be me, and it would take almost a body and a memory; or it is a pure soul, and one struggles to recognize anyone in it.

**⇒** So immortality, supposing it occurs, might save something while losing precisely the person: what would survive may no longer be my [identity](../glossary/personal-identity.md).

## Deepenings

### Memory and narrative

1. [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) ties identity not to a substance but to the continuity of consciousness and memory: I am the same as the one whose deeds I remember.
2. Ricoeur adds narrative to this: a person is the story they tell themselves and that others tell of them. In both cases, the survival of a soul without memory or history would be no one's in particular.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: what would survive without body or memory would no longer really be me. Better to accept that I end with my body.** : You let the objection decide: to accept finitude rather than a survival that would be no one's is the path of [being-toward-death](../glossary/being-toward-death.md).
- **I keep the hope, but without claiming to know what remains: a meaning that exceeds me, rather than a double of myself.** : You shift the hope from the "self that endures" toward [a meaning that exceeds the person](../axes/death.md).
- **I refute it: what makes the self is not the body but consciousness, and it is precisely that which one hopes will endure.** : You hold consciousness to be the true seat of identity: this is the reply of [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) and [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md).
