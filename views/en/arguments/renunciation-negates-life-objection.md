# The cure removes the patient

*Objection to [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md) : Renunciation — attacks the position*

**A desire extinguished is a life extinguished: by wanting nothing more so as to suffer no more, one does not answer the enigma of meaning, one stops posing it by ceasing to live.**

*[Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md), *On the Genealogy of Morality, III*, 1887*

## The argument

1. Renunciation promises peace by extinguishing desire, which it holds to be the source of torment.
2. But a desire extinguished is a life extinguished: with no impulse toward anything, no more love, work, project; there remains only the absence of suffering, all but indistinguishable from the absence of life.
3. The cure thus heals the patient by removing him: by wanting nothing more so as to suffer no more, one has not solved the enigma of meaning, one has stopped posing it, for want of a living being to carry it.

**⇒** So renunciation does not solve the enigma, it dissolves it by dissolving the one who poses it: it remains to be seen whether a life is better without pain, or fully lived.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The objection lands: I do not want the peace of the dead. Better to desire, love, create, even at the cost of suffering, than to extinguish myself so as to feel nothing more.** : You choose the affirmation of life over its extinction: on the side of lucid revolt, or of a meaning one builds.
- **I keep detachment, but set it apart from extinction: loosening the grip of the desires that gnaw at us, without ceasing to love or to act. To want less, not to want nothing.** : You hold a measured renunciation, closer to ataraxia than to the negation of the will: quieting desire without extinguishing life.
- **I refute it: what you call 'living' is precisely the torment I free myself from. The peace of the detached sage is not death, it is the only life not gnawed by lack.** : You hold [Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md)'s reply: the negation of the will is not nothingness but a higher peace, feared only by one still enslaved to desire.
