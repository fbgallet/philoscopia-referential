# From desired to desirable

*Objection to [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : Hedonism — attacks the reasoning*

**That we all seek pleasure does not entail that it is the good: it remains to be seen whether what nature makes us want is what is worth wanting.**

*[Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), Moore, *[A Treatise of Human Nature](../works/treatise-of-human-nature-hume.md)**

## The argument

1. The cradle argument moves from fact to worth: because every creature spontaneously pursues pleasure, pleasure is supposed to be the good.
2. But between 'this is what we desire' and 'this is desirable, worth desiring' lies a step that nothing crosses on its own: a child also seeks what flatters it and flees what shapes it, yet we make no rule of the good out of that.
3. Nature inclines us; it does not judge for us: from a spontaneous leaning one cannot infer that it leads to the best. One would have to show, in addition, that pleasure **is worth** something, not merely that it attracts, and that proof the cradle does not give.

**⇒** So the argument proves too little: it establishes that pleasure is a good we seek, not that it is the highest good; the step from desired to desirable is still to be made.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: what I want spontaneously is not necessarily what is good for me. Understanding, creating, being just may be worth something independently of the pleasure they bring.** : You open the door to goods one may have reason to want, health, knowledge, friendship, independently of what one feels.
- **I keep pleasure as the first good, but grant that it does not justify itself alone: it still has to be a pleasure that does not debase me.** : You reintroduce a hierarchy of pleasures, in the line of [Mill](../philosophers/mill.md): not all are equal, even at equal intensity.
- **I refute it: to seek, beyond pleasure, a 'good in itself' that no one experiences is to invent a value. 'Good' means nothing other than good-for-someone, that is, pleasant.** : You hold the hedonist's reply: to refuse the step from desired to desirable is to demand a good without proof, one that nothing in experience fills in.
