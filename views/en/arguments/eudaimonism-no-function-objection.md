# Existence without essence

*Objection to [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : Eudaimonism — attacks the reasoning*

**A knife has a function because a craftsman made it to cut. But who made the human being, and for what?**

*[Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md), *Existentialism Is a Humanism*, 1946*

## The argument

1. The argument draws the human good from a 'proper function', as one draws the good of a knife from the use for which it is made.
2. But a tool has a function because a craftsman designed it for an end; an organ, because it serves the life of the organism. Does the human being, as such, have in the same way a task assigned to it in advance?
3. If the human has no nature fixed before existing, if it begins by being nothing and makes itself, then there is no 'work of man' from which to deduce its good: it falls to each to invent what, for them, makes a fulfilled life.

**⇒** So the argument assumes what it should prove, that the human has a proper function: remove that premise, and flourishing ceases to be a given measure and becomes a work to invent.

## Ways out of the objection

- **I grant it: nothing assigns me in advance a 'nature' to fulfill. My good life is mine to define and to make, not for a function to dictate.** : You make the highest good a creation rather than a discovery: each invents what, for them, makes a good life.
- **I keep the idea of flourishing, but without resting it on a metaphysical 'function': let us say there are richer and poorer ways of unfolding a human life, without any being written in advance.** : You hold a eudaimonism without teleology: fulfillment remains an ideal, but the given nature is dropped, in the line of Nussbaum's capabilities.
- **I refute it: even without craftsman or destiny, there remain properly human needs and capacities, speaking, understanding, living together, whose fulfillment is the measure. To 'make oneself' is still to develop those capacities.** : You hold the neo-Aristotelian reply: the function needs no god to assign it, it can be read off the form of life of the human animal.
