# The concentric circles

*Argument for [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md) : Concentric circles*

**Everyone counts equally, but concern unfolds in circles, from the near to the far.**

## The argument

1. Picture yourself at the center of nested circles: yourself first, then those close to you, your friends, your fellow citizens, and all around them the whole of humanity.
2. We owe consideration to each, but we can effectively care only for those near us; a world in which each watches first over their own circle is also the one in which each has the best chance of being protected.
3. Partiality is then not a breach of equality, but its lived form: the moral task, the [Stoics](../movements/stoicism.md) said, is to draw the circles gradually inward, extending to the distant the attention owed to the near.

**⇒** One can therefore hold the equal worth of all at the foundation, and priority to those close to us in action: this is the graded [appropriation](../glossary/oikeiosis.md) of the concentric circles.

## Deepenings

### One morality, on two levels

1. Two levels of moral thinking are sometimes distinguished. At a reflective, impartial level, one justifies the rules; at the everyday level, one cultivates love for one's own, precisely because that is, seen from above, what best serves the good of all.
2. Partiality then becomes what impartiality itself recommends: no longer its opposite, but its implementation. [This idea](../movements/utilitarianism.md) answers the charge of instability.

*R. M. Hare, *Moral Thinking*, 1981*
