# The blind-desire objection

*Objection to [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : Affirmation*

**To say yes to all your desires is to say yes also to those that destroy you.**

*[Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md)*

## The argument

1. Affirmation draws its strength from refusing nothing: it says yes to what moves through life, without judging it in the name of an external morality.
2. But not all desires uplift: some consume, obsession, jealousy, the never-slaked thirst to possess or to dominate. To affirm without sorting is to say yes to these too, and to leave them the life they ravage.
3. To tell the desire that enlarges life from the one that undoes it, one needs a criterion, hence an examination, hence the very sorting that affirmation claimed to dismiss.

**⇒** So affirmation, if it is not to become the slave of its worst leanings, must borrow from the wisdom it refused: learning to choose among one's desires.

## Deepenings

### Are all desires equal?

1. [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) himself does not say yes to just anything: he distinguishes the forces that affirm, generous and creative, from those born of [ressentiment](../glossary/ressentiment.md) and rancor against life.
2. But then affirmation is no longer blind: it too presupposes ranking desires, holding some nobler than others.

## Ways out of the objection

- **That's right: one cannot say yes to everything. Some desires undo me, and recognizing them calls for sorting them, not merely following them.** : You grant affirmation the limit it lacked: a desire is worth what it makes of you, and that can be discerned.
- **I keep affirming my desires, except those that destroy me or others: those, I will not follow.** : You keep the impulse, with a single dike: no longer every desire, but every desire that does not ruin the life it animates.
- **What you call a destructive desire is often just the fear of strong desires. A life without risk is not a life saved, it is a life avoided.** : You hold to affirmation's demand: it is by curbing life out of caution that we maim it most surely.
