# The timber from which no straight plank is made

*Objection to [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md) : Moral plasticity — attacks the position*

**One does not remake man at will: certain bents, fear, envy, the preference for one's own, reappear in all cultures, whatever education is given.**

*[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), *Idea for a Universal History with a Cosmopolitan Aim*, 1784*

## The argument

1. Plasticity bets that education and institutions can shape man almost without limit, for the better.
2. But certain traits recur everywhere, in every age and under every culture: the fear of death, the attachment to one's own against the stranger, envy, the thirst for status. This constant bedrock resists every education.
3. And the attempts to manufacture a 'new man', by remaking mores from top to bottom, have produced not angels but, often, terror: the timber of humanity is too crooked for any straight plank ever to be cut from it.

**⇒** So malleability has bounds: education can discipline this depth, not abolish it. To count on a limitless plasticity is to court its return, all the more violent for having been denied.

## Ways out of the objection

- **Under all cultures, the same passions return: there is a depth that education contains without remaking it.** : You lean toward pessimism: a bedrock of passions resists history and bounds what education can do.
- **I keep plasticity, but bounded: much is possible, not all; education works with constants.** : A tempered plasticity: malleable within limits set by nature and history.
- **These 'constants' are more plastic than they seem: fear, belonging, status take a thousand forms across cultures. It is not the depth that is fixed, it is our institutions that lack imagination.** : The plasticity reply: the variety of cultures shows that the 'invariants' are themselves shaped.
