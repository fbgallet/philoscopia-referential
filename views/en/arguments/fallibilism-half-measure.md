# The half-measure

*Objection to [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Fallibilism*

**Is knowledge said to be forever revisable still knowledge at all?**

## The argument

1. The fallibilist wants two things at once: to keep knowledge, and yet to admit that none of his beliefs is certain.
2. But "to know" usually carries a more demanding sense: I do not say I know a thing if I grant at the same time that it might be false. At that point it is no longer knowledge, it is a cautious opinion.
3. The fallibilist then seems to waver without settling: either he genuinely trusts some of his beliefs, and he rejoins the [dogmatist](../glossary/dogmatism.md); or he holds them all to be uncertain, and he rejoins the [skeptic](../glossary/skepticism.md).

**⇒** So the middle way risks being not a position, but a hesitation between the other two.

## Deepenings

### Knowledge and certainty

1. Tradition long defined knowledge as a true and justified belief, to which a requirement of certainty was readily added: one would know only what one cannot doubt.
2. The fallibilist contests precisely that last addition: to demand certainty, he says, is to confuse knowledge with infallibility, and to condemn all human knowledge in advance.

## Ways out of the objection

- **You are right: a belief I know might be false is no knowledge. Better to admit it and suspend my judgment.** : You pull the half-measure toward doubt: if one cannot be sure, better to affirm nothing. This is [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md).
- **Or else the opposite: if I really want to know, I do need a few firm points to lean on.** : You pull the half-measure toward foundations: real knowledge would require first certainties. This is [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md).
- **I refute it: to hold a belief as the best grounded, while knowing it revisable, is in no way incoherent. It is the very honesty of one who knows he can be wrong.** : You hold [fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md) to be a straightforward position, not a hesitation: one can commit without believing oneself infallible.
