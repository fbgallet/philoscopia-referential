# Where does the idea of cause come from?

*Objection to [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Empiricism — attacks the position*

**We never see more than one thing following another, never the necessary link: where, then, does the idea of cause come from, which experience never shows?**

*[Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md), *[A Treatise of Human Nature](../works/treatise-of-human-nature-hume.md)**

## The argument

1. Empiricism promises to trace every idea back to a sense impression. Let us test it on the idea of cause: when one ball strikes another, what exactly do we perceive?
2. Two motions in succession, a constant conjunction, but never the force that would produce one by the other, nor the necessary link between them. The eye sees no 'must': necessity is not perceived.
3. Yet we judge that the cause necessarily produces the effect, and that judgment holds for all cases to come. But such universal necessity cannot be read off a collection of particular, past cases: something is added that does not come from the senses.

**⇒** So either the idea of necessary connection is an illusion (Hume), or the mind supplies it from its own resources (Kant): in either case, experience alone does not suffice to ground knowledge.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: the necessity I ascribe to causes is not seen. It must come from the mind, which gives form to what the senses supply, rather than from experience alone.** : You slide toward [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)'s criticism: causality is a form the understanding imposes, not a datum received.
- **I keep empiricism, but grant that some ideas, like cause or infinity, resist simple analysis: experience remains the source, without explaining everything at once.** : You hold a loosened empiricism: the source stays experience, but you leave open how the mind works up what it gives.
- **I refute it: the idea of cause adds nothing mysterious. It is a habit, the expectation formed by repetition. We do not know a necessity, we anticipate, and that too comes from experience.** : You hold [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md)'s own reply: no innate idea of cause, but a custom of the mind, the product of repeated experience.
