# Letting die is not innocent

*Objection to [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md) : Minimalism — attacks the reasoning*

**Minimalism rests on a sharp line between harming and not helping; but letting someone die when one could easily save them can be as culpable as killing.**

*James Rachels, *'Active and Passive Euthanasia'*, 1975*

## The argument

1. Minimalism draws a sharp line between harming, which it forbids, and not helping, which it leaves free: I owe nothing so long as I refrain.
2. But compare two men: one drowns his nephew for the inheritance; the other, having come to drown him, sees him slip in the bath and withholds the gesture that would have saved him at no risk. Did the second act less wrongly than the first?
3. Our judgment says no: what counts is the motive and the outcome, not the difference between a movement and a stillness. Letting die when one could easily have saved is a way of causing death.

**⇒** So the line between harming and not helping is not so sharp: if the omission can be as culpable as the act, minimalism cannot found a right to indifference upon it.

## Ways out of the objection

- **True: what counts is what I could have prevented, not the gesture or its absence. My duties include the harm I let happen.** : You extend duty to omissions: morality then demands far more than refraining from harm.
- **I grant that some omissions are wrongs, when rescue was easy; but letting die far away does not equal killing, and all the world's ills are not my fault.** : An intermediate position: culpable omission exists, but graded by proximity, cost, and the certainty of rescue.
- **The example is rigged by the murderous motive: the motionless uncle wanted the death. Remove that intention, and the difference between pushing into the water and not diving in is real indeed.** : The minimalist reply: with equal intention the cases match, but in ordinary life not helping is not willing harm.
