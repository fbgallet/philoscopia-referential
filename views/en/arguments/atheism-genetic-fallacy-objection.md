# Origin does not settle truth

*Objection to [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : Atheism — attacks the reasoning*

**Explaining why we believe says nothing about what we believe: a belief born of desire can still be true.**

## The argument

1. The projection argument moves from the origin of the belief, our fears and desires, to the falsity of its object: God would not exist since we have human reasons to believe.
2. But the origin of a belief and its truth are two distinct questions: desiring that something exist proves neither that it exists nor that it does not. To infer falsity from genesis is the **genetic fallacy**.
3. The thirsty man who dreams of a spring is not wrong to believe water exists: that a belief meets a need makes it suspect, not false. One would still have to show, on other grounds, that nothing answers to it.

**⇒** So the argument shows at best that the belief is humanly explicable, not that God does not exist: projection casts suspicion, it does not settle the matter.

## Ways out of the objection

- **Indeed: my unbelief leaned too much on that explanation. If it proves nothing, the question of God is more open than I thought.** : You step back toward suspension of judgment: the psychological explanation loses its probative force, and agnosticism becomes tenable again.
- **I keep my atheism, but grant that projection does not prove it: it explains the belief, while other reasons, the world's silence, the needlessness of the hypothesis, carry the rest.** : You demote projection to a supporting explanation: it accounts for the belief once atheism is established on other grounds, in [Russell](../philosophers/russell.md)'s manner.
- **I refute it: when a hypothesis rests on no independent evidence AND is fully explained by our desires, the reasonable conclusion is that it is our own work. Here, suspicion is enough.** : You sharpen the argument rather than abandon it: genesis does not refute, but added to the absence of evidence, it carries the decision.
