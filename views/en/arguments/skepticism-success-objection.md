# The success of knowledge

*Objection to [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Skepticism*

**If nothing were more assured than its opposite, how could knowledge ever succeed?**

## The argument

1. The skeptic holds that to every claim there stands an opposing claim of equal strength: none would deserve our assent more than another.
2. Yet some knowledge succeeds where its opposite would fail: the astronomer announces the eclipse to the minute, medicine pushes back diseases, the engineer launches a probe that reaches its target billions of kilometres away.
3. If all theses were of equal worth, these repeated successes would be a sheer miracle. The simplest thing is to admit that some beliefs are better grounded than their opposite, without being definitive for all that.

**⇒** So the perfect balance of reasons is an illusion: experience decides, and some of our beliefs have earned the right to be preferred.

## Deepenings

### The no-miracles argument

1. The philosopher Hilary Putnam made it into a famous argument: the lasting success of science would be a miracle if its theories grasped nothing real.
2. The skeptic may reply that "it works" does not prove "it is true": an effective instrument can rest on a false theory, just as people long calculated correctly from imaginary heavens.

*Hilary Putnam*

## Ways out of the objection

- **True enough: faced with such successes, one cannot put everything on the same footing. Some truths are well and truly established.** : You grant some knowledge a solidity that places it beyond doubt: your position moves closer to [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md).
- **I grant that not everything is of equal worth, without going as far as the definitive: some beliefs are better grounded, but none is safe from revision.** : You leave general suspension for a graduated, revisable confidence: this is [fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md).
- **I refute it: that a method succeeds does not prove it true. Many effective theories have rested on principles later judged false.** : You distinguish effectiveness from truth: to predict is not to know, and success does not end doubt.
