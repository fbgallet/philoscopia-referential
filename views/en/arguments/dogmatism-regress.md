# Agrippa's trilemma

*Objection to [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : Dogmatism*

**Every foundation calls for a further foundation, without end.**

*Agrippa, [Sextus Empiricus](../philosophers/sextus-empiricus.md)*

## The argument

1. Every claim taken to be certain must be justified by a reason that supports it; otherwise it is posited arbitrarily.
2. But that reason in turn calls for a justification, and this one for another, indefinitely.
3. To halt the chain, only three exits, all disappointing: continue to infinity, turn in a **circle** (the reason leans on what it was meant to prove), or stop by fiat on a claim posited without proof.

**⇒** Therefore no belief reaches a truly final foundation: the definitive certainty dogmatism demands stays out of reach.

## Deepenings

### The Cartesian circle

1. Descartes was charged with this very circle: he secures evidence by the truthfulness of God, yet he establishes God's existence from ideas he calls evident.
2. If the foundation presupposes what it founds, the first certainty is no longer first: it borrows its warrant from what it was meant to warrant.

## Ways out of the objection

- **The regress is unanswerable: none of my beliefs is definitively grounded. Better to suspend.** : You let the trilemma dissolve the claim to a foundation: your position slides toward [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md).
- **I give up indubitable foundations, but I keep knowledge that is solid though revisable.** : This is [fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md): one can ground one's beliefs without demanding that none ever be revisable.
- **I refute it: some starting points impose themselves, like the cogito, and call for no prior proof.** : This is the foundationalist reply: there are first self-evident truths that halt the regress without arbitrariness.
