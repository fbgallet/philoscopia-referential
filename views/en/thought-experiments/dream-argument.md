# The dream and the evil demon

*Descartes, *Meditations on First Philosophy*, 1641*

You have surely dreamed a scene so vivid that, in the moment, nothing set it apart from reality. But then, what assures you that at this very instant you are not dreaming? Descartes presses the test further: imagine an all-powerful, deceiving mind, an evil demon, bending all its cunning to make you take the false for true, down to the sky, the earth, and your own body. As long as this hypothesis is not ruled out, almost all your beliefs could be false without your being able to know it.

**Faced with this doubt, what remains to you? Does anything withstand the test, or must we admit that no definitive certainty is within our reach?**

**Axes at stake** : [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) · [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

**Possible stances** :

- One thing resists: even deceived, I think, therefore I am. There lies the rock of a certainty.
- The lesson is that the senses can deceive: solid knowledge cannot rest on them alone.
- Nothing truly resists: if even this can be doubted, no certainty is definitive.
- It is a classroom game: in real life, I cannot seriously doubt the world I experience.
