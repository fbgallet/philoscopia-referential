# The Chinese Room

*Searle, *Minds, Brains, and Programs*, 1980*

You are locked in a room. Through a slot, sheets covered in Chinese symbols are passed to you, symbols you do not understand in the slightest. But you have a thick rulebook, written in your own language, that tells you: "to this mark, reply with that one." You follow the rules, you pass sheets back, and outside a Chinese reader finds your answers flawless: to them, the room understands Chinese. You, though, have understood nothing. A machine that handles our words, a chatbot that answers so well, is it doing anything other than what you do in that room?

**Is running a program, however flawless, enough to **understand**, or does handling **symbols** by their form alone stay blind to their **meaning**?**

**Axes at stake** : [Can a machine think](../axes/machine-mind.md)

**Possible stances** :

- The room understands nothing: arranging symbols by their form is not grasping their meaning, and running a program does not suffice for it.
- The man alone does not understand, but the whole system, he and the rulebook and the room together, genuinely does.
- Hard to decide: perhaps understanding is, in the end, nothing more than that.
- If a system carries out all the functions by which we understand, it understands: requiring some hidden understanding on top is a prejudice.
