# The endless life

*Bernard Williams, *The Makropulos Case*, 1973*

At thirty-seven, Elina Makropulos drank an elixir that suspends death. Three centuries later, she has known everything, loved everything, lost everything several times over. Passions have dulled, encounters repeat, nothing tastes new any longer: her unending life has frozen into a glacial indifference, and she finally refuses to renew the elixir, as one at last consents to rest. Her story raises an uncomfortable question: what if death, far from being only what threatens us, were also what gives our days their worth?

**Would you accept an endless life? If you hesitate, what does that reveal about the place death holds in a life that has meaning?**

**Axes at stake** : [Death](../axes/death.md)

**Possible stances** :

- I would refuse: a life without horizon would lose its savor. It is because my time is finite that it has worth.
- I would gladly accept it: there is always more to learn, to love, to discover. Elina's boredom betrays a failure of imagination, not a law of life.
- Only if I could keep changing and renewing myself: it is repetition, not duration, that undid Elina.
