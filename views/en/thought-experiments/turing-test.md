# The Imitation Game

*Turing, *Computing Machinery and Intelligence*, 1950*

Turing finds the question "can a machine think?" too vague, and proposes to replace it with a test. A judge converses in writing, without seeing them, with a human being and a machine; the judge must guess which is which. If, over the exchange, the judge can no longer unmask the machine, on what grounds deny it thought? Turing sees in it less a proof than an **operational criterion**, and wagers that machines will one day succeed at it. What we now call the "Turing test" is a loose version of it: programs sustain a conversation well enough to fool us, but is the test really passed, or did it never measure the right thing?

**Does being unable to tell a machine from a person by conversation prove that it thinks, or does behaviour never prove the presence of a mind?**

**Axes at stake** : [Can a machine think](../axes/machine-mind.md)

**Possible stances** :

- Passing the test is enough: of a mind, even in others, we never have any proof but behaviour.
- It is a serious clue, though not a proof: speaking well makes thought plausible, not certain.
- Imitating conversation is not thinking: one can produce every sign of it without understanding anything one says.
