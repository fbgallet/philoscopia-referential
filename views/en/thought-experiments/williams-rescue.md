# Saving a loved one rather than a stranger

*Bernard Williams, *Moral Luck*, 1981*

A shipwreck: two people are about to drown, and you can save only one. One is a stranger, the other is your spouse. You rush, of course, toward the one you love. But a philosopher stops you: did you first check that morality permitted it, that no impartial reason gave priority to the stranger? [Bernard Williams](../philosophers/williams.md) replies that anyone who needs that check already has "one thought too many": she was your spouse, and that should be enough.

**You save your loved one, granted. But what, deep down, makes that act good?**

**Axes at stake** : [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

**Possible stances** :

- Deep down, one should weigh it up: save whoever's life will do the most good. Here the lives are equal, so saving my loved one is allowed.
- There is a rule, valid for everyone, that permits giving priority to one's own: my act is good because it stays within what duty allows.
- A person of good character rushes in without calculating: that very spontaneity, that character, is what makes the act good.
- I act from the bond that ties me to her: morality is rooted in these concrete attachments, not in principles valid for everyone.
