# Saving a loved one rather than a stranger

*Bernard Williams, *Moral Luck*, 1981*

A shipwreck: two people are about to drown, and you can save only one. One is a stranger, the other is your spouse. You rush, of course, toward the one you love. But a philosopher stops you: did you first check that morality permitted it, that no impartial reason gave priority to the stranger? [Bernard Williams](../philosophers/williams.md) replies that anyone who needs that check already has "one thought too many": she was your spouse, and that should be enough.

**You save your loved one, granted. But what, deep down, makes that act good?**

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : Deontologism — attacks the position

To check that it is 'permitted' to save the one you love is one thought too many: a morality that routes our attachments through the impartial rule alienates what makes a moral life worth living.

**The argument it makes**

1. Your spouse and a stranger are drowning, you can save only one. The morality of duty first has you check that it is permitted to save your own.
2. But this check is 'one thought too many': the person who, before the one they love, first asks whether the rule allows it has already failed what the bond requires.
3. A morality that requires passing through the impartial rule, even in our most intimate attachments, alienates what makes a moral life worth living: the bonds themselves.

**⇒** So the impartiality of duty misses the concrete heart of morality: it is not the rule that commands saving the one you love, it is the bond, and a morality that forgets this asks one thought too many.

- **The objection hits home: before the one you love, invoking the rule is already the wrong morality. What obligates first is the bond, not the impartial principle.** : You cross over to the ethics of care: morality starts from concrete bonds, not from the rule valid for all.
- **I keep duty, but relieve it of that heaviness: the rule need not be present to mind at every act; it frames action from afar, without stepping between me and the one I love.** : You hold a loosened deontologism: duty sets the limits, without demanding to be the present motive of every act.
- **I refute it: that saving one's spouse is permitted is exactly what tells love apart from unjust favor. Without that underlying permission, we would no longer know why it is wrong to save one's own in contempt of everything.** : You hold the Kantian reply: the impartial permission does not oppose the bond, it guarantees its legitimacy.

**Puts to the test** : [Never a mere means](../arguments/deontologism-humanity-as-end.md)

**Possible stances** :

- Deep down, one should weigh it up: save whoever's life will do the most good. Here the lives are equal, so saving my loved one is allowed.
- There is a rule, valid for everyone, that permits giving priority to one's own: my act is good because it stays within what duty allows.
- A person of good character rushes in without calculating: that very spontaneity, that character, is what makes the act good.
- I act from the bond that ties me to her: morality is rooted in these concrete attachments, not in principles valid for everyone.
