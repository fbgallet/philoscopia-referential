# Mary's room

*Frank Jackson, *"Epiphenomenal Qualia"*, 1982*

Mary is a brilliant vision scientist: shut all her life in a black-and-white room, she has nonetheless learned, on a monochrome screen, everything physical science can say about color. Every wavelength, every cell of the retina, every circuit of the brain: no part of the mechanism escapes her. One day she is let out, and for the first time she sees a red rose. Does she then learn something? If she does, then a complete knowledge of the [physical](../glossary/materialism.md) still left a remainder: what it is, for a living being, to **see** red.

**On seeing red for the first time, does Mary learn something that science had not given her?**

**Axes at stake** : [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md) · [The enigma of consciousness](../axes/consciousness-nature.md)

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md) : Scientism — attacks the position

Mary knows all the physical facts about color without having seen it; on seeing red, she learns something. A complete physical knowledge thus left a remainder: science does not exhaust the real.

**The argument it makes**

1. Mary knows everything physical science says about color, but has never seen anything except in black and white.
2. The day she comes out and sees a red rose, she learns something new: what it is to see red, which no textbook contained.
3. There are, then, truths that a complete physical knowledge let slip: the real overflows what science, however accomplished, can put into statements.

**⇒** So science does not exhaust the real: against scientism, lived first-person experience gives an access of its own, irreducible to objective knowledge.

- **Seeing red teaches her a fact no textbook contained: lived life yields truths that measurement does not reach.** : You hold Frank Jackson's knowledge argument: objective knowledge does not exhaust the real, first-person experience adds to it.
- **She gains not a fact but a familiarity; science keeps the last word on the facts, without supplying every form of knowledge.** : The middle path: granting science its authority over the facts without ascribing to it every form of knowing, by acquaintance or by ability.
- **No new fact: she reaches through another channel what she already knew. Feeling red and knowing it are two ways of grasping the same fact.** : The physicalist reply: Mary's 'new' is old information reached in a new way, not one more truth.

**Puts to the test** : [The argument from success](../arguments/scientism-self-correction.md)

**Possible stances** :

- She learns something: seeing red reveals to her what no textbook contained. There are, then, truths that science, however complete, cannot reach.
- She learns no new fact: she already knew everything about red, she merely undergoes the direct experience of it for the first time. Nothing, in principle, escapes science.
- She gains not a new piece of knowledge but a familiarity, a know-how: from now on she can recognize red, imagine it, remember it. Neither a bare scientific fact nor an unreachable truth.
