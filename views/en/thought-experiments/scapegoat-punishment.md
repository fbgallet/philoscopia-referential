# Punishing an innocent to keep the peace?

*H. J. McCloskey, 1957*

A small town is on the brink of a riot. The crowd, convinced that a murder by an outsider will go unpunished, threatens to burn everything down, killing several people, unless a culprit is handed over. The sheriff knows that none of his suspects did anything, but he holds a man he could credibly have convicted. By sacrificing him he would likely save several lives and restore order; by refusing, he honors the truth, but lets the carnage happen.

**In his place, would you hand over the innocent to save the others? And if punishment is justified only by its effects, what still forbids doing so?**

**Axes at stake** : [The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md)

**Possible stances** :

- The lives saved weigh more than one: if punishment is worth what it prevents, the man must be handed over.
- Punishing someone who did nothing is not punishing: with no wrong, there is no deserved penalty, only one more crime.
- Condemning an innocent repairs nothing and ruins the trust that holds the community together: it would break the very thing one claims to save.
