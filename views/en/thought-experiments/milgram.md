# The Milgram experiment

*Stanley Milgram, 1963*

In a famous psychology experiment, a scientist asks ordinary volunteers to inflict ever-stronger electric shocks on a stranger each time he answers wrong, in the name of a study on learning. The stranger, an actor, cries out, pleads, then falls silent. Despite their visible distress, most volunteers obey up to the level marked "danger: severe shock", simply because a lab coat keeps repeating: "The experiment requires that you continue."

**What does this obedience teach us about human beings?**

**Axes at stake** : [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md) : Anthropological optimism — attacks the position

Ordinary people inflict the worst on a mere order, without hatred or grievance: if man were naturally good and only corrupted by an unjust society, how is it that he obeys so readily?

**The argument it makes**

1. Ordinary volunteers, neither monsters nor wretches, inflict what they believe to be dangerous shocks on the mere order of an experimenter.
2. They do so without hatred, without grievance, without personal gain, in a peaceful setting where no inequality or misery drives them to it.
3. If man were good by nature and only corrupted by an unjust society, whence would come his obeying so readily an order to harm? The disposition to do evil is closer to the surface than optimism admits.

**⇒** So primal goodness is not so assured: neither misery nor injustice is needed for the ordinary man to do the worst, a mere authority suffices.

- **It takes little for the ordinary man to do the worst: this bent is in us, not laid down by society alone.** : You lean toward the pessimism of [Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md) and [Freud](../philosophers/freud.md): aggression is there, ready to resurface the moment order loosens.
- **Neither good nor evil: it is circumstances, authority, the setup that decide. Man is what he is made into.** : [Arendt](../philosophers/arendt.md)'s 'banality of evil': the worst comes from ordinary people caught in a situation, not from a nature.
- **It is precisely the apparatus of authority that produces this: remove that social pressure, and these same people, who suffer and resist, are helpful.** : [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md)'s reply: natural pity resists, visible in the distress of those who obey against their will; it is the situation, not nature, that corrupts.

**Puts to the test** : [Man is good, society corrupts him](../arguments/optimism-natural-goodness.md)

**Possible stances** :

- A cruelty sleeps in each of us: it takes only a permission to wake it.
- Nothing about our nature: it is the setup, the authority, the frame that make people act this way; other conditions would produce the opposite.
- On the contrary: they obeyed trembling, protesting, and some refused; the moral sense was not extinguished.
