# The black swan

*Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*, 1934*

For centuries Europeans held it obvious that all swans are white: every swan they met confirmed it. Then, in Australia, black swans were found. A single encounter overturned what millions of observations had seemed to establish. How, then, does science establish a general law, "all swans are white", holding for every case to come, from observations that are always finite in number?

**To ground a general law: by piling up favourable cases, by seeking the case that would disprove it, or by adjusting its probability?**

**Axes at stake** : [Scientific method](../axes/scientific-method.md)

**Possible stances** :

- By accumulating observations: the more white swans one meets, the more firmly the rule "all swans are white" is established. Science starts from the facts and generalises.
- By trying to disprove it: no white swan proves the rule, a single black swan refutes it. A law holds as long as it withstands attempts to break it; one does not establish, one eliminates.
- By adjusting its probability: each white swan makes the rule a little more probable without ever guaranteeing it, a black one would make it collapse. One neither proves nor refutes at a stroke; one revises one's degrees of belief.
