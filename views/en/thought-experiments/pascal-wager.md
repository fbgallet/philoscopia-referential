# Pascal's Wager

*Pascal, *Pensées*, 1670*

You cannot know whether God exists: reason, says Pascal, can neither prove nor disprove it. Yet you cannot abstain, for to live as though God did not exist is already to have bet against. So weigh the stakes. Bet on God, and if God exists you win an eternity of happiness; if not, you will have given up little, a few pleasures of a finite life. Bet against, and if God exists you lose that infinite happiness. But an infinite gain, however unlikely, outweighs any finite stake: to bet on God's existence would thus be the most reasonable choice, not because it is true, but because it pays best.

**Can an advantage stand in for a reason to believe? And can one even decide to believe what one is not convinced of?**

**Axes at stake** : [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) · [Ethics of belief](../axes/belief-ethics.md)

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : Agnosticism (Huxley) — attacks the position

Uncertainty does not justify neutrality: living already stakes a bet, and with stakes so asymmetric, suspending judgment is itself a wager, the worst one.

**The argument it makes**

1. The agnostic is right on one point: reason can neither prove nor refute God's existence. But he concludes that one may abstain from choosing.
2. Yet one cannot not play: to live is already to stake. Whoever suspends judgment lives, in practice, as though God did not exist; his neutrality is a wager that does not speak its name.
3. And the stakes are asymmetric: to bet on God is to risk a finite stake for an infinite gain; to bet against, to risk a finite gain against an infinite loss. Faced with such a gap, the calculation leaves no room for unscathed neutrality.

**⇒** So uncertainty does not ground suspension: since one must stake, prudential reason counsels betting on God, and agnosticism ceases to be the fallback position it took itself to be.

- **The wager convinces me: since I stake anyway, better to stake on the side where everything can be won. I seek God, even if faith comes through practice.** : You follow [Pascal](../philosophers/pascal.md) to the end: prudential reason sets you on the path, and the means of belief, prayer and practice, do the rest.
- **I grant that my neutrality is not neutral: I live as an unbeliever. But I own it as a practical wager, without pretending my suspension has no consequences.** : You distinguish theoretical agnosticism, which you keep, from the practical wager, which you acknowledge: life decides even while the mind suspends.
- **I refute it: one does not believe on command, and a belief adopted by calculation is no belief. Suspending judgment remains the only honest attitude, whatever I "stake" in practice.** : You meet the wager with doxastic voluntarism and the ethics of belief: credence follows evidence, not interest.

**Puts to the test** : [Neither prove nor refute](../arguments/agnosticism-limits-of-reason.md)

**Possible stances** :

- The wager is reasonable: an eternal happiness, even if uncertain, is worth infinitely more than the few renunciations of a finite life; faced with such a gap, betting on God is the obvious move.
- One does not choose to believe: conviction can no more be willed than love.
- Which God? The calculation would hold for a thousand rival religions without saying which to choose.
