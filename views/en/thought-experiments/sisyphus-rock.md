# Sisyphus and his rock

*Albert Camus, *The Myth of Sisyphus*, 1942*

The gods had condemned Sisyphus to ceaselessly roll a rock to the top of a mountain, from which the stone would fall back of its own weight. They had thought, with some reason, that there is no more dreadful punishment than futile and hopeless labor. Each day, the same effort; each evening, the stone at the bottom. Imagine this life of toil that leads nowhere and never ends: where is meaning to be found, if there is any at all?

**Faced with this life of effort with no end and no result, where can meaning be found, or must we give up looking for one?**

**Axes at stake** : [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

**Possible stances** :

- Such an effort has meaning only if it serves an end beyond itself: an order, a destiny, a God. Failing that, one must at least hope for it.
- Meaning is not in the rock, but in the way one carries it: Sisyphus can make his fate his own and decide for himself what counts.
- No meaning, given or invented, erases the absurd. What remains is to face it and live all the same, without hope: one must imagine Sisyphus happy.
- If there is nothing to find, better to stop rolling the rock in one's mind: to give up demanding a meaning, and quiet within oneself the craving that torments.
