# The last man

*Richard Routley (Sylvan), *Is There a Need for a New, an Environmental Ethic?*, 1973*

Imagine you are the very last human being. A catastrophe has carried off all the others, and you yourself will die in a few moments, with no descendants and no witness to come. Before you, a device could wipe out at a stroke, and without the slightest pain, everything still alive on the planet: the forests, the oceans, the animals. No human being will ever again exist to rejoice in it or to suffer from it. Nothing you do can therefore harm anyone. Would laying your hand on that device be a wrong, or a matter of indifference?

**As the last human being, you could painlessly wipe out all remaining life. Is it wrong?**

**Axes at stake** : [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md) · [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md)

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md) : Anthropocentrism — attacks the position

The last man could painlessly wipe out all remaining life: if only humans count, the act would be indifferent; yet it seems a grave wrong, a sign that value did not rest on us alone.

**The argument it makes**

1. Be the last human, dying, with no descendants: you can destroy at a stroke and without pain all remaining life, forests, oceans, animals.
2. No human will exist any longer to suffer from it or rejoice in it: according to anthropocentrism, for which all value is value for humans, the act wrongs no one and remains indifferent.
3. Yet it strikes us as a devastation, a wrong: this living world had worth, it seems, independently of any human gaze.

**⇒** So value is not only value-for-us: if destroying life without harming any human remains a wrong, then the moral circle overflows humanity.

- **The act would be a wrong: life, ecosystems are worth something in themselves, not through the use we make of them.** : You grant the living an intrinsic worth, with [Næss](../philosophers/naess.md) and [Leopold](../philosophers/leopold.md): the circle extends to life, even to the land itself.
- **At least sentient animals would be lost for themselves: sentience, beyond humans, deserves consideration.** : You widen the circle to sentient beings, without going as far as non-conscious life.
- **My refusal rests on this world having been the world of humans, or on what the act would say of me: value remains a value for humans, real or imagined.** : The anthropocentric reply: your scruple still projects a human gaze onto the world, it does not prove a value without us.

**Puts to the test** : [The dignity of the person](../arguments/anthropocentrism-dignity.md)

**Possible stances** :

- No. With no human left to be deprived of it, nothing that had value remains: that value was always a value for us.
- I could not do it. This world sheltered life; one does not lay waste to what was entrusted to us, even with no one left to answer to.
- It would be a devastation. The forests, the animals would be lost to no one, yet lost in themselves: their existence had worth independently of us.
