# The Just Man Struck by Misfortune

*Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics**

Picture a man who lived an upright life: honest, courageous, devoted to those close to him. But fate turns against him: he loses his children, his health, his possessions, and ends in pain and abandonment. Nothing can be held against him, his virtue remains intact, and yet his life closes in the most utter misfortune.

**Was his life, in spite of everything, a good one? In other words: is happiness safe from misfortune, or can it be torn away from us?**

**Axes at stake** : [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

**Possible stances** :

- His life was good in spite of everything: virtue is sufficient unto itself, and no misfortune can strip it of its worth.
- No: a life that ends in so much pain and loss cannot be called good, however deserving the man was.
- His virtue makes his life admirable, but misfortune did mar it: a fully good life also needs external goods and a measure of luck.
