# The Just Man Struck by Misfortune

*Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics**

Picture a man who lived an upright life: honest, courageous, devoted to those close to him. But fate turns against him: he loses his children, his health, his possessions, and ends in pain and abandonment. Nothing can be held against him, his virtue remains intact, and yet his life closes in the most utter misfortune.

**Was his life, in spite of everything, a good one? In other words: is happiness safe from misfortune, or can it be torn away from us?**

**Axes at stake** : [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : Eudaimonism — attacks the position

If flourishing requires external goods, the highest good stays hostage to fortune: illness, exile, the death of loved ones can shatter it.

**The argument it makes**

1. Flourishing, the eudaimonist grants, also requires certain external goods: health, friends, the means to live, a measure of good fortune.
2. But then the highest good depends on what no one controls: illness, exile, the death of loved ones can shatter it, like old king Priam who loses everything at the end of his life.
3. A final end that a stroke of luck can tear away is not fully our own: one would have built one's life on what fortune lends and takes back.

**⇒** So either the highest good remains hostage to fortune, or it must be sought on the side of what alone depends entirely on us.

- **I do not want a happiness that bad luck can destroy: what really counts, firmness of soul, uprightness, depends on me alone.** : You cross over to moral perfectionism, the inner citadel: the only true good would be the one nothing external can take away.
- **I keep flourishing as my end, but I learn to hang my happiness less on external goods.** : A eudaimonism tempered by a concern for control: activity is kept as the good, fortune's grip is loosened.
- **A life sheltered from all risk is not the highest but the poorest: flourishing is worth something precisely because it stakes real goods, friends, works, that can be lost.** : You hold [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s reply: vulnerability is not a flaw in the human good, it is its price.

**Puts to the test** : [The function argument (ergon)](../arguments/eudaimonism-function-argument.md)

### Objects to [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : Virtue / moral perfectionism — attacks the position

Holding virtue to be the only good forces one to call the misfortune-stricken just man happy: making the good invulnerable means denying that loss and pain are real evils.

**The argument it makes**

1. The perfectionist holds virtue to be the only good, and the rest, health, possessions, loved ones, to be 'indifferent': the upright person would stay happy even in the most utter misfortune.
2. But to call this man happy strains language: the loss of his children, the pain, the abandonment are real evils, not mere indifferents.
3. Making the good invulnerable to fortune comes at a price: one must deny that these losses are evils, and so impoverish what we call a good life.

**⇒** So virtue may not suffice for happiness: to grant that misfortune mars a good life is to admit goods that uprightness alone does not provide.

- **A life shattered by misfortune is not a successful one, however upright: happiness also wants bonds, a work, a life that stands.** : You return toward eudaimonism: virtue is a part of the good, not the whole.
- **I maintain the primacy of uprightness, but grant that misfortune is a real evil: virtue is the highest of goods, without being the only one.** : A softened perfectionism, Aristotelian rather than strictly Stoic: virtue comes first, but the other evils cease to be 'indifferent'.
- **The sage on the rack suffers, but is not unhappy in the sense that counts: he has lost nothing of what makes his worth. To confuse pain with misfortune is already to yield to fortune.** : You hold the Stoic firmness: pain is an evil of the body, not of the soul; the only true misfortune would be to become unjust.

**Puts to the test** : [The only unconditional good](../arguments/perfectionism-unconditional-good.md)

**Possible stances** :

- His life was good in spite of everything: virtue is sufficient unto itself, and no misfortune can strip it of its worth.
- No: a life that ends in so much pain and loss cannot be called good, however deserving the man was.
- His virtue makes his life admirable, but misfortune did mar it: a fully good life also needs external goods and a measure of luck.
