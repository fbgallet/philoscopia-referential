# Wilt Chamberlain: Liberty against Equality

*Nozick, *Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974*

Start from a society where wealth is distributed exactly as you judge just, in equal shares if you like. A much-admired basketball player, Wilt Chamberlain, offers his games; a million delighted spectators each freely drop a small coin to watch him play. By the end of the season he has become far richer than everyone else. No one was wronged, each gave what they wished, and yet the fine equality of the start has vanished.

**Is this new inequality unjust? And to correct it, would we have to forbid people from freely using what belongs to them?**

**Axes at stake** : [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md) : Egalitarianism — attacks the position

Maintaining an equal distribution requires forbidding free exchanges without end: liberty upsets any imposed pattern.

**The argument it makes**

1. Start from a distribution the egalitarian judges perfectly just, equal if you like.
2. Each person, of their own free will, slips a small coin to someone they admire, a player, an artist; in the end that person is far richer than the others, without anyone having been wronged.
3. If the starting point was just and each transfer free, at what moment would injustice have arisen? To restore equality, one would have to undo these exchanges, hence forbid people from using as they see fit what is theirs.

**⇒** So to maintain a pattern of distribution is to have to control people's lives without end: liberty upsets patterns.

- **True: as long as the starting point and the exchanges are just, so is the result. To keep correcting would forbid me from disposing of what is mine.** : You join [Nozick](../philosophers/nozick.md): justice depends on how holdings arose, not on the overall shape of wealth.
- **I do not want to freeze equality, but I insist that no one falls too low: the gap matters little to me as long as each has the means to live decently.** : You slide toward sufficientarianism: what matters is the floor, not the gap above it.
- **These free gestures, once they accumulate, remake masters and dependents; a just society corrects such gaps continually, through taxation, without 'forbidding' anything.** : You hold the egalitarian reply: taxing a flow forbids no exchange, it only corrects its accumulated effect.

**Puts to the test** : [The natural lottery argument](../arguments/egalitarianism-natural-lottery.md)

**Possible stances** :

- Nothing unjust: if the starting point was just and each transfer free, so is the result; correcting it would mean banning voluntary exchanges.
- As long as no one falls below a decent floor, these gaps do not trouble me: it is destitution, not inequality, that must be prevented.
- Such gaps are acceptable only if they also benefit the least advantaged, through taxation for instance; otherwise they should be corrected.
- A just society does not let such gaps widen: they must be continually corrected back toward equality.
