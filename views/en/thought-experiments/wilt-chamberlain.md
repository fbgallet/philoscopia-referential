# Wilt Chamberlain: Liberty against Equality

*Nozick, *Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974*

Start from a society where wealth is distributed exactly as you judge just, in equal shares if you like. A much-admired basketball player, Wilt Chamberlain, offers his games; a million delighted spectators each freely drop a small coin to watch him play. By the end of the season he has become far richer than everyone else. No one was wronged, each gave what they wished, and yet the fine equality of the start has vanished.

**Is this new inequality unjust? And to correct it, would we have to forbid people from freely using what belongs to them?**

**Axes at stake** : [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

**Possible stances** :

- Nothing unjust: if the starting point was just and each transfer free, so is the result; correcting it would mean banning voluntary exchanges.
- As long as no one falls below a decent floor, these gaps do not trouble me: it is destitution, not inequality, that must be prevented.
- Such gaps are acceptable only if they also benefit the least advantaged, through taxation for instance; otherwise they should be corrected.
- A just society does not let such gaps widen: they must be continually corrected back toward equality.
