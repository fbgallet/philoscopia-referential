# Molyneux's problem

*Molyneux, *reported in Locke's Essay*, 1688*

Imagine a man blind from birth who has learned to tell a cube from a sphere by touch: hands on them, he can say which is which. One day, through an operation, he gains his sight. The cube and the sphere are set before him, without his being able to touch them. Will he be able, by sight alone, to say which is the cube and which the sphere?

**Will he recognize the shapes by sight alone, or is everything to be learned from the senses?**

**Axes at stake** : [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : Rationalism — attacks the position

If the ideas of shape were innate, the newly sighted man would recognize them at once by eye; that he must relearn them through a new sense argues against innatism, the core of rationalism.

**The argument it makes**

1. If reason carried innate ideas within it, geometry would be in us before any sensory experience, independent of the sense through which one learns it.
2. Yet a man born blind who has learned by touch to tell a cube from a sphere, and who suddenly gains sight, would he recognize them by eye, without touching? Molyneux and Locke answer: no, he would first have to learn to see.
3. If the ideas of shape were an innate stock that reason unfolds, he would recognize them at once through any sense; that he must relearn them shows they are forged in experience, sense by sense.

**⇒** So ideas are not an innate treasure that reason unfolds, but something acquired through experience: the Molyneux case argues against innatism, and so against rationalism.

- **The case convinces me: the ideas of shape are not innate, one must learn to see them. Reason alone does not contain them, experience has its share.** : You step back from rationalism toward criticism, even empiricism: experience is necessary, even if the mind gives form to what it provides.
- **I maintain that reason has resources of its own, but grant that they need experience to be exercised: an innate structure, yes, but awakened by the senses.** : You hold [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md)'s reply: ideas are innate like veins in the marble, which experience uncovers without creating them.
- **I refute it: nothing says he would not recognize the shapes. The structure of space may be so deeply inscribed that he would at once link the seen to the touched.** : You hold the innatist line: some studies of cataract patients reopen the debate without settling it.

**Puts to the test** : [What the senses cannot give](../arguments/rationalism-necessary-truths.md)

**Possible stances** :

- Yes, he would recognize them at once: the idea of a cube, of a sphere, the mind already holds; the eye merely finds again what reason and touch already knew.
- By sight alone, no: at first he would see only nameless patches. Yet his mind is not empty for all that: it brings the form of space in which sight and touch come to be ordered. Experience gives the matter, the mind the frame.
- No: at first he would see only patches of light, void of meaning, until he learns, by touching and looking, to connect them. All our ideas, even that of shape, are built up through the senses.
