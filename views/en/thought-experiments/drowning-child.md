# The Drowning Child

*Singer, *'Famine, Affluence, and Morality'*, 1972*

You are walking past a shallow pond when you notice a child drowning in it. You could wade in and save the child at no danger to yourself: you would lose only your clothes, muddied or ruined, and a missed appointment. No one else is in sight. Everyone judges that a passer-by who walked on to stay dry would be a monster. Yet at that very moment, elsewhere in the world, children are dying of hunger or preventable disease, whom a modest donation might suffice to save.

**If the omission is a wrong at the edge of the pond, is it also one at a distance? What exactly does morality require of us?**

**Axes at stake** : [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md)

## What it does in the debate

### Objects to [Moral demandingness](../axes/moral-demand.md) : Minimalism — attacks the position

The minimalist himself judges monstrous the passer-by who lets the child drown to stay dry; yet that passer-by harmed no one. 'Do no harm' thus fails to cover what the minimalist himself demands.

**The argument it makes**

1. A child is drowning in a shallow pond before you; saving him costs only muddied clothes. The minimalist himself judges that a passer-by who refrained in order to stay dry would be a monster — yet that passer-by has harmed no one.
2. That judgment commits us to a principle: when one can prevent a great harm at the cost of a minor sacrifice, refraining is a wrong. 'Do no harm' thus fails to cover all that we ourselves judge to be required.
3. And neither distance, nor number, nor the fact that others could act seems a moral reason to erase that principle: the distant child whom a donation would suffice to save is in the situation of the one in the pond.

**⇒** So minimalism fails its own test: since it condemns the indifferent passer-by, it already grants that morality demands more than not harming.

- **I grant it: if the omission is a wrong at the edge of the pond, it is one at a distance too. Not harming is not enough, one must also come to the rescue.** : You join [Singer](../philosophers/singer.md): the duty of rescue extends as far as our power to prevent a great harm at small cost.
- **I add to 'do no harm' a duty of rescue when the harm is grave and the cost small, without turning it into an endless demand.** : A broadened minimalism: easy rescue becomes a duty, but a bounded one, without absorbing a whole life.
- **The pond is misleading: there I am alone, the emergency single, the cost one-off and the effect certain. The world's misery is permanent, uncertain, and everyone's business: strict duty stops at not harming and immediate rescue.** : The minimalist reply: what made the duty obvious, uniqueness, urgency, certainty, vanishes in the global case.

**Puts to the test** : [The harm principle](../arguments/minimalism-harm-principle.md)

**Possible stances** :

- You must save him, and the lesson carries beyond: whenever a modest sacrifice can prevent a great harm, here or far away, I ought to make it.
- You must save him, but because he is here, before my eyes, and I alone can act: that does not make me the debtor of all the world's misery.
- I would save him, of course; but beyond emergency rescue, giving is a matter of generosity, not of duty: no one may demand it of me.
