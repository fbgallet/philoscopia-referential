# Romanticism

*1795 – 1850*

An artistic, literary and philosophical movement born in Germany and England at the turn of the nineteenth century, Romanticism is the great reaction against the [Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md). To universal reason, mechanism and the cult of progress it opposes the claims of feeling, [imagination](../glossary/imagination-creatrice.md) and [genius](../glossary/genie.md), the mystery of a living nature, and the [singular genius of each people](../glossary/volksgeist.md) and each individual. Heralded by the sensibility of [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) and by the Sturm und Drang, carried by [Herder](../philosophers/herder.md), [Schelling](../philosophers/schelling.md), [Novalis](../philosophers/novalis.md) and [Schiller](../philosophers/schiller.md) in Germany, [Coleridge](../philosophers/coleridge.md) and Wordsworth in England, it refuses to reduce reality to what reason can calculate. The cultural form of the « Counter-Enlightenment », it fed nationalisms as much as avant-gardes, and remains the source of our modern idea of art as creation and expression.

**Key figures** : [Herder](../philosophers/herder.md) · [Schelling](../philosophers/schelling.md) · [Novalis](../philosophers/novalis.md) · [Schiller](../philosophers/schiller.md) · [Coleridge](../philosophers/coleridge.md)

## Core positions

1. [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : **The truth of the human being speaks in feeling, enthusiasm and passion, not in the cold reason that claims to judge them.**
2. [Nature of art](../axes/art-nature.md) : **Art does not imitate nature, it creates it: the work springs from genius, which does not receive its rules from without but gives them to itself.**
3. [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : **Nature is not a machine but a living organism, animated by a soul and by forces that mechanism could never exhaust.**
4. [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md) : **There is no universal reason but the proper genius of each people: each culture carries its own measure within it.**

## Positions

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue ethics 55% · Ethics of care 30% · Deontologism 10% · Consequentialism 5%

Romanticism opposes to the Kantian rule and the utilitarian calculus a morality of the heart: value is born of feeling, of sympathy and of the beauty of the soul, not of an abstract principle. To be good is to be true to oneself and attuned to others through empathy, an ethics of sensible virtue and of care where the authenticity of feeling is what counts.

*Sources : Rousseau, Emile (conscience) · Schiller, On the Aesthetic Education of Man*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Theism 75% · Atheism 25%

Romanticism does not deny the divine, it displaces it: against the remote God and the mechanism of the Enlightenment, it finds it immanent in living nature, in the infinite and in feeling. From the revival of Spinoza to Schelling's Naturphilosophie and Wordsworth's divinized nature, the sacred pervades the world more than it overhangs it: a diffuse pantheism rather than an orthodox theism or an atheism.

*Sources : Schelling, Ideas for a Philosophy of Nature · Wordsworth, The Prelude*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Sentimentalism 90% · Intellectualism 10%

To the Enlightenment's primacy of cold reason, Romanticism opposes the truth of feeling: it is through the heart, enthusiasm and passion, not through calculation, that one reaches the deepest in oneself and in the world. [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) had given the watchword by rehabilitating sensibility against the reasoners; the Sturm und Drang and then the Romantics make it the mainspring of art and of knowledge. Emotion is no longer a weakness to be mastered but a faculty that reveals.

*Sources : Rousseau, Julie, or the New Heloise · Schiller, Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man*

### [Nature of art](../axes/art-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Creation 93% · Mimesis 7%

Art ceases to imitate nature in order to create it: the work springs from [genius](../glossary/genie.md), that original power which does not receive its rules from without but gives them to itself. Against classical [mimēsis](../glossary/mimesis.md) and the canons inherited from the age of Enlightenment, Romanticism makes [imagination](../glossary/imagination-creatrice.md) a creative faculty and the artist a kind of second creator. Originality becomes the supreme value, and the work the expression of a unique inwardness.

*Sources : Coleridge, Biographia Literaria · Novalis, Fragments*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Vitalism 70% · Finalism 20% · Mechanism 10%

To the Enlightenment's universe-machine, Romanticism opposes a living nature: an animate organism, shot through with forces and a soul, not a system of gears without inwardness. [Schelling](../philosophers/schelling.md)'s [Naturphilosophie](../glossary/naturphilosophie.md) thinks nature as a subject in becoming, in which spirit gradually awakens to itself. Mechanism is rejected not by a return to theological finalism, but in the name of a life that precedes and overflows its parts.

*Sources : Schelling, Ideas for a Philosophy of Nature · Goethe, The Metamorphosis of Plants*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Relativism 60% · Constructivism 25% · Universalism 15%

Against the Enlightenment's abstract universalism, which measures all peoples by one and the same reason, [Herder](../philosophers/herder.md) holds that each culture bears within it its own [genius](../glossary/volksgeist.md), its values and its meaning, incommensurable with the others. There is no single model of civilization but a diversity of forms of life, each legitimate in its time and place. This historical and cultural relativism undermines the idea of a linear progress toward a universal norm.

*Sources : Herder, This Too a Philosophy of History · Herder, Ideas on the Philosophy of the History of Humanity*

### [Beauty in life](../axes/aesthetic-life.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Aestheticism 80% · Primacy of the ethical 20%

Romanticism makes art and beauty the highest place of existence, at times above the morality and utility the Enlightenment prized. To live poetically, to make one's life a work, to touch the absolute through beauty: the aesthetic becomes a way of being, even a path of salvation, and not a mere ornament.

*Sources : Schiller, Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man · Novalis, Hymns to the Night*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Organic community 75% · Contractualism 25%

The nation is not an aggregate of individuals bound by a contract, but an organic community, woven of a shared language, history and traditions. Against the Enlightenment's contractualist fiction, Romanticism recovers, at times alongside [Burke](../philosophers/burke.md), the idea of a people as a living whole, prior to its members and not instituted at will.

*Sources : Herder, Treatise on the Origin of Language*

### [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Techno-criticism 85% · Techno-optimism 15%

Just as the machine and the factory tear human beings from the land and from craft, Romanticism is the first to name the loss: industrial power is bought at the price of a **disenchantment** of the world, in which living nature becomes inert matter and the human a cog. [Schiller](../philosophers/schiller.md) already mourns a nature « un-godded » by calculation, Blake curses the « dark Satanic mills ». This protest opens the great lineage of the critique of technology, down to [Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md) and Ellul.

*Sources : Schiller, The Gods of Greece · Blake, Milton (preface)*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Member among others 65% · Stewardship 30% · Mastery 5%

The human being is not the master and possessor of a nature-object, but one voice within the great living whole: rooted in it and communing with it, it is through the feeling of this belonging, not through domination, that one comes to oneself. Against the Baconian and Cartesian project of subjecting nature, the Romantic wanderer seeks in it a mirror of the soul and the presence of the infinite; [Schelling](../philosophers/schelling.md) finds there one and the same spirit that sleeps in the stone and awakens in us.

*Sources : Wordsworth, Tintern Abbey · Schelling, Ideas for a Philosophy of Nature*

### [Purpose of art](../axes/art-purpose.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Truth 60% · Transformation 30% · Pleasure 10%

If art creates rather than imitates, it is because it reaches what neither concept nor science can utter: the absolute, the infinite, the lost unity of spirit and nature. The work is neither ornament nor diversion but an organ of truth, the highest one, there where discursive philosophy fails. [Schelling](../philosophers/schelling.md) crowns art as the completion of the system; [Novalis](../philosophers/novalis.md) seeks to « romanticize » the world so as to give it back its mystery.

*Sources : Schelling, System of Transcendental Idealism · Novalis, Pollen*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Communitarianism and patriotism 80% · Cosmopolitanism 20%

One is not a human being in general but first of a language, a soil and a people, and it is this rootedness, not the cosmopolitan abstraction of the [Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md), that gives a life its depth. [Herder](../philosophers/herder.md) awakens the proper genius of each nation in its songs, its tales and its language; from this will be born, for better and for worse, the modern idea of the nation as a community of destiny.

*Sources : Herder, Voices of the Peoples in their Songs · Herder, Ideas on the Philosophy of the History of Humanity*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The examined life 85% · Primacy of action 15%

The highest reality is not reached without, in action or fact, but within: dream, memory, night, the dark depths of the self become the true homeland. The Romantic's path toward the infinite runs through the descent into oneself rather than through a grip on the world.

> “The mysterious path leads inward. In us, or nowhere, is eternity with its worlds, the past and the future.” (Novalis, Pollen)

*Sources : Novalis, Pollen · Rousseau, Reveries of the Solitary Walker*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Affirmation 70% · Reasoned selection 20% · Extinction and detachment 10%

Far from tempering or extinguishing desire, as Stoics and Epicureans wished, Romanticism exalts it as the very life of the soul: it is infinite aspiration, [Sehnsucht](../glossary/sehnsucht.md), that desire with no assignable object which carries one toward the distant, the absent, the unreachable. Dissatisfaction is not an ill to be cured but the sign of our vocation to the infinite.

*Sources : Novalis, Henry of Ofterdingen · Schiller, Longing (Sehnsucht)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 80% · Abolitionism 20%

Suffering is not an accident to be removed but a trial that deepens and reveals: it is in melancholy, mourning and the « mal du siècle » that the Romantic soul touches its truth and the work is born. The suffering hero, from Werther to René, makes his wound the sign of a higher sensibility and the night the place of a revelation.

*Sources : Goethe, The Sorrows of Young Werther · Chateaubriand, René*

### [Wholes and parts](../axes/reduction-emergence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Emergentism 85% · Reductionism 15%

The whole precedes and overflows its parts: an organism, a poem, a people are not the sum of juxtaposed elements but a living unity from which each member draws its meaning. Against the analysis that decomposes and the mechanism that reduces, Romanticism thinks in organic totalities, where the higher is never explained by the lower.

*Sources : Schelling, Ideas for a Philosophy of Nature · Coleridge, Biographia Literaria*

### [Beauty](../axes/beauty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Subjective beauty 75% · Objective beauty 25%

Beauty no longer obeys objective proportions or the canons inherited from Antiquity: it is born of the expression of an inwardness and is measured by the emotion it arouses. Above all, Romanticism shifts the centre of aesthetics toward the [sublime](../glossary/sublime.md), that grandeur of the boundless, the formless and the fearful, which exceeds all measure and opens onto the infinite.

*Sources : Burke, A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful · Schiller, On the Sublime*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Culturalism 75% · Naturalism 25%

What makes a human being is not a universal, fixed nature but their language, their traditions, the spirit of the people that formed them: [Herder](../philosophers/herder.md) shows that to think, feel and judge are first of all inherited ways. Humanity exists only in the plural of its cultures, and one understands a person only by going back to the genius of their time and place.

*Sources : Herder, Treatise on the Origin of Language · Herder, Ideas on the Philosophy of the History of Humanity*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Epistemic pluralism 80% · Scientism 20%

Science has no monopoly on the true: by reducing everything to measure and calculation, it misses the living, the singular and the meaningful, which poetry, myth and intuition grasp better than it does. Romanticism contests the [Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md)'s claim to set scientific knowledge as the model of all knowing, and rehabilitates ways of reaching the real that calculating reason had disqualified.

*Sources : Novalis, The Novices of Sais · Schelling, Ideas for a Philosophy of Nature*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Immanence 65% · Transcendence 35%

The sacred lies not in a separate world beyond but is spread through nature and in the depths of the soul: all is alive, all is divine, and it is the infinite that shines through the finite. This pantheist re-divinization, inherited from [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), nonetheless borders on a [Sehnsucht](../glossary/sehnsucht.md) for the beyond, a longing for an absolute never reached, which draws Romanticism toward transcendence as much as it pulls it away.

*Sources : Novalis, Hymns to the Night · Schleiermacher, On Religion: Speeches*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anthropological optimism 75% · Anthropological pessimism 25%

Heir to [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md), Romanticism holds the human being to be naturally good and the spontaneity of the heart surer than the artifices of civilization: the child, the common people and the poet are nearer the truth than polished man. Yet this confidence is doubled by a fascination with the abysses of the soul, the demonic and evil, which tempers its optimism.

*Sources : Rousseau, Emile, or On Education · Schiller, On Naive and Sentimental Poetry*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Idealism 65% · Dualism 20% · Materialism 15%

For German philosophical Romanticism, spirit is primary and nature is its visible manifestation: far from being dead matter, the world is the work and the mirror of a spiritual activity that seeks itself in unfolding. [Schelling](../philosophers/schelling.md) posits the identity of spirit and nature in the absolute, and [Novalis](../philosophers/novalis.md) makes imagination the power that constitutes the real as much as it discovers it.

*Sources : Schelling, System of Transcendental Idealism · Novalis, Fragments*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Partialism 75% · Impartialism 25%

To love humanity in general is a cold abstraction: it is to the near, the familiar, to what is our own that the heart goes first, and this partiality of feeling is worth more than the calculated impartiality of the [Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md). Romanticism prefers the warmth of particular bonds, the homeland, the family, friendship, to the universal and disembodied benevolence of the cosmopolitan.

*Sources : Burke, Reflections on the Revolution in France · Herder, Ideas on the Philosophy of the History of Humanity*

### [Nature of love](../axes/love-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eros as lack 65% · Agape as gift 25% · Ruse of the species 10%

Romantic love is wholly aspiration and lack: in the beloved it desires the infinite itself, and it is from this never-filled distance that it draws its intensity. Far from tranquil possession, it is a surge toward a glimpsed absolute, the longing for a union that slips away, which is why it borders so readily on night and on the unreachable.

*Sources : Novalis, Hymns to the Night · Hölderlin, Hyperion*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Equity-phronesis 75% · Legalism 25%

No abstract rule can enclose life: genius does not receive its law from without but gives it to itself, and the singular case, the exception, the impulse of the heart are worth more than the uniform letter. Against the moral legalism of the [Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md) and the coldness of the universal maxim, Romanticism trusts the living spirit, the situation and sensuous intuition rather than the formal command.

*Sources : Schiller, Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man · F. Schlegel, Athenaeum Fragments*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 60% · Constructed meaning 30% · Absurdism 10%

The world is neither an indifferent mechanism nor an absurd chaos: it is shot through with a meaning, a spirit unfolding within it to which the human being is called to respond. The Romantic feeling of the infinite and the longing for a lost unity presuppose that existence has a direction and a depth, however veiled: meaning is not to be invented from nothing but to be sensed and recovered in nature, art and history.

*Sources : Novalis, The Novices of Sais · Schelling, Ideas for a Philosophy of Nature*

## Influenced by

- **[The Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md)** (reacts against) : [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md) is born in head-on reaction to the Enlightenment: to universal reason, mechanism and the cult of progress it opposes feeling, imagination, the singular genius of a people and the mystery of a living nature. It nonetheless extends the sensibility of [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md), an Enlightenment philosopher turned into its own counter-current: the Romantic reaction thus grows from within the very thing it contests.
- **[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)** (reinterprets) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)'s third Critique arms Romanticism: his theory of [genius](../glossary/genie.md), to whom « nature gives the rule to art », his analysis of the [sublime](../glossary/sublime.md) and his productive imagination become the bedrock of Romantic aesthetics. Romanticism also extends the momentum of post-Kantian idealism, while overstepping the limit Kant set to reason: it claims to reach, through art and intuition, the very absolute Kant declared unknowable.
- **[Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md)** (continues) : [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md) recognizes [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) as its precursor: it takes up and radicalizes his rehabilitation of feeling against reason, his cult of nature and of the authenticity of the self, and his critique of civilization. The Confessions and the Reveries inaugurate the Romantic exploration of inwardness, and the « religion of the heart » of the Savoyard vicar heralds the religious feeling of the Romantics.
- **[Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md)** (reinterprets) : [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md) reawakens [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), long held to be accursed: the pantheism controversy (Jacobi, 1785) places him back at the centre, and his single, immanent, living God-nature becomes the soul of [Schelling](../philosophers/schelling.md)'s [Naturphilosophie](../glossary/naturphilosophie.md) and of [Novalis](../philosophers/novalis.md)'s cosmic piety. The Romantics make of him less the cold geometer of the Ethics than a mystic of the unity of the whole, the « God-intoxicated man ».

## Influence on

- **[Environmental Ethics](../movements/environmental-ethics.md)** (reinterprets) : Environmental ethics inherits from [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md), often without saying so, its revolt against nature reduced to a machine and a stock of resources: the living, sublime nature, worthy of respect for itself, which ecology turns into a moral value and no longer merely an aesthetic one.
- **[Existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md)** (continues) : [Existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md) gathers from Romanticism the exaltation of the singular individual against the system and the abstract universal, the primacy of [lived experience](../axes/interiority.md), passion and anguish, and the demand for [authenticity](../glossary/authenticity.md). [Kierkegaard](../philosophers/kierkegaard.md), a critical reader of Romanticism and of Hegel, is its decisive relay: the Romantic « self », irreducible to reason, becomes the existing individual who chooses himself.
- **[Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md)** (reinterprets) : [Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md) takes up from Romanticism its critique of the world-as-machine and of calculative reason, its listening to nature and its privileging of the poetic as access to being. His reading of Hölderlin, the Romantic poet held to voice the « default of the gods » and the danger of technology, lies at the heart of his late thought: « to dwell poetically » extends the Romantic dream of a re-enchanted world.
- **[Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md)** (reinterprets) : [Nietzsche](../philosophers/nietzsche.md) inherits more from Romanticism than he admits: the Dionysian of The Birth of Tragedy, art held to be « the metaphysical activity par excellence », the revolt against Socratic reason and the Enlightenment all come from this source, transmitted through [Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md) and Wagner. But he soon turns these weapons against Romanticism itself, which he denounces as nostalgia, weakness and flight: he gathers its energy in order to reject its melancholy.
