# The Enlightenment

*1685 – 1804*

A vast European intellectual movement of the eighteenth century, the « Age of Enlightenment », which extends the scientific revolution of Newton and the empiricism of [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) to make critical reason the instrument of a general emancipation. Carried in France by the philosophes, [Voltaire](../philosophers/voltaire.md), [Montesquieu](../philosophers/montesquieu.md), [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md), [Diderot](../philosophers/diderot.md) and [d'Alembert](../philosophers/d-alembert.md) whose Encyclopédie drew up an inventory of all knowledge, relayed in Scotland by [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) and [Smith](../philosophers/adam-smith.md), and in Germany by the [Aufklärung](../glossary/lumieres.md) that [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) summed up in a motto, « Sapere aude », it spread through the salons, the journals and the Republic of Letters. Aimed against « throne and altar », its ideas of tolerance, natural rights and government by consent ran through the American and French revolutions and the declarations of rights. Opposed from the outset by [Burke](../philosophers/burke.md) and the counter-revolutionary current, then by [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md), its rationalist legacy would also be questioned in the twentieth century, from the « dialectic of reason » to critiques of the idea of progress.

**Key figures** : [Montesquieu](../philosophers/montesquieu.md) · [Voltaire](../philosophers/voltaire.md) · [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) · [Diderot](../philosophers/diderot.md) · [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) · [Condorcet](../philosophers/condorcet.md)

## Core positions

1. [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md) : **No authority, tradition, custom or revelation escapes examination: everything must appear before the tribunal of reason and there justify its claims.**
2. [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md) : **Humanity progresses: through enlightenment and good institutions it tears itself from ignorance and servitude, and that progress has no assignable limit.**
3. [Nature of rights](../axes/rights-nature.md) : **Every human being possesses, by the mere fact of his rational nature, universal and inalienable rights that no power confers and that none may legitimately abolish.**
4. [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md) : **The only legitimate source of power is the consent of the governed, not force, tradition or the divine right of kings.**
5. [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md) : **Man is not corrupt by nature but perfectible: his vices stem from ignorance and bad institutions, and education can remake him.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 80% · Fecundity and redemption 20%

The Enlightenment makes the reduction of suffering an object of progress: against judicial torture (Beccaria), fanaticism and its cruelties (Voltaire), the diseases that science must push back, it refuses to sanctify pain or to resign oneself to it. Avoidable suffering is an evil that reason and reform are charged with fighting.

*Sources : Beccaria, On Crimes and Punishments · Voltaire, Treatise on Tolerance*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 75% · Teleology and providence 20% · Absurdism 5%

The Enlightenment shifts meaning from providence and tradition toward man himself: it is through the public use of reason, the progress of knowledge and autonomy that humanity makes its own history and improves its condition. Even among the deists, the remote God no longer destines: meaning is built here below, through emancipation from tutelage.

*Sources : Kant, What Is Enlightenment? · Condorcet, Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Scientism 78% · Epistemic pluralism 22%

The model is the physics of Newton: a handful of mathematical laws accounts for the whole system of the world, with no occult qualities or providential ends. The Enlightenment turns this success into a universal method; what experimental reason has done for the heavens it can do for morals, politics and religion. No authority, tradition, custom or revelation is exempt from examination; everything must appear before the tribunal of reason. This is not yet the scientism of [Comte](../philosophers/comte.md), which will make positive science the only knowledge, but science here becomes the paradigm of legitimate knowledge and the lever of emancipation.

> “Sapere aude! Have the courage to use your own understanding: that is the motto of the Enlightenment.” (Kant, What Is Enlightenment? (1784))

*Sources : Kant, What Is Enlightenment? · d'Alembert, Preliminary Discourse to the Encyclopédie*

### [Scientific method](../axes/scientific-method.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Inductivism 80% · Bayesianism 20%

Hostile to a priori systems and gratuitous hypotheses, the Enlightenment makes observation and experiment the source of all physics: one rises from facts to laws by [induction](../glossary/induction.md), in the manner of [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md), whom the Encyclopédie claims as its patron. [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) nonetheless radicalizes this programme by showing that induction itself is grounded not in reason but in habit. In parallel, the calculus of probabilities of Laplace and [Condorcet](../philosophers/condorcet.md) opens a quantitative path to uncertainty.

*Sources : Bacon, Novum Organum · Condorcet, Essay on the Application of Analysis to the Probability of Majority Decisions*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Skepticism 57% · Dogmatism 43%

The inaugural gesture is critical: against the [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md) of school metaphysics and revealed truths, the task is to measure reason against its own limits. But this distrust is not the destructive [scepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) of [Pyrrho](../philosophers/pyrrho.md): [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) tempers his doubt into a mitigated, workable scepticism, and [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) turns it into a critique that bounds knowledge the better to ground it. Certainty now aims at what withstands examination, not at what imposes itself by authority, a [fallibilist](../glossary/fallibilism.md) stance.

*Sources : Kant, Critique of Pure Reason (preface) · Hume, Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. XII*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Empiricism 70% · Rationalism 30%

The dominant epistemology of the philosophes is that of [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md): the mind is at first a [blank slate](../glossary/tabula-rasa.md), and all our ideas come from experience. [Condillac](../philosophers/condillac.md) draws from this a thoroughgoing sensationism in which even the operations of the mind reduce to transformed sensations. This empiricism feeds the critique of innate ideas, the supposed weapons of prejudice and authority. The current is not unanimous, however: a rationalist strand inherited from [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) persists, and [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) will go beyond the alternative by showing that experience itself presupposes a priori forms of the understanding.

*Sources : Locke, Essay Concerning Human Understanding, book II · Condillac, Treatise on the Sensations*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Scientific realism 80% · Constructivism 20%

Newtonian confidence carries a realist thesis: the laws of nature are not convenient conventions but the real structure of an ordered world, which reason deciphers rather than constructs. To know is to lift the veil of prejudice and appearance and reach things as they are; error stems from ignorance and imposture, not from any relativity built into knowledge itself. This objectivism grounds the authority the Enlightenment grants to science over opinions and traditions.

*Sources : d'Alembert, Preliminary Discourse to the Encyclopédie · Voltaire, Elements of Newton's Philosophy*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 70% · Existentialism 30%

The project of a science of man presupposes a universal and stable human nature: beneath the diversity of customs, everywhere the same faculties, the same passions, the same reason. It is this underlying identity that makes possible rights valid for all and a morality that is not the mere reflection of local custom. Yet the essentialism remains moderate: human nature is one, but its faculties are latent and develop through education, so that man is less a fixed essence than a capacity to form himself.

*Sources : Hume, Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, sect. VIII · Diderot, article "Homme" in the Encyclopédie*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Progressivism 90% · Conservatism 10%

From the accumulation of knowledge and the spread of enlightenment the current draws a new thesis: humanity advances, and that march can be conscious and willed. Institutions and laws are not sacred merely for being old; they must prove their present usefulness, failing which reform is legitimate. [Condorcet](../philosophers/condorcet.md) pushes the idea to a law of history, the human mind progressing by stages toward truth, liberty and happiness. Against this creed [Burke](../philosophers/burke.md) will set the accumulated wisdom of tradition: this is the birth certificate of the divide between progressivism and conservatism.

> “The perfectibility of man is truly indefinite; and the progress of this perfectibility has no other limit than the duration of the globe on which nature has placed us.” (Condorcet, Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind (1795))

*Sources : Condorcet, Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind · Turgot, On the Successive Advances of the Human Mind*

### [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Directional meaning 80% · Contingency 20%

History ceases to be a succession of reigns or a decline from a golden age and becomes the directed development of reason. [Condorcet](../philosophers/condorcet.md) divides it into ten epochs marching toward emancipation; [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) reads in it the unfolding, through the very « unsociable sociability » of men, of a design of nature toward a cosmopolitan society. Meaning is not conferred by an external providence but is immanent to the movement of societies, legible to whoever can interpret it. This philosophy of history will be inherited by [Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md) and [Marx](../philosophers/marx.md).

*Sources : Kant, Idea for a Universal History with a Cosmopolitan Aim · Condorcet, Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind*

### [Nature of rights](../axes/rights-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Natural rights 70% · Rights from utility 15% · Political constructivism 10% · Legal positivism 5%

Prior to all society and all positive law, man possesses as his own, by the mere fact of his rational nature, rights that no power has conferred and that none may legitimately abolish: to life, to liberty, to the property of his own person. [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) makes them the measure of every government, instituted only to secure them. These rights are universal because they flow from a common humanity, not from the privileges of an order or a birth: this is the weapon against absolutism and the society of estates, which the Declaration of 1789 will inscribe into law, « “men are born and remain free and equal in rights” ». [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md), from within the century, will nonetheless object that a right without positive law is mere « nonsense upon stilts ».

> “Being all equal and independent, no one ought to harm another in his life, health, liberty, or possessions.” (Locke, Second Treatise of Government, §6)

*Sources : Locke, Two Treatises of Government, II · Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789)*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Universalism 70% · Relativism 15% · Constructivism 15%

If reason is one and everywhere the same, then the principles of justice hold universally, and not by divine decree or local custom. Morality is secularized: it no longer depends on a revelation but rests on reason or on sentiments common to all men, which makes it possible to judge established mores rather than submit to them. [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) will give it its most rigorous form, a moral law valid for every rational being. Yet the current carries its own limit within it: [Montesquieu](../philosophers/montesquieu.md)'s attention to the diversity of laws according to climate and people opens the way to the relativism that will soon contest this universalism.

*Sources : Kant, Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals · Montesquieu, The Spirit of the Laws, book I*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Contractualism 90% · Organic community 10%

Political society is not a natural order or one willed by God, but a human institution born of the agreement of individuals: its sole source of legitimacy is the consent of the governed, not force, tradition or the divine right of kings. [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) makes it a revocable pact that the people may renounce if power betrays its end; [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) seeks the form in which, uniting with all, each « still obeys only himself » and remains free. Against this founding fiction [Burke](../philosophers/burke.md) will set the organic community, inherited rather than contracted: but it is contractualism that gives the age of revolutions its grammar of legitimacy.

> “Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains.” (Rousseau, The Social Contract (1762), I, 1)

*Sources : Rousseau, The Social Contract · Locke, Two Treatises of Government, II*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Authority 50% · Anarchism 50%

Against divine-right absolutism, the Enlightenment advocates not the absence of power but its limitation by law. [Montesquieu](../philosophers/montesquieu.md) supplies the mechanism: for « power to check power », the legislative, executive and judicial must be separated and balanced. Liberty is not release from all authority but the security afforded by good laws. The century is nonetheless crossed by a tension: from [Voltaire](../philosophers/voltaire.md) to [Diderot](../philosophers/diderot.md), several philosophes also look to the reforms of an enlightened despot, not always with a constitutional guarantee.

*Sources : Montesquieu, The Spirit of the Laws, book XI · Voltaire, Philosophical Letters*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Individualism 70% · Holism 30%

Society is thought from the individual, not the reverse: it is the individual, subject of rights and reason, who comes first, and institutions exist only to serve his ends. This individualism, extended by the nascent economics of [Smith](../philosophers/adam-smith.md), underlies the critique of the bodies, estates and corporations that hem the person in. [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) is the exception here, subordinating the individual to the [general will](../glossary/volonte-generale.md), a sign that the current is not monolithic.

*Sources : Locke, Two Treatises of Government · Adam Smith, An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Cosmopolitanism 80% · Communitarianism and patriotism 20%

A citizen of the world before being a citizen of his city, the man of the Enlightenment claims a belonging that overflows borders and confessions. Reason and humanity being common to all, national and religious particularisms weigh less than what unites the human race. [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) draws from this a project of cosmopolitan right and « perpetual peace » among nations; the Republic of Letters already offers its lived figure, a learned community without a homeland. This cosmopolitanism nourishes the critique of war, slavery and fanaticism.

*Sources : Kant, Toward Perpetual Peace · Voltaire, Philosophical Dictionary, art. "Fatherland"*

### [The meaning of punishment](../axes/punishment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Deterrence 50% · Rehabilitation 35% · Restorative justice 10% · Retributivism 5%

[Beccaria](../philosophers/beccaria.md) shifts punishment from the register of expiation to that of social utility: the penalty should not avenge the crime or satisfy a retributive justice, but prevent it. From this flow his reforms, the abolition of torture and the death penalty, the proportion of penalties to offences, the certainty rather than the cruelty of punishment, and prevention through education and good laws. Punishment is measured by its effects on the social body, not by the moral evil committed: a reforming programme that utilitarianism will take up.

*Sources : Beccaria, On Crimes and Punishments (1764)*

### [Political liberty](../axes/political-liberty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Negative liberty 45% · Republican non-domination 30% · Positive liberty 20% · Capability freedom 5%

The political liberty of the century comes in several voices. For the liberal strand descended from [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md), it is first of all negative: a sphere of security and property in which the individual is unhindered. For [Montesquieu](../philosophers/montesquieu.md), it is non-domination, « the right to do everything the laws permit », secured against arbitrariness by the [separation of powers](../glossary/separation-des-pouvoirs.md). [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) proposes its positive version, the autonomy of a people that gives itself its own laws. These distinct, sometimes rival accents share the refusal of arbitrary subjection.

*Sources : Montesquieu, The Spirit of the Laws, XI, 3-6 · Rousseau, The Social Contract, book I*

### [Vision of humanity](../axes/human-vision.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Anthropological optimism 63% · Anthropological pessimism 38%

Against the dogma of original sin, which makes man a corrupt creature to be redeemed, the Enlightenment wagers on his [perfectibility](../glossary/perfectibilite.md): his vices owe less to his nature than to bad institutions and to ignorance. If the mind is a [blank slate](../glossary/tabula-rasa.md), then education can do almost everything, holds [Helvétius](../philosophers/helvetius.md): change the laws, the enlightenment and the mores, and you change men. [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) radicalizes the wager, man being naturally good and corrupted by society, while [Voltaire](../philosophers/voltaire.md), more sombre, keeps it within measure. Hence a tempered optimism, founded not on what man is but on what he can become.

*Sources : Helvétius, On the Mind · Rousseau, Discourse on the Origin of Inequality · Locke, Some Thoughts Concerning Education*

### [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Techno-optimism 80% · Techno-criticism 20%

The Encyclopédie performs an unprecedented gesture: bringing the mechanical arts, the trades and the machines into knowledge, on an equal footing with the sciences. Technology is here no mere convenience but a vehicle of emancipation, which lifts man out of misery and need and extends his dominion over nature. Heirs to [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md)'s programme, the Enlightenment ties the progress of the arts to the progress of enlightenment: mastering nature and instructing men go together.

*Sources : Diderot and d'Alembert, Encyclopédie, art. "Art" · Bacon, Novum Organum*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mastery 75% · Member among others 15% · Stewardship 10%

In the wake of [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md) and [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md), the current treats nature as a field to be known in order to master it and put it at the service of man: knowledge is power, and the progress of the sciences is worth the ills it relieves. Yet this conquering anthropocentrism is not without counterpoint: [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md) already contests the idea that the arts and the domestication of the world have made us better, opening a critique that will only ripen later.

*Sources : Bacon, Novum Organum, book I · Buffon, Natural History*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mechanism 55% · Vitalism 25% · Finalism 20%

The Newtonian universe lends itself to a mechanist reading: a system of bodies in motion governed by laws, without ends or souls. [La Mettrie](../philosophers/la-mettrie.md) draws the extreme consequence with « man a machine », and the materialism of [d'Holbach](../philosophers/holbach.md) reduces everything to matter and motion. The century is not mechanist without remainder: [Diderot](../philosophers/diderot.md) and the naturalists grant matter a sensibility and activity of its own, the sketch of a vitalism, while a deist finalism persists that reads in the order of the world the mark of a great clockmaker.

*Sources : La Mettrie, Man a Machine (1747) · d'Holbach, The System of Nature · Diderot, D'Alembert's Dream*

### [Faith and reason](../axes/faith-reason.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Conflict 45% · Separate domains 30% · Harmony 25%

On religion the current makes common front even where it diverges about God: reason, not revelation or the Church, is judge in the last instance. Many hold revealed religion to be incompatible with it, mixed with superstition and fanaticism: it is the « Infamous Thing » that [Voltaire](../philosophers/voltaire.md) would « crush ». Others substitute for it a natural religion, or [deism](../glossary/deisme.md), attuned to reason: a clockmaker God without miracles or clergy. From this primacy follow the cardinal demand of [tolerance](../glossary/tolerance.md) and the separation of the civil from the religious: [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) shows that no one can be compelled to believe, and that salvation is no concern of the State.

*Sources : Voltaire, Treatise on Tolerance · Locke, A Letter Concerning Toleration*

## Influenced by

- **[Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md)** (continues) : [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md) bequeaths to the Enlightenment the programme of a knowledge founded on experience and turned toward utility, « knowledge is power ». [Diderot](../philosophers/diderot.md) and [d'Alembert](../philosophers/d-alembert.md) salute him as the inspiration of the Encyclopédie, whose Preliminary Discourse traces his genealogy of the sciences.
- **[Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)** (continues) : [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)'s *[Essay](../works/essay-concerning-human-understanding-locke.md)* gives the philosophes their epistemology, the [blank slate](../glossary/tabula-rasa.md) and the critique of innate ideas; his *[Two Treatises](../works/two-treatises-of-government-locke.md)* and *[Letter Concerning Toleration](../works/letter-concerning-toleration-locke.md)* supply them with natural rights, government by consent and [tolerance](../glossary/tolerance.md). [Voltaire](../philosophers/voltaire.md) hails him from the Philosophical Letters onward as the model of the English sage.
- **[Newton](../philosophers/newton.md)** (continues) : [Newton](../philosophers/newton.md)'s mechanics, deducing the system of the world from a few mathematical laws, becomes the Enlightenment's paradigm of knowledge: [Voltaire](../philosophers/voltaire.md) popularizes it in France, and the programme of extending the « Newtonian method » to morals and society runs through the whole century.
- **[Rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md)** (continues) : From [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md) the Enlightenment inherits confidence in a reason able to judge on its own, and [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s methodical doubt as a weapon against prejudice. The current's radical wing also extends, more underground, [Spinozism](../philosophers/spinoza.md) and its critique of revealed religion.

## Influence on

- **[Comte](../philosophers/comte.md)** (continues) : [Comte](../philosophers/comte.md) systematizes the authority the Enlightenment granted to science: his law of three stages makes the « positive » age the culmination of the progress of mind sketched by [Condorcet](../philosophers/condorcet.md), whom he claims as his chief precursor.
- **[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)** (continues) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) brings the Enlightenment to its philosophical completion: he overcomes the quarrel of [empiricism and rationalism](../axes/knowledge-source.md) in his criticism, and makes the autonomy of reason the heart of his morality and of his very definition of Enlightenment, « man's emergence from his immaturity ».
- **[Liberalism](../movements/liberalism.md)** (continues) : Political liberalism extends the [Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md): from it, it draws the natural rights of the individual, toleration, government founded on consent, and distrust of any absolute power. [Montesquieu](../philosophers/montesquieu.md) adds the separation of powers as a safeguard against despotism. From the Enlightenment liberalism keeps the individual come of age, capable of conducting themselves by their own reason.
- **[Utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md)** (continues) : [Utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md) inherits the secular, reforming morality of the Enlightenment: [Bentham](../philosophers/bentham.md)'s principle of utility extends [Beccaria](../philosophers/beccaria.md)'s reformed penal thought and the calculus of pains and pleasures already present in [Helvétius](../philosophers/helvetius.md).
- **[Burke](../philosophers/burke.md)** (reacts against) : In his Reflections on the Revolution in France, [Burke](../philosophers/burke.md) attacks Enlightenment politics: to abstract rights and the rationalist [blank slate](../glossary/tabula-rasa.md) he opposes inherited rights, useful prejudices and the accumulated wisdom of tradition. It is the founding act of modern conservatism.
- **[Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md)** (reacts against) : [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md) is born in head-on reaction to the Enlightenment: to universal reason, mechanism and the cult of progress it opposes feeling, imagination, the singular genius of a people and the mystery of a living nature. It nonetheless extends the sensibility of [Rousseau](../philosophers/rousseau.md), an Enlightenment philosopher turned into its own counter-current: the Romantic reaction thus grows from within the very thing it contests.
