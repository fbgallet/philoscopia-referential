# Confucianism

*500 BCE – ?*

A tradition born from the Analects (Lunyu) of [Confucius](../philosophers/confucius.md) (Kongzi) in the China in crisis of the Spring and Autumn period, and anchored in the ancient Classics (the Odes, the Documents, the Rites). In the age of the Warring States two heirs carried it in opposite directions: Mencius held human nature to be spontaneously good, endowed with sprouts of virtue that one need only cultivate, while Xunzi judged it bad and entrusted the rites (li 禮) and education with the task of straightening it. Sidelined and then rehabilitated under the Han, the school became the imperial orthodoxy and the matter of the mandarin examinations, which for centuries recruited officials on their mastery of the Classics. The Neo-Confucianism of the Song era added to it a metaphysics of li (principle) and qi (vital energy). Spread across all of East Asia, in Korea, Japan and Vietnam, this thought answers one constant question: how to remake social and political order through the moral transformation of persons rather than by force.

**Key figures** : [Confucius](../philosophers/confucius.md) · [Mencius](../philosophers/mencius.md) · [Xunzi](../philosophers/xunzi.md)

## Core positions

1. [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md) : **One must love by degrees, beginning with one's own: the impartial love (jian'ai) that treats one's father like a stranger in truth loves no one.**
2. [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md) : **Led by punishment, people obey without shame; led by virtue and example, they reform of their own accord.**
3. [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : **It is character that must be transformed, not a rule prescribed: one becomes good by cultivating ren 仁 (humaneness) up to the figure of the junzi.**
4. [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md) : **The Way is not to be invented but recovered: the sage-kings already realized it, and it is by transmitting their rites (li 禮) that order is remade.**
5. [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md) : **Order holds when each fulfills the function of his name, the ruler being ruler and the son son: such is the rectification of names (zhengming 正名).**

## Positions

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*contextualist*

Confucian thought falls under neither personal theism nor atheism: Heaven (tian) is here a moral order and a regulating principle more than a personal creator God. Confucius honors the spirits and the ancestors but refrains from discoursing on them, shifting attention onto the duties of this life. The Western alternative "God exists / does not exist" does not capture this position.

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 75% · Abolitionism 25%

Confucianism grants ordeal a formative fruitfulness: it is in adversity that the gentleman is distinguished, and Mencius teaches that Heaven hardens through suffering and toil those it destines for high tasks. Ordeal is no pure misfortune but the occasion to temper character and to strengthen virtue.

*Sources : Mencius, VI B, 15 · Analects (Lunyu), XV.2*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 60% · Constructed meaning 40%

Confucianism sets meaning within the Mandate of Heaven, a normative order and Way that surpass the individual; but this order holds only as accomplished in self-cultivation, the rites and the rectitude of human relationships. Meaning is thus received from a heavenly order and realized through ethical effort, never left to individual choice alone nor held to be absent.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu) · Doctrine of the Mean (Zhongyong)*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Freeing ourselves from the fear 50% · Preparing for the passage 40% · Living in view of one's death 10%

Confucianism turns away from speculation about the hereafter to shift care onto present life and duties, but surrounds death with rites and perpetuates the cult of ancestors. The dead thus remain present in the memory and piety of the lineage: not an annihilation to be feared, nor truly a survival of the soul, but a continuity through filial piety and transmission.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu); Classic of Filial Piety*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 65% · Eudaimonism 30% · Hedonism 5%

The Confucian highest good is moral: to become fully humane (ren), an exemplary person (junzi) accomplished through long self-cultivation, the rites and study. This moral excellence is also a flourishing, the human being realizing his proper vocation in society and right relationships, but pleasure is never the end.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu) · Mencius*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Reasoned selection 70% · Affirmation 15% · Extinction and detachment 15%

Confucianism civilizes desire through the rites and propriety rather than extinguishing it or affirming it without restraint: one satisfies one's inclinations in the right forms and to the right measure. Moral education aims at the accord of desire and norm, where propriety becomes second nature and the good is done without constraint.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), II.4 and XII.1 · Xunzi (on rites and desires)*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 60% · Existentialism 40%

Confucianism assumes a common human nature, held to be good in its orthodoxy (Mencius' four sprouts), but one that is fulfilled only through long self-cultivation, study and the rites. A moderate essentialism, then: a given essence, whose realization nonetheless depends entirely on formation.

*Sources : Mencius, II A and VI A · Analects, XVII.2*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 60% · Hard determinism 40%

Confucianism divides one's lot between Heaven and humanity: the span of life, wealth and honors fall under the decree (ming) that the sage accepts, but self-cultivation and virtue depend entirely on the will. Moral betterment is always within reach of whoever wills it, which sets the movement on the side of a moral freedom, against a background of accepted outward destiny.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), XII.5 · Mencius, VII A*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 70% · Rationalism 30%

Confucian knowledge is won through study, observation and the imitation of the ancients, not through innate ideas. To learn is to steep oneself in the texts, the rites and the examples, a patient formation through experience and tradition rather than through rational deduction; the Master himself claims to be not one born wise, but a lover of antiquity, quick to seek it there.

> “I am not one who was born with knowledge; I love the ancients and earnestly seek it there.”, Analects, VII.20

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), VII.1 and VII.20 · Analects, II.15*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Skepticism 60% · Dogmatism 40%

Confucian wisdom rests on a measured **epistemic humility**: “to know that you know what you know, and to know that you do not know what you do not know, that is knowledge”. Confucius sets aside speculation about spirits or about death, not yet having understood life, and prefers to metaphysics the study of the texts, the rites and the example of the sages. Neither systematic doubt nor dogmatic system: a practical trust in the Way, proven in conduct rather than in theory.

*Sources : Analects (Lunyu), II.17 and XI.12*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 90% · Deontologism 10%

No law and no calculation can stand in for an upright character: what makes the good is the acquired disposition from which the right act springs naturally, not an external rule one applies. Hence the primacy of self-cultivation (xiushen) over prescription: the moral life is the gradual acquisition of virtues, chief among them ren 仁 (humaneness), whose accomplished figure is the junzi (the person of noble character). This is why Confucianism distrusts solutions by decree, whether the universal love decreed by the Mohists or the order imposed by the penal law of the Legalists: only the transformation of the heart yields a reliable agent.

> “The noble person seeks it in himself; the small person seeks it in others.”, Analects (Lunyu), 15.21

*Sources : Confucius, Analects (Lunyu) · Mencius (Mengzi), 2A6*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Partialism 90% · Impartialism 10%

Real affection does not arise from an abstract principle but from the lived bond, and it is in the family that it is first learned, through filial piety (xiao), before extending outward in circles to community and world. On this rootedness depends the very possibility of a workable morality: a love claiming to embrace all people equally holds to nothing and moves no one. This is the decisive objection to the Mohists' impartial love (jian'ai): Mencius charges them with treating one's own father as a passing stranger, which amounts to abolishing humaneness itself in the name of humaneness for all. To love by degrees is not to fail the universal but to give it the only root that makes it effective.

*Sources : Mencius (Mengzi), 3B9 · Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 1.2*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Conservatism 90% · Progressivism 10%

Human order has already been realized once, by the sage-kings of antiquity (Yao, Shun, the Zhou): there is therefore an accomplished model to recover, not a Way to invent from scratch. The present is in disorder precisely because it has broken with that model, hence the task: to transmit and revive the inherited rites (li 禮) rather than replace them. This trust in the legacy sets Confucianism apart both from [Daoist](../movements/taoism.md) naturalism, which would undo the artifice of the rites to recover the spontaneity of the [dao](../glossary/dao.md), and from Legalist innovation, which grounds order on new laws indifferent to the past. For the Confucian, moral authority is not decreed: it is received from a tested tradition.

> “I transmit but do not innovate; I trust in and love the ancients.”, Analects (Lunyu), 7.1

*Sources : Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 7.1 · Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 3.14*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Communitarianism and patriotism 90% · Cosmopolitanism 10%

The moral identity of the Confucian subject is woven from its belongings: the family, the lineage, the local community, and the state ordered by the rites (li 禮). One becomes fully human only within these relationships (the five cardinal relations), not as an abstract individual or an undifferentiated citizen of the world.

*Sources : Mencius (Mengzi), 3A4 · Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 12.11*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Organic community 93% · Contractualism 7%

Since the person exists only as caught within its relations, social order is not built by convention among already-constituted individuals but is restored when each fulfills the function of his name: let the ruler be ruler, the father father, the son son. Such is the sense of the rectification of names (zhengming 正名): naming correctly is no logical game but the precondition of all government, for disorder begins where names and conduct come apart. This way sets aside the contract, which presupposes individuals prior to their bonds, as much as the Legalist solution: it is not punishment that aligns acts with roles, but the shame felt by one who falls short of his name.

*Sources : Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 12.11 · Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 13.3*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Memory and transmission 80% · Living in the present 10% · Project 10%

Confucianism orients existence toward transmission: the past of the sage-kings is the model to study, recover, and hand down, and the veneration of ancestors anchors the present in the continuity of the generations. To learn is to revisit the old so as to draw the new from it: memory is the mainspring of moral and political life.

*Sources : Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 2.11 · Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 1.9*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The examined life 70% · Primacy of action 30%

Self-cultivation (xiushen) lies at the heart of the Confucian Way: the daily examination of one's conduct precedes and grounds action upon the world, following the sequence of The Great Learning (Daxue), which runs from rectifying the heart to governing the realm. Master Zeng says he examines himself daily on three points: loyalty, faithfulness, study. Yet this inwardness remains turned toward relational practice, never toward pure contemplation.

*Sources : Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 1.4 · The Great Learning (Daxue)*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Holism 85% · Individualism 15%

The Confucian person exists fully only as caught within the web of his relations: the self is constituted by its roles and duties toward others, not as an autonomous atom prior to society. Personal accomplishment and the order of the social whole are bound together: to cultivate oneself is already to work for the harmony of family, state, and world.

*Sources : The Great Learning (Daxue) · Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 6.30*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Authority 85% · Anarchism 15%

Governing by law and punishment yields at best subjects who avoid the penalty without blushing at the fault: coercion regulates acts but leaves hearts untouched, and the order so kept stays external and fragile. Governing by virtue and example, by contrast, gives a sense of shame and a reformed heart, hence an obedience that comes of itself. This is Confucianism's decisive argument against the Legalists: the hierarchical authority it embraces, of ruler and of father alike, is legitimate only as moral and benevolent, never as arbitrary domination, for its efficacy lies in its power to attract rather than to compel.

> “He who governs by virtue may be compared to the pole star, which keeps its place while all the other stars turn toward it.”, Analects (Lunyu), 2.1

*Sources : Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 2.1 · Confucius, Analects (Lunyu), 2.3*
