# Vedānta (Advaita)

*700 – ?*

Vedānta ("the end of the Veda") is the great tradition that interprets the Upaniṣads, the speculative texts that close the Vedic corpus, through the threefold canon (prasthāna-traya) formed by those Upaniṣads, the Brahma-Sūtras ascribed to Bādarāyaṇa (around the second century) and the Bhagavad-Gītā. Its most influential school, Advaita ("not-two"), received its systematic form from Gauḍapāda and then above all from Śaṅkara around the eighth century, amid a close debate with Buddhism, whose tools it borrows even as it fights it, and with the realist and dualist schools (Nyāya, Sāṅkhya, ritualist Mīmāṃsā). The problem it confronts is that of saṃsāra and karman: how to win final release from the cycle of rebirths and the suffering it carries. Vedānta is not monolithic: alongside non-dualist Advaita stand the theistic Viśiṣṭādvaita of Rāmānuja (qualified non-dualism) and the dualist Dvaita of Madhva, which uphold a real distinction between the soul and God. Carried into the modern era by Vivekananda and the neo-Vedānta, it has become one of the matrices of contemporary Hinduism and the most widely received Indian philosophy in the West, from [Schopenhauer](../philosophers/schopenhauer.md) to twentieth-century spiritualities.

**Key figures** : [Śaṅkara](../philosophers/shankara.md) · [Gauḍapāda](../philosophers/gaudapada.md) · [Bādarāyaṇa](../philosophers/badarayana.md)

## Core positions

1. [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md) : **Only [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md), pure consciousness, is real; the plurality of the world is mere [māyā](../glossary/maya.md), appearance superimposed on the one absolute.**
2. [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : **The [Self (ātman)](../glossary/atman.md) is identical with [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md): "thou art that" (tat tvam asi), and no flux of aggregates could appear without this permanent witness.**
3. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **The ultimate absolute is without attributes (nirguṇa); the personal creator God is only its conventional (vyāvahārika) figure, seen through [māyā](../glossary/maya.md).**
4. [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md) : **Suffering arises from an error of knowledge, not from being: like the fear of a snake that was only a rope, it is dispelled, not redeemed.**
5. [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md) : **The end of life is [mokṣa](../glossary/moksha.md): not a goal to be built but the conscious return of the Self to what it has always been, through knowledge and not through act.**

## Positions

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Deontologism 50% · Virtue ethics 45% · Consequentialism 5%

Vedanta's ethics is rooted in dharma, the duty proper to each according to one's order and station, to be fulfilled for its own sake. The Bhagavad Gita gives its formula, disinterested action (nishkama karma): to act without attachment to the fruits, from duty and not from calculation. To which is added the cultivation of detachment and self-knowledge, a virtue leading to liberation.

*Sources : Bhagavad Gita, II-III · Shankara*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 70% · Eudaimonism 25% · Hedonism 5%

The highest good of Vedanta is liberation (moksha): release from the cycle of rebirths through the knowledge that the Self (atman) is identical with Brahman. It is neither a pleasure nor a worldly virtue, but the supreme realization, a bliss (ananda) beyond all sensible satisfaction, which presupposes detachment and spiritual discipline.

*Sources : Upanishads (Chandogya, Mundaka) · Shankara, The Crest-Jewel of Discrimination*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Hard determinism 65% · Free will 35%

At the empirical level, the embodied self (jiva) is bound by karma and wrapped in maya: its acts bear their fruits and keep it within the cycle of rebirths, a largely determined order. True freedom is therefore not the self-determination of a will, but liberation (moksha), the recognition that the Self (atman) is identical with Brahman, beyond the causal order of acts.

*Sources : Shankara, Commentary on the Brahma Sutras · Upanishads (Katha, Mundaka)*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Idealism 85% · Dualism 10% · Materialism 5%

The criterion of the real, for Advaita, is permanence: only what is never contradicted across states and resolves into nothing else is fully real. But every plural thing arises, changes and passes, and the experience of waking dissolves the dream-world as waking dissolves illusion: plurality therefore has only an empirical, dependent reality, [māyā (appearance)](../glossary/maya.md) superimposed by avidyā upon [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md) (the absolute, being-consciousness-bliss, sat-cit-ānanda) alone, which remains self-identical. Against the dualism of Sāṅkhya, which posits two irreducible principles, spirit (puruṣa) and nature (prakṛti), Advaita refuses any ultimate duality: there are not two reals but one, of which matter is only the appearing. And against the materialist it objects that the consciousness which attests every thing cannot itself be one material object among others.

*Sources : Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya), I · Chāndogya Upaniṣad, VI*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Substantialism 100%

The decisive argument is that of the witness: whatever I can deny or objectify, the body, the sensations, the passing thoughts, I grasp as "that," distinct from the "I" that observes it; this witnessing subject can therefore never be reduced to the object it lights up, and the consciousness that would establish its own annihilation would have, in order to observe it, to remain. This Self-consciousness, the condition of all experience, is uncompounded, and what is uncompounded cannot fall apart: it is eternal, and its identity with [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md) is the very teaching of the "great sayings" of the Upaniṣads. Here Advaita parts squarely from Buddhism: where [anattā (non-self)](../glossary/anatta.md) sees behind the flux of aggregates only an impermanent series without a subject, Advaita retorts that no flux could appear as a flux without a stable consciousness running through it and attesting to it.

> “Thou art that.” (Chāndogya Upaniṣad, VI, 8, 7 (tat tvam asi))

*Sources : Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad; Chāndogya Upaniṣad, VI, 8 (tat tvam asi) · Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya)*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 90% · Constructed meaning 10%

If the Self is already [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md) and has fallen into saṃsāra only through error, then the end of existence is not a goal to be invented but a truth to be recovered: meaning is inscribed in the structure of the real, and life has for its term [mokṣa (liberation)](../glossary/moksha.md), the conscious return of the Self to what it has never ceased to be. This spiritual teleology marks itself off on two fronts: against the absurdity of an existence without direction, it posits an objective end; but against the ritualism of Mīmāṃsā, which made meaning the correct performance of Vedic acts for the sake of future fruits, it objects that no action, by nature productive of fruits and so of fresh karman, can liberate: only the knowledge of what is already accomplished can.

*Sources : Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya), I, 1, 4 · Kaṭha Upaniṣad*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 90% · Reasoned selection 10%

The path of knowledge presupposes detachment (vairāgya): so long as the subject desires the objects of the empirical world, it remains caught in [māyā (appearance)](../glossary/maya.md) and in identification with body and mind. Advaita prescribes renunciation (saṃnyāsa) and discrimination (viveka) between the eternal and the perishable, so as to turn desire toward liberation alone. Desire for external things is to be extinguished.

*Sources : Śaṅkara (attributed), Vivekacūḍāmaṇi · Kaṭha Upaniṣad, I, 2-3*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Abolitionism 85% · Fecundity and redemption 15%

Suffering, for Advaita, has its source not in being but in an error of knowledge, avidyā: it is the superimposition (adhyāsa) that takes the Self for the body and the appearance for the real, as one sees a snake where there is only a rope. But an illusion is not redeemed, it is dispelled: the moment the rope is recognized, the fear of the snake vanishes without a trace, and no act or merit was needed. This is why Advaita rejects the path on which suffering would be fruitful or redemptive: since its cause is cognitive, only liberating knowledge (jñāna) abolishes it at the root, not endured hardship nor accomplished work.

*Sources : Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya), introduction (adhyāsa) · Gauḍapāda, Māṇḍūkya-Kārikā*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Advaita is dogmatic in the sense that it holds a final liberating truth to be certain: the identity of the [ātman (the Self)](../glossary/atman.md) and [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md), attested by scripture (śruti) and confirmed by direct realization. Far from any [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md), the knowledge that liberates is held to be indubitable and irreversible, since it coincides with the very nature of the knowing subject.

*Sources : Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya), I · Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 93% · Existentialism 8%

One's identity is not to be constructed through one's choices: it is a given, eternal essence, the [ātman (the Self)](../glossary/atman.md), which precedes all existence and all action. The spiritual path creates nothing, it unveils what already is: to become oneself is to recognize the timeless essence that avidyā (ignorance) had concealed. This is a radical essentialism.

*Sources : Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad, IV · Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

The examined life 93% · Primacy of action 8%

The Advaitic path is wholly turned toward interiority: liberation is decided in self-knowledge, through hearing the teaching (śravaṇa), reflection (manana) and deep meditation (nididhyāsana). It is not in action upon the world but in the reflexive turn upon the knowing subject that the absolute is disclosed. The examined life, down to the root of the "I," takes precedence over outward action.

*Sources : Śaṅkara (attributed), Vivekacūḍāmaṇi · Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad, II, 4, 5*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Intellectualism 80% · Sentimentalism 20%

Since bondage rests on a cognitive error, avidyā (ignorance), it is knowledge, not the cultivation of feeling, that transforms the subject. Emotions and attachment are effects of the false identification with body and mind, which discrimination (viveka) between the real and the apparent comes to correct. The lever of liberation is intellectual, which inclines toward intellectualism.

*Sources : Śaṅkara (attributed), Vivekacūḍāmaṇi · Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya)*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Transcendence 73% · Immanence 28%

The status of [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md) is delicate to place: it is at once the immanent ground of all that appears and the absolute that transcends every determination, beyond names and forms (nirguṇa). The world has no being of its own apart from it, which rules out a dualist world-beyond, yet the ultimate real radically exceeds the order of appearances. The stance leans toward [transcendence](../glossary/transcendence.md), tempered by a non-duality in which the absolute is not simply [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md) to the world either.

*Sources : Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad; Gauḍapāda, Māṇḍūkya-Kārikā · Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Theism 75% · Atheism 25%

An absolute that is being-consciousness-bliss without a second can, strictly, receive no determination: to qualify it ("creator," "good," "personal") is to set it against something else, hence to limit it. From this Advaita draws its decisive distinction of two registers: on the ultimate plane (pāramārthika), the absolute is without attributes (nirguṇa [brahman](../glossary/brahman.md)), beyond all qualification; on the conventional plane (vyāvahārika), that same absolute, seen through [māyā](../glossary/maya.md), appears as a personal, creator God (saguṇa brahman, Īśvara), a legitimate object of devotion. This is therefore neither atheism, which would deny the absolute, nor classical personal theism, which would make it the final term: against Rāmānuja and the devotional schools, Advaita subordinates the personal God to an impersonal absolute of which it is only the conventional figure.

*Sources : Śaṅkara, Commentary on the Brahma-Sūtras (Brahma-Sūtra-Bhāṣya), I, 1 · Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*not applicable*

Advaita's decisive contribution on truth is not a theory of what makes a statement true (correspondence, coherence or success), but a stratification into two levels: conventional truth (vyāvahārika), valid for ordinary experience within [māyā (appearance)](../glossary/maya.md), and ultimate truth (pāramārthika), that of the non-dual absolute. This [two-truths](../glossary/two-truths.md) structure, kin to that of Buddhism, cannot be reduced to the three poles of this axis.

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*contextualist*

The knowledge that liberates falls under neither empiricism nor pure rationalism as this axis opposes them: its proper means (pramāṇa) is revealed scripture (śruti, the Upaniṣads), since the identity of the Self and the absolute can be neither perceived by the senses nor demonstrated by reason alone. Reason plays a subordinate role, removing objections and interpreting the text, and the outcome is a direct realization. The empiricism/rationalism pair therefore fails to capture this scriptural and realizational epistemology.

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Immortalism 60% · Death is nothing 20% · Being-toward-death 20%

For Vedanta, the Self (atman) is eternal and does not die: death reaches only the body and the empirical person, while the atman transmigrates from life to life according to karma. It is therefore first an immortalism (the Self survives, indestructible), coupled with a passage (death is but a stage) and a preparation (dying well orients what follows), until the liberation (moksha) that dissolves transmigration itself.

*Sources : Bhagavad Gita, II · Katha Upanishad*
