# Buddhism

*500 BCE – ?*

Buddhism arises from the awakening of Siddhārtha Gautama, the Buddha, around the fifth century BCE in the Ganges plain, and from the community of practitioners, the monastic community (saṅgha), that he gathered around him. The tradition is carried by the Three Jewels, the Buddha, his teaching (the Dharma), and the saṅgha, and by a vast canon of discourses first recited and later written down. Addressing one underlying problem, the [unsatisfactoriness (dukkha)](../glossary/dukkha.md) that pervades every existence bound to the cycle of rebirth, the teaching spread across Asia and branched into great vehicles: the Theravāda of the South, the Mahāyāna of China, Korea, and Japan, and the Tibetan Vajrayāna. It was deepened by [Nāgārjuna](../philosophers/nagarjuna.md), thinker of emptiness, and Dōgen, master of Zen, while keeping a shared doctrinal and practical core.

**Key figures** : [The Buddha](../philosophers/buddha.md) · [Nāgārjuna](../philosophers/nagarjuna.md) · [Dōgen](../philosophers/dogen.md)

## Core positions

1. [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md) : **There is no self: what you take for a permanent ego is only a flux of impermanent aggregates, with no [ātman](../glossary/atman.md) bearing them.**
2. [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md) : **[Suffering (dukkha)](../glossary/dukkha.md) is a dis-ease to be cured, never a trial to be sanctified: the whole teaching aims at its complete cessation.**
3. [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : **The root of suffering is [craving (taṇhā)](../glossary/tanha.md): to be freed is not to satisfy desire but to extinguish its very grasping.**
4. [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md) : **The meaning of existence is neither invented nor decreed by a God: it is [nirvāṇa](../glossary/nirvana.md), an end discovered as the very nature of things.**
5. [Attention and diversion](../axes/attention.md) : **It is sustained attention that undoes the illusion of the self: [mindfulness (sati)](../glossary/sati.md) makes [impermanence](../glossary/anicca.md) directly observable.**

## Positions

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue ethics 45% · Ethics of care 40% · Consequentialism 15%

Buddhism grounds morality on the cultivation of virtue (sila) and on compassion (karuna) and loving-kindness toward all sentient beings, down to animals. Merit lies in the quality of intention and the transformation of the mind more than in obedience to a law; consequences count through the karmic fruits they carry.

*Sources : Dhammapada · Metta Sutta*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Immortalism 40% · Death is nothing 30% · Being-toward-death 30%

For Buddhism, death is neither the annihilation of a self nor the survival of a soul, but a passage within the cycle of rebirths governed by karma: a mere stage, yet one to be carefully prepared, since the dying person's state of mind orients the rebirth, and something continues without any permanent soul remaining. A balanced position, until the liberation that alone halts the cycle.

*Sources : Bardo Thödol (Tibetan Book of the Dead) · Suttas on rebirth*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 65% · Eudaimonism 25% · Hedonism 10%

Buddhism places the highest good in nirvana, the cessation of craving and suffering, a supreme peace beyond pleasure and pain. It is won through the eightfold path, which unites wisdom, ethical conduct and meditation: a liberating perfection, not a happiness of enjoyment nor a mere moral uprightness.

*Sources : Sermon at Benares (the four noble truths) · Dhammapada, XXV*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Existentialism 75% · Essentialism 25%

Buddhism denies any permanent essence: there is no self (anatta), only a flux of impermanent, conditioned aggregates, with no substantial core that would persist. The denial of essence is radical, but it issues in no freedom of self-creation: it is the very idea of a fixed self that dissolves.

*Sources : Samyutta Nikaya, XXII (the aggregates) · Milindapañha (the parable of the chariot)*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Hard determinism 60% · Free will 40%

Buddhism holds every phenomenon to be dependently arisen: there is no uncaused will of a substantial self, and the sense of being a free author proceeds from ignorance. But far from fatalism, intention (cetana) is the karmic act par excellence, and it can be trained: the path is a real effort to transform one's own conditioning, between determinism and free will.

*Sources : Samyutta Nikaya, XII · Dhammapada, I*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 70% · Rationalism 30%

Buddhism makes personal examination and direct experience, not authority or tradition, the source of liberating knowledge. Decisive knowledge proceeds from a perception refined by meditation, more than from a priori intellection: one tests for oneself the root of suffering and its cessation, as one verifies a remedy by applying it.

> “Do not go by hearsay or tradition, but when you know for yourselves: this is wholesome, then take it up.” (Kalama Sutta)

*Sources : Kalama Sutta (Anguttara Nikaya III.65) · Satipatthana Sutta*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Skepticism 60% · Dogmatism 40%

The Buddha turns away from metaphysical speculation: to questions about the eternity of the world or the hereafter he opposes **silence**, just as the wounded man need not know the archer before the arrow is drawn from the wound. Truth is not established by authority, tradition or reasoning alone, but tested in direct experience, “come and see”. The stance is more pragmatic than skeptical: no certain dogma to embrace, but a path whose efficacy each verifies for oneself, the Dharma realized in **meditative insight**.

*Sources : Kalama Sutta (Anguttara Nikaya) · Cula-Malunkyovada Sutta (the parable of the arrow)*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Bundle theory 90% · Psychological continuity 10%

Look for the self among the five aggregates (form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness) and you find only their shifting flux: none of them is stable, none is yours, none is a subject bearing them. From this analysis Buddhism draws [not-self (anattā)](../glossary/anatta.md) against the [ātman](../glossary/atman.md) of the Upaniṣads, that permanent Self identified with [Brahman](../glossary/brahman.md): where [Vedānta](../movements/vedanta.md) posits an unchanging subject beneath becoming, the Buddha holds that positing such a substrate is precisely the delusion (avidyā) that feeds attachment. The subject is only a bundle of impermanent processes, which makes Buddhism the great historical instance of bundle theory.

> “Form is not-self, feeling is not-self, perception is not-self, formations are not-self, consciousness is not-self.” (Anattalakkhaṇa Sutta (Saṃyutta Nikāya 22.59))

*Sources : Anattalakkhaṇa Sutta (the discourse on the not-self characteristic) · Nāgārjuna, Mūlamadhyamakakārikā (Fundamental Verses on the Middle Way)*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 95% · Reasoned selection 5%

If suffering has a locatable cause, it can be removed by addressing that cause: through [dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda)](../glossary/pratityasamutpada.md), the second Noble Truth traces suffering back to [craving (taṇhā)](../glossary/tanha.md), the grasping thirst that clings to pleasures, to existence, or to non-existence and keeps the wheel of rebirth turning. It follows that liberation requires neither satisfying desire nor forcibly repressing it, but extinguishing its root, the thirst itself: Buddhism thus falls squarely on the side of detachment, not from contempt for pleasure but because craving is the very engine of dis-ease.

> “This is the noble truth of the origin of suffering: it is this craving which leads to renewed existence.” (Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta (Saṃyutta Nikāya 56.11))

*Sources : Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta (the sermon at Benares) · Dhammapada, ch. XXIV (Craving)*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Abolitionism 93% · Fecundity and redemption 7%

The Buddha proceeds like a physician, and it is this schema that drives the position: the [Four Noble Truths](../glossary/four-noble-truths.md) state the disease ([suffering, dukkha](../glossary/dukkha.md)), its cause (craving), its prognosis (cessation is possible), and its cure (the path). Once suffering is treated as a curable symptom, it can carry no redemptive value: to exalt it as a sanctifying trial would be to cling to the disease rather than treat it. Against every spirituality of sacrifice, the declared goal is the complete extinction of suffering, which places Buddhism on the abolitionist side.

*Sources : Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta (the sermon at Benares) · Dhammapada, ch. XX (The Path)*

### [Attention and diversion](../axes/attention.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Recollection 95% · Diversion embraced 5%

If the self is an illusion born of inattention, it is sustained attention that undoes it: [mindfulness](../glossary/sati.md) (sati, continuous present attention) is no mere relaxation but the instrument of knowledge that makes [impermanence (anicca)](../glossary/anicca.md) and [not-self (anattā)](../glossary/anatta.md) directly observable in experience. A limb of the [Eightfold Path](../glossary/eightfold-path.md), it is exercised, according to the Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta, upon the body, feelings, mind, and phenomena, against scattering. Recollection is thus not an end in itself but the very means by which liberating understanding takes hold, which makes Buddhism a discipline of recollection par excellence.

*Sources : Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta (the discourse on the foundations of mindfulness) · Dhammapada, ch. II (Heedfulness)*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Teleology and providence 85% · Constructed meaning 10% · Absurdism 5%

Since the cessation of suffering is possible, it gives existence an end toward which everything is ordered: to be freed from the cycle of rebirths so as to reach [nirvāṇa](../glossary/nirvana.md) (the going-out of greed, hatred, and delusion). Meaning is therefore neither invented by each person nor decreed by a creator, unlike ends fixed by a God: it is discovered as the very nature of things, which gives Buddhism a teleological structure without transcendence, the goal lying in the possibility of extinction rather than in any external will.

*Sources : Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta (the sermon at Benares) · Dhammapada, ch. XXVI (The Brahmin)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The examined life 85% · Primacy of action 15%

The transformation aimed at proceeds first through inner examination: observing one's own mental states, [impulses (taṇhā)](../glossary/tanha.md), and perceptions in meditation and introspection. The good life turns on work upon the mind more than on outward action, even though ethical conduct (sīla) is its support.

> “All that we are is the result of what we have thought: it is founded on our thoughts, it is made up of our thoughts.” (Dhammapada, I, 1)

*Sources : Dhammapada, ch. I (The Pairs) · Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta (the discourse on the foundations of mindfulness)*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Intellectualism 78% · Sentimentalism 22%

The ultimate root of suffering and afflictive emotions is ignorance (avidyā), the misapprehension of the impermanent, selfless nature of reality. Greed, hatred, and delusion dissolve through right view and penetrating understanding: it is by knowledge that affect is transformed, which aligns Buddhism with a therapeutic intellectualism, tempered by the central role of meditative practice.

*Sources : Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta (the sermon at Benares) · Dhammapada, ch. XX (The Path)*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Asceticism 65% · Celebration 35%

The sermon at Benares sets aside two extremes, the pursuit of sensual pleasures and self-mortification, in favor of the [Middle Way](../glossary/the-middle-way.md). Without condemning the body, Buddhism treats sensory pleasures as attachments that feed craving, and commends measured sobriety: the tendency is ascetic, but moderate and free of harsh rigor.

*Sources : Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta (the sermon at Benares)*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Impartialism 85% · Partialism 15%

Loving-kindness (mettā) and compassion (karuṇā) are cultivated toward all beings without distinction: the practice consists precisely in extending loving-kindness beyond loved ones, to the indifferent and then to the enemy. Dissolving the separate self removes the basis of partial preference, orienting Buddhism toward a benevolent impartialism.

*Sources : Karaṇīya Mettā Sutta (the discourse on loving-kindness) · Dhammapada, ch. I (The Pairs)*

### [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Pathocentrism 85% · Biocentrism 7% · Anthropocentrism 5% · Ecocentrism 3%

Non-harming (ahiṃsā) and the first precept extend moral consideration to all sentient beings capable of suffering, not to humans alone. Compassion expressly takes in the whole of living beings caught in the cycle of rebirth, which places Buddhism on the pathocentric side.

*Sources : Karaṇīya Mettā Sutta (the discourse on loving-kindness) · Dhammapada, ch. X (Violence)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Atheism 80% · Theism 20%

A being itself subject to becoming and suffering cannot be the source of salvation: this is why Buddhism is non-theist. Deities (devas) do populate the cosmology, but they are caught in the cycle of rebirth and hence powerless to liberate, and a creator God would not account for the suffering he would have brought into being. Against Brahmanical theism, liberation therefore depends on no grace but on one's own effort and understanding; this absence of a creator is held serenely, without militant polemic against belief.

*Sources : Brahmajāla Sutta (the discourse on the all-embracing net) · Aggañña Sutta (the discourse on origins)*
