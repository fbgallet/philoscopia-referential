# Rationalism

*1637 – 1716*

A continental current born in the heart of the 17th century, in the age of the scientific revolution of Galileo and Kepler, when the success of mathematics in studying nature seemed to call for a rebuilding of knowledge on equally secure foundations. Its lineage runs from [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md), who opens the way with the *[Meditations](../works/meditations-descartes.md)* and the Discourse on the Method, to [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), who draws from it in the *[Ethics](../works/ethics-spinoza.md)* a geometric system, and on to [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md), who crowns it in the Monadology. The problem it addresses is that of foundations: on what can certain knowledge be secured after the collapse of scholastic physics and under the threat of [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md)? Against the [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) of [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md) and [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), which derives all knowledge from experience, the school would be held to be superseded by [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)'s synthesis, which keeps its a priori component while stripping away its claim to know things in themselves. Even so it fixed the modern ideal of science for centuries and remains one of the two poles around which classical epistemology is organised.

**Key figures** : [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) · [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) · [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md)

## Core positions

1. [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md) : **The senses yield only the particular and the contingent: the [understanding](../glossary/innate-ideas.md) alone, with its innate ideas, reaches necessary and universal truths.**
2. [Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md) : **Certain knowledge is built by starting from self-evident first principles and deducing the rest, more geometrico, as one proves a theorem.**
3. [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : **[Skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) does not have the last word: there are truths no doubt can shake, and reason attains them.**
4. [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : **Nothing exists or happens without a reason: the real is intelligible through and through, and the [sufficient reason](../glossary/principle-of-sufficient-reason.md) of each thing can be found.**
5. [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md) : **Things have intelligible essences that the mind defines a priori: one can deduce their properties without awaiting anything from experience.**

## Positions

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Virtue ethics 80% · Deontologism 10% · Consequentialism 5% · Ethics of care 5%

The rationalists converge on an intellectualized virtue ethics: the good lies in the perfection and power of the rational soul. Descartes' [generosity](../glossary/generosity.md), Spinoza's active blessedness, Leibniz's wisdom make moral life the flourishing of a character enlightened by reason, more than obedience to a law or the calculation of effects.

*Sources : Spinoza, Ethics, IV-V · Descartes, The Passions of the Soul*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eudaimonism 55% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 35% · Hedonism 10%

The rationalists converge on an intellectual highest good: the perfection and joy of the knowing soul. Descartes' firm contentment, Spinoza's blessedness as the intellectual love of God, and Leibniz's increasing perfection place the good life in the accomplished exercise of reason, where flourishing and virtue unite, more than in pleasure.

*Sources : Spinoza, Ethics, V · Descartes, The Passions of the Soul · Leibniz, Monadology*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Rationalism 93% · Empiricism 7%

The decisive point is that a necessary and universal truth cannot come from the senses: these yield only particular, contingent cases, never the « it cannot be otherwise » of mathematics. A non-sensory source is therefore required, and it is the [understanding](../glossary/innate-ideas.md) with its [innate ideas](../glossary/innate-ideas.md). [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md) turns the [empiricist](../movements/empiricism.md) maxim against itself: if nothing is in the mind that was not first in the senses, one must still except the intellect itself, whose structures make any experience thinkable. Where [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md) sees in the senses only impressions, [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) sets this confused knowledge of the first kind apart from knowledge through [adequate ideas](../glossary/adequate-idea.md), the only kind that grasps things through their causes.

> “Nothing is in the intellect that was not first in the senses, except the intellect itself.”, New Essays on Human Understanding, II, 1

*Sources : Descartes, Meditations on First Philosophy, III · Leibniz, New Essays on Human Understanding, II, 1*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dogmatism 93% · Skepticism 8%

To answer [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) it is not enough to pile up probable opinions: a truth is needed that no argument can shake. [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) makes this his strategy, pushing [hyperbolic doubt](../glossary/hyperbolic-doubt.md) as far as the hypothesis of the evil demon so as to keep only what survives it: the very act of thinking, which outlasts every possible doubt. Once that rock is found, the task is to propagate its certainty to the rest of knowledge, and the model for this is geometry: ordering knowledge more geometrico, from self-evident axioms to their necessary consequences, as [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) deduces the *[Ethics](../works/ethics-spinoza.md)* in the manner of the geometers. The certainty aimed at is not a psychological assurance but the compelling self-evidence of demonstration.

> “I think, therefore I am.”, Discourse on Method, IV

*Sources : Descartes, Meditations on First Philosophy, II · Spinoza, Ethics, opening of Part I*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theism 93% · Atheism 8%

God is not received from faith but required by reason: without him the deduction would not hold. [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) needs him to move beyond the cogito and guarantee that our clear ideas are not the work of a deceiver, and he proves him in the Third and Fifth *[Meditation](../works/meditations-descartes.md)*. [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) identifies him with the single, necessary substance from which everything follows ([Deus sive Natura](../glossary/causa-sui.md)), and [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md) deduces him from the [principle of sufficient reason](../glossary/principle-of-sufficient-reason.md) as that which accounts for the existence of the world and grounds the pre-established harmony. The doctrine is not uniform: Descartes' personal creator God, Spinoza's immanent God and Leibniz's perfectly rational God differ deeply. What they share is a family resemblance: a divine principle that reason establishes and that secures the intelligibility of the world, rather than a primarily personal, revealed God.

*Sources : Descartes, Meditations on First Philosophy, V · Spinoza, Ethics, I, propositions 11 and 14 · Leibniz, Monadology, §§ 36-45*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Correspondence 55% · Coherence 45%

Truth is conceived as the adequacy of an idea to its object, but an adequacy secured from within by rational coherence: for Spinoza the order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things, and the true is its own standard. The Cartesian criterion of the clear and distinct idea and the deductive chaining of reasons draw the school toward an ideal in which correspondence and coherence converge.

*Sources : Spinoza, Ethics, II, proposition 7 and scholium to proposition 43 · Descartes, Meditations on First Philosophy, IV*

### [Nature of the self](../axes/self-nature.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Substantialism 85% · Psychological continuity 15%

The self is thought as a substance or a simple thing, not as a bundle of impressions: Descartes defines the subject as a thinking thing (res cogitans), a substance whose whole essence is to think, and Leibniz makes the soul a monad, a simple indivisible substance. The substantialist position is clearest in these two thinkers.

*Sources : Descartes, Meditations on First Philosophy, II · Leibniz, Monadology, §§ 1-7*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Scientism 80% · Epistemic pluralism 20%

Mathematics is the model of all certain knowledge: Descartes, inventor of analytic geometry, makes the order of mathematical reasons the paradigm of method, and Leibniz, co-inventor of the infinitesimal calculus, dreams of a universal characteristic that would turn every disagreement into a calculation. Confidence in a unified deductive science inclines the school toward a strong epistemic optimism.

*Sources : Descartes, Discourse on the Method, part two · Leibniz, Discourse on Metaphysics*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Mechanism 60% · Finalism 40%

If reason can know the real a priori, it is because the real is rational through and through: nothing exists or happens without a reason that makes it intelligible, on pain of leaving brute facts opaque to thought. This postulate, which [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md) raises into the [principle of sufficient reason](../glossary/principle-of-sufficient-reason.md), governs the whole of rationalism and divides it from the [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) of [Hume](../philosophers/hume.md), for whom causality is only a habit of associating impressions, not a necessary connection. Physical nature is read mechanistically, through figures and motions in [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) and through necessary laws in [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), but finality returns at the metaphysical level, with [Leibniz](../philosophers/leibniz.md) subordinating the mechanism of bodies to ends willed by God.

*Sources : Leibniz, Monadology, §§ 31-32 · Descartes, Principles of Philosophy, part II*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 90% · Existentialism 10%

Deduction needs premises: to demonstrate a thing's properties one must start from a definition of its essence, and that essence must be graspable by the understanding alone, without recourse to experience. Hence the school's essentialism: things have intelligible, definable essences that reason reaches a priori. [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md) thus deduces all the properties of substance from its definition alone, and [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) grasps the essence of matter as pure extension, through the analysis of the piece of wax rather than through the senses. This is the exact reverse of [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md), for which no essence precedes the observation of particular cases.

*Sources : Spinoza, Ethics, I, definitions and first propositions · Descartes, Principles of Philosophy, part I*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of reason 83% · The primacy of feeling 18%

The passions stem from confused ideas and must be mastered by the understanding: Descartes offers a mechanical analysis and an art of governing them through knowledge, and Spinoza holds that an emotion ceases to be a passion as soon as we form a clear and distinct idea of it. Correcting affect by reason draws the school toward a moral intellectualism.

*Sources : Descartes, The Passions of the Soul · Spinoza, Ethics, V, proposition 3*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Scientific realism 90% · Constructivism 10%

The school is firmly realist about knowledge: reason does not construct its object, it reaches the real order of things as it is in itself. The order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things, writes Spinoza, the true idea agreeing with what it represents. Descartes' clear and distinct ideas grasp the real essence of things, and the intelligible order of Leibniz's monads is the very architecture of reality: to know is to read the structure of the world, not to fabricate it.

> “The order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things.”, Ethics, II, proposition 7

*Sources : Spinoza, Ethics, II, proposition 7 · Descartes, Meditations on First Philosophy, V · Leibniz, Monadology, §§ 56-62*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Universalism 80% · Relativism 10% · Constructivism 10%

A corollary of the metaphysical realism: truths, practical ones included, are objective and accessible to reason, not relative to a culture or an individual. Spinoza deduces the true good from the nature of substance, more geometrico, as a necessary and universal truth, and Leibniz grounds justice on eternal truths inscribed in the divine understanding. Reason, everywhere the same, gives access to a universal normative order.

*Sources : Spinoza, Ethics, IV, preface and propositions 18-37 · Leibniz, Meditation on the Common Concept of Justice*

### [Ethics of belief](../axes/belief-ethics.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Evidentialism 93% · Non-evidentialism 8%

The norm of assent is evidence: one should affirm only what the understanding perceives clearly and distinctly. Descartes makes error a fault of the will judging beyond what the understanding clearly conceives, and prescribes withholding assent outside of evidence. For Spinoza it is the [adequate idea](../glossary/adequate-idea.md) that carries a necessary and true affirmation. Assent is thus regulated by the clarity of the idea, not by a will to believe.

*Sources : Descartes, Meditations on First Philosophy, IV · Spinoza, Ethics, II, proposition 49*

### [Justification of belief](../axes/epistemic-justification.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Foundationalism 85% · Coherentism 10% · Reliabilism 5%

Every justification, on pain of an infinite regress, must come to rest on principles that depend on no other: rationalism settles the matter by holding that these stopping points are first truths grasped with self-evidence by intellectual intuition, not ever-revisable sense data. This is a thoroughgoing foundationalism: knowledge is built like a deductive edifice, from axioms toward theorems, each step preserving the certainty of the last. Against [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md), which backs justification onto singular impressions unable to ground the necessary, rational self-evidence alone can serve as the base, because it shows itself indubitable in the very act of being conceived.

*Sources : Descartes, Meditations; Spinoza, Ethics*

## Influence on

- **[The Enlightenment](../movements/enlightenment.md)** (continues) : From [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md) the Enlightenment inherits confidence in a reason able to judge on its own, and [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s methodical doubt as a weapon against prejudice. The current's radical wing also extends, more underground, [Spinozism](../philosophers/spinoza.md) and its critique of revealed religion.
- **[Kant](../philosophers/kant.md)** (synthesizes) : [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) takes from [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md) the demand for a priori knowledge, universal and necessary, but confines it to the conditions of possible experience: pure concepts know nothing beyond appearances.
- **[Empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md)** (reacts against) : British [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) defines itself against [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md): the first book of [Locke](../philosophers/locke.md)'s *[Essay](../works/essay-concerning-human-understanding-locke.md)* sets out to refute innate principles, before the second derives all our ideas from experience, first sensation then reflection. Against the order of reasons deduced a priori, it sets the patient gathering of simple ideas.
