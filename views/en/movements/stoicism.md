# Stoicism

*300 BCE – 180*

A school founded in Athens by Zeno of Citium around 300 BCE, which taught under the "Painted Porch" (stoa poikilē) from which it takes its name. A complete system of logic, physics and ethics, Stoicism was carried by Chrysippus and then, in Rome, by Seneca, Epictetus and the emperor Marcus Aurelius. Its ethics of wisdom and self-mastery deeply marked Christianity, [Spinoza](../philosophers/spinoza.md), [Kant](../philosophers/kant.md) and even contemporary self-help movements.

**Key figures** : [Zeno of Citium](../philosophers/zeno-of-citium.md) · [Seneca](../philosophers/seneca.md) · [Epictetus](../philosophers/epictetus.md) · [Marcus Aurelius](../philosophers/marcus-aurelius.md)

## Core positions

1. [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md) : **Some things are up to us, others are not: wisdom is to act only on the former and to welcome the latter.**
2. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **Virtue is the only good and [suffices for happiness](../glossary/eudaimonism.md): health, wealth and pleasure are mere [indifferents](../glossary/adiaphora.md).**
3. [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md) : **The passions are mistaken judgments: one is freed from them by correcting the judgment, all the way to [apatheia](../glossary/apatheia.md).**
4. [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md) : **Whatever happens was willed by the world's reason: one must love one's [fate](../glossary/determinism.md) rather than merely endure it.**
5. [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md) : **The world is a rational whole ordered by the [logos](../glossary/logos.md): to live well is to live in accord with this nature.**

## Positions

### [The point of philosophy](../axes/metaphilosophy.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Transforming oneself 80% · Understanding the world 20%

Stoic philosophy is an exercise (**askêsis**) more than a body of knowledge: a daily discipline of judgment, desire, and action, whose raw material is the very life of the one who practices it. Physics and logic keep their place, but in the service of the art of living: to know the order of the world is to learn to consent to it.

> “The subject-matter of the art of living is each person's own life.” (Epictetus, Discourses, I.15.2)

*Sources : Epictetus, Discourses, I.15 · Seneca, Letters to Lucilius, 16*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Essentialism 80% · Existentialism 20%

Stoicism assigns the human being a determinate nature, reason, a fragment of the logos that orders the cosmos; the very telos is formulated as “living in agreement with nature”. The good life is not self-invention but the fulfillment of this rational essence, common to all human beings.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VII · Cicero, On Ends, III*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Empiricism 73% · Rationalism 28%

For the Stoics, the soul at birth is like a blank sheet: all knowledge begins with the impressions (phantasiai) that the senses inscribe on it, and common notions form by the accumulation of experiences. The criterion of truth is the **cataleptic** impression, the one that grasps its object with such self-evidence that it compels assent. An empiricism of the source, then, but governed by a reason that assents or suspends.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VII · Cicero, Academica, II*

### [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

What is up to us 95% · Prometheanism 5%

The [division](../glossary/dichotomy-of-control.md) is the founding act: only our judgments, desires and impulses are truly up to us, not the body, reputation or fortune. The argument is practical: tying one's good to what is not up to us dooms one to disturbance, whereas concentrating effort on the one masterable domain makes serenity invulnerable to circumstance, even in exile or prison.

> “Of things, some are in our power, and others are not.” (Epictetus, Enchiridion, §1)

*Sources : Epictetus, Enchiridion, §1 · Epictetus, Discourses*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 85% · Eudaimonism 15%

The good, the Stoics argue, must be what always benefits and never harms; yet health, wealth or pleasure can be misused: they are not goods but [indifferents](../glossary/adiaphora.md), at most « preferred ». Only virtue, the right use of reason, meets the criterion, hence the school's most disputed thesis, against [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md): it [suffices for happiness](../glossary/eudaimonism.md), and the sage stays happy even amid torments.

*Sources : Seneca, Letters to Lucilius · Cicero, On Moral Ends, book III*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Intellectualism 93% · Sentimentalism 8%

A passion is not an undergone disturbance but an [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) given to a false [representation](../glossary/phantasia.md) of what is good or bad, followed by an excessive impulse. Since assent is up to us, a passion is a correctable error: to aim at [apatheia](../glossary/apatheia.md) is therefore not to stifle a feeling but to rectify the judgment, a far sharper position than [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md)'s mere moderation.

*Sources : Seneca, On Anger · Cicero, Tusculan Disputations, books III-IV*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Extinction and detachment 90% · Reasoned selection 10%

The discipline of desire demands desiring only what is [up to us](../glossary/dichotomy-of-control.md) and consenting to the rest: desires for external things are to be suspended, even extinguished. Epictetus advises not to ask that things happen as you wish, but to wish them as they happen.

> “Seek not that the things which happen should happen as you wish; but wish the things which happen to be as they are, and you will have a tranquil flow of life.” (Epictetus, Enchiridion, §8)

*Sources : Epictetus, Enchiridion, §8*

### [Relation to the past](../axes/past-relation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Amor fati 93% · Revolt 8%

Physics commands ethics: every event is a link in the rational chain of [fate](../glossary/determinism.md), ordered by [providence](../glossary/providence.md). To rebel is therefore both futile and unreasonable, since one fights the very reason one shares in; the [sage](../glossary/the-sage.md) aligns his will with the course of things, not from passive resignation but because he recognises in it the work of the [logos](../glossary/logos.md): “the fates lead the willing and drag the unwilling”.

> “Lead me, Zeus, and you too, Destiny, wherever you have appointed me: I shall follow unflinching. And should I refuse, turned coward, I shall follow none the less.” (Cleanthes, quoted in Seneca, Letters to Lucilius, 107)

*Sources : Marcus Aurelius, Meditations · Seneca, Letters to Lucilius, 107*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Teleology and providence 90% · Constructed meaning 10%

The cosmos is a single living being pervaded by the [logos](../glossary/logos.md), the divine reason; man is a part of it endowed with a fragment of that reason. « Living according to nature » therefore means living according to reason, one's own and that of the whole, which coincides with virtue. The meaning of existence is not to be invented: it lies in the right place one holds within the rational order, against an Epicurean world of indifferent atoms.

*Sources : Marcus Aurelius, Meditations · Cicero, On the Nature of the Gods, book II*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Hard determinism 57% · Free will 43%

Stoicism asserts thoroughgoing causal [determinism](../glossary/determinism.md) (fate) while holding that our [assent](../glossary/synkatathesis.md) is in our power: the classic [compatibilist](../glossary/compatibilism.md) position, illustrated by Chrysippus' cylinder, which rolls from an external push but according to its own shape. The position tilts slightly toward determinism, since fate links all causes.

*Sources : Cicero, On Fate · Aulus Gellius, Attic Nights, VII, 2*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Cosmopolitanism 90% · Communitarianism and patriotism 10%

The Stoics articulated ancient cosmopolitanism: every human being, as a rational creature, is a citizen of the world before being a citizen of any particular city. Marcus Aurelius calls himself a citizen of Rome as Antoninus, but a citizen of the world as a man.

*Sources : Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, VI, 44 · Epictetus, Discourses, I, 9*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 90% · Deontologism 10%

Stoic ethics is a virtue ethics par excellence: moral good lies in excellence of character and rightness of intention, not in consequences nor in obedience to an external rule. Virtue, one and indivisible, is the only good and suffices for happiness, and it is virtue that qualifies the agent as wise.

*Sources : Cicero, On Moral Ends, book III · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VII*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Against the [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) of the New Academy, the Stoics are [dogmatists](../glossary/dogmatism.md): they hold that certain knowledge is possible through the [kataleptic impression](../glossary/kataleptic-impression.md), an impression so clear and distinct that it grasps its object and can come only from what is real. It is the criterion of truth and the foundation of the [sage](../glossary/the-sage.md)'s science.

*Sources : Cicero, Academica · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VII, 46*

### [Attention and diversion](../axes/attention.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Recollection 90% · Diversion embraced 10%

Prosochē, the vigilant attention turned at every moment upon the judgments and impulses of the [ruling faculty](../glossary/hegemonikon.md) (the hēgemonikon), is the fundamental attitude of the practicing Stoic. Marcus Aurelius constantly reminds himself to watch over his [impressions](../glossary/phantasia.md): the aim is to recollect oneself rather than be scattered.

*Sources : Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, III, 4 · Epictetus, Discourses, IV, 12*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Theism 85% · Atheism 15%

Stoicism is theistic, but with a pantheist theism: god is not a transcendent person but is identified with the [logos](../glossary/logos.md), the seminal reason and [providence](../glossary/providence.md) that pervades and animates the whole cosmos. Zeno and Cleanthes, in the Hymn to Zeus, celebrate this immanent divinity that orders all things.

*Sources : Cleanthes, Hymn to Zeus · Cicero, On the Nature of the Gods, book II*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Immanence 93% · Transcendence 8%

The Stoic divine is not beyond the world: divine reason is [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md), mixed with matter as a breath (the pneuma) running through and structuring reality. There is no [transcendence](../glossary/transcendence.md), no world beyond, only this single, rational, divine cosmos.

*Sources : Cicero, On the Nature of the Gods, book II · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VII, 138-139*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Materialism 85% · Dualism 10% · Idealism 5%

Stoic physics is corporealist: only what acts or is acted upon exists, and only bodies can do so. Even the soul and the divine are corporeal, made of a subtle breath, the pneuma. This is a materialism, though one infused with internal reason, which sets Stoicism apart from Platonic idealism.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VII, 134-135 · Seneca, Letters to Lucilius, 65*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Finalism 75% · Mechanism 25%

The Stoic cosmos is ordered by [providence](../glossary/providence.md): fate is the rational chaining of all causes, directed toward the good of the whole. Causality is therefore fundamentally finalist, teleological, even as it unfolds as an unbreakable chain of causes that nothing interrupts.

*Sources : Cicero, On Fate · Cicero, On the Nature of the Gods, book II*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Universalism 90% · Relativism 5% · Constructivism 5%

Stoic morality is universalist: there is a **natural law**, the expression of the single [logos](../glossary/logos.md), binding on all rational beings everywhere. Good and evil depend neither on convention nor on cities: to live according to nature is to live according to this reason common to all humankind.

*Sources : Cicero, On the Republic, book III · Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, IV, 4*

### [Self-interest and others](../axes/self-interest.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Altruism 80% · Ethical egoism 20%

The doctrine of [oikeiōsis](../glossary/oikeiosis.md) describes the natural extension of belonging: starting from concern for oneself, it widens in concentric circles to embrace family, city, then all of humanity. The Stoic [sage](../glossary/the-sage.md) thus comes to treat another's good as his own, beyond narrow self-interest.

*Sources : Cicero, On Moral Ends, book III · Hierocles, Elements of Ethics*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Impartialism 78% · Partialism 22%

Stoic cosmopolitanism implies equal concern for every rational being: since all humans share the same [logos](../glossary/logos.md) and form a single world-city, the [sage](../glossary/the-sage.md) in principle owes them impartial regard. Hierocles even urges drawing the circles of [oikeiōsis](../glossary/oikeiosis.md) inward, treating the most distant as kin.

*Sources : Hierocles, Elements of Ethics · Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, VII, 13*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 85% · Abolitionism 15%

For the Stoics, hardship is not an evil but the occasion to exercise virtue: adversity reveals and strengthens the [sage](../glossary/the-sage.md) as training reveals the athlete. Seneca writes that the divine tests good men as a stern father does, and that no excellence shows itself without a difficulty to overcome.

*Sources : Seneca, On Providence · Epictetus, Discourses, I, 24*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Asceticism 73% · Celebration 28%

Bodily pleasure is a mere [indifferent](../glossary/adiaphora.md): neither good nor evil, it warrants neither pursuit nor cult. Without condemning the body, the Stoics commend a disciplined sobriety and training in doing without the superfluous, so that nothing external holds the soul in its power. The tendency is ascetic, but moderate.

*Sources : Seneca, Letters to Lucilius, 18 · Epictetus, Enchiridion, §33*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Death is nothing 65% · Being-toward-death 30% · Immortalism 5%

Death is a natural process, in keeping with the order of the cosmos, and therefore an [indifferent](../glossary/adiaphora.md) not to be feared. The meditation on death (the premeditation of evils and the memento mori) serves to free present life from anxiety rather than to promise survival. Marcus Aurelius sees in it the mere dissolution of the elements back into the whole.

*Sources : Seneca, Letters to Lucilius, 26 · Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, II, 17*

### [Temporal orientation](../axes/time-orientation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Presentism 70% · Project 20% · Memory and transmission 10%

Stoicism calls for inhabiting the present, the only time we possess: the past is no more, the future not yet, and one can lose only the moment one is living. Marcus Aurelius reminds himself that each person lives only this fleeting instant, and that consenting to it fully suffices for the good life.

*Sources : Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, III, 10 · Seneca, On the Shortness of Life*

### [Hope](../axes/hope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Lucidity without hope 75% · The principle of hope 25%

To hope and to fear, for the Stoics, is to cast oneself into a future not up to us and to surrender to disturbance: these movements of the soul are to be corrected. Wisdom lies in seeing lucidly what is, consenting to fate, and expecting nothing from outside. The stance is not despair but a lucidity without hope.

*Sources : Seneca, Letters to Lucilius, 5 · Epictetus, Enchiridion, §8*

### [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Theodicy 80% · Free-will defence 10% · Argument from evil (atheism) 10%

The world is governed by a providential divine reason: what appears as evil has its place in the rational order of the whole, or serves as a trial for virtue. Nothing happens that is not in accord with universal nature.

*Sources : Seneca, On Providence; Epictetus, Discourses; Marcus Aurelius, Meditations*

## Influenced by

- **[Cynicism](../movements/cynicism.md)** (continues) : Zeno of Citium was a pupil of Crates the Cynic: [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md) takes from [Cynicism](../movements/cynicism.md) the ideal of self-sufficiency and life “according to nature”, but tempers its provocation and anticonformism by embedding it in a systematic physics and logic.
- **[Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md)** (reacts against) : A founding rivalry of the Hellenistic age: [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md) forges many of its theses against [Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md), setting against the swerve of atoms and pleasure the doctrines of providence, fate and virtue as the only good.
- **[Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)** (continues) : [Stoic](../movements/stoicism.md) physics draws from [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s Timaeus the idea of a living cosmos, ordered and governed by a rational providence, which it recasts into a materialism of breath and immanent logos.
- **[Socrates](../philosophers/socrates.md)** (continues) : [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md) claims [Socrates](../philosophers/socrates.md) as its model of the sage: virtue as knowledge, the unity of the virtues and the idea that no one does wrong willingly pass from the dialogues into the ethics of the Stoa.

## Influence on

- **[Arcesilaus](../philosophers/arcesilaus.md)** (reacts against) : [Arcesilaus](../philosophers/arcesilaus.md)'s Academic skepticism is forged by refuting [Stoic](../movements/stoicism.md) epistemology: against the “cognitive impression” supposed to guarantee truth, he sets the suspension of judgement.
- **[Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md)** (reacts against) : In return, [Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md), notably in Lucretius, fights [Stoic](../movements/stoicism.md) providence and fate in the name of a world without design, born of the chance of atoms and in which the soul is mortal.
