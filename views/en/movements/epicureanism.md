# Epicureanism

*307 BCE – 200*

A school founded in Athens by [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) around 307 BCE, which taught in the garden beside his house, whence the name « the Garden » given to a community open, rare for the time, to women and slaves. Epicurus answers the anxiety of his age, troubled by wars and by fear of the gods and of death: he builds a therapeutics of happiness that anchors ethics in a physics, the atomism taken over from Democritus. His treatises being almost all lost, the doctrine survives chiefly in three summary letters (among them the *[Letter to Menoeceus](../works/letter-to-menoeceus-epicurus.md)*), the *[Principal Doctrines](../works/principal-doctrines-epicurus.md)* and, in the first century BCE, the great poem of Lucretius, *[On the Nature of Things](../works/on-the-nature-of-things-lucretius.md)*, which carried the school to Rome. Long caricatured as an apology for debauchery, Epicureanism was rediscovered in the Renaissance and fed the tradition of modern materialism, from Gassendi to Marx.

**Key figures** : [Epicurus](../philosophers/epicurus.md) · [Lucretius](../philosophers/lucretius.md) · [Metrodorus](../philosophers/metrodorus.md)

## Core positions

1. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **[Pleasure](../glossary/hedonism.md) is the sovereign good, yet its summit is not enjoyment but [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md), the stable state where no pain is left to remove.**
2. [Death](../axes/death.md) : **Death is nothing to us: while we are, it is not, and when it is, we are no longer; to fear it is to dread a nothing that will never reach us.**
3. [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md) : **Nothing exists but atoms and void: the soul itself is an assembly of grains that comes apart at death, with no world beyond and no design.**
4. [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md) : **Not all desires are equal: satisfying the natural and necessary ones, which have a limit, and extinguishing the vain ones, which have none, is enough to live untroubled.**
5. [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md) : **The gods exist, but being perfectly blessed they take no part in the world: neither anger nor favor is to be expected from them, hence nothing to fear.**

## Positions

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 75% · Existentialism 25%

Epicureanism grounds ethics on a given nature: every living being, from birth, seeks pleasure and shuns pain, and it is this natural constitution that reveals the human end. To live well is to follow nature rightly understood, not to redefine oneself freely.

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus · Lucretius, On the Nature of Things*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Hedonism 85% · Eudaimonism 15%

The criterion of the good is read in nature before any reasoning: from birth every living thing seeks [pleasure](../glossary/hedonism.md) and flees pain, untaught. What the animal pursues spontaneously is therefore the first good in accordance with our nature, and no one proves it, any more than one proves that fire is hot. Yet the same argument turns against unbridled enjoyment: pleasure admits no degree beyond the removal of pain, so its summit is a stable state, the absence of bodily suffering and of mental disturbance ([ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md)), not the heaping-up of vivid sensations. Against the Stoics, who demote pleasure to an [indifferent](../glossary/adiaphora.md) and reserve the title of good for virtue alone, Epicureanism holds pleasure to be the end itself, [prudence](../glossary/phronesis.md) being only the art of reckoning it.

> “Pleasure is the beginning and the end of the blessed life.”, Letter to Menoeceus, 128

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus, 128-129 · Epicurus, Principal Doctrines, III*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Freeing ourselves from the fear 95% · Living in view of one's death 5%

All good and evil reside in sensation, and death is precisely the privation of all sensation: physics guarantees it, since the soul, made of subtle atoms, scatters when the body comes apart. Hence the decisive argument: death is nothing to us, for while we exist death is not present, and when death is present we no longer exist; the subject that could suffer and the dreaded event never coexist. The fear of death therefore aims at a nothing and rests on the illusion of still witnessing one's own absence. This targets the terrors of the afterlife, punishment and survival, which Epicureanism holds to be fictions without object: there is no infinite span to dread, and a finite but unanxious life is worth an eternity.

> “Death is nothing to us, for that which is dissolved is without sensation, and that which lacks sensation is nothing to us.”, Letter to Menoeceus, 124-125

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus, 124-125 · Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, book III*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Reasoned selection 85% · Extinction and detachment 10% · Affirmation 5%

If suffering springs chiefly from thwarted desires, it is their object that must be examined: a natural and necessary desire (food, shelter, freedom from fear) has a limit and is easily filled, whereas a vain desire (wealth, glory) is by nature unlimited and never quieted, for it rests on a false opinion. Wisdom therefore consists in selecting the first and extinguishing the rest: not by renunciation, but because the reckoning shows that necessary goods are easy to obtain and that pursuing the unlimited is to doom oneself to disturbance. Where the Stoic suspends every desire for an external object, the Epicurean keeps a part of it, the part that bounds need, and through this sorting attains [self-sufficiency (autarkeia)](../glossary/autarkeia.md).

> “Of desires, some are natural and others vain; and of the natural, some are necessary and others merely natural.”, Letter to Menoeceus, 127

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus, 127-128 · Epicurus, Principal Doctrines, XXIX*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Abolitionism 93% · Fecundity and redemption 7%

For Epicureanism, pain holds no redemptive value: it is an evil to be removed or neutralized, not a trial that ennobles the soul. The [tetrapharmakos](../glossary/tetrapharmakos.md) states it: what is bad is easy to bear, for intense pain is brief and lasting pain is mild. The whole art of living aims to minimize suffering.

*Sources : Epicurus, Principal Doctrines, IV · Philodemus, Tetrapharmakos (Herculaneum)*

### [Nature of reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Materialism 100%

Two sure principles govern the whole physics: nothing comes from nothing and nothing returns to nothing, and the very motion of bodies proves there is void in which they move. From this it follows that there exist only atoms, full and indivisible particles, and the void that separates them: everything else, qualities, souls, gods, is but a transient arrangement of these grains. The stake is not speculative but liberating: if the soul itself is an aggregate of atoms that dissolves at death, then the afterlife, judgment and providence collapse at a stroke, and with them the two great fears. This materialism, taken from Democritus but redirected toward happiness, stands squarely against the Stoics' cosmos animated by a [logos](../glossary/logos.md) and against [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s world of Forms.

> “The universe consists of bodies and void.”, Letter to Herodotus, 39-40

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus, 38-44 · Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, book I*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Immanence 100%

Epicureanism is a pure [immanentism](../glossary/immanence.md): there is no world beyond, no providence, no [transcendent](../glossary/transcendence.md) purpose. Everything, the gods included, belongs to material nature; happiness lies entirely within this life and this world.

*Sources : Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, book I · Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Theism 70% · Atheism 30%

The reasoning starts from the notion all men form of the divine, the preconception (prolēpsis) of a being blessed and imperishable. But one who is perfectly happy has neither needs nor cares: such a being can know neither anger nor favor, for anger and benevolence are marks of weakness. To govern the world, heed prayers and punish faults would be so many disturbances unworthy of bliss: the gods therefore exist, made of atoms and lodged in the spaces between worlds, yet wholly detached from us. Epicurus thus inverts ordinary piety, which fears the gods and flatters them, and cuts off Stoic [providence](../glossary/providence.md): he keeps theism while emptying it of all intervention, which removes the first of the fears.

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus, 123-124 · Epicurus, Principal Doctrines, I*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 80% · Hard determinism 20%

Against the rigorous fate of the Stoics, Epicurus introduces the [clinamen](../glossary/clinamen.md), the spontaneous swerve of atoms that breaks the causal chain and makes room for [freedom](../glossary/free-will.md). It is better to follow the myths about the gods than to be a slave to the [fate](../glossary/determinism.md) of the physicists, for against fate nothing can be done, whereas with the gods hope remains. The position thus leans clearly toward free will.

*Sources : Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, book II, ll. 251-293 · Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus, 133-134*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of reason 70% · The primacy of feeling 30%

Mental disturbances stem from false opinions, above all the fear of the gods and of death: it is by correcting judgment, through the study of nature, that one attains [ataraxia](../glossary/ataraxia.md). Epicureanism thus leans toward intellectualism, yet without the Stoic eradication of the passions, since it grants a positive place to natural affects, such as friendship (philia).

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus, 128-132 · Epicurus, Principal Doctrines, XXVII*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Asceticism 60% · Celebration 40%

Against the caricature of debauchery, Epicurus advocates a sober life: the pleasure sought is chiefly katastematic, the stable pleasure of satisfied need, rather than the kinetic pleasure of enjoyment. The position thus leans slightly toward frugality, without condemning the body.

> “Bread and water yield the highest pleasure when brought to hungry lips.”, Vatican Sayings, 33

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus, 130-131 · Epicurus, Vatican Sayings, 33*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 85% · Absurdism 15%

The Epicurean universe, made of atoms and void, is governed by no purpose or providence: there is no preestablished cosmic meaning. The meaning of existence is therefore to be constructed by the individual, in the pursuit of tranquillity and the cultivation of friendship (philia), without tipping into absurdity, since nature offers a clear criterion, pleasure.

*Sources : Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, book II · Epicurus, Vatican Sayings, 52*

### [Tonality of reality](../axes/cosmic-outlook.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Metaphysical optimism 75% · Tragic thought 25%

The world is not tragic for the Epicurean: nature, rightly understood, supplies all that happiness requires, for what is necessary is easy to obtain and what is hard is not necessary. Without a benevolent providence, the universe nonetheless remains hospitable, which warrants a measured optimism about the possibility of a serene life.

*Sources : Epicurus, Principal Doctrines, XV and XXI · Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, book V*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Empiricism 95% · Rationalism 5%

The Epicurean Canonic, the theory of the criterion of truth, is a forthright empiricism: all knowledge derives from sensations, from preconceptions (prolēpsis), the general concepts sedimented by repeated experience, and from the feelings of pleasure and pain. The senses by themselves never err: error arises in the judgment that opinion adds. Reason only combines what experience supplies.

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus, 37-38 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, X, 31-34 (the Canonic)*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mechanism 100%

Epicurean physics explains every phenomenon by the motion, collision and aggregation of atoms in the void, with no final cause: nature acts toward no end, and our organs were not created for a use, rather their existence made use possible. This explicit rejection of teleology places the school firmly on the side of mechanism.

*Sources : Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, book IV, ll. 823-857 · Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus, 38-44*

### [Wholes and parts](../axes/reduction-emergence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Reductionism 85% · Emergentism 15%

Since everything, the soul and thought included, is but an aggregate of atoms and void, Epicureanism tends toward reductionism: the properties of compounds are explained by the shape, size and motion of their constituents. Lucretius nonetheless grants assemblages secondary qualities, which tempers the reductionism without abandoning it, hence a marked but not extreme position.

*Sources : Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, book III (the atomic soul) · Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus, 68-71*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Consequentialism 60% · Virtue ethics 40%

Epicurean ethics weighs actions by their balance of pleasure and pain: one may choose a pain when it yields a greater pleasure, this hedonistic consequentialist calculus being the ultimate criterion. Yet [prudence](../glossary/phronesis.md), held to be the greatest good and the source of all the virtues, also gives this morality a strong cast of practical virtue, hence a split between consequentialism and virtue ethics.

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus, 129-132 · Epicurus, Principal Doctrines, V*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 80% · Skepticism 20%

Against the [scepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) of the Academy, Epicureanism asserts a clear [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md): all sensations are true, and to deny that anything can be known refutes itself. Nature gives us firm criteria, sensations and preconceptions, on which secure knowledge can be built. Radical doubt is held to be an untenable stance, even though the school admits several possible explanations for distant celestial phenomena.

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus, 37-38 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, X, 31-32*

### [The problem of evil](../axes/problem-of-evil.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Evil refutes God 95% · Unjustifiable evil 5%

The gods, perfectly blessed, do not meddle with the world: there is no providence. The dilemma transmitted under Epicurus's name concludes that the world's evil is incompatible with a god at once good and all-powerful.

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Menoeceus; Lactantius, On the Anger of God, 13*

### [The enigma of consciousness](../axes/consciousness-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Reductive physicalism 90% · Illusionism 10%

The soul is corporeal: an assembly of very fine atoms that dissolves at death like the rest. Thought and sensation are material processes, with nothing immortal or immaterial.

*Sources : Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus; Lucretius, On the Nature of Things, III*

## Influenced by

- **[Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md)** (reacts against) : [Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md), notably in [Lucretius](../philosophers/lucretius.md), fights [Stoic](../movements/stoicism.md) providence and fate in the name of a world without design, born of the chance of atoms and in which the soul is mortal.

## Influence on

- **[Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md)** (reinterprets) : The materialism of [Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md) reaches [Hobbes](../philosophers/hobbes.md) through a documented but indirect relay, his friend Gassendi, restorer of Epicurus in Mersenne's circle: atoms, fear and covenant there prefigure his physics and his contract.
- **[Utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md)** (reinterprets) : A reconstructed, unclaimed filiation: the hedonism of [Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md), which measures the good by pleasure and the absence of disturbance, distantly prefigures the calculus of pains and pleasures of [Utilitarianism](../movements/utilitarianism.md), which Bentham nonetheless refounds on collective happiness.
- **[Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md)** (reacts against) : A founding rivalry of the Hellenistic age: [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md) forges many of its theses against [Epicureanism](../movements/epicureanism.md), setting against the swerve of atoms and pleasure the doctrines of providence, fate and virtue as the only good.
