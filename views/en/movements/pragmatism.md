# Pragmatism

*1870 – ?*

The first distinctively American philosophy, born in the United States after the Civil War, within the **Metaphysical Club** that gathered in Cambridge around 1872 Charles Sanders Peirce, William James and a few jurists and scientists intent on thinking without dogmas. Peirce forged its method, James spread it to a wide public, John Dewey broadened it into a vast philosophy of experience, education and democracy, before Richard Rorty revived it late in the twentieth century. Shaped by Darwinism, which casts thought as an organ of adaptation rather than a mirror, and by the democratic ideal of a collective inquiry with no final authority, pragmatism sought to escape the sterile quarrels between [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md) and [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) by referring every dispute back to what would, concretely, make a difference. Long eclipsed by analytic philosophy, it has since seen a marked revival and remains the matrix of a whole American strand in ethics, education and social theory.

**Key figures** : [Charles Sanders Peirce](../philosophers/peirce.md) · [William James](../philosophers/william-james.md) · [John Dewey](../philosophers/dewey.md)

## Core positions

1. [The meaning of words](../axes/meaning.md) : **The whole meaning of an idea lies in its conceivable practical effects: where no difference of effects can be imagined, there are not two ideas but one, and the quarrel is merely verbal.**
2. [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md) : **The true is not a copy of a ready-made reality but what gets verified in inquiry: an idea becomes true by guiding us successfully through experience.**
3. [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md) : **No belief rests on a first, indubitable foundation: we always inquire from inherited and [fallible](../glossary/fallibilism.md) beliefs that no final certainty shields from revision.**
4. [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md) : **To think is to act: a belief is a habit of action, and inquiry does not contemplate the world but turns a troubled situation into a controlled one.**

## Positions

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Consequentialism 45% · Virtue ethics 40% · Ethics of care 15%

Pragmatism holds morality to be experimental: no fixed end or a priori law, but an intelligence that, in situation, evaluates conduct by its effects on experience and growth. Dewey joins to this the formation of dispositions and habits (a share of virtue) and the concern for social consequences. The right is decided in the testing, like a hypothesis to be verified.

*Sources : Dewey, Theory of the Moral Life · Dewey, Reconstruction in Philosophy*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 80% · Fecundity and redemption 20%

Pragmatism is meliorist: it holds evils and sufferings to be concrete problems to be reduced through intelligence in action and social reform, not a fatality to be interpreted. Dewey refuses both resignation and metaphysical consolation: the task is to act on the conditions that produce avoidable suffering, wagering on their possible improvement.

*Sources : Dewey, Reconstruction in Philosophy · Dewey, The Public and Its Problems*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 85% · Teleology and providence 10% · Absurdism 5%

Pragmatism does not receive meaning from a ready-made order: it is fashioned in action, through the growth of experience and the resolution of problems that make life richer. Dewey rejects fixed ends in favor of an open, meliorist meaning, and James makes confidence in the future a faith that helps to realize it: a human, practical and revisable significance.

*Sources : Dewey, Reconstruction in Philosophy · James, The Will to Believe*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eudaimonism 70% · Hedonism 20% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 10%

For pragmatism, the good is not a fixed end to contemplate but growth itself: the continuous enrichment of experience, the development of capacities, the intelligent resolution of problems that makes life fuller. Dewey rejects the idea of an immutable highest good in favor of an open, revisable flourishing.

*Sources : Dewey, Reconstruction in Philosophy · Dewey, Democracy and Education*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Reasoned selection 50% · Affirmation 45% · Extinction and detachment 5%

Pragmatism naturalizes desire: far from opposing it to reason, Dewey sees in it the spring of value, the interest that moves inquiry and orients conduct. Desire is neither to be extinguished nor followed as is, but to be educated by intelligence, which turns it into a reflective end: an affirmation of desire ordered by its practical examination.

*Sources : Dewey, The Quest for Certainty · Dewey, Theory of Valuation*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 70% · Hard determinism 30%

William James rejects the determinist **block universe**, in which the future would already be contained in the past, in favor of an open world where chance, novelty and choice are real. His argument is as moral as it is theoretical: without genuine alternative futures, regret and effort lose their sense. Through its Jamesian lineage, pragmatism leans toward free will and indeterminism, even if its figures qualify the claim.

*Sources : James, The Dilemma of Determinism · James, The Principles of Psychology*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Pragmatism 75% · Coherence 15% · Correspondence 10%

Asking whether an idea faithfully « copies » a given reality is, for James, a pseudo-problem: a copy we could never set beside its model makes no detectable difference. One workable criterion then remains: an idea is true if, put to the test, it guides us smoothly through experience, knits our beliefs together and keeps its promises. The true thereby becomes a **process**, what gets verified in the course of inquiry, and not a property already lodged in the statement. The thesis targets the correspondence realism shared by [rationalism](../movements/rationalism.md) and classical [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) alike: Peirce tempers it, though, by placing at the horizon of indefinitely pursued inquiry a final opinion on which investigators would converge, which reintroduces a regulative form of reality.

> “The true, to put it very briefly, is only the expedient in the way of our thinking.”, Pragmatism, lecture VI

*Sources : Peirce, 'How to Make Our Ideas Clear' (1878) · James, Pragmatism (1907), lectures II and VI*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Skepticism 80% · Dogmatism 20%

No belief rests on a first, indubitable foundation: it is the error of [dogmatism](../glossary/dogmatism.md), and of Cartesian doubt, to seek outside inquiry a bedrock that would secure it from without. Peirce reverses the order: one never starts from scratch but from a fabric of inherited beliefs that only a real doubt, sprung from a concrete difficulty, can unsettle, never a feigned doubt posited on principle. Hence [fallibilism](../glossary/fallibilism.md): our best-assured opinions stay correctable by future experience, none escaping revision by right. The position thereby parts company both with the rationalist quest for a certain starting point and with the [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) that, lacking such a point, would suspend action: one inquires from within revisable beliefs, with no outside to ground them.

*Sources : Peirce, 'The Fixation of Belief' (1877) · Dewey, Logic: The Theory of Inquiry (1938)*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructivism 80% · Scientific realism 20%

Inquiry, for Dewey, does not passively mirror a pre-existing object: it transforms an indeterminate situation into a determinate one, and so shapes its own objects of knowledge. Knowing is an active, instrumental construction, embedded in a practice, rather than the tracing of an independent reality. The leaning is constructivist, without denying that reality offers resistance.

*Sources : Dewey, Logic: The Theory of Inquiry (1938) · Dewey, The Quest for Certainty (1929)*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 78% · Rationalism 22%

Pragmatism is rooted in experience rather than pure reason: James develops a **radical empiricism** in which relations themselves are given within the flux of experience, and Dewey grounds all thought in the organism's transaction with its environment. Knowledge proceeds from experimentation and its observable consequences, in the lineage of British [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md), which it extends while ridding it of the idea of an inert sense datum.

*Sources : James, Essays in Radical Empiricism (1912) · Dewey, Experience and Nature (1925)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Primacy of action 85% · The examined life 15%

A belief is not a contemplative mental state but a habit of action: what tells one belief from another is the conduct it disposes us toward, as Peirce establishes. Thought then arises from a break in action, doubt, and is stilled only by the restoration of settled conduct, fixed belief. Inquiry is thus no spectacle set before a ready-made world: in Dewey it is the active transformation of a troubled situation into a controlled one, a stretch of the organism's continuous dealing with its environment. The thesis rejects the inherited split between contemplative theoria and subordinate [praxis](../glossary/praxis.md), a split that demeaned doing in favor of seeing: for pragmatism, to know is a kind of acting, and the detached « spectator » is a philosophical fiction.

*Sources : Peirce, 'How to Make Our Ideas Clear' (1878) · Dewey, Democracy and Education (1916)*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Progressivism 88% · Conservatism 13%

Meliorism holds the world to be neither fatally good nor fatally bad, but improvable through intelligent effort: this drives Dewey's reformism, which invests education and democracy as levers of gradual, experimental progress. Society is not an order to be preserved but a project to be revised without end, through trial, error and correction.

*Sources : Dewey, The Public and Its Problems (1927) · James, Pragmatism (1907), lecture VIII*

### [Place of science](../axes/science-place.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Epistemic pluralism 70% · Scientism 30%

Pragmatism erects the sciences' **experimental method** as the model of all rational inquiry, moral and social included, yet without reducing all value to the measurable: James defends the rights of religious and moral experience, and Dewey refuses to treat science as the only form of meaning. The stance is therefore favorable to science while remaining pluralist, keeping its distance from scientism.

*Sources : Dewey, The Quest for Certainty (1929) · James, The Will to Believe (1897)*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructivism 70% · Relativism 20% · Universalism 10%

Moral values, for the pragmatists, are neither eternal essences to be discovered nor purely arbitrary conventions: they are worked out and justified within inquiry, tested by their consequences for shared experience. Dewey treats value judgments as revisable hypotheses, submitted to the same practical test as statements of fact. The good is constructed in social deliberation.

*Sources : Dewey, Reconstruction in Philosophy (1920) · Dewey, Theory of Valuation (1939)*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Existentialism 80% · Essentialism 20%

Pragmatism distrusts fixed essences posited before experience: concepts, species and institutions are products of a history and a practice, shaped by their functions rather than given once and for all. Dewey, marked by Darwinism, thinks of forms as provisional stabilizations within a becoming: doing and becoming take priority over finished being. The leaning is existentialist in the broad sense of the primacy of existence over essence.

*Sources : Dewey, The Influence of Darwin on Philosophy (1910) · James, Pragmatism (1907), lecture IV*

### [The meaning of words](../axes/meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Use theory 80% · Referentialism 10% · Speaker's intention 10%

Two ideas between which we could imagine no difference of practical effects have, for Peirce, exactly the same meaning: such is the **pragmatic maxim**, a rule of clarity more demanding than the « clear and distinct ideas » of [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md). To fix a concept's meaning, one lists the whole set of sensible or practical consequences one would expect from it, and nothing more: that inventory of conceivable effects exhausts the meaning. Its bearing is polemical: a metaphysical dispute whose outcome would make no difference to experience is thereby reduced to a verbal conflict with no object. This is the current's founding gesture, from which both its conception of truth and its dismissal of pseudo-problems derive, and which sets it apart from a referential semantics where meaning would precede all use.

*Sources : Peirce, How to Make Our Ideas Clear; James, Pragmatism*

## Influenced by

- **[Empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md)** (continues) : [Pragmatism](../movements/pragmatism.md) extends [empiricism](../movements/empiricism.md) by anchoring meaning and truth in experience, but shifts the criterion toward the practical consequences of an idea rather than its sensory origins. James indeed claims a “radical empiricism”.
