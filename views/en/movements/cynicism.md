# Cynicism

*400 BCE – 200 BCE*

Born in Athens in the fourth century BCE, Cynicism traces back to Socrates through Antisthenes, but it was Diogenes of Sinope, an exile said to have lived in a barrel, who turned it into provocation raised to philosophy: here the public scandal, the biting anecdote and the gesture count for as much as the argument. The word « cynic » comes from the Greek kynikos (« dog-like »), a nickname the school turned into a badge of pride, and bears nothing of its modern pejorative sense. The doctrine reaches us only indirectly, through the chreiai (anecdotes) compiled by Diogenes Laertius, for the Cynics wrote little and held philosophy to be a matter of living rather than of treatises. Crates of Thebes softened the figure and passed the teaching to Zeno of Citium: through him Cynicism becomes one of the roots of [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md), which would inherit its cosmopolitanism and the idea that virtue suffices, while adding the physics and logic the Cynics disdained.

**Key figures** : [Diogenes of Sinope](../philosophers/diogenes.md) · [Antisthenes](../philosophers/antisthenes.md) · [Crates](../philosophers/crates.md)

## Core positions

1. [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md) : **Almost everything men believe they owe to nature is mere nomos, groundless convention: to live according to nature is to strip away that artifice down to real need alone.**
2. [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md) : **Whoever desires nothing external depends on no one: [self-sufficiency](../glossary/autarkeia.md) makes the most destitute of men freer than the mightiest of kings.**
3. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **Virtue alone is a good, for it alone cannot be taken from us; and it is a strength won through [training](../glossary/askesis.md) and proven by deeds, not a piece of knowledge.**
4. [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md) : **To speak the truth without circumlocution, even to the powerful, and to transgress accepted modesties without blushing: [frankness](../glossary/parrhesia.md) proves by deed that social shame has no natural ground.**
5. [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md) : **No city has any natural claim on me: I am citizen of no particular homeland but a kosmopolitēs, a citizen of the whole world.**

## Positions

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Virtue ethics 95% · Consequentialism 5%

For Cynicism, virtue is the only good and suffices for happiness: it is not taught in theory but acquired through exercise (askesis), as an art of living in accordance with nature. The whole of ethics lies in the formation of a free, self-sufficient character, indifferent to conventions, with neither rule nor calculation of consequences.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Atheism 65% · Theism 35%

The Cynics profess no theoretical atheism, but a practical irreligion: Diogenes mocks the rites, the mysteries and the oracles, and holds the city's religious beliefs and fears to be vain. Indifferent to the gods of cult, they reduce the divine to virtue and to nature, which sets them clearly apart from theism without making them militant atheists.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI*

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Fecundity and redemption 85% · Abolitionism 15%

Cynicism makes painful effort (ponos) the very means of virtue: Diogenes trains himself to cold, hunger and destitution to harden himself and become free. Suffering voluntarily embraced is no evil but an askēsis, a gymnastics of the soul that forges self-sufficiency and frees one from the blows of fortune.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 55% · Teleology and providence 45%

The Cynics measure life against nature as opposed to conventions, but refer it to no providence: meaning lies in the virtuous, free and self-sufficient life one forges through exercise (askēsis). More than discovering a given end, the point is to win an unconditioned way of existing, which tips meaning toward human work.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Freeing ourselves from the fear 80% · Living in view of one's death 10% · Preparing for the passage 10%

The Cynics flaunt a provocative indifference toward death: Diogenes, it is said, asks that his corpse be thrown over the wall to the beasts, mocking the care of burial, since he will no longer be there to feel anything. Death is nothing that should trouble the self-sufficient sage, and the fear of it belongs to the empty conventions he sets out precisely to undo.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Essentialism 75% · Existentialism 25%

Cynicism measures life against nature (physis) as opposed to convention (nomos): there is a natural human condition, self-sufficient and shameless like that of the animals, which civilization has covered over and which must be recovered. Nature is the norm, not an identity to be invented.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 70% · Hard determinism 30%

The Cynic makes freedom (eleutheria) and self-sufficiency (autarkeia) the supreme good: through exercise and stripping away, he makes himself independent of desires, of opinion and of the blows of fortune. Diogenes means to be the free man par excellence, master of himself because he needs nothing. A freedom more practical than metaphysical: not a thesis about the will, but the conquest of an unconditioned life.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 75% · Rationalism 25%

Antisthenes, the cynics' forerunner, opposes Platonism with a radical **nominalism**: “I can see a horse, but not horseness”. Only sensible individuals exist, grasped by perception; general Ideas are empty words. The knowledge that counts is taken from experience and concrete practice, not from the contemplation of intelligible essences.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI · Simplicius, Commentary on the Categories*

### [Belonging](../axes/belonging.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Cosmopolitanism 98% · Communitarianism and patriotism 3%

If the borders of cities are only nomos, convention without grounding in nature, then none can command the allegiance of a rational being: a city has no more natural claim on me than the accident of my birth. Hence the word kosmopolitēs that Diogenes coins, the first to call himself a citizen of the world: not love of an enlarged homeland, but the refusal to acknowledge any, all of humanity taken as the only horizon. The provocation strikes at Greek civic pride, which counted the exile as fallen: the Cynic makes that his claimed condition.

> “When asked where he came from, he said: "I am a citizen of the world."”, Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 63

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives of Eminent Philosophers, VI, 63*

### [Scope of action](../axes/control-scope.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

What is up to us 95% · Prometheanism 5%

One is free only in proportion to what one can do without: every need is a hold that the world, fortune or the powerful have on us. From this the Cynic draws [autarkeia](../glossary/autarkeia.md) (self-sufficiency) as the goal, living so that nothing external is necessary and no one therefore holds anything we depend on. When Alexander, master of the known world, offered to grant him any wish, Diogenes asked only that he stand out of his sunlight: this shows that the most destitute of men, if he desires nothing, is more invulnerable than the mightiest of kings, who remains at the mercy of what he owns.

> “"Ask of me any boon you like." "Stand out of my sunlight."”, Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 38

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 38*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue / moral perfectionism 75% · Eudaimonism 25%

Whatever can be misused, or lost to a stroke of fortune, cannot be a true good: neither health, nor wealth, nor pleasure passes the test. Antisthenes, a pupil of Socrates, concludes that virtue alone deserves the name, because it alone always benefits and cannot be taken away; once acquired it is not lost, and it therefore suffices for happiness. But the Cynics harden the Socratic legacy on one decisive point: virtue is not knowledge but a strength, won through [training](../glossary/askesis.md) and proven by deeds, not by definition, against the intellectualism that would make the good a science teachable in words.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 11 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 104-105*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Asceticism 93% · Celebration 8%

Cynicism is a radical asceticism: through [askēsis](../glossary/askesis.md) (training), the sage makes do with water, bread, and a coarse cloak, rejecting luxury and refinements as so many forms of bondage. Seeing a child drink from its cupped hands, Diogenes threw away his bowl, judging that a child had outdone him in simplicity.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 37 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 22-23*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Naturalism 95% · Culturalism 5%

Cynicism rests entirely on one cut: on one side physis (nature), which sets man simple needs and one common lot; on the other the nomos (convention) of cities, which heaps on wealth, rank, modesties and honours with no grounding in things. And man makes himself wretched precisely where he takes convention for nature, toiling for factitious goods and blushing at acts no animal deems shameful. To live according to nature is thus to undo this varnish: not an idealised return to a primal goodness, but a stripping away, bringing conduct back to what real need requires and counting the rest as smoke. Nature here is not a teleological norm but a negative criterion, what remains once artifice is removed.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 70-71*

### [Sincerity](../axes/sincerity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Truthful rigorism 98% · Benevolent lying permitted 3%

If social shame is only nomos, then to keep it quiet or humour it is to become complicit in the collective lie on which conventions rest. Hence two bound demands. [Parrhēsia](../glossary/parrhesia.md) (frank speech) requires telling the truth without circumlocution or flattery, even to the faces of the powerful, for whom deference has become an entitlement: it is, Diogenes answered, the finest thing men possess. Anaideia (deliberate shamelessness) is its proof by deed: to perform in public, without blushing, what accepted modesty forbids is to demonstrate experimentally that this modesty had no natural ground. Cynic frankness is thus no gratuitous coarseness but a method of refutation, which takes the body and the scandal as its arguments.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 69*

### [Relation to authority](../axes/authority.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Anarchism 88% · Authority 13%

The Cynic despises institutions, magistracies, and civic conventions, holding them to be arbitrary constructions with no natural worth. This distrust of any established order, with no political project to replace it, tends toward a form of anarchism of the self-sufficient individual: Diogenes searched in broad daylight, lamp in hand, for a true human being among the figures of power.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 41*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Primacy of action 93% · The examined life 7%

Since philosophy's sole object is to live well, and living well is a matter of conduct, physics, logic and learned disputation are wasted time: one does not become temperate by defining temperance. Hence the Cynic rejection of theory, which is not lack of culture but consistency with the primacy of [praxis](../glossary/praxis.md). A thesis is refuted by an act as much as by an argument: hearing motion denied, Diogenes, for all reply, stood up and walked. The gesture serves as demonstration, and the sage's life, exposed in public, stands in for a treatise.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 39 · Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 70-71*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Extinction and detachment 80% · Reasoned selection 20%

The Cynic reduces desire to the strictly natural and detaches himself from all the rest: the less one desires, the freer one is. [Askēsis](../glossary/askesis.md) consists precisely in training oneself to want nothing more from external things, so that nothing can be lacking or enslaving.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 105*

### [Hierarchy of tastes](../axes/taste-hierarchy.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Egalitarianism of taste 85% · Elitism of taste 15%

The Cynic mocks refinement, luxury arts, and distinguished culture as so many social vanities: what passes for good taste is mere nomos, a status marker detached from natural need. The stance is egalitarian, debunking the pretension behind hierarchies of taste.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 24*

### [Status of norms (metaethics)](../axes/moral-scope.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Universalism 85% · Relativism 10% · Constructivism 5%

By opposing nature to nomos, Cynicism presupposes a criterion of the good independent of conventions and valid for all: what accords with nature is good everywhere, what is merely local custom does not bind. The sage judges customs in the name of a universal natural norm rather than according to each city.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI, 70-71*

### [The value of truth](../axes/truth-value.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of truth 80% · Vital illusions 20%

Defacing the currency of conventions: the Cynic program is a demonetizing of social illusions, honours, riches, proprieties, of which the life of [Diogenes](../philosophers/diogenes.md) is the living demonstration. Frank speech ([parrhēsia](../glossary/parrhesia.md)) tells the truth to the face of power, and life according to nature is a truth lived rather than demonstrated.

*Sources : Diogenes Laertius, Lives, VI*

## Influence on

- **[Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md)** (continues) : [Stoicism](../movements/stoicism.md) arises in the wake of [Cynicism](../movements/cynicism.md), from which it takes the ideal of self-sufficiency, life “according to nature” and cosmopolitanism; but it tempers its provocation and anticonformism by embedding it in a systematic physics and logic, where virtue is no longer a defiance of convention but accord with the reason that orders the world.
