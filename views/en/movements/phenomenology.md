# Phenomenology

*1900 – ?*

Inaugurated by Edmund Husserl at the turn of the twentieth century, phenomenology was born of a precise battle: against psychologism, which reduced the laws of logic to mere facts of our mind, and against naturalism, which treats consciousness as one object in the world among others. Husserl forged a **method** of rigorous description, extended and then bent by his heirs, whose divergence is the school's very history: Heidegger, his assistant, turned it into an analytic of existence ( Being and Time, 1927); Merleau-Ponty shifted it toward perception and the body ( Phenomenology of Perception, 1945); [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md) drew from it an ontology of freedom. Out of this lineage came [existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md), and its influence later ran through much of French thought, from hermeneutics to deconstruction. What the school shares lies less in a doctrine than in a demand: to return "to the things themselves" as they give themselves.

**Key figures** : [Husserl](../philosophers/husserl.md) · [Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md) · [Merleau-Ponty](../philosophers/merleau-ponty.md) · [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md)

## Core positions

1. [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md) : **Philosophy must first describe faithfully experience as it gives itself in the first person, rather than explain it from outside as one fact among facts.**
2. [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md) : **All consciousness is consciousness of something: the meaning of the world does not lie ready-made in nature, it is constituted in the subject's intentional relation to what appears to it.**
3. [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md) : **I do not own a body like an object, I am a living body: it is through it, not above it, that the world appears to me.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 80% · Teleology and providence 10% · Absurdism 10%

Phenomenology holds meaning to be constituted, not found ready-made in things: through intentionality, consciousness gives significance to the world, and Heidegger's Dasein is the being for whom its own being is in question. The meaning of existence is neither inscribed in a cosmic order nor purely absurd: it is won in the way each takes up and unfolds its relation to the world.

*Sources : Husserl, The Crisis of the European Sciences · Heidegger, Being and Time*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Living in view of one's death 85% · Freeing ourselves from the fear 10% · Preparing for the passage 5%

With Heidegger, phenomenology makes death a structure of existence: Dasein is being-toward-death, and it is by resolutely anticipating its ownmost, insuperable and non-shareable end that it tears itself from the idle talk of the "they" and authentically takes hold of itself. Death is not a distant event but what, here and now, individualizes existence and gives it its weight.

*Sources : Heidegger, Being and Time, §§ 46-53*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Free will 70% · Hard determinism 30%

Against the **naturalism** that ranks consciousness among determined processes, phenomenology restores a free subject, an intentional activity that gives meaning to the world and is not reduced to it. Husserl sets the mind against the causality of nature; his heirs, Sartre and Merleau-Ponty, radicalize this motif into an embodied, situated freedom. The movement therefore leans clearly toward freedom, against psychophysical determinism.

*Sources : Husserl, The Crisis of the European Sciences · Merleau-Ponty, Phenomenology of Perception*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 50% · Rationalism 50%

Phenomenology refuses the empiricism / rationalism alternative: against the sensualism that reduces the mind to impressions, and against deductive rationalism, it returns to the intuition of what gives itself. The [epoché](../glossary/epoche.md) brings out the essential structures of experience, grasped in an **intuition of essences** (Wesensschau) that is both a priori and grounded in lived experience. Hence a third-way position, close to criticism.

*Sources : Husserl, Ideas Pertaining to a Pure Phenomenology, I · Husserl, Logical Investigations*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Dogmatism 75% · Skepticism 25%

Husserlian phenomenology means to be a **rigorous science**, able to ground knowledge against **psychologism** and skeptical relativism. Its gesture is the [epoché](../glossary/epoche.md): suspending the naive thesis of the world in order to return to the things themselves, as they give themselves to consciousness. There lie **apodictic** data, indubitable because grasped without intermediary in intuitive self-evidence, the bedrock of a certain knowledge. This quest for an absolute foundation brings Husserl close to a Cartesian-inspired foundationalism, even if his heirs, Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty, will abandon that ideal of apodicticity.

*Sources : Husserl, Philosophy as Rigorous Science · Husserl, Cartesian Meditations*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The examined life 93% · Primacy of action 8%

Every causal explanation already presupposes a world that appears to someone: science itself operates on phenomena given to a consciousness it forgets. From this observation the phenomenologists draw an order of priority: one must first describe faithfully how things give themselves to lived experience, before seeking their cause. The method, against common sense, requires the [epoché](../glossary/epoche.md), the bracketing of our spontaneous theses about the existence of objects, so as to retain only their mode of appearing. This is why Heidegger questions being from the entity that we are, and Merleau-Ponty makes lived perception, not the physiological fact, the first ground of philosophy.

> “We want to go back to the things themselves.”, Logical Investigations, II

*Sources : Husserl, Ideas Pertaining to a Pure Phenomenology (1913) · Husserl, Cartesian Meditations (1931) · Merleau-Ponty, Phenomenology of Perception (1945), preface*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructivism 73% · Scientific realism 28%

The fulcrum is intentionality, taken over from Brentano: all consciousness is consciousness of something, never a content sealed upon itself. It follows that the object cannot be severed from the act that aims at it: meaning and objectivity are not brute facts deposited in nature but are constituted in that relation. The target is objectivist naturalism, which takes the world for an in-itself independent of all consciousness and forgets that it has itself posited it. Husserl thus shows that scientific idealities are rooted in the lifeworld (Lebenswelt), forgotten as their ground; Merleau-Ponty recalls that science is a second-order expression of the perceived world. The stance leans toward constructivism, without sliding into relativism.

*Sources : Husserl, The Crisis of European Sciences and Transcendental Phenomenology (1936) · Merleau-Ponty, Phenomenology of Perception (1945), preface*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

The primacy of feeling 70% · The primacy of reason 30%

Against reducing affects to merely subjective, irrational states, the phenomenological tradition grants them a cognitive reach: moods open a distinctive access to the world. Heidegger makes attunement (Stimmung) a fundamental mode through which Dasein finds itself always already disclosed to its world, anxiety revealing its very being; Sartre analyzes emotion as a meaningful way of apprehending the world, not a mere disturbance. Affect is thus held to be disclosive, without thereby making feeling the foundation of morality.

*Sources : Heidegger, Being and Time (1927), §29-30 · Sartre, Sketch for a Theory of the Emotions (1939)*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The lived body 93% · The body as instrument 7%

The description of perception reveals what Cartesian dualism conceals: I never experience my body as an extended object set before a consciousness, but as the lived body from which a world is ordered, with an up and a down, a near and a far. My body is not in space, it is my anchorage in space: perspective, sidedness and touch prove that every point of view on the world is first a bodily position. Hence the refutation of [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md)'s disembodied subject: there is no thought that would own a body as an instrument, but an embodied subject for whom perceiving and inhabiting the world are one and the same.

*Sources : Merleau-Ponty, Phenomenology of Perception; Husserl, Ideas II*

## Influence on

- **[Existentialism](../movements/existentialism.md)** (continues) : Twentieth-century existentialism inherits its method from [phenomenology](../movements/phenomenology.md): to describe phenomena as they give themselves to consciousness, prior to theories. But it shifts the object, from constituting consciousness toward concrete existence, freedom, anguish and the body: [Sartre](../philosophers/sartre.md) draws from intentionality a consciousness given over to nothingness and choice, [Heidegger](../philosophers/heidegger.md) recasts description as an analytic of being-in-the-world. The heirs reshape even the method itself, [Merleau-Ponty](../philosophers/merleau-ponty.md) anchoring it in the perceiving body.
