# Aristotelianism

*335 BCE – 1274*

A tradition stemming from the school [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) founded at the Lyceum in Athens around 335 BCE, where his disciples, the Peripatetics, taught while walking about. Trained under [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md) but breaking with him, Aristotle built philosophy's first encyclopaedic system, from logic to biology, out of the patient observation of the sensible world rather than the contemplation of a realm of Ideas. After a long eclipse, his work was commented on and extended by Averroes in the Arab-Muslim world, then absorbed into Christianity by Thomas Aquinas: Aristotelianism became the backbone of medieval scholasticism and the dominant authority of the universities, until modern science challenged its finalism. Its logic, its virtue ethics and its metaphysical vocabulary still run through Western thought.

**Key figures** : [Aristotle](../philosophers/aristotle.md) · [Thomas Aquinas](../philosophers/aquinas.md) · [Averroes](../philosophers/averroes.md)

## Core positions

1. [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md) : **To explain a thing is to state what it is for: every being tends toward its proper end, and nothing in nature is in vain.**
2. [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md) : **The soul is the [form](../glossary/hylomorphism.md) of the living body, not a substance housed within it: the human being is the inseparable unity of a soul and a body.**
3. [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md) : **Virtue is neither a calculation nor obedience to a rule, but a [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md) between excess and deficiency, discerned by [practical wisdom](../glossary/phronesis.md).**
4. [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md) : **A thing's essence is an [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md) [form](../glossary/hylomorphism.md), not a separate Idea: form exists only in individual substances.**
5. [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md) : **The supreme good is [eudaimonia](../glossary/eudaimonism.md), an activity of the soul in accord with virtue: humans flourish by exercising their proper function, reason.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Abolitionism 65% · Fecundity and redemption 35%

Aristotelianism holds pain and misfortune to be genuine evils, against Stoic indifference: happiness needs external goods, and great misfortunes can impair it. Suffering has no value in itself and should be avoided; but the courageous person endures it nobly when a good end demands it, without seeking or sacralizing it.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, I and III*

### [Death](../axes/death.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Death is nothing 45% · Revolt / refusal 25% · Immortalism 20% · Being-toward-death 10%

Aristotelianism holds death to be the natural end of the compound of soul and body: a real evil, which deprives of all goods, but to be faced with courage rather than fled or denied. Except for the intellect, whose eternity remains impersonal, nothing of the person survives: a lucid finitude, which grants death its gravity without making of it either a nothing or a passage.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, III*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Free will 65% · Hard determinism 35%

Aristotle forges the analysis of the voluntary act: voluntary is that whose principle (arche) lies in the agent who knows the circumstances, as opposed to what is done under constraint or through ignorance. Since the origin of our acts is in us, they are “up to us” (eph' hemin), and that is why we praise and blame: virtue and vice alike are voluntary. A stance of responsibility, with neither a deterministic system nor a metaphysical free will.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, III, 1-5*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Eudaimonism 85% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 15%

The argument proceeds from the proper function: as the flute-player's good lies in performing his function well, the human good lies in the activity specific to humans, the exercise of reason, which neither plant nor animal shares. The sovereign good thus cannot be pleasure, which every beast pursues, against [hedonism](../glossary/hedonism.md); nor an external good like honour, which depends on others. It is [eudaimonia](../glossary/eudaimonism.md), an activity of the soul in accord with virtue: an active fulfilment, not a possessed state, unfolding across a whole life and requiring also goods of body and fortune.

> “The good of man is an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are several virtues, in accordance with the best and most complete.” (Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, I, 7, 1098a)

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, I, 7 · Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, I-II, qq. 1-5*

### [Criterion of right action](../axes/ethics-foundation.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Virtue ethics 100%

One does not become just by knowing justice, against Socratic intellectualism, nor by following a rule that would hold identically for all: virtue is a stable disposition of character, acquired by habituation like a skill. Hence the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md): courage is not a fixed quantity but the point adjusted between cowardice and rashness, relative to us and varying with circumstance. No formula can settle it in advance; only [practical wisdom](../glossary/phronesis.md), the practical reason of the experienced person, discerns it in the concrete case. This is why right action is a matter of situated judgement, not of calculation or obedience.

> “Virtue is a state of character concerned with choice, lying in a mean relative to us, this being determined by reason and in the way the person of practical wisdom would determine it.” (Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, II, 6, 1106b-1107a)

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, II, 6 · Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, I-II, qq. 55-67*

### [Explaining nature](../axes/causality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Finalism 90% · Mechanism 10%

The starting point is the observation of living things: an acorn becomes an oak and not a beech, an organ develops for a function, signs that an end orients the process. To treat this regularity as a mere accident of matter, as Democritus' mechanism does, explains nothing; beyond matter one needs the [final cause](../glossary/four-causes.md), the that-for-the-sake-of-which a thing exists. Finalism is thus not projected onto nature but read within it, and it is the internal natures of beings, not an external craftsman, that carry them toward their end.

> “God and nature do nothing in vain.” (Aristotle, On the Heavens, I, 4, 271a; cf. Physics, II, 8)

*Sources : Aristotle, Physics, II, 3 and 8 · Aristotle, Metaphysics, A, 3*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Essentialism 93% · Existentialism 8%

Knowledge aims at the universal and the stable: there are indeed essences, that by which a thing is what it is, distinct from its accidental features. But to lodge those essences in a separate world, like [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s Ideas, needlessly doubles reality and explains neither how the Idea makes the thing be nor where it resides. Aristotle therefore brings the [form](../glossary/hylomorphism.md) back into the individual substance: it exists only as [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md) in this horse, this man. Essentialism is thus preserved without a dualism of worlds, which grounds knowledge of the sensible rather than turning away from it.

*Sources : Aristotle, Metaphysics, Z (book VII) · Thomas Aquinas, On Being and Essence*

### [Wholes and parts](../axes/reduction-emergence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Emergentism 83% · Reductionism 18%

The soul is defined as the [form](../glossary/hylomorphism.md), or first actuality, of the organized living body: a living thing is not reducible to its material parts, for it is its form that unifies it and makes it what it is. The whole is thus prior in nature to its parts, aligning Aristotelianism with a kind of emergentism avant la lettre.

*Sources : Aristotle, On the Soul, II, 1 · Aristotle, Parts of Animals, I, 1*

### [Letter or spirit of the rule](../axes/rule-spirit.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Equity-phronesis 80% · Legalism 20%

Aristotelianism makes a theme of epieikeia, the equity that corrects the law where its generality renders it unjust in a particular case. Since no rule can anticipate every case, justice requires the discernment of [practical wisdom](../glossary/phronesis.md), tilting the position toward the spirit rather than the letter.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, V, 10*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Empiricism 75% · Rationalism 25%

Against Platonic innatism, Aristotelianism holds that all knowledge begins in sense perception: nothing is in the intellect that was not first in the senses. Induction rises from perceived particulars to universal principles, yielding a moderately empiricist position, the intellect remaining required to grasp essences.

*Sources : Aristotle, Posterior Analytics, II, 19 · Aristotle, On the Soul, III, 4-8*

### [Nature of truth](../axes/truth-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Correspondence 90% · Coherence 10%

The Aristotelian definition of truth is the paradigm of correspondence: thought is true when it accords with what is in the thing, a formula scholasticism would restate as the adequation of intellect and thing.

> “To say of what is that it is not, or of what is not that it is, is false; while to say of what is that it is, and of what is not that it is not, is true.” (Aristotle, Metaphysics, Γ (book IV), 7, 1011b)

*Sources : Aristotle, Metaphysics, Γ (book IV), 7 · Thomas Aquinas, Disputed Questions on Truth, q. 1*

### [The origin of the social bond](../axes/social-contract.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Organic community 93% · Contractualism 7%

Far from a contractual artifice, the city is for Aristotelianism a natural reality: man is by nature a political animal, and only a god or a beast could live outside the city. The political community is the natural culmination of simpler communities, the household and then the village.

*Sources : Aristotle, Politics, I, 2*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Holism 80% · Individualism 20%

The city is prior in nature to the individual, as the whole is to the part: in isolation the individual is not self-sufficient and cannot actualize a properly human end. This priority of the whole leans holist, without dissolving the individual, since the end remains the flourishing of citizens.

*Sources : Aristotle, Politics, I, 2*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Teleology and providence 90% · Constructed meaning 10%

The meaning of existence is teleological: since every being tends toward its proper end, human life finds its meaning in the actualization of its function, the life according to reason, whose summit is contemplative activity. Meaning is therefore neither wholly self-constructed nor absurd, but inscribed in the very nature of the human being.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, X, 7-8*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Dogmatism 85% · Skepticism 15%

Aristotelianism is [dogmatic](../glossary/dogmatism.md) in the noble sense: genuine science, epistēmē, is demonstrative knowledge proceeding from indemonstrable first principles, themselves grasped with certainty by the intellect. Scientific knowledge bears on the necessary and the universal, which excludes [sceptical](../glossary/skepticism.md) doubt about its foundations, even as [practical wisdom](../glossary/phronesis.md) acknowledges the inherent uncertainty of the domain of action.

*Sources : Aristotle, Posterior Analytics, I, 2 and II, 19*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Theism 80% · Atheism 20%

Aristotelianism affirms a divine principle, the [unmoved mover](../glossary/unmoved-mover.md), the final cause of the universe's motion, which it moves as the beloved moves the lover. Yet Aristotle's god is impersonal: pure thought thinking itself, it is neither creator nor providence and takes no concern for the world. Hence a clearly non-extreme theist position that marks this impersonality, before scholasticism identified it, not without tension, with the personal and providential God.

> “Thought thinks itself in grasping the intelligible, and its thinking is a thinking of thinking.” (Aristotle, Metaphysics, Λ (book XII), 9, 1074b)

*Sources : Aristotle, Metaphysics, Λ (book XII), 7 and 9*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Reasoned selection 80% · Affirmation 10% · Extinction and detachment 10%

Desire is neither to be affirmed without restraint nor extinguished, but ordered by practical reason: the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md) consists in desiring what one ought, when and as one ought. The temperate person has not abolished appetite but brought it into accord with deliberate choice, yielding a reasoned selection of desires rather than detachment or sheer affirmation.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, III, 10-12*

### [Status of emotions](../axes/emotions.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Intellectualism 60% · Sentimentalism 40%

Against Socratic intellectualism, Aristotelianism grants the passions a properly ethical role: to feel fear, anger or pity when one ought, toward whom one ought and as one ought is itself virtue. Well-trained emotions carry a cognitive dimension and accompany sound judgement, without grounding it on their own: the position is moderate, leaning slightly toward judgement.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, II, 5-6 · Aristotle, Rhetoric, II, 1-11*

### [Partiality](../axes/partiality.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Partialism 80% · Impartialism 20%

Friendship, philia, occupies two whole books of the *[Nicomachean Ethics](../works/nicomachean-ethics-aristotle.md)* and is an indispensable good of the good life: one owes more to kin, benefactors and fellow citizens. This avowed priority of particular bonds, up to the civic friendship that holds the city together, places Aristotelianism on the partialist side.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, VIII-IX*

### [Bodily pleasures](../axes/bodily-pleasures.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Celebration 55% · Asceticism 45%

Aristotelianism rejects both asceticism and intemperance: pleasure is not the good, but it completes activity as beauty completes youth. Bodily pleasures are legitimate so long as they keep to the [mean](../glossary/golden-mean.md) set by temperance, yielding a very moderate position, barely on the accepting side.

*Sources : Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, X, 4-5 · Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, III, 10-11*

### [Ideal and reality](../axes/political-ideal.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Political realism 80% · Utopianism 20%

Against the Platonic ideal city, Aristotelianism seeks the best achievable constitution for a given people under real conditions. The most stable regime is the mixed one resting on a broad middle class, the politeia, which tempers the excesses of democracy and oligarchy: a political realism attentive to circumstances.

*Sources : Aristotle, Politics, IV, 11*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mastery 70% · Stewardship 20% · Member among others 10%

For Aristotelianism the natural order is hierarchical and goal-directed: nothing in nature is in vain, and plants exist for the sake of animals, animals for the sake of human beings. This teleological anthropocentrism, in which the rest of nature is ordered to human use, leans clearly toward mastery, without thereby denying that the human being remains a living thing within that order.

*Sources : Aristotle, Politics, I, 8*

### [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Anthropocentrism 90% · Pathocentrism 10%

Moral consideration is reserved for beings endowed with reason: justice and friendship, properly speaking, can exist only among humans, for animals, lacking [logos](../glossary/logos.md), do not share in the political community. This moral anthropocentrism, extended by scholasticism, places Aristotelianism firmly at the first pole, animals entering the circle only marginally.

*Sources : Aristotle, Politics, I, 8; Nicomachean Ethics, VIII, 11*

### [Nature of art](../axes/art-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Mimesis 80% · Creation 20%

The *[Poetics](../works/poetics-aristotle.md)* conceives art as mimēsis, the imitation of human action: tragedy represents people in action. This imitation is no servile copy, for the poet portrays what might happen according to probability and necessity, yet it remains a representation of the real rather than creation ex nihilo, hence a position on the mimēsis side.

*Sources : Aristotle, Poetics, ch. 1-4 and 9*

### [Universals](../axes/universals.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Realism about universals 80% · Nominalism 20%

That several men are called « man » presupposes a real universal, not a mere name: science bears on it. But to make it a separate Idea, in [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md)'s manner, raises the third-man objection, for one would then need a further form to explain what the sensible man and Man-himself have in common, and so on without end. Aristotelianism therefore keeps the realism of universals while rendering them [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md): genus and species exist only in individuals, and it is the intellect that abstracts them. Neither nominalism nor subsisting Ideas.

*Sources : Aristotle, Metaphysics, Z; Categories*

### [Being or having a body](../axes/embodiment.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

The body as instrument 50% · The lived body 50%

If the soul were a separate substance housed in the body, as for [Plato](../philosophers/plato.md), the evident unity of the living thing would be unintelligible, and so would the question of why this soul animates this body. [Hylomorphism](../glossary/hylomorphism.md) dissolves the difficulty: every substance is a compound of matter and form, and the soul is precisely the form, the actuality, of the organized body, as sight is the actuality of the eye. To ask whether soul and body are one, Aristotle concludes, is as idle as to ask it of the wax and its imprint: the human being is neither an imprisoned spirit nor a mere machine, but a single animated reality.

*Sources : Aristotle, On the Soul, II*
