# Environmental Ethics

*1949 – ?*

A current born in the twentieth century from the ecological crisis and the animal question, which contests the dominant anthropocentrism: moral worth does not stop at humanity. [Leopold](../philosophers/leopold.md) and his land ethic extend the moral community to soils, waters and species; [Næss](../philosophers/naess.md) grounds deep ecology in the intrinsic worth of all living things and the human as one member among others; [Singer](../philosophers/singer.md) and [Regan](../philosophers/regan.md) carry the animal cause, one in the name of suffering, the other of rights; [Schweitzer](../philosophers/schweitzer.md) preaches reverence for life, [Jonas](../philosophers/jonas.md) a responsibility toward future generations. A family shot through with tensions, from concern for the sentient individual to concern for ecological wholes, it shares the refusal to treat nature as a mere resource.

**Key figures** : [Leopold](../philosophers/leopold.md) · [Næss](../philosophers/naess.md) · [Singer](../philosophers/singer.md)

## Core positions

1. [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md) : **Moral worth does not stop at humanity: the [circle](../axes/moral-circle.md) must open to sentient animals, to the living, even to ecosystems.**
2. [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md) : **The human is not nature's [master](../axes/human-place-nature.md) but a [member](../axes/human-place-nature.md) of the community of the living, or its responsible steward.**

## Positions

### [Moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Ecocentrism 45% · Pathocentrism 35% · Biocentrism 20%

The founding gesture of the current is to widen the [moral circle](../axes/moral-circle.md) beyond humans alone, against the tradition's [anthropocentrism](../axes/moral-circle.md). Where to draw the new boundary divides the family: to every [sentient](../axes/moral-circle.md) being capable of suffering for [Singer](../philosophers/singer.md), to every [living](../axes/moral-circle.md) being for [Schweitzer](../philosophers/schweitzer.md), to [ecological wholes](../axes/moral-circle.md) themselves for [Leopold](../philosophers/leopold.md) and [Næss](../philosophers/naess.md). All reject the idea that the human alone counts.

*Sources : Singer, Animal Liberation (1975) · Leopold, A Sand County Almanac (1949)*

### [Humanity's place in nature](../axes/human-place-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Member among others 60% · Stewardship 40%

Against the program of [Descartes](../philosophers/descartes.md) and [Bacon](../philosophers/bacon.md), the human as "master and possessor of nature", the current refuses [mastery](../axes/human-place-nature.md) as the primary relation to the natural world. It divides between two answers: the human as a [member among others](../axes/human-place-nature.md) of a community of the living, with no privileged position, in [Næss](../philosophers/naess.md) and [Leopold](../philosophers/leopold.md); or the responsible [steward](../axes/human-place-nature.md) whom their very power obliges, in [Jonas](../philosophers/jonas.md).

*Sources : Næss, Ecology, Community and Lifestyle (1976) · Jonas, The Imperative of Responsibility (1979)*

## Influenced by

- **[Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md)** (reinterprets) : Environmental ethics inherits from [Romanticism](../movements/romanticism.md), often without saying so, its revolt against nature reduced to a machine and a stock of resources: the living, sublime nature, worthy of respect for itself, which ecology turns into a moral value and no longer merely an aesthetic one.
