# Marxism

*1848 – ?*

A tradition founded by [Karl Marx](../philosophers/marx.md) and Friedrich Engels, from the Communist Manifesto (1848) to Capital (1867), at once an analysis of capitalism, a philosophy of [history](../axes/history-meaning.md), and a project of emancipation. Marxism is born of a twofold inverted inheritance: [Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md)'s dialectic, which it "sets back on its feet" by tearing it from idealism, and English political economy (Smith, Ricardo), of which it makes a critique to expose exploitation within it. To the problem of the workers' misery of industrialization it opposes not a moral appeal but a science of the contradictions of capital. After Marx the current split into rival lineages: social democracy (Kautsky, Bernstein), Leninism and its twentieth-century regimes, and finally Western Marxism (Gramsci and his hegemony, the Frankfurt School, Althusser), which extends its critique of culture and ideology far from any state power. Few bodies of thought have so marked political history, historiography, and the social sciences.

**Key figures** : [Karl Marx](../philosophers/marx.md) · [Friedrich Engels](../philosophers/engels.md) · [Antonio Gramsci](../philosophers/gramsci.md)

## Core positions

1. [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md) : **It is not consciousness that determines life, but life that determines consciousness: ideas, law and religion are only reflections of the material base.**
2. [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md) : **The history of all society is the history of class struggle: its modes of production succeed one another through the contradiction between productive forces and social relations, all the way to the classless society.**
3. [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md) : **Profit arises from the surplus value extracted from unpaid labor: exploitation is not an abuse but the normal law of capital, which only the abolition of private ownership of the means of production can lift.**
4. [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md) : **Labor ought to realize the human being; under capital it cripples him: the worker is alienated from his product, his activity, his species-being and from others.**
5. [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md) : **The philosophers have only interpreted the world: the point is to change it, through the revolutionary [praxis](../glossary/praxis.md) of a class and not through reform or utopia.**

## Positions

### [The meaning of suffering](../axes/suffering-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Abolitionism 90% · Fecundity and redemption 10%

Marxism holds the suffering of the exploited to be a historical product of oppression, not an ordeal to be sanctified: religious consolation, “the opium of the people”, is only its veil. The point is not to give misery a meaning but to abolish it by overturning the social relations that engender it, emancipation being the end of avoidable suffering.

*Sources : Marx, Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right · Marx, Capital*

### [The meaning of life](../axes/life-meaning.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructed meaning 75% · Teleology and providence 20% · Absurdism 5%

Marxism denies any providential finality and locates meaning in history as made by human beings: it is in liberated labor, struggle and the project of a classless society that existence finds its scope. An immanent direction animates history, but it proceeds from material contradictions and collective action, not from a transcendent design.

*Sources : Marx, Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844 · Marx and Engels, The Communist Manifesto*

### [The highest good](../axes/sovereign-good.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Eudaimonism 80% · Hedonism 10% · Virtue / moral perfectionism 10%

Marxism locates the good in human flourishing: not pleasure or private virtue, but the free development of everyone's faculties and needs, made possible by the abolition of alienation and exploitation. The society aimed at is one where the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all.

*Sources : Marx, Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844 · Marx and Engels, The Communist Manifesto*

### [Relation to desire](../axes/desire.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Affirmation 55% · Reasoned selection 40% · Extinction and detachment 5%

Marxism rejects both asceticism and mere consumption: it denounces the mutilation of needs under capitalism and makes emancipation the free development of human needs and powers. Desire, far from an evil, is a wealth to be unfolded collectively, once the conditions that reduce it to survival or to having are abolished.

*Sources : Marx, Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844 · Marx, Grundrisse*

### [Essence and existence](../axes/essence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Existentialism 75% · Essentialism 25%

Marxism historicizes human nature: there is no fixed essence inherent in the individual, man is the product of social relations that change with modes of production. What we are is not a prior given but the transformable result of a material history.

*Sources : Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, VI · Marx and Engels, The German Ideology*

### [Source of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-source.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Empiricism 75% · Rationalism 25%

Historical materialism derives knowledge from material conditions and social activity, not from first ideas: it is life that determines consciousness, not the reverse. Against passive empiricism as much as against idealism, knowledge is worked out in [praxis](../glossary/praxis.md), the practical, transforming relation of human beings to their world.

*Sources : Marx and Engels, The German Ideology · Marx, Theses on Feuerbach*

### [Relation to certainty](../axes/certainty.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Dogmatism 70% · Skepticism 30%

**Scientific socialism** sets itself against utopian socialism by claiming knowledge of the objective laws of historical development: social reality is intelligible and certain, not given over to doubt. The truth of a thought is, besides, proven in [praxis](../glossary/praxis.md), by transforming the world, and not in mere theoretical contemplation. Marxism thus sets aside [skepticism](../glossary/skepticism.md) and resignation, entrusting to critique and practice the power to dispel ideological illusions.

*Sources : Engels, Socialism: Utopian and Scientific · Marx, Theses on Feuerbach*

### [The meaning of history](../axes/history-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Directional meaning 93% · Contingency 8%

The mainspring of historical change is located in a material contradiction: at a certain stage the productive forces come into conflict with the relations of production that constrain them, and that tension is resolved by a social, usually revolutionary, transformation. History thereby ceases to be a chaos of events or the work of great men: it obeys a discernible logic in which each mode of production (feudal, bourgeois, communist) prepares its own supersession. This turns against the idealism of history (that of Providence or of Spirit) an explanation drawn from economics, and grounds the forecast of a classless society not on a wish but on a "law of social motion."

> “The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles.”, The Communist Manifesto

*Sources : Marx, A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy, preface (1859) · Marx and Engels, The Communist Manifesto (1848)*

### [Nature of mind and reality](../axes/reality-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Materialism 90% · Idealism 5% · Dualism 5%

The starting point is not an idea but a fact: humans must first produce their means of subsistence before they can think, pray, or legislate. From this Marx "inverts" [Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md)'s dialectic, in which Spirit unfolded itself in the world: it is not the idea that begets the real, but material activity that begets ideas. Law, religion, and philosophy become superstructures, reflections of the economic base and not first causes. The target is not only [Hegel](../philosophers/hegel.md) but every idealism that believes it can change the world by changing ideas: for Marx, that is to mistake the reflection for the source.

*Sources : Marx and Engels, The German Ideology (1845-1846) · Engels, Ludwig Feuerbach and the End of Classical German Philosophy (1888)*

### [Distributive justice](../axes/distributive-justice.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Egalitarianism 95% · Libertarianism 5%

The argument begins from an analysis, not from indignation: the wage pays the value of labor power (what it costs to reproduce it), but the worker produces more than that, and this surplus, surplus value, is captured by the owner of the means of production. Exploitation is therefore not an abuse or a contingent injustice but the normal mechanism of profit, concealed beneath the appearance of a free and fair exchange of wages for work. This rejects both classical political economy, which took that exchange to be just, and market libertarianism: so long as private ownership of the means of production persists, formal equality masks a real domination that only a classless society can lift.

> “From each according to his ability, to each according to his needs.”, Critique of the Gotha Programme

*Sources : Marx, Critique of the Gotha Programme (1875) · Marx, Capital, vol. I, on surplus value*

### [Meaning of work](../axes/work-meaning.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Alienation 70% · Fulfillment 25% · Necessity, leisure as the end 5%

Labor is held to be the activity in which the human being objectifies and realizes himself: he imprints his mark on nature and recognizes himself in it. This is what makes the diagnosis so severe under capital: alienation is not mere drudgery or low pay but the turning of that activity against the one who performs it, along four dispossessions: of the product, which belongs to another and confronts him as an alien power; of the labor process, undergone rather than chosen; of his species-being, what makes the human a creative being; and of other people, reduced to competitors. Marxism thereby shifts the question of labor from reward to the very form of the activity: even well paid, wage labor remains crippling so long as the worker masters neither its end nor its process.

*Sources : Marx, Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844, "Estranged Labour" · Marx, Capital, vol. I*

### [Tradition and transformation](../axes/conservatism-progressism.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Progressivism 98% · Conservatism 3%

If exploitation lies in the very structure of the relations of production, then reform can only manage it: only their overthrow abolishes it. Hence the revolutionary character of Marxist progressivism and its refusal of the alternatives: neither awaiting a moral evolution, nor the ideal cities of utopian socialism, nor mere philosophical critique will do, since to interpret the world leaves it intact. Thought itself has worth only as [praxis](../glossary/praxis.md), a moment of real transformation borne by the class that has "nothing to lose but its chains." Philosophy ceases to be contemplation and becomes a weapon, as the eleventh thesis on Feuerbach sums up.

> “The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it.”, Theses on Feuerbach, XI

*Sources : Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, XI (1845) · Marx and Engels, The Communist Manifesto (1848)*

### [Individual and society](../axes/individual-society.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Holism 93% · Individualism 7%

To start from the individual in order to explain society is to take for a starting point what is in fact a result: the "isolated, self-sufficient" individual of liberalism and the social contract is itself a late product of a given society, not its cause. Marx reverses the order of explanation: the human is understood from class and place in the relations of production, for the human essence has no seat in the individual but in the ensemble of social relations. This holism is not a collectivist preference but a thesis of method, aimed against the abstract individualism of political economy and natural right.

*Sources : Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, VI (1845) · Marx, Introduction to the Critique of Political Economy (Grundrisse, 1857)*

### [Nature and culture](../axes/human-nature.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Culturalism 90% · Naturalism 10%

"It is not the consciousness of men that determines their being, but, on the contrary, their social being that determines their consciousness": there is no fixed human nature given outside history. Human character, values, and needs are shaped by social conditions and the mode of production, which anchors Marxism on the side of culturalism.

*Sources : Marx, A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy, preface (1859) · Marx and Engels, The German Ideology (1845-1846)*

### [Existence of God](../axes/god-existence.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Atheism 88% · Theism 13%

Marx's atheism does not stop at denying God: it asks why humans need one. Religion is neither a mere error nor a priestly imposture, but at once the expression of real distress and the protest against it, an imaginary consolation that relieves suffering while making it bearable, and so lasting. Hence the decisive shift: to criticize heaven is useless unless one criticizes earth, that is, the social relations that make religion necessary. Abolishing the illusion presupposes abolishing the conditions that call for it, which ranges Marxism on the side of atheist materialism.

> “Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world, the soul of soulless conditions. It is the opium of the people.”, Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right, introduction

*Sources : Marx, Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right, introduction (1844)*

### [Where the sacred resides](../axes/transcendence.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Immanence 95% · Transcendence 5%

Consistent with its materialism, Marxism rejects any world beyond: there is no afterlife nor transcendent ground of values, only the [immanent](../glossary/immanence.md) history of humans producing their conditions of existence. Emancipation is expected from humans' earthly action, not from a salvation come from elsewhere.

*Sources : Marx and Engels, The German Ideology (1845-1846) · Marx, Theses on Feuerbach (1845)*

### [Relation to technology](../axes/technology.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Techno-optimism 73% · Techno-criticism 28%

Marx holds the development of the productive forces to be the motor of historical progress: machinery and large-scale industry create the material conditions for a society of abundance freed from want. Yet under capital, that same machinery intensifies exploitation and alienation: the optimism bears on technology freed from capitalist relations, not on technology as such.

*Sources : Marx, Capital, vol. I, chapter on machinery and modern industry · Marx and Engels, The Communist Manifesto (1848)*

### [Freedom of the will](../axes/freedom.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Hard determinism 68% · Free will 32%

"Men make their own history, but they do not make it as they please, under circumstances they have not chosen": human action is real, yet tightly [determined](../glossary/determinism.md) by inherited economic structures. The position leans toward social conditioning without denying all initiative, placing it short of a strict determinism.

*Sources : Marx, The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte (1852)*

### [Status of knowledge](../axes/knowledge-status.md)

*positioned · inferable · minor*

Constructivism 73% · Scientific realism 28%

The theory of ideology holds that the ruling ideas of an age are the ideas of its ruling class: consciousness is socially situated and tends to disguise the real relations. Knowledge is not a neutral contemplation but is shaped by class interests, which brings Marxism close to a social constructivism, without renouncing a truth unveiled by critique.

*Sources : Marx and Engels, The German Ideology (1845-1846)*

### [Interiority](../axes/interiority.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · minor*

Primacy of action 93% · The examined life 7%

Marxism resolutely privileges praxis: the truth of a thought is proven in practice, and philosophy is worth only what it does as a force of transformation. The eleventh thesis on Feuerbach disqualifies contemplative interpretation in favor of collective action directed at social change.

*Sources : Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, II and XI (1845)*

### [Social identity](../axes/social-identity.md)

*positioned · explicit in the texts · major*

Social realism 75% · Social construction 25%

What defines a class is neither rank, nor income, nor a sense of belonging, but the objective place one holds in the relations of production: whether or not one owns the means of production, whether one sells or buys labor power. Social identity is thus not a choice or a label one adopts, but a material condition that precedes the awareness one has of it, and that can remain hidden until a class has constituted itself "for itself." This rejects both the naturalization of hierarchies and the liberal picture of a society of interchangeable individuals.

*Sources : Marx, Manifesto; Preface to A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy*

### [Political liberty](../axes/political-liberty.md)

*positioned · inferable · major*

Capability freedom 60% · Positive liberty 40%

Formal rights (to vote, to contract, to own) leave economic compulsion intact: the worker "free" to sell his labor power is just as free to starve if he refuses. Marxism therefore holds political emancipation to be real but insufficient, and sets against it a concrete freedom, the effective capacity for self-realization, which presupposes abolishing the rule of economic necessity and alienation. Freedom is not a legal status but a material condition to be won: the free development of each becomes possible only where that of all is too, which subordinates political liberty to the transformation of the relations of production.

*Sources : Marx, 1844 Manuscripts; Capital, III*
